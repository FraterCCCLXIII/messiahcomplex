# The Septuagint as transmission mechanism

## Context

- **Audience**: readers who want to understand *how* Iranian-influenced Jewish eschatology became the theology of a Greco-Roman religion — the specific linguistic and conceptual bridge.
- **Question**: what does the Septuagint translation do to Jewish eschatological vocabulary, and why does that make it the crucial mechanism in the Iran→Judaism→Christianity transmission chain?
- **Scope**: the translation process, its Alexandrian context, the key vocabulary choices, Philo as the synthesis figure, and Paul's dependence on the LXX as his Bible.

---

## 1. What the Septuagint is

The **Septuagint** (abbreviated LXX, from the Latin *septuaginta*, "seventy") is the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible produced by Jewish scholars in **Alexandria, Egypt**, beginning approximately **250 BCE** under Ptolemy II Philadelphus and extending through the 2nd century BCE for the remaining books.

**The Letter of Aristeas** (~2nd cent. BCE) gives a legendary account: 72 Jewish scholars (six from each tribe) were summoned to Alexandria and produced an identical translation independently, proving divine inspiration. Modern scholarship regards this as an origin legend; the translation was actually produced by multiple translators over roughly a century, with varying quality and theological tendencies across different books.

**Why it matters for the transmission argument**: the LXX is the Bible of:
- Every Greek-speaking Jew in the Diaspora from ~250 BCE onward
- Paul — who quotes scripture almost always from the LXX, rarely from the Hebrew
- The authors of the New Testament — the majority of OT citations in the NT follow the LXX text, not the Hebrew
- The early Church fathers
- The Council of Nicaea

The LXX is the **single most important transmission vehicle** by which Second Temple Jewish eschatology — already shaped by four centuries of Achaemenid contact — entered Greco-Roman intellectual culture. It is not a neutral translation. It is a **theological document** that made specific choices encoding specific interpretive traditions, and those choices became the vocabulary of Christian theology.

## 2. The vocabulary chain: Persian → Hebrew/Aramaic → Greek → Christian theology

The table below traces the key theological terms from their Persian-era origins through the LXX translation into Christian theological vocabulary. This is the chain in linguistic form.

| Concept | Persian/Iranian origin | Hebrew/Aramaic | LXX Greek | NT/Christian use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **The "anointed" savior figure** | Cyrus called *māšîaḥ* in Isaiah 45:1 | *māšîaḥ* (anointed one) | ***christos*** (anointed) | "Jesus *Christos*" — the title that defines the religion |
| **The divine name** | — | *YHWH* (the personal name) | ***kyrios*** (Lord) | "Jesus is *kyrios*" (Phil. 2:11; Rom. 10:9) — the earliest Christian confession |
| **The eschatological garden** | Old Persian *paridaida-* (walled garden) → Jewish *pardēs* | *pardēs* (Persian loanword) | ***paradeisos*** | Luke 23:43; 2 Cor. 12:4; Rev. 2:7 — "paradise" |
| **The divine savior** | Zoroastrian *saošyant* (one who brings benefit) | No single Hebrew word; expressed through *yāšaʿ* (save) | ***sōtēr*** (savior) | "Jesus *sōtēr*" — applied directly to Christ in Titus 2:13, 2 Pet. 1:1 |
| **The virgin mother** | — | *ʿalmāh* (young woman; Isaiah 7:14) | ***parthenos*** (virgin) | Matthew 1:23 — the *parthenos* translation drives the virgin birth doctrine |
| **Divine wisdom as mediator** | — | *Ḥokmāh* (Wisdom, Proverbs 8) | ***Sophia*** / *Logos* associations | Proverbs 8 in LXX: Wisdom as pre-existent agent of creation → directly feeds Philo and John 1 |
| **The divine mystery/secret** | Iranian *rāz* (mystery/secret) | *rāz* (Aramaic loanword in Daniel) | ***mystērion*** (mystery) | Paul: "the *mystērion* hidden for ages" (Col. 1:26; Eph. 3:4–6); "mystery of Christ" |
| **The divine decree/law** | Iranian *dāta-* (decree/law) | *dāt* (Persian loanword in Esther, Daniel) | *dogma*, *nomos* (context-dependent) | "Decrees" (*dogmata*) in Eph. 2:15; Col. 2:14 |

**The *christos* translation is the most consequential single decision in the history of Western religion.** When the LXX translators chose *christos* ("anointed one") to render *māšîaḥ*, they created a Greek title that:
1. Carried the full weight of Jewish messianic expectation (Davidic king, end-of-age restorer)
2. Was ready-made for application to Jesus of Nazareth by his followers
3. Eventually became a *proper name* rather than a title ("Jesus Christ" rather than "Jesus the Anointed One")

The chain runs: Persian imperial Cyrus called *māšîaḥ* (Isaiah 45:1) → *māšîaḥ* becomes an eschatological title → LXX renders it *christos* → "Jesus *Christos*" becomes the founding confession of Christianity. Persian imperial ideology, Jewish eschatology, and Greek vocabulary are all present in that two-word phrase.

## 3. The *parthenos* translation and its consequences

Isaiah 7:14 in Hebrew reads: *"Behold, the *ʿalmāh* shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel."*

The Hebrew word *ʿalmāh* means "young woman" — it does not specifically mean "virgin" (*betûlāh* is the Hebrew word that specifically means "virgin"). The LXX translators rendered *ʿalmāh* as ***parthenos*** — Greek for "virgin."

This is a **translation interpretation**, not a translation of the literal Hebrew. Whether it reflects a pre-existing Jewish interpretive tradition, a theological choice by the translators, or simply a linguistic equivalence that happened to be available, the consequence is direct: Matthew 1:22–23 cites the LXX version ("a *parthenos* shall conceive") as a fulfillment prophecy for the virginal conception of Jesus. **The virgin birth doctrine rests on a translation decision made in Alexandria ~250 BCE.**

**Tier**: Tier A — the linguistic facts are not contested. Whether the LXX translators *intended* a virgnity doctrine is debated; what is not debated is that Matthew read the LXX and built his account around it.

## 4. The Wisdom books and the Logos bridge

The LXX translation of the Wisdom literature — especially **Proverbs 8** and **Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) 24** — creates the conceptual bridge between Jewish eschatology and Greek philosophy that Philo and John's Gospel will later cross.

**Proverbs 8:22–30** in the LXX:
> "The Lord created me at the beginning of his work, the first of his acts of old. Ages ago I was set up, at the first, before the beginning of the earth... When he established the heavens, I was there... I was beside him, like a master workman, and I was daily his delight, rejoicing before him always."

This passage — in the LXX version — presents **Wisdom (Sophia/Ḥokmāh) as a pre-existent figure present at creation, beside God, as his agent in making the world**. This is not a late Hellenistic idea imposed onto Judaism; it is a Second Temple Jewish articulation of divine agency that the LXX makes available in Greek philosophical vocabulary.

**Sirach 24** (written ~190 BCE; part of the LXX but not the Hebrew canon) develops this further: Wisdom descends from the heavenly realm, searches the earth for a dwelling, and is told by God to dwell in Israel.

These passages are the **direct precursors to John 1:1**: "In the beginning was the Logos (*En archē ēn ho Logos*)." John's Prologue applies the LXX Wisdom tradition's imagery (pre-existent, beside God, agent of creation, descended to dwell among humans) to Jesus — the Logos who "became flesh" (*sarx egeneto*, 1:14). See [Logos doctrine and Neoplatonic Christology](logos-doctrine-neoplatonic-christology.md).

## 5. Philo of Alexandria: the synthesis in one person

**Philo of Alexandria** (~20 BCE – 50 CE) is the single most important figure for understanding how Jewish eschatology and Platonic philosophy merged into the conceptual world that early Christianity inhabited.

Philo was:
- A devout **Diaspora Jew** who read scripture exclusively in the LXX
- A sophisticated **Middle Platonist** who read Plato's *Timaeus* alongside the Torah
- The person who **systematically identified** the Jewish *Logos* (divine Word/Reason) with:
  - Plato's *Demiurge* (the craftsman who shapes matter in *Timaeus*)
  - The Hebrew Bible's divine *Wisdom* (*Ḥokmāh* / LXX *Sophia*)
  - The Stoic *logos spermatikos* (the rational principle pervading the cosmos)

### Philo's Logos attributes — the vocabulary Paul and John inherit

| Philonic Logos attribute | Source text | NT parallel |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Image (*eikōn*) of God" | *On the Making of the World* 24 | Col. 1:15: "He is the image (*eikōn*) of the invisible God" |
| "Firstborn (*prōtogonos*) of God" | *On the Confusion of Tongues* 146 | Col. 1:15: "firstborn (*prōtotokos*) of all creation" |
| "Son of God" | *On the Confusion of Tongues* 147 | Throughout NT |
| "High Priest before God" | *On Dreams* 1.215 | Hebrews 4:14: "a great high priest" |
| "Paraclete / Intercessor" | *On the Life of Moses* 2.134 | John 14:16: "another Paraclete (*paraklētos*)" |
| "Instrument (*organon*) of creation" | *On the Cherubim* 127 | Col. 1:16: "all things were created through him" |
| "Beginning (*archē*) and name of God" | *On the Confusion of Tongues* 146 | John 1:1: "In the beginning (*En archē*) was the Logos" |

Philo did not know Paul. Paul probably did not read Philo. But **both inhabited the same conceptual world** created by the LXX tradition, Alexandrian Judaism, and Middle Platonism. When Paul writes about Christ as "image of God," "firstborn of all creation," and the agent through whom all things were created (Col. 1:15–17), he is drawing on exactly the same conceptual vocabulary Philo used for the Logos — because both are heirs to the LXX Wisdom tradition.

## 6. Paul's LXX Bible — the chain in action

Paul's dependence on the LXX is demonstrable by simple text comparison: **the majority of Paul's scriptural quotations follow the LXX text, not the Hebrew Masoretic text**, even in places where the two differ significantly.

**Example 1 — Romans 3:10–18**: Paul's catena of "no one is righteous" quotations combines Psalms 14:1–3, 5:9, 140:3, 10:7, Isaiah 59:7–8, and Psalm 36:1 — all in LXX form. The argument depends on the LXX's specific wording.

**Example 2 — Galatians 3:13**: Paul cites Deuteronomy 21:23 in LXX form ("cursed is everyone who hangs on a tree") to argue that Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse himself. The Hebrew text has a different nuance; it is the LXX form that enables Paul's argument.

**Example 3 — Romans 10:13**: "Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord (*kyrios*) will be saved" — Joel 2:32 (LXX). Paul applies this to Jesus, which works precisely because the LXX used *kyrios* for YHWH. Paul is saying: the God-name of the Hebrew Bible, rendered as *kyrios* in the LXX, now refers to Jesus. The entire argument is a LXX-dependent move.

**The chain in full**: Iranian concepts (*pardēs*, *rāz*, resurrection, cosmic tribunal) → enter Jewish literature during Achaemenid contact (Persian period) → expressed in Greek via LXX translation choices (*paradeisos*, *mystērion*, *christos*, *kyrios*, *sōtēr*) → available to Diaspora Jews (Philo) and Hellenistic Jewish missionaries (Paul) → become the foundational vocabulary of Christian theology.

## 7. The LXX and Islam

The LXX chain does not end with Christianity. The Quran's term ***Firdaws*** (paradise, Surah 18:107; 23:11) is a loanword that preserves the entire chain in a single word:

Old Persian *paridaida-* → Aramaic/Hebrew *pardēs* → LXX Greek *paradeisos* → Syriac/Arabic *firdaws* → Quranic *Firdaws*

This is the vocabulary of Persian imperial gardens arriving intact in the Quran via a 1,000-year transmission chain. It is the most economical demonstration that the entire argument is not speculative but philologically documented.
