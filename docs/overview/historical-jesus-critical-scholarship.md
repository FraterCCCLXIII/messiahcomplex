# Historical-Critical Scholarship on the New Testament

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers who have worked through the [Paul pages](/docs/overview/paul-celestial-christology), [Canon as imperial artifact](/docs/overview/canon-as-imperial-artifact), or [Bardiya: Jesus historicity](/docs/bardiya/jesus-historicity), and want a single orientation to the academic-critical field behind those discussions.
- **Question:** What can historical method actually establish about Jesus of Nazareth and the origins of Christianity—as distinct from what theology claims?
- **Scope:** NT historical-critical scholarship (source criticism, form criticism, redaction criticism, textual criticism, and related fields). Not apologetics, not mythicism; the aim is to map what mainstream academic scholarship debates, tier by tier.
- **Epistemic status:** The *existence* of these problems is Tier A—they are the standard curriculum of academic NT study. The *conclusions* scholars draw from them range from Tier A (no autographs survive) to Tier C (specific diffusion claims). This page maps the terrain without collapsing the distinction.

---

## Consensus snapshot

| Claim | Tier |
|---|---|
| A historical Jesus existed and was crucified under Pontius Pilate | A — near-universal scholarly consensus |
| Paul's letters are the earliest textual witnesses (~50–62 CE) | A |
| The Gospels are theologically shaped documents, not neutral biography | A |
| The Gospel of Mark is the earliest Gospel (~70 CE) | A — Markan priority consensus |
| Several letters attributed to Paul are pseudonymous | A/B — consensus on the Pastorals; debated for Colossians, Ephesians |
| Q (a lost sayings source) existed | B — working hypothesis of the majority; a significant minority rejects it |
| Specific Persian or Greco-Roman ideas were borrowed by early Christians | B/C — depends on the specific claim and proposed mechanism |
| The Trinity was revealed intact in the New Testament | Not a historical-critical consensus position; contested theologically |

## The twelve major problem areas

### 1. The historical Jesus problem

No writings from Jesus himself survive. The earliest sources (Paul's letters, ~50s CE) were written roughly twenty years after the crucifixion and focus almost entirely on theology, not biography. The narrative Gospels (Mark ~70 CE; Matthew and Luke ~80–90 CE; John ~90–100 CE) were composed forty to seventy years after the events they describe, drawing on oral tradition and community reflection rather than eyewitness notes.

This creates a fundamental reconstruction problem. Scholars working on a "minimal historical Jesus" broadly agree on: Jewish Galilean preacher, disciple of John the Baptist, active c. 28–30 CE, crucified under Pontius Pilate. Beyond that, competing portraits diverge sharply:

- Albert Schweitzer (*The Quest of the Historical Jesus*, 1906): Jesus as an apocalyptic prophet who expected an imminent divine intervention—and was mistaken.
- John Dominic Crossan (*The Historical Jesus*, 1991): Jesus as a peasant Cynic-style wisdom teacher and social reformer.
- E. P. Sanders (*The Historical Figure of Jesus*, 1993): Jesus as a Jewish restorationist within mainstream Second Temple expectations.
- Paula Fredriksen (*Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews*, 1999): Jesus intelligible within Jewish apocalypticism, crucified for political rather than theological reasons.

There is no scholarly consensus on the reconstruction beyond the minimal core.

**Tier:** The existence of a historical Jesus is Tier A. Any specific reconstruction of his biography, teachings, or intentions is Tier B at best, and most details are Tier C.

---

### 2. The Synoptic problem and the Q hypothesis

Matthew, Mark, and Luke share large amounts of material—sometimes word-for-word identical in Greek—yet they also diverge in significant ways. The dominant scholarly explanation (the "two-source hypothesis") holds that:

1. Mark was written first and used by both Matthew and Luke.
2. Matthew and Luke share additional material (roughly 235 verses of teaching material) not found in Mark; this is attributed to a hypothetical lost source called **Q** (*Quelle*, German for "source").

Q has never been found as a physical document. Some scholars (notably Michael Goulder and Mark Goodacre) reject Q entirely, arguing that Luke simply used Matthew. Others propose four-source models. The Synoptic problem remains technically unsolved, though Markan priority with Q is the working model for most NT scholars.

**Why it matters:** If Q existed, early Christianity had a strong "wisdom sayings" strand that predated the narrative Gospels. If it did not, the editorial decisions of Luke (or Matthew) shaped what looks like independent attestation.

**Tier:** Markan priority = Tier A. Q as a working hypothesis = Tier B. Specific reconstructions of Q's theology or origin = Tier C.

---

### 3. Authorship and pseudonymity

Traditional attribution of NT texts to apostolic authors is disputed across the board by modern scholarship:

- **Gospel of Matthew**: Almost certainly not written by the apostle Matthew. The author depends on Mark (writing ~40 years after the crucifixion) and shows no signs of being an eyewitness. The attribution is a later ecclesiastical tradition.
- **Pauline letters**: Seven letters are widely accepted as authentically Pauline (Romans, 1–2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon). The remainder are disputed:
  - **Probably pseudonymous** (consensus): 1–2 Timothy, Titus (the "Pastoral Epistles")—different vocabulary, ecclesiology, and historical setting from the undisputed letters.
  - **Significantly disputed**: Ephesians and Colossians—elevated theological style, cosmic Christology, and structural differences from the undisputed letters have led many scholars to attribute them to a Pauline school rather than Paul himself.
  - **2 Thessalonians**: Debated; the eschatology appears to contradict 1 Thessalonians.
- **The Johannine corpus**: The Gospel of John, the three Johannine epistles, and Revelation are traditionally attributed to "John the Apostle," but most scholars treat them as products of distinct communities or authors. The author of Revelation explicitly names himself "John of Patmos"—but whether this is the apostle is contested.

**Why it matters:** If authoritative texts are pseudonymous, it raises questions about the basis of apostolic authority and how early communities established which voices counted as normative.

**Tier:** Pseudonymous Pastorals = Tier A in critical scholarship. Disputed status of Colossians/Ephesians = Tier A/B (real scholarly debate). Theological implications of pseudonymity = outside historical-critical scope.

---

### 4. Development of doctrine

Christian theology did not emerge fully formed from a single revelatory event. Key doctrines developed through centuries of debate, and their development is well-documented:

**The Trinity:** The word "Trinity" does not appear in the New Testament. The theological formulation—one God in three coequal, coeternal persons (Father, Son, Holy Spirit)—was formally defined at the **Council of Nicaea (325 CE)** and refined through subsequent councils (Constantinople 381, Chalcedon 451). Earlier NT texts present a more fluid picture:

- In Paul's letters, Christ is exalted and pre-existent but the precise relationship to God is not the Nicene formula.
- In Mark, Jesus is adopted at baptism (a reading later called "adoptionist").
- In John, the Logos is "with God" and "is God" in a formulation that became central to later Trinitarian theology—but still resists simple equation with Nicene categories.

**Christology:** The question of whether Jesus was fully divine, fully human, or some mixture was debated for centuries and resolved (provisionally) only by imperial-era councils. Subordinationist, adoptionist, docetic, and Arian views all had substantial early Christian communities. See also [Canon as imperial artifact](/docs/overview/canon-as-imperial-artifact) for how these debates intersected with political power.

**Why it matters:** If core doctrines are fourth-century formulations rather than first-century givens, Christianity looks less like a deposit of revelation and more like an evolving theological project shaped by historical forces.

**Tier:** Development of Trinitarian doctrine through councils = Tier A. How to interpret that development theologically = outside historical-critical scope.

---

### 5. Competing early Christianities

What we call "orthodox Christianity" represents one strand among many early movements, most of which were eventually declared heretical. Bart D. Ehrman's framing—that orthodoxy is "the version that won"—reflects a broad scholarly consensus about early diversity, if not always his specific conclusions:

- **Gnosticism**: Emphasized secret knowledge (*gnosis*) for salvation; the material world as created by a lesser, ignorant deity (the Demiurge); the divine spark trapped in matter needing release. The Nag Hammadi library (discovered 1945) revealed texts like the *Gospel of Thomas* and *Gospel of Philip*.
- **Marcionites**: Followers of Marcion (c. 85–160 CE), who argued that the God of the Hebrew Bible was a different, inferior deity from the Father of Jesus. Marcion created one of the earliest NT canons, rejecting the Hebrew Bible and accepting only a version of Luke and ten Pauline letters.
- **Ebionites**: Jewish Christians who maintained Torah observance and rejected the virgin birth and pre-existence of Jesus, viewing him as a righteous human prophet adopted by God at baptism.
- **Docetists**: Held that Jesus only *appeared* to be human (Greek *dokein*, "to seem"); his suffering and death were illusory. This view was vigorously opposed by Ignatius of Antioch (~107 CE).

Significant texts outside the eventual canon—*Gospel of Thomas*, *Gospel of Judas*, *Gospel of Mary*, *Shepherd of Hermas*, *Didache*, *1 Clement*—were read as authoritative scripture by some early communities.

**Why it matters:** If early Christianity was radically diverse, the question of "original Christianity" loses coherence; the canon and creed represent a fourth-century settlement, not a first-century given. See [Gnosticism and Mandaeanism](/docs/overview/gnosticism-and-mandaeanism) for the Gnostic strand in detail.

**Tier:** Early Christian diversity = Tier A. Whether "orthodoxy won by accident" (Ehrman's rhetorical framing) vs. internal theological coherence as a factor = interpretive debate (Tier B–C).

---

### 6. Canon formation

The New Testament canon we know was not fixed in the first century. The process unfolded over roughly three hundred years:

- **1st century**: Texts written (Paul's letters, Gospels, Revelation) but circulated as community letters; no fixed list.
- **Late 2nd century**: Irenaeus (~180 CE) is among the first to insist on four and only four Gospels.
- **Marcion's canon (~140 CE)** prompted catholic-leaning communities to define boundaries more explicitly—what counts as authoritative partly formed in reaction to what was being excluded.
- **Eusebius (~325 CE)** distinguishes "universally acknowledged," "disputed," and "spurious" books—showing the canon was still contested when Constantine commissioned fifty Bibles.
- **Athanasius's Festal Letter (367 CE)**: First document listing exactly the 27-book NT as we have it.

The political dimension is real: Constantine's commissioning of imperial Bibles coincided with the Nicene settlement. See [Canon as imperial artifact](/docs/overview/canon-as-imperial-artifact) for this pattern across multiple traditions.

**Why it matters:** The canon reflects decisions made by particular communities in particular political contexts; it was not a natural or inevitable selection.

**Tier:** The historical timeline of canon formation = Tier A. Causal weight of imperial politics vs. theological discernment in those decisions = Tier B–C.

### 7. Textual variants and manuscript tradition

No original manuscripts (*autographs*) of any New Testament text survive. All we have are copies of copies, the earliest substantial ones dating to the second and third centuries CE. The discipline of **textual criticism** attempts to reconstruct the most likely original text from thousands of manuscripts.

The scale is significant: there are roughly 5,800 Greek NT manuscripts, and scholars have catalogued approximately 400,000 textual variants. Most are trivial (spelling, word order). Some affect meaning.

**Famous cases:**

- **Mark 16:9–20 (the "Longer Ending")**: The two earliest complete manuscripts (Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus, 4th century) end at Mark 16:8—"they said nothing to anyone, for they were afraid." The longer ending, including resurrection appearances, snake handling, and speaking in tongues, is absent. Most scholars consider it a later addition.
- **John 7:53–8:11 (the "Woman Caught in Adultery")**: Absent from many early manuscripts; appears to have been added later, possibly from an oral tradition.
- **1 John 5:7–8 (the "Comma Johanneum")**: The explicit Trinitarian formula ("the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit: and these three are one") is absent from Greek manuscripts before the 4th century and from most early translations. It was inserted in later Latin manuscripts and found its way into the *Textus Receptus* and from there into the KJV.

**Why it matters:** Textual criticism is not a fringe concern—it is the necessary foundation of any serious NT reading. Editions like Nestle-Aland (NA28) represent the best current reconstruction of the earliest recoverable text and differ from the KJV in hundreds of places.

**Tier:** Existence of variants = Tier A. Most scholarly reconstructions (NA28) = Tier A/B. Specific interpretive conclusions about what variants reveal about early Christianity = Tier B–C.

### 8. Influence from earlier traditions

Christianity did not emerge in a vacuum. Its core ideas arose at an intersection of traditions:

- **Jewish apocalypticism**: The Kingdom of God, Son of Man, resurrection of the dead, final judgment, and angelic hierarchies are Second Temple Jewish categories, not Christian inventions. This is the primary formative context—see [Achaemenid & West Asian Judaism](/docs/overview/achaemenid-and-west-asian-judaism).
- **Persian / Zoroastrian elements**: Cosmic dualism (light vs. darkness), resurrection as divine vindication, angelic and demonic hierarchies, and eschatological judgment have antecedents in Persian religion. These entered Jewish thought during and after the Babylonian exile and Persian period (6th–4th centuries BCE). The *degree* of genealogical influence vs. parallel development is the debate—see [Dead Sea Scrolls and Iranian parallels](/docs/overview/dead-sea-scrolls-dualism-and-iranian-parallels).
- **Greco-Roman religious environment**: The language of *sōtēr* (savior), *kyrios* (lord), divine sonship, mystery initiation, and celestial ascent was ambient in the Mediterranean world. Paul wrote to communities embedded in this environment. See [Mediterranean mysteries](/docs/overview/hero-cult-and-mystery-cult-atlas) and [Emperor cult and civic *sōtēr*](/docs/overview/emperor-cult-and-civic-soter).

**Why it matters:** The question of which ideas are distinctively Christian versus inherited from earlier traditions is central to understanding what was new about early Christianity. It also underlies this site's comparative project.

**Tier:** Jewish apocalyptic as primary context = Tier A. Persian influence on Jewish eschatology = Tier B (well-documented). Specific Greek mystery religion genealogies for Christian rites = Tier B–C depending on claim. See [Method: evidence, influence, and milieu](/docs/overview/method-evidence-and-influence) for tier framework.

### 9. Resurrection and the limits of historical method

The resurrection of Jesus is the central claim of Christianity (1 Corinthians 15:14: "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile"). It is also the point where historical method reaches its limit.

Historians work with ordinary causation. They can establish:

- That early Christians *believed* Jesus had been raised (Tier A—Paul's testimony in 1 Cor 15 is our earliest evidence, written c. 54 CE)
- That the tomb was reported empty (Tier B—attested in multiple sources, but all from interested parties)
- That resurrection appearances were claimed by multiple people (Tier A—Paul lists witnesses)

What historians *cannot* establish using ordinary historical methods is whether a supernatural event occurred. This is not an anti-religious claim; it is a methodological one. The tools of historical criticism are calibrated for causally regular events.

This creates a structural impasse: believers accept resurrection as historical *and* miraculous; critical historians can describe the belief without adjudicating the metaphysics. Neither position is falsified by the other on its own terms.

**Tier:** Early Christian resurrection belief as a historical fact = Tier A. Whether the resurrection occurred as a physical event = outside historical-critical scope.

---

### 10. Bias in sources

Every primary source for early Christianity was written by a believer for a believing community. This is not unusual for ancient history (most ancient sources are insider documents), but it creates a specific problem for reconstruction: the texts are designed to persuade, not to report neutrally.

This means:
- Miracle stories cannot be treated as independent corroboration.
- Speeches in Acts are literary compositions (in the Greco-Roman historiographical convention), not transcripts.
- Omissions are as significant as inclusions (the Synoptics have no logos prologue; John has no institution narrative).
- The portrait of Jewish leaders in the Passion narratives reflects anti-Jewish polemic developed in communities in conflict with synagogue Judaism after 70 CE.

The Testimonium Flavianum (Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.63–64) and Tacitus (*Annals* 15.44) are the most significant non-Christian references, but both are brief, and the Josephus passage is widely considered to contain Christian interpolations (see Key passages below).

**Tier:** Insider nature of sources = Tier A. Specific conclusions about what the silences mean = Tier B–C.

---

### 11. Why Christianity succeeded: a meta-problem

Christianity's eventual dominance of the Roman Empire is a historical fact requiring historical explanation. Scholars have proposed various factors, none mutually exclusive:

- **Paul's mission**: Deliberate extension beyond Jewish communities to Gentiles throughout the Roman provincial network.
- **Roman infrastructure**: Roads, trade networks, common Greek language (*koinē*), and urban connectivity made rapid spread possible.
- **Social services**: Rodney Stark (*The Rise of Christianity*, 1996) argues that Christian community care networks (care for the sick, feeding the poor, burying the dead) gave Christianity a competitive advantage.
- **Constantine's adoption (312 CE)**: Imperial patronage transformed a minority movement into the favored religion of the empire. The relationship between Christianity's theological appeal and its political adoption is debated.
- **Organizational coherence**: Episcopal structure gave Christianity a more durable institutional form than many mystery cults.

**Why it matters:** If Christianity's success is partly contingent on historical and sociological factors, this complicates purely theological explanations of its spread. Success is not the same as truth—a point methodologically parallel to asking why Zoroastrianism survived under the Sasanians or why Buddhism spread with Kushan patronage.

**Tier:** These factors as contributing causes = Tier B (plausible, documented). Any single factor as decisive = Tier C.

---

### 12. Internal ethical and theological tensions

Critical scholarship also surfaces tensions internal to the tradition:

- **Violence in the Hebrew Bible vs. Jesus' teachings**: Commands to genocide (1 Samuel 15; Deuteronomy 20) sit alongside "love your enemies" (Matthew 5:44). Early Christians resolved this variously: allegorical reading, Marcion's rejection of the Hebrew Bible, or dispensationalist frameworks.
- **Hell and eternal punishment**: The Greek texts use *Gehenna* (a specific valley near Jerusalem), *Hades* (the Underworld), and *Tartarus* in distinct senses that were collapsed into a single concept in later theology. The doctrine of eternal conscious torment is not unambiguous in the earliest texts.
- **Problem of evil**: Theodicy—how an omnipotent, omniscient, omnibenevolent God permits suffering—is not a modern invention; it runs through Job, through Gnostic responses, through Augustine, and into contemporary philosophy of religion.

**Tier:** These as internal textual tensions = Tier A. Theological resolutions = outside historical-critical scope.

## Key flashpoint passages

These specific texts have driven major scholarly debates because they directly challenge traditional portraits of Jesus or early Christianity.

### Paul's biographical silence (1 Cor 15:3–8; Gal 1:11–12)

Paul is the earliest Christian author (letters dated ~50–62 CE). In 1 Corinthians 15:3–8 he gives what looks like an early creedal formula about the death, burial, and resurrection of Jesus—but provides no narrative detail. In Galatians 1:11–12 he insists his gospel came "not from any human source" but by "revelation." Across the authentic Pauline letters, there are no parables, no Sermon on the Mount, no miraculous healings, and almost no biographical information about Jesus' earthly life.

**The debate:** Does this silence reflect Paul's focus on the cosmic Christ over the earthly Jesus? Or does it suggest that the detailed narrative biography of Jesus developed later, in the Gospels, rather than being part of the earliest layer of Christianity?

---

### The Gospel timeline gap

The earliest narrative Gospel (Mark) was written approximately forty years after the crucifixion. Matthew and Luke are typically dated to approximately fifty to sixty years after. John, probably ninety or more years after. All were composed after the destruction of the Jerusalem Temple (70 CE), an event that dramatically reshaped the communities producing these texts.

**The debate:** Oral tradition over decades was subject to expansion, theological shaping, and selective memory. Scholars using form criticism attempt to identify earlier layers within the texts, but there is no consensus method for doing so with confidence.

---

### Resurrection narrative contradictions

The four canonical Gospels diverge significantly in their resurrection accounts:

| Detail | Mark 16:1–8 | Matthew 28 | Luke 24 | John 20–21 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Who goes to the tomb? | Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James, Salome | Mary Magdalene and "the other Mary" | Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary the mother of James, and others | Mary Magdalene alone (then Peter and the Beloved Disciple) |
| What do they find? | Stone rolled away; young man in white inside | Angel descends, rolls back stone; guards faint | Stone rolled away; two men in dazzling clothes | Stone removed; two angels inside |
| Appearances | None in earliest ending (16:8) | Galilee (Matt 28:16–20) | Jerusalem and Emmaus | Jerusalem then Galilee (ch. 21) |

**The debate:** Contradictions in the central claim of Christianity are not marginal. Apologetic harmonization is possible but requires significant supplementation of the texts. Critical scholars treat these as evidence of independent communities developing the tradition in different directions.

---

### The Messianic Secret (Mark 8:29–30; pattern throughout Mark)

In Mark's Gospel, Jesus repeatedly heals people, then instructs them to tell no one (1:44; 5:43; 7:36; 8:26). When Peter declares Jesus the Messiah (8:29), Jesus "sternly ordered them not to tell anyone" (8:30).

**William Wrede's argument (1901):** The Messianic Secret is a literary device by the author of Mark to explain why, if Jesus was the Messiah, he was not generally recognized as such during his lifetime. In other words, the messianic identity was projected backward onto Jesus after the resurrection.

**Counter-arguments:** The secrecy motif may reflect historical caution about politically dangerous claims under Roman occupation; or it may be authentic memory of Jesus' own ambivalence about titles. The debate continues. But Wrede's challenge established that even the earliest Gospel's structure raises questions about what Jesus himself claimed.

---

### The failed apocalyptic prophecy (Mark 13:30)

"Truly I tell you, this generation will not pass away until all these things have taken place."

This is set in the context of cosmic signs—tribulation, darkening of the sun and moon, and the coming of the Son of Man in glory. Albert Schweitzer's interpretation: Jesus expected an imminent apocalyptic transformation within the lifetime of his audience, and it did not happen.

**Apologetic responses:** "This generation" refers to the Jewish people as an ethnic group (still existing), or to a future generation that will witness the signs, or the saying refers only to the destruction of the Temple (70 CE) and not to the full eschatological scenario.

**Critical position (Schweitzer, Ehrman, and others):** If read straightforwardly, this is a failed prophecy—and if so, it raises significant questions about what kind of prophet Jesus was. This is a live scholarly debate, not a fringe observation.

---

### Birth narrative contradictions (Matt 1–2 vs Luke 1–2)

Matthew and Luke are the only Gospels with birth narratives, and they contradict each other on multiple points:

| Detail | Matthew | Luke |
|---|---|---|
| Genealogy from Abraham? | Yes (Matt 1:1–17; 42 generations) | No; traces back to Adam and God (Luke 3:23–38; 77 generations) |
| Joseph's father | Jacob (Matt 1:16) | Heli (Luke 3:23) |
| Primary residence | Bethlehem (implied home) | Nazareth (travel to Bethlehem for census) |
| Census of Quirinius | Not mentioned | Dated to ~6 CE — problematic since Herod died 4 BCE |
| Magi and star | Yes (Matt 2:1–12) | Absent |
| Shepherds | Absent | Yes (Luke 2:8–20) |
| Flight to Egypt | Yes (Matt 2:13–15) | Absent; family returns to Nazareth directly |

**The debate:** Luke's Quirinius census is historically problematic (Josephus dates it to ~6 CE, years after Herod's death). The two genealogies cannot be straightforwardly harmonized. Most critical scholars treat both infancy narratives as theological constructions designed to fulfill scriptural expectations (Micah 5:2, Isaiah 7:14, Hosea 11:1) rather than independent historical records.

---

### "My God, why have you forsaken me?" (Mark 15:34)

At the crucifixion, Mark's Jesus quotes Psalm 22:1 in Aramaic: *Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani*—"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"

**The trajectory:** In Luke's Gospel, the last words are "Father, into your hands I commend my spirit" (Luke 23:46—Psalm 31:5)—serene, composed. In John, the final statement is "It is finished" (John 19:30)—triumphant, controlled. Mark's despairing cry is softened or replaced as the Gospels develop.

**The debate:** Does Mark preserve an authentic tradition of a Jesus who experienced abandonment, later smoothed over as Christology developed? Or is the Psalm 22 quotation a literary citation designed to frame the crucifixion as scriptural fulfillment (Psalm 22 goes on to declare vindication)? Both readings are defended in scholarship.

---

### The empty tomb and Mark's earliest ending (Mark 16:8)

The best-attested early manuscripts of Mark end at 16:8: the women "said nothing to anyone, for they were afraid." No resurrection appearances. No commissioning of disciples. Just abrupt silence.

The Longer Ending (Mark 16:9–20)—which includes resurrection appearances, Jesus' ascension, and signs like snake-handling and speaking in tongues—is absent from Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus (our two earliest complete manuscripts) and is written in a vocabulary and style different from the rest of Mark.

**The debate:** Was the original ending lost? Did Mark intend to end at 16:8 (the women's fear as a literary device)? If the Longer Ending is a later addition, then the resurrection appearances in Mark's Gospel are not original to the text—they were borrowed from or harmonized with Matthew and Luke. The empty tomb without appearances is the earliest layer of the Markan tradition.

---

### The Testimonium Flavianum (Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.63–64)

Josephus (37–c. 100 CE), a Jewish historian writing under Roman patronage, contains the most-cited non-Christian reference to Jesus:

> "At this time there appeared Jesus, a wise man, if indeed one should call him a man. For he was a doer of startling deeds, a teacher of such people as accept the truth gladly. And he gained a following both among many Jews and among many of Greek origin. He was the Messiah. And when Pilate, because of an accusation made by the leading men among us, condemned him to the cross, those who had loved him previously did not cease to do so. For he appeared to them on the third day, living again, just as the divine prophets had spoken of these things and countless other wonders about him."

**The problem:** Josephus was Jewish and showed no sign of Christian belief elsewhere in his writings. The phrases "if indeed one should call him a man," "He was the Messiah," and the resurrection claim read as Christian interpolations—a devout Jew would not have written them. Most scholars hold that a shorter, authentic Josephus notice about Jesus exists beneath the Christian additions, but the exact original wording is unknown.

**The Arabic version** (found in a 10th-century Arabic chronicle by Agapius) is less theologically charged and may be closer to the original Josephan text. Regardless, even the authentic core provides only brief confirmation of a figure named Jesus executed by Pilate—not biographical details.

---

### Silence of contemporary sources

No Roman records from Jesus' lifetime mention him. No writings from Philo of Alexandria (~20 BCE–50 CE), Seneca (~4 BCE–65 CE), or other first-century contemporaries refer to Jesus during his ministry. The Roman-era writers who do mention Christians (Tacitus, Pliny the Younger, Suetonius) are writing in the early second century, well after the events.

**The debate:** This silence is not decisive proof of anything—many historical figures leave no contemporary trace. But it does mean that everything we know about Jesus during his lifetime comes from sources written by his followers, years or decades after his death.

---

### John vs. the Synoptics

The Gospel of John diverges so markedly from Matthew, Mark, and Luke that some scholars have questioned whether it draws on a common tradition at all:

| Feature | Synoptics | John |
|---|---|---|
| Length of ministry | Roughly one year | Roughly three years (three Passovers mentioned) |
| Cleansing of the Temple | Final week (Matt 21; Mark 11) | Beginning of ministry (John 2) |
| Jesus' self-disclosure | Gradual; messianic secret | Open from the beginning ("Before Abraham was, I am") |
| Literary style of Jesus | Parables, aphorisms | Long theological discourses (Farewell Discourse, ch. 14–17) |
| Institution of the Eucharist | Present (Matt 26; Mark 14; Luke 22) | Absent; replaced by foot-washing |
| Chronology of crucifixion | After Passover meal | Before Passover (Jesus dies as the lambs are slaughtered) |

**The debate:** John either represents an independent early tradition with historical value in places (the Johannine community thesis—Raymond Brown), or it represents a late theological elaboration of the Synoptic tradition, written specifically to address the increasing claims of Jesus' divinity. The elevated Christology in John—where Jesus openly declares "I and the Father are one" (10:30)—is not found in the Synoptics, suggesting theological development.

---

## Synthesis: the critical scholarly picture

| Traditional model | Critical reconstruction |
|---|---|
| Jesus' life and teachings recorded by witnesses | No contemporary records; earliest narratives 40–70 years later |
| Four Gospels provide a coherent biography | Four Gospels diverge significantly; contradictions in key events |
| Paul's letters corroborate the Gospel narrative | Paul shows almost no interest in biographical details; cosmic Christ focus |
| NT texts written by apostles | Most attributions are traditional or pseudonymous per critical scholarship |
| Christian theology revealed intact from the beginning | Core doctrines (Trinity, canon) formalized through 4th-century debate |
| Christianity's success reflects divine truth | Success also reflects sociological, political, and historical factors |

The critical view does not *disprove* Christianity—it recasts what kind of claim Christianity is. It shifts the evidential weight from "documented history" to "evolving tradition." That is not the same as fraud or invention; it is the normal condition of all ancient religion.

The closer you approach the earliest sources, the more ambiguous and less biographically detailed Jesus becomes. The more biographically detailed and theologically elaborate the portrait, the later the source.

## The Christ myth theory: what it is and how it differs

The problems surveyed above are all *within* mainstream academic NT scholarship. There is also a fringe position—the **Christ myth theory** (mythicism)—that makes a more radical claim: Jesus never existed as a historical person at all.

This is categorically different from mainstream critical scholarship. Critical scholars accept that a historical Jesus existed (Tier A) while questioning the details of his biography. Mythicists deny his existence entirely—a position rejected by virtually all academic specialists in NT, ancient history, and archaeology.

Key issues where mythicism differs from mainstream critical scholarship:

- **Paul's biographical silence** is the most credible starting point for mythicism. But mainstream scholarship notes that Paul explicitly met Peter and James (Jesus' brother) in Jerusalem circa 36 CE, and implies biographical knowledge in multiple places (Gal 4:4; 1 Cor 15:3–8; 1 Thess 2:14–16).
- **The "dying-and-rising god" parallels** that mythicists invoke to explain Jesus' origins are considered methodologically discredited by historians of religion; per Jonathan Z. Smith (*Encyclopedia of Religion*, 2005), "There is no unambiguous instance in the history of religions of a dying and rising deity."
- **No ancient source** — not even enemies of Christianity — claimed that Jesus had never existed. The non-existence claim is not attested before the Enlightenment.
