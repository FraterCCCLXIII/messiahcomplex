# Preliminary conclusions

This page draws together what the project has established, what it supports at varying levels of confidence, and what it cannot claim. It is explicitly *preliminary*: the research is ongoing, the evidence tiers can shift as new material appears, and no section should be read as a final verdict. The aim is to give a reader who has spent time with the individual research zones a single place to see how the findings fit together — and to be honest about where they do not.

For the method underlying the tier labels used throughout, see [Method: evidence, influence, and milieu](method-evidence-and-influence.md).

---

## What this research establishes (Tier A)

These findings reflect mainstream scholarly consensus, primary-source attestation, or direct material evidence. They are the fixed points around which the rest of the argument is organized.

**Babylonian literary parallels to Genesis.** The *Enuma Elish* creation epic shares a near-verbal creation sequence with Genesis 1; the Atrahasis/Gilgamesh XI flood narrative has the same structure, bird-release sequence, and mountaintop landing as Genesis 6–9. These are not analogies — they are the level of parallel that scholars call documented literary relationship. The Babylonian exile (587–538 BCE) placed the Jewish scribal class inside the most sophisticated cosmological system of the ancient world for two generations.

**Persian loanwords in Biblical Hebrew.** *Pardēs* (paradise), *rāz* (mystery/secret), and *dāt* (law/decree) are embedded in the Hebrew text. Loanwords cannot be argued away; they are direct linguistic fossils of cultural absorption. The theophoric name *Mithredath* (מִתְרְדָת, "given by Mithra") appears in Ezra 1:8 inside the Jewish temple-restoration narrative, placing the Iranian divine name in contact with Jewish liturgical infrastructure during the Achaemenid period.

**Eschatological cluster coherence.** Six concepts — bodily resurrection, cosmic dualism, final judgment, paradise vocabulary, a named cosmic adversary, and angelic hierarchies — are logically interdependent in Zoroastrian theology, where they form a unified eschatological system. They appear in Jewish literature as a coherent cluster in the same Persian-period century in which Iranian contact is independently attested through loanwords, onomastics, and administrative overlap. The arrival of six interdependent concepts as a system in the same century is more parsimonious as adoption of a coherent theological package than as six coincidentally parallel developments. See [Achaemenid empire and West Asian Judaism](achaemenid-and-west-asian-judaism.md) and [Eschatological vocabulary timeline](eschatological-vocabulary-timeline.md).

**The Mitanni treaty anchor (~1400 BCE).** A cuneiform treaty between the Mitanni and the Hittites, found at Boğazköy, invokes *Mitra* alongside Indra, Varuṇa, and the Nāsatya twins as divine oath-witnesses — before any Vedic or Avestan text was composed. This is the oldest documentary anchor for a named deity in this entire corpus, and it places the contract-god at the intersection of the Indo-Iranian world and the ancient Near East before the Indian and Iranian branches fully diverged.

**Kharoshthi script from Aramaic.** The script used in the earliest Buddhist manuscripts is derived from Aramaic — the Achaemenid administrative script. The word *lipi* ("writing") is a Persian loanword in Indian languages (Salomon 1999). These are Tier A evidence for Achaemenid administrative contact shaping the physical infrastructure of early Buddhist textual transmission.

**Indian soldiers in Xerxes' army.** Herodotus (*Histories* 7.65, 7.70, 7.86) specifically names Indian contingents in Xerxes' army at Thermopylae and Salamis (480–479 BCE). These soldiers returned to India. This is a documented pre-Alexander mechanism for cultural information to move between the Persian empire, the Greek world, and India — before Aśoka, before Indo-Greek kingdoms.

**Kushan coin evidence.** Kushan royal coins depict *MIIRO* (Mithra) and *BODDO* (Buddha) struck by the same mint. This is dated material evidence of religious co-existence in a single political milieu — not analogy but direct numismatic documentation of the Kushan horizon as a zone of religious synthesis.

**The Gandhāran radiate halo.** Hellenistic solar-crown iconography predates Gandhāran Buddha images chronologically. The borrowing direction is established by the archaeology. The first anthropomorphic Buddha images — also a Gandhāran innovation — were produced in a workshop tradition that drew directly on Hellenistic models, as the drapery, facial type, and halo all attest.

## Well-supported but actively debated (Tier B)

These findings are argued with named mechanisms and real evidence, but face legitimate scholarly objections. They represent the hypothesis layer where most of the project's productive exploration lives.

**The porous milieu model.** The best explanatory frame for most of the structural convergences documented here is neither "X borrowed from Y" nor "mere coincidence." It is shared conceptual environment: neighboring and overlapping communities, under similar pressures, drawing on the same available vocabulary. Ancient observers performing *interpretatio* — routinely equating Christ with Mithras, Mithras with Sol — were recognizing figures occupying the same inherited function-space, not confusing distinct systems. This model replaces both the polemical identity-claim frame and the apologetic coincidence frame with the more historically accurate category of shared milieu. See [Syncretism — concepts and methodology](syncretism-methodology.md) and [Soteriological convergence of empire](soteriological-convergence-of-empire.md).

**Philo's Logos as the immediate context for John 1:1.** Philo of Alexandria's Logos theology — where a pre-existent divine agent mediates between the transcendent God and the created world — provides the most direct intellectual parallel for the Prologue to the Gospel of John. This is the mainstream scholarly position on the Fourth Gospel's intellectual background, not a minority hypothesis.

**The Dead Sea Scrolls Two Spirits doctrine.** The Community Rule (1QS 3–4) contains what scholars call "the most extensive non-biblical statement of Jewish dualism from antiquity." Its structure — Prince of Lights vs. Angel of Darkness, two spirits created as equals for the current cosmic period, pre-determined final age — maps directly onto the Zoroastrian Ahura Mazda / Angra Mainyu dualism, including the specific claim that both operate until a final cosmic resolution. The Two Spirits teaching has no precedent in earlier Hebrew scripture; this absence supports rather than undermines the case for Iranian input.

**The Śākya-Saka cultural contact hypothesis.** Attwood (2012, *JOCBS* 3) argues not that the Buddha was ethnically Scythian, but that some features of early Buddhism reflect cultural inheritance from an earlier period of Iranian contact in the Śākya clan's history. The cumulative evidence — *xvaetvadatha* sibling marriage parallels, the *stūpa*/kurgan structural parallel, the body-speech-mind ethical triad matching the Zoroastrian *humata/hūxta/hvaršta*, the Deva/Daeva inversion, and the √*śak* etymological cluster — is individually explicable by alternatives but cumulatively exceeds what shared PIE background alone accounts for. The chronological gap (main Saka migrations postdate the Buddha by 400 years) remains the hypothesis's single most damaging weakness; Parpola's 800 BCE scenario and Van Geel et al.'s climate-driven steppe expansion are plausible but not independently attested in Indian archaeology. See [Śaka–Scythian hypotheses and Buddhism](saka-scythian-hypotheses.md).

**The Deva/Daeva inversion and the √śak cluster.** Zoroastrianism explicitly demonized Indra (as *Indar* / daeva). The Saka Haumavargā sat outside the Zoroastrian reform and retained Indra as a chief deity under his pre-reform Vedic associations. Early Buddhism retained Indra as a positive cosmic figure and renamed him Śakra/Sakka — from the root √*śak*, the same root as both *Śaka* (the steppe peoples) and *Śākya* (the Buddha's clan). The alignment of early Buddhism with the pre-Zoroastrian Saka veneration of Indra — rather than with the reformed tradition that demonized him — is a substantive argument about religious family resemblance that critics have not directly addressed.

**Pyrrho of Elis and early Buddhist dialectic.** Beckwith (*Greek Buddha*, 2015) argues that Pyrrho's key philosophical formula — things are "undifferentiated, unmeasurable, and undecidable" — directly parallels the Trilakṣaṇa (impermanence, *dukkha*, no-self), and that Pyrrho encountered an early or variant form of this teaching during his time in India with Alexander's army (327–325 BCE). The parallel is striking enough to have generated genuine scholarly engagement (Bett 2000). The specific claim that Pyrrho encountered *Buddhist* rather than Jain or Ājīvika dialectic remains Tier C; the philosophical parallel itself is Tier B.

**Paul's celestial Christology and its milieu.** Paul's heavenly-spheres Christ — exalted above all principalities and powers, pre-existent mediator of creation — stands closer to the Enochic Son of Man (1 Enoch 37–71) and to Hellenistic solar cosmology than to the Galilean teacher of the Synoptic Gospels. The structural position (cosmic lord accessible through initiation anywhere in the empire) maps onto the same function-space occupied by Roman Mithras and the universal Dionysus. This is not a borrowing argument; it is a milieu argument.

## Structural / synthetic claims (component tiers vary)

These frameworks integrate evidence from multiple research zones into a single explanatory model. Each draws on Tier A and B material, but the unified synthesis is Tier C — which means it names what would need to be falsified, not what has been proven.

**The Wave 1 / Wave 2 model.** PIE dispersal (c. 3300–1500 BCE) distributed a *structural grammar* — a function-space for a solar mediator between the sky deity and humanity, associated with contract, light, and cosmic order. Vedic Mitra, Avestan Mithra, Greek Apollo/Helios, and Roman Mithras all fill this inherited slot. The structural parallels at the deepest level exist because multiple traditions inherited the function-space from a common ancestor. The Axial Age (c. 600–300 BCE) then filled those slots with named, universalized cosmic figures under imperial pressure — the second wave operating through the specific mechanisms of the Achaemenid, Hellenistic, and Kushan empires. The two levels use different mechanisms: Wave 1 distributed the grammar; Wave 2 populated it with historically specific content.

**The Axial Age delocalization thesis.** Across Indo-European cultures before the Axial period, the honoring of exceptional dead was localized: the Greek *heroon* at a specific tomb, the steppe kurgan, the Vedic fire ritual for ancestors. The structural assumption was consistent — the dead person of exceptional power remains available to the living through the site of the body. What the Axial Age transformations did, in every tradition this project covers, was delocalize that contact point: the Greek hero bound to a specific tomb became the cosmic Dionysus accessible through initiation anywhere; the ancestor honored at a specific kurgan became the Saoshyant who would restore all humanity at the end of time; the particular dead teacher became the Buddha whose relic fragments made him universally accessible; the figure executed at a specific location became the cosmic Christ accessible through meal and baptism anywhere in the empire. The Axial breakthrough, on this reading, is the transformation of locally-bound hero honors into portable, universalizable cosmic soteriology. The Achaemenid empire appears to be the specific historical mechanism that forced this delocalization: communities whose deity was tied to a temple, a tomb, or a homeland were rendered theologically homeless by imperial displacement, and the traditions that survived were those whose mediator became universally portable.

**The three-layer transmission model.** The Wave 1 / Wave 2 model describes two levels of transmission (PIE structural grammar and Axial Age filling-in). The research on the Neo-Assyrian sacred tree, Mandaean cosmology, and stupa architecture reveals a third layer between them: a **Mesopotamian formal encoding** phase (~2400–600 BCE) in which the structural grammar inherited from PIE was given specific architectural, iconographic, and cosmological form by Sumerian, Babylonian, and Assyrian civilizations — *before* the Iranian amplification of the Axial Age. The full model is therefore: (1) PIE substrate distributes the grammar; (2) Mesopotamian civilizations encode it in durable monuments, texts, and administrative religion; (3) Iranian Axial-Age universalization amplifies specific figures (Mithra, Saoshyant, the cosmic yazatas) and transmits them into the Jewish, Greek, Buddhist, and ultimately Christian milieu. This three-layer reading makes Parpola's Neo-Assyrian tree hypothesis (see [Parpola and the Assyrian roots of the Sefirot](assyrian-tree-sefirot-parpola.md)) significant beyond its specific claim: it is the middle layer that is otherwise missing from the site's narrative. (Tier C — interpretive synthesis across Tier A and B components.)

**The deepest structural continuity: PIE sacrifice → eucharist.** The PIE cosmogonic sacrifice template — a primordial being (Puruṣa, Ymir, Gayōmart, *\*Yemo*) killed and divided so the world can come into being, later to be restored by a savior — does not merely survive as mythology. It is the *operational logic* of the most consequential religious rituals in Western history. The chain runs: PIE willing-sacrifice cosmogony → Vedic agnicayana (ritual reassembly of Prajāpati's body from bricks) → Iranian Saoshyant reconstituting Gayōmart's body at Frashokereti → mystery cult transformation of the cosmogonic sacrifice into a symbolic animal or human substitute (Mithraic tauroctony, where the bull's blood fertilizes the cosmos as Kingu's blood created humanity) → Pauline eucharist ("This is my body... this is my blood"), where the cosmic-body sacrifice is individualized in bread and wine and repeated by initiates as a shared rehearsal of the eschatological restoration. The tauroctony is the middle step: not a direct borrowing from any single predecessor, but a *structural transformation* of the cosmogonic sacrifice into a mystery-cult ritual form — consistent with all its Babylonian, Iranian, and PIE predecessors — that is then paralleled by the eucharist. None of these traditions need to have borrowed from one another for this chain to be real; what it reveals is that the liturgical logic of body-sacrifice → world-generation → body-restoration is the deepest surviving stratum of Eurasian religious thought. (Tier C for the unified chain; Tier A for its individual documented components. See [Primordial Cosmic Man](primordial-cosmic-man.md).)

**The barbarian inaugurator pattern.** Across the corpus, a recurrent meta-pattern appears: politically motivated outsider groups — seeking legitimacy within established cultural systems — end up inaugurating those systems' most durable aesthetic and textual forms. The Indo-Greeks produced the first anthropomorphic Buddha image (borrowing Hellenistic workshop conventions). The Sakas inaugurated Sanskrit imperial inscriptions (*praśastis*), elevating Sanskrit from a priestly to a prestige language (Pollock). The Achaemenids patronized the canonization of the Hebrew Bible (Cyrus as *māšîaḥ*; Ezra's legal mission under Persian authorization). The Kushans funded the Silk Road infrastructure that carried Buddhist manuscripts east. Each instance is individually attested; the pattern across them has not been argued as a unified thesis in any single monograph. See [Barbarian inaugurator pattern](barbarian-inaugurator-pattern.md).

## What this research does not claim

**Popular comparisons and fabricated parallels.** Mithras's virgin birth, his twelve apostles, his crucifixion — these have no ancient attestation. They were fabricated in 19th-century polemical literature (Higgins 1836; Doane 1882) and recycled without fact-checking ever since. This project explicitly replaces those fabrications with an honest account of what actually overlapped, through which mechanisms, and at what horizons.

**That any living tradition borrowed its core doctrines in a way that delegitimizes them.** The porous milieu model is the intellectually honest alternative to both the polemical "Christianity comes from Mithraism" frame and the apologetic "all similarities are coincidence" frame. Shared conceptual environment means that multiple communities facing similar pressures reached for similar solutions using available vocabulary — not that any tradition is a copy of another.

**Direct literary dependence between most traditions.** Motif similarity does not establish that a specific text caused a specific other text. The site's strongest arguments (eschatological cluster coherence, loanwords, coin evidence) rest on mechanisms more robust than pattern-matching. Where mechanism is absent, the comparison remains typological — useful for generating hypotheses, insufficient for establishing genealogy.

**Identity of figures across traditions.** That Mithra, Mithras, Christ, Maitreya, and the Mahdi all fill the same functional slot in their respective traditions does not mean they are "really the same god." It means that every major tradition in the Indo-Iranian and Semitic sphere generated a figure to fill a structurally similar role, drawing on inherited function-space and shaped by specific historical contacts. The table is an observation, not an equation.

## Highest-value open questions

These are research directions with the greatest potential to shift findings from Tier B toward Tier A, or to decisively close off hypotheses currently held open. They are ranked by likely evidential impact.

**Ancient DNA from the Terai region (6th–5th century BCE sites).** No ancient DNA exists from Nepalese lowland or Himalayan-foothill sites dated to the Śākya period. Steppe/Central Asian admixture in Śākya-region burials would substantially support the Śākya-Saka hypothesis; its absence would be significant evidence against. This is the single test that could shift the debate most conclusively in either direction.

**Iranian theological loanwords in the oldest Pāli strata.** K.R. Norman's philological work identifies the *Aṭṭhakavagga* and *Pārāyanavagga* of the *Sutta Nipāta* and the *Dhammapada* as the oldest recoverable Buddhist textual layers. A dedicated search for Iranian theological vocabulary — beyond the already-known administrative loanwords (*lipi*, etc.) affecting all of north India — would be significant. Presence would directly support Śākya-specific Iranian contact; absence would strengthen the mainstream view that cultural contact was general rather than clan-specific.

**Gandhārī manuscript linguistic analysis.** The Gandhārī Buddhist manuscripts (1st–4th c. CE; British Library and Schøyen collections) are written in Kharoshthi from the exact northwest contact zone. Systematic study of Iranian influence on their doctrinal vocabulary — compared against Pāli parallels — could reveal whether specifically Iranian theological language entered Buddhist transmission in the northwest. This is technically feasible given the ongoing Gandhari manuscript projects (Salomon's group at the University of Washington).

**The *Saccakiriyā* tradition and Zoroastrian *asha*: a comparative study.** The Buddhist "Act of Truth" — where a truthful declaration has apotropaic power — is structurally very close to the Zoroastrian *asha* force (truth as a metaphysical power, not merely a social norm). A dedicated comparison of the role of truth in Gāthic Zoroastrianism, early Buddhism (*sacca* as *pāramitā*), and Vedic *ṛta* could establish whether the parallel reflects genuine Iranian transmission or independent convergence via the shared PIE substrate.

**Beckwith's Pyrrho claim: direct engagement with Bett (2000) and Flintoff (1980).** The philosophical parallel between Pyrrho's formula and the Trilakṣaṇa is the most specific and testable claim for early Buddhist-Greek philosophical contact. A focused response addressing whether Pyrrho's formula can be distinguished from Jain or Ājīvika dialectic — not just from Brahmanical philosophy — would either strengthen or close the argument, which currently stands as the strongest single data point connecting early Buddhism to Central Asian philosophical transmission.
