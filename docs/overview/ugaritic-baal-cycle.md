# The Ugaritic Baal Cycle and its biblical traces

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers who know that Canaanite religion is somehow "background" to the Hebrew Bible but want to understand what the actual primary sources are, what the Baal Cycle contains, and what specific traces of it can be identified in the Hebrew scriptures with scholarly confidence.
- **Question:** What do the Ugaritic texts show about Canaanite religion, and what specific connections to the Hebrew Bible are regarded as secure vs. debated by mainstream scholarship?
- **Scope:** The Ras Shamra discovery and corpus; the Baal Cycle narrative; El and the divine council; Asherah; specific biblical parallels; the dying-and-rising god debate as it applies to Baal.

---

## 1. The discovery: Ras Shamra and the KTU corpus

In 1929, French archaeologists excavating at **Ras Shamra** (Arabic: "fennel head") on the Syrian coast, near the ancient city of **Ugarit**, discovered a remarkable archive of clay tablets. Subsequent seasons uncovered thousands of tablets, a substantial portion in a previously unknown alphabetic cuneiform script — **Ugaritic**.

Ugaritic is a **Northwest Semitic language**, closely related to Phoenician and Hebrew (and more distantly to Akkadian). The script uses 30 alphabetic signs, making it much more accessible than Akkadian syllabic cuneiform. Tablets date from approximately **1400–1185 BCE**, when Ugarit was destroyed, probably by the Sea Peoples.

The standard scholarly edition is ***Keilalphabetische Texte aus Ugarit*** (**KTU**, now *CAT* in updated editions), providing reference numbers for all tablets. The mythological texts — including the Baal Cycle — are designated KTU 1.1–1.6 (Baal Cycle), 1.17–1.19 (Aqhat), 1.14–1.16 (Keret).

**Why Ugarit matters:** Ugarit was a cosmopolitan Canaanite city-state on the Levantine coast, a trading hub that imported Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Aegean goods and culture. Its religious texts represent the most direct and earliest surviving documentation of the Canaanite religious tradition that was the **immediate cultural milieu** of ancient Israel. The biblical polemic against Baal (Hosea, Jeremiah, 1 Kings 18) is not fighting an abstract concept; it is fighting the cult system whose mythology these tablets preserve.

## 2. The divine characters

### El (*ʾIl*) — the patriarch-god

**El** is the father and king of the Ugaritic pantheon — a figure of enormous prestige but physical remoteness. He is described as:
- **"Father of Years"** (*ʾab šnm*) — a primordial, ancient figure
- **"Bull El"** (*ṯr ʾil*) — bovine imagery suggesting power, fertility, patriarchal authority
- Dwelling at "the Source of the Two Rivers, amid the springs of the Two Deeps" — a cosmic dwelling at the source of the world's waters
- Presiding over the **divine assembly** (*pḫr ʾilm* / *pḫr bn ʾil*, "assembly of the gods" / "assembly of the sons of El")

El is generally benign and accessible — he hears petitions, grants children to the childless, and adjudicates disputes. His authority is acknowledged but largely delegated; it is Baal who actively battles and rules.

**Hebrew Bible connection:** The Hebrew *El* and *Elohim* (plural of majesty, used as a singular for Israel's God) directly derive from this Canaanite figure. Specific epithets transfer: *El Shaddai* (the Almighty), *El Elyon* (God Most High, Genesis 14:18), *El Olam* (the Everlasting God). The divine council (*sod YHWH*, *bênê ha-ʾĕlōhîm*) in Job 1–2, Psalm 82, and Deuteronomy 32:8 (in the LXX/DSS variant) directly reflects the Ugaritic *pḫr ʾilm*. The scholarly consensus (Cross, Smith) is that the pre-Israelite high god *El* was identified with YHWH in the early monarchic period.

### Baal Hadad — the storm king

**Baal** (literally *bʿl*, "lord, owner, master") is specifically **Baal Hadad** — the storm god. He is:
- **"Rider of Clouds"** (*rkb ʿrpt*) — the storm god mounted on the storm cloud
- **"Mightiest of warriors"** (*aliyan bʿl*) — his standard epithet
- The **active deity** who brings rain, vegetation, and life to the world
- The son of Dagan (a grain god); nephew/son of El

Baal is the **protagonist** of the major mythological cycle. His victories bring fertility; his defeat means drought and death.

**Hebrew Bible connection:** Psalm 68:4 (MT) describes YHWH as *"He who rides through the deserts"* — but the Hebrew *bā-ʿărābôt* ("through the deserts/heavens") uses the exact root of the Ugaritic epithet *rkb ʿrpt* ("rider of clouds"), and many scholars read it as a transferred epithet. More clearly, the description of YHWH in Psalm 29 — a storm theophany with the voice of thunder ("the voice of YHWH over the waters") — uses vocabulary so close to Ugaritic Baal hymns that Cross (*Canaanite Myth and Hebrew Epic*, 1973) argued it was virtually a re-used Baal hymn with YHWH substituted.

### Yamm (*Yamm*) — Sea/Chaos

**Yamm** (literally "sea/river") is Baal's primary antagonist in the first part of the cycle. Yamm is favored by El and given kingship; Baal challenges this and defeats Yamm with two magical weapons forged by Kothar-wa-Khasis (the craftsman god).

**Hebrew Bible connections:**
- YHWH's defeat of *Yam* (*Yamm*), *Rahab*, and *Leviathan* in Psalms 74:12–14, 89:9–10; Job 26:12–13; Isaiah 27:1 — these are echoes of the cosmic battle
- Isaiah 27:1: *"In that day the LORD with his hard and great and strong sword will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent, and he will slay the dragon that is in the sea"* — **compare** KTU 1.5.i.1–3: *"When you killed Litan [Lotan], the fleeing serpent, destroyed the twisting serpent, the tyrant with seven heads…"* The verbal correspondence is exact.
- The primordial sea (*tĕhôm*) in Genesis 1:2 is cognate with Babylonian *Tiamat* and Ugaritic *yamm/tehom* — the watery chaos defeated to create the world

### Mot — Death

**Mot** (literally "death") is ruler of the underworld and Baal's existential opponent. He invites Baal to a feast in the underworld; Baal descends and dies; vegetation ceases; the gods mourn. El descends from his throne, pours earth on his head, performs mourning rites. Anat then finds and kills Mot, winnowing, grinding, and burning him like grain; Baal revives; rain returns.

### Asherah (*ʾAṯirat*) — El's consort

Asherah is El's wife — "Lady Asherah of the Sea" — and the "mother of the gods." In the Baal Cycle she intercedes with El on Baal's behalf.

**Hebrew Bible connections:** This is among the most important archaeological findings for biblical history. The word *asherah* (plural *asherot* or *asherim*) appears 40 times in the Hebrew Bible — sometimes referring to a goddess, sometimes to a wooden cult pole placed near altars.

**Physical inscriptions:**
- **Kuntillet Ajrud** (northeastern Sinai, 9th–8th cent. BCE): storage jars with the inscription *"I bless you by YHWH of Samaria and by his asherah [*wĕ-ʾăšêrātô*]"* — the suffix *-ô* ("his") treats Asherah as a personal noun, not an object. This is widely interpreted as direct evidence that a YHWH-Asherah cult was practiced in Israel/Samaria
- **Khirbet el-Qom** (Judah, 8th cent. BCE): a tomb inscription blessing by "YHWH and his asherah"

These inscriptions show **Canaanite polytheism operating inside Israelite cultic practice** at the popular or even official level, at exactly the period the Hebrew prophets were condemning it.

## 3. The Baal Cycle narrative

The Baal Cycle (KTU 1.1–1.6) is a sequence of six major tablets with significant lacunae. The standard narrative reconstruction:

### Tablet I — Yamm's claim

El grants kingship to Yamm (*yamm u nahar*, "sea and river") over the divine assembly. Baal challenges Yamm's authority. Yamm's messengers demand that Baal be handed over to Yamm; Baal defies them and prepares for war.

### Tablet II — The weapons of Kothar

The craftsman god **Kothar-wa-Khasis** ("Skillful and Cunning") forges two magical weapons for Baal: *Yagrush* ("Driver") and *Aymur* ("Expeller"). With these, Baal defeats Yamm:

> "Yagrush, be driven from Yamm's hand, from the hand of Judge River. Leap in the hand of Baal, like an eagle between his fingers; smite the back of Prince Yamm, between the arms of Judge River."
>
> — KTU 1.2.iv.11–18 (Smith–Parker translation, adapted)

Baal defeats Yamm, and Anat and Astarte intercede to restrain Baal from further killing.

### Tablet III–IV — Baal's palace

Baal demands a palace (*bētu*, "house/temple") befitting his kingship — a heavenly palace on Mount Zaphon. This requires convincing both Asherah (to intercede with El) and El himself. El grants permission; Kothar builds a magnificent palace of cedar, silver, gold, and lapis lazuli.

**Zaphon and Jerusalem:** The connection between Baal's palace on Mt. Zaphon (the sacred mountain of the north) and the Jerusalem Temple theology is documented in **Psalm 48:2–3**: *"Great is the LORD and greatly to be praised in the city of our God … Mount Zion, in the far north [*yarkĕtê ṣāpôn*], the city of the great King."* The phrase *yarkĕtê ṣāpôn* literally means "the uttermost parts of Zaphon" — using the exact Ugaritic place-name for Baal's mountain to describe Jerusalem.

### Tablet V–VI — Baal's death and return

Mot (*Môt*, "Death") invites Baal to a feast in the underworld. Despite divine warnings, Baal descends. He dies; vegetation ceases; the world mourns. El performs mourning rites: descends from his throne, tears his garments, pours earth on his head (standard ANE mourning).

Anat finds Baal's body, mourns, then confronts Mot directly. She **kills Mot** — cuts him with a sword, winnows him, burns him, grinds him in a mill, scatters him in the fields. This is the agricultural motif at the heart of the myth: Mot is grain; his grinding-and-sowing is the planting of crops. Baal revives and returns; El has a vision in a dream:

> "The heavens rained oil, the wadis ran with honey. So I know that Aliyan Baal lives, that the Prince, Lord of Earth, is alive!"
>
> — KTU 1.6.iii.12–14 (Smith–Parker translation, adapted)

The cycle ends with Baal reestablishing his kingship and defeating Mot in a final combat.

## 4. The divine council and Psalm 82

The **divine council** (*sod*, *ʿedah*, *qahal* — various Hebrew terms) appears repeatedly in the Hebrew Bible as the assembly in which YHWH presides over other divine beings. The Ugaritic *pḫr ʾilm* ("assembly of the gods") is its direct precursor.

**Psalm 82** is the most explicit biblical text:

> "God (*Elohim*) has taken his place in the divine council (*ʿădat ʾel*); in the midst of the gods (*ʾĕlōhîm*) he holds judgment: 'How long will you judge unjustly and show partiality to the wicked? … I said, "You are gods (*ʾĕlōhîm*), sons of the Most High (*bĕnê ʿelyôn*) all of you; nevertheless, like men you shall die, and fall like any prince."'"
>
> — Psalm 82:1–7 (ESV, modified)

The psalm describes YHWH judging and condemning the *ʾĕlōhîm* — other divine beings — for failing to uphold justice among the nations. This makes no sense within strict monotheism but makes perfect sense within a **monolatrous** framework where YHWH is the highest God in a council of genuine but subordinate divine beings — exactly the Ugaritic structure.

**Deuteronomy 32:8** (LXX/DSS variant): *"When the Most High gave to the nations their inheritance, when he divided mankind, he fixed the borders of the peoples **according to the number of the sons of God** [*bĕnê ʾĕlōhîm* — LXX and 4QDeut*]"* vs. Masoretic: *"according to the number of the sons of Israel."* The DSS and LXX preserve the older reading — the nations were assigned to divine overseers (*bĕnê ʾĕlōhîm*), and Israel was YHWH's own portion. The MT emended this theologically uncomfortable polytheism.

## 5. The dying-and-rising debate as applied to Baal

The claim that Baal is a "dying and rising god" — analogous to Osiris, Tammuz/Dumuzi, Adonis, and ultimately Christ — was central to the Frazer *Golden Bough* tradition (1890–1915). The subsequent scholarly debate has been intensive:

**Frazer's original claim:** Vegetation deities who die and return seasonally are a universal type; Baal's annual death (summer drought) and return (autumn rains) exemplify this type; the same type underlies dying-and-rising saviors including Christ.

**J.Z. Smith's critique (*Dying and Rising Gods*, ERE 1987; *Drudgery Divine*, 1990):** There is no "dying and rising" god in the ancient world prior to Christianity — Osiris does not "rise," Tammuz is mourned but not clearly shown to return in the texts, and Baal's "resurrection" is questionable. The category is a modern scholarly construction.

**Tryggve Mettinger's response (*The Riddle of Resurrection*, 2001):** Mettinger conducted a systematic text-critical study of each candidate deity and concluded:
- **Baal**: genuine revival is attested in the Ugaritic texts (Baal returns to life after Anat kills Mot) — **confirmed** as a dying-and-rising deity
- **Adonis**: death and return attested in Byblos cult (Lucian, *De Dea Syria*) — probable
- **Osiris**: more complex — he is "restored" but rules the dead, not the living — debated
- **Tammuz**: mourned; return implied but not clearly stated in primary texts — debated

Mettinger's conclusion: the dying-and-rising deity category is real for Baal and Adonis but the texts should not be pushed to make them all identical. More importantly: none of these cults provides direct evidence of **influence** on Christianity; the Christian resurrection claim rests on its own historical foundation. The parallels illuminate the *cultural grammar* of resurrection theology without proving genealogical dependence.

## 6. Why this matters for the site's research program

The Ugaritic Baal Cycle is significant for this site's questions at multiple levels:

**Zone 5 (PIE & deep grammar):** The Baal Cycle's cosmic-battle narrative (storm god defeats sea-monster) is the Canaanite variant of a pattern attested across IE and ANE traditions — YHWH vs. Rahab/Leviathan/Yamm; Marduk vs. Tiamat; Zeus vs. Typhon; Indra vs. Vritra. Whether this reflects PIE inheritance, ANE diffusion, or independent development is debated; the structural parallel is secure.

**Zone 2 (Achaemenid contact):** The divine council theology that Ugaritic texts document most clearly — and that the Hebrew Bible inherits — becomes the **background** for Jewish angelology: the divine council members become named angels (Michael, Gabriel, Raphael), and their hierarchy eventually parallels the Zoroastrian Amesha Spentas. The connection from Ugaritic divine council → Hebrew angelology → Zoroastrian influence is the deep structural context for the angelology arguments in [Jewish angelology and yazata parallels](jewish-angelology-and-yazata-parallels.md).

**Zone 4 (Mediterranean mysteries):** Baal → Adonis → Greek mystery religion is an attested transmission path: the Baal cult enters Phoenician religion, is transmitted to Greece through Phoenician trade (8th–7th cent. BCE), and becomes the Adonis cult that feeds into Hellenistic mystery religion. This is a documented case of Levantine religious material moving westward.
