# Śaka–Scythian hypotheses and Buddhism

The **source compilation** foregrounds **steppe** peoples (**Saka / Śaka**, "**Scythians**") as carriers of **Iranian**-flavored ideas toward **India** and **Gandhāra**, including conjectures about the **Śākya** clan of the historical **Buddha**. Greeks called some of these groups Scythians; Achaemenid sources use *Saka*; Indic texts use *Śaka*. Scholars such as **Michael Witzel**, **Jayarava Attwood**, and **Christopher I. Beckwith** have explored whether the Śākyas should be read against that Central Asian horizon — not as established fact, but as a research program with significant pushback from mainstream Indology.

This page assembles all available evidence, pro and contra, with explicit tier labels throughout.

---

## 1. Who were the Saka?

### The three groups in Achaemenid inscriptions

The Achaemenid Empire distinguished three categories of Saka in official inscriptions (Behistun, Persepolis, Naqsh-e Rostam):

- **Saka Tigraxaudā** ("pointed-hat Saka") — the iconic group, recognizable by their distinctive tall conical headdress, attested at the Persepolis Apadana reliefs bringing tribute to Darius. Their territory was the Fergana Valley and Tien Shan region.
- **Saka Haumavargā** ("haoma-drinking Saka") — named for their ritual use of the plant *haoma* (cognate with Vedic *soma*); located in Sogdiana and Bactria. These are the most theologically significant group for transmission theories.
- **Saka Paradraya** ("Saka beyond the sea") — western Saka, possibly the Massagetae or the Pontic Scythians known to Herodotus.

**Evidence tier: A** — all three designations appear in primary Achaemenid epigraphic sources.

### Origin and chronology

The proto-Saka/Scythian cultures emerge from the Andronovo and Srubnaya horizons of the Eurasian steppe (c. 2000–1200 BCE). By c. 1000–800 BCE, distinctive kurgan burials and "animal style" art appear in the Altai and Yenisei regions. The timeline of their documented expansion:

| Date | Event |
| --- | --- |
| c. 800–700 BCE | Massagetae migrate into Central Asia; westward Scythian displacement |
| c. 700 BCE | Saka presence attested in Tarim Basin |
| c. 700–600 BCE | Scythian expansion into Western Asia; impact on Mesopotamia, Anatolia, Egypt |
| c. 653–625 BCE | Scythian dominance in parts of the Near East; interaction with Assyrians and Medes |
| c. 600–550 BCE | Saka-Median wars; Queen Zarinaea leads Saka forces |
| c. 550 BCE | Saka kurgans in Tian Shan region (30 kurgans dated 550–250 BCE) |
| c. 520–518 BCE | Darius I campaigns against Massagetae/Saka Tigraxaudā; captures King Skunxa |
| c. 480–479 BCE | Indian contingents serve in Xerxes' army at Thermopylae and Salamis |
| c. 400–300 BCE | Pazyryk culture in Altai; elaborate frozen kurgans |
| c. 300–100 BCE | Saka migration into Bactria and Arachosia; precursor to Indo-Scythian kingdoms |
| c. 130–100 BCE | Indo-Scythian movement into northwestern India (Gandhara) |
| c. 78 CE | Śaka Era begins — adopted as official calendar of the Republic of India in 1957 |

## 2. Saka religion and culture

Understanding what the Saka *believed* is essential context for assessing any transmission hypothesis. The sources are primarily Herodotus, Achaemenid administrative texts, and the extraordinary evidence of frozen kurgan burials.

### Deities

Herodotus provides the fullest account of Scythian religion (Histories 4.59), identifying Greek equivalents:

| Deity | Tribe | Function | Greek Equivalent |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Tabiti** | Pontic Scythians | Supreme goddess; hearth; fire; sovereignty; royal oaths | Hestia |
| **Papaios** | Pontic Scythians | Sky father; thunder; cosmic rulership; husband of Api | Zeus |
| **Api** | Pontic Scythians | Earth; fertility; motherhood; snake associations | Gaia |
| **Oitosyros / Goitosyros** | Pontic Scythians | Sun; light; oaths; associated with gold | **Apollo** |
| **Artimpasa / Argimpasa** | Pontic Scythians and Sarmatians | Fertility; sovereignty; war; female power; sacred prostitution | Aphrodite Urania |
| **Thagimasadas** | Royal Scythians | Water; horses; oaths; river crossings | Poseidon |
| **Targitaos** | Royal Scythians | Divine ancestor; founder; son of Zeus and a daughter of the Borysthenes River | — |
| **Sun Deity** | Massagetae | Primary deity; received horse sacrifices | Helios |
| **Sword God (Akinakes)** | Pontic Scythians | War; oaths; sacrifices; anthropomorphized weapon | Ares |

**The Apollo identification** (Oitosyros = Apollo) is significant for this site's broader connections: the Scythian solar-oath deity, associated with gold, overlaps structurally with Apollo as solar purifier. The Hyperborean Apollo traditions in Greek mythology (Abaris, Aristeas of Proconnesus) are set in Scythian territory and may reflect real cultural contact. Herodotus (4.32–36) writes extensively about Apollo's association with the Hyperboreans.

### The Saka Haumavargā and the haoma-soma connection

The group named "haoma-drinking Saka" (*haumavargā*) practiced ritual ingestion of *haoma* — the Avestan counterpart of Vedic *soma*. This is documented in both their Achaemenid designation and in Avestan texts (Yasna). The *soma* ritual is central to the Rigveda and persists in early Brahmanical religion; the Saka's ritual use of the same or similar substance is among the strongest arguments for direct cultural overlap between steppe Iranian and northwest Indian religious practice.

**Evidence tier: A** for the Achaemenid designation; **Tier B** for any argument about direct influence on Vedic soma practice.

### Ritual practices attested by Herodotus and archaeology

| Practice | Attestation | Relevance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Ritual cannabis use** (hemp vapor baths for purification) | Herodotus 4.73–75; archaeological finds of censers and cannabis seeds in burial contexts | Psychoactive ritual for spiritual purification — structural parallel to ascetic practice |
| **Horse sacrifice** | Throughout steppe territory; especially elaborate in royal kurgans | Supreme offering; funerary rite |
| **Blood-oath ceremony** | Herodotus; specialized vessels found | Mixing blood with wine; binding agreements |
| **Scapulimancy** (reading cracks in heated sheep shoulder blades) | Burned scapulae in ritual contexts | Divination |
| **Enareism** | Herodotus as "Enarees": cross-gender shamans/priests with special religious status | Suggests a fluid gender-religious role with shamanic functions |
| **Kurgan burial** | Archaeological record throughout steppe | Earthen mound over wooden-chambered grave — the stupa parallel |
| **Fire altars** | All Iranian steppe groups; archaeological remains at settled sites | Direct parallel to Zoroastrian and Vedic fire ritual |
| **Tattoos** | Preserved examples on Pazyryk mummies (Ukok Plateau) | Animal and mythological designs; ritual body-marking |

### Indra (Śakra / Sakka) as the Saka chief deity — and the √śak triangulation

The Pontic Scythian pantheon as recorded by Herodotus does not directly feature Indra. But the **eastern Saka** of Central Asia and the Tarim Basin occupied a different religious landscape — one in which Vedic influence was stronger and Zoroastrian reform had not fully taken hold. The source compilation (`saka-religion.csv`) identifies **Indra (Śakra/Sakka)** explicitly as the **"Saka chief god"** under Vedic influence, PIE cognate *Perkʷunos (the thunder deity). The `mithras-timeline.csv` further records at c. 300 BCE: *"Scythian solar gods, Indra (as Sakra/Sakka), Varuna"* and at c. 100 BCE: *"Sakka as king of gods (Indra); Saka rulers as dharma kings, solar symbolism."*

This produces a striking three-way convergence on a single root:

| Word | Meaning | Root |
|---|---|---|
| **Śaka** | "the Scythians/Saka" — the steppe Iranian peoples | √śak "to be capable, powerful" |
| **Śākya** | the Buddha's clan name | √śak (same root) |
| **Śakra / Sakka** | the Buddhist king of the gods (= Indra) | √śak (same root) |

All three are standard etymologies; none is contested in philology.

**Evidence tier: A** for the etymology. **Tier B** for the Saka Haumavargā's specific Indra/Śakra veneration.

#### The Deva/Daeva inversion

The theological significance of this convergence is sharpened by comparing how different Iranian traditions treated Indra:

| Tradition | Indra's status |
|---|---|
| Vedic Hinduism | Warrior king of the devas; principal deity of the Rigveda |
| Zoroastrianism (reformed) | *Indar* — explicitly a daeva (demon), condemned in the *Vendidad* |
| Saka Haumavargā | Chief god (pre-reform Vedic veneration retained alongside haoma ritual) |
| Early Buddhism | Positive deity; renamed Śakra/Sakka; devoted protector of the Dharma |

Buddhism's retention of Indra as a positive cosmic figure — and its choice of the epithet Śakra (√śak) rather than the Vedic name Indra — aligns early Buddhism with the pre-Zoroastrian *haoma*-drinking Saka tradition rather than with Zoroastrian reformism. This is consistent with Attwood's broader argument: the Śākyas may have shared religious culture with Saka groups who sat outside the Zoroastrian reform, keeping the old steppe Iranian veneration of the warrior storm god under his "powerful one" title.

**Evidence tier: B** — the Deva/Daeva inversion is Tier A fact; using it as an argument about Śākya-Saka religious alignment is Tier B inference.

#### Sakka as patron of the Śākyas in Pāli texts

The connection is not merely etymological. In the Pāli Canon, Sakka (= Indra) functions as a **devoted protector** of the Buddha and the Śākya lineage. He is present at the Buddha's birth, attends the parinibbāna, and appears repeatedly in the Jātakas testing and rewarding Bodhisattvas:

- **Sasa Jātaka** (#316): Sakka tests a rabbit-Bodhisattva; rewards his self-sacrifice by placing his image on the moon.
- **Sivi Jātaka** (#499): Sakka in disguise receives the Bodhisattva's eyes; sight is restored by the power of truth.
- **Sakka-pañha Sutta** (*DN* 21): Sakka asks the Buddha doctrinal questions and receives instruction — a notably humble role for a king-of-gods figure.

The Buddhist Sakka is "domesticated" compared to the thundering Vedic Indra: he is subject to death and rebirth, subordinate to the Buddha's authority, and defined primarily by his devotion to the Dharma. This reformulation makes the king-of-gods a devoted follower of the Śākya teacher — a relationship structurally unusual in world religion, where cosmic royalty is ordinarily the supreme authority.

**Evidence tier: A** — all Pāli textual references are well-established. The *interpretation* of patron-relationship as ethnic-religious memory is **Tier C**.

#### The speculative edge

The strongest speculative claim — **Tier C** — would be that Buddhist authors *chose* the epithet Śakra/Sakka for Indra precisely because it resonated with the Saka/Śākya ethno-religious identity; that the king-of-gods was narratively constructed as a Śaka-patron to legitimate the Śākyas' divine protection claim. No direct textual evidence supports intentionality of this kind. What can be said is: the etymological triangulation is real, the Saka veneration of Indra/Śakra is attested, and the Pāli texts' depiction of Sakka as a devoted protector of the Śākyas and their teacher is consistent with — though not proof of — a deeper cultural alignment between the Śākya clan and Saka religious heritage.

### The Issyk Golden Man and sacral kingship

The **Issyk kurgan** (c. 400–350 BCE, Semirechye, Kazakhstan) contained a Saka warrior-priest buried in an elaborate golden costume — over 4,000 golden plaques sewn onto garments, a tall conical golden headdress (*Tigraxaudā* type), and weapons. The burial strongly suggests a **sacral kingship** tradition: the ruler as both political and religious authority, clothed in cosmic symbolism. This is the most visually spectacular material evidence for Saka elite culture.

**Evidence tier: A** (excavated and published; Akishev, *Kurgan Issyk*, 1978).

### Pazyryk frozen tombs

The Pazyryk burials in the Altai Mountains (c. 400–300 BCE) preserve organic materials through permafrost: felt tapestries with mythological scenes, tattooed human bodies, wooden horse masks, and evidence of **cannabis smoking** equipment. The Ukok "Ice Maiden" (c. 500 BCE) was buried with cannabis seeds and shows elaborate tattoos — strong evidence for a female priestly or shamanic role.

## 3. Indian soldiers in the army of Xerxes: the earliest contact vector

Before Alexander, before Aśoka, and before the Indo-Greek kingdoms, there is one documented and datable mechanism by which Indians received direct information about both the Persian empire and the Greek world: **Indian soldiers served in the Achaemenid army of Xerxes during the second Persian invasion of Greece (480–479 BCE)**.

This is not a hypothesis. Herodotus (*Histories* 7.65, 7.70, 7.86) specifically lists Indian contingents in Xerxes' assembled force, describing their dress, weapons (cane bows and iron-tipped arrows), and commanders. Indian troops participated directly in the battles of Thermopylae and Salamis.

These soldiers returned to India. By the time they returned, Indians had a name for the Persians — *Kamboja* — and a name for the Greeks — *Yona* or *Yavana* (from *Iāwān*, the Semitic name for Ionians, transmitted through the Achaemenid multilingual bureaucracy). Both names appear in later Indian texts, including Aśokan edicts.

**Evidence tier: A** (Herodotus is a primary-source report; Indian contingents are listed specifically). The *interpretation* of how much cultural information was transmitted back remains Tier B.

**References:** Herodotus, *Histories* 7.65, 7.70, 7.86; Briant, Pierre. *From Cyrus to Alexander* (2002); Thapar, Romila. *Early India* (Penguin, 2002).

### Darius I, Gandhāra, and the earlier contact

Witzel and Beckwith additionally propose a **Darius I** vector, distinct from the Xerxes-era Indian soldiers: that Saka soldiers and settlers arrived in northwestern India as part of or following Darius I's conquest of the Indus Valley (c. 520 BCE), and that **wandering Saka ascetics** — the nomadic lifestyle transposed into a religious mode — were the cultural precursor to the wandering renunciant tradition that early Buddhism represents. On this reading, the Buddha's milieu was shaped not by soldiers returning from Greece but by an earlier Saka demographic presence in Gandhāra.

**Evidence tier: B** — the argument from Saka nomadism → wandering asceticism is a plausible typological parallel. The specific mechanism (Darius I military settlement) is not independently attested for this period.

### Three documented routes of Saka migration into India

The later (c. 2nd–1st century BCE) Saka migrations into the subcontinent took three documented paths, each with distinct archaeological and textual traces:

| Route | Path | Region entered |
|---|---|---|
| **Northern** | Pamir routes through the Karakoram Mountains (possibly via Khotan) | Swat and Gandhāra |
| **Central** | Via the Hindu Kush under Yuezhi displacement pressure | Northeastern Afghanistan (Bactria/Arachosia) |
| **Southwestern** | From Sakastan (modern Sistan/Baluchistan) | Modern Sindh (southern Pakistan) |

All three routes eventually connected to northwestern India. Saka kingdoms are attested in Gandhāra, Taxila, Mathura, and western India by the 1st century BCE. The **main Saka migration postdates the Buddha by 400+ years** — this is the central chronological objection to the Śākya-Saka identity hypothesis. Defenders of the hypothesis require an *earlier*, undocumented migration (c. 800–600 BCE, consistent with Parpola's and Van Geel et al.'s climate-driven scenario in §6.8).

## 5. The Pyrrho–Buddha parallel (Beckwith's core argument)

This is Beckwith's most specific and testable claim. **Pyrrho of Elis** (c. 360–270 BCE) traveled to India with Alexander's army (327–325 BCE). On his return, he founded Greek Skepticism — a philosophy that was recognized by contemporaries as radically different from earlier Greek thought.

The ancient testimony is that Pyrrho's key philosophical claim was that things are "**undifferentiated by logical differentia, unmeasurable, and undecidable**" (*adiaphora, astathmēta, anepikrita*). Beckwith argues this formulation directly parallels the **Trilakṣaṇa** (three characteristics of existence): impermanence, dissatisfaction/imperfection (*dukkha*), and no-self (*anattā*).

The parallel with **Nagarjuna** (c. 150–250 CE) is also noted: Nagarjuna's *catuskoti* (four-cornered logic) states: "it no more *is* than it *is not*, or it both *is* and *is not*, or it neither *is* nor *is not*" — which structurally mirrors Pyrrho's skeptical formula.

| Pyrrho's formula | Buddhist parallel |
| --- | --- |
| Things are undifferentiated (*adiaphora*) | *Śūnyatā* (emptiness) — no inherent differentiation |
| Unmeasurable (*astathmēta*) | *Anicca* (impermanence) — nothing has fixed, measurable essence |
| Undecidable (*anepikrita*) | *Anattā* (no-self) — no fixed subject to make the decision |

**Beckwith's additional observation:** Pyrrho's formula presents the characteristics in reverse order from the standard Buddhist Trilakṣaṇa — suggesting he encountered an early or variant form of the teaching, not the later standardized version.

**Evidence tier: B** — the philosophical parallel is striking and has generated genuine scholarly debate (see Richard Bett, *Pyrrho: His Antecedents and his Legacy*, Oxford 2000, for a skeptical but engaged treatment). The claim that Pyrrho specifically encountered *early Buddhist* teaching (rather than Jain, Ājīvika, or other śramaṇa dialectic) is Tier C.

## 6. Greater Magadha: framing the question

Before cataloguing the specific evidence, a crucial framing point from **Johannes Bronkhorst** (*Greater Magadha: Studies in the Culture of Early India*, Brill, 2007). Bronkhorst is not a Scythian-origin advocate — he explicitly criticizes Beckwith's hypothesis. But his framework is indispensable for understanding why the "local versus imported" question is a live historical question at all.

Bronkhorst argues that the region in which early Buddhism, Jainism, and the Ājīvika movement all emerged — roughly northeastern India, east of the Gandak river, the historical "Greater Magadha" — constituted a **distinct cultural zone** with its own non-Vedic religious ecology. This region was characterized by:

- *Śramaṇa* religiosity (wandering renunciants, asceticism, non-violence) that predated and was independent of Brahmanical Vedic tradition
- Different attitudes toward karma, rebirth, and the efficacy of ritual action
- Weak Brahmanical presence compared to the Vedic *Āryāvarta* heartland to the west
- Political organization via *gaṇasaṅgha* republics rather than monarchies

The Śākyas are placed at the **northwestern edge** of Greater Magadha — on the boundary between this non-Vedic śramaṇic zone and the Himalayan foothills. This geographical liminal position is important: the Śākyas are simultaneously "inside" the Greater Magadha cultural region (participating in its śramaṇic religiosity) and "at the frontier" (adjacent to the Himalayan passes through which Central Asian contacts could travel).

**Why this matters for the Śākya-Saka question:** Bronkhorst's framework makes the Manusmṛiti and Baudhāyana brahmanical anxiety intelligible without relying on the polemical caste language those texts use. The northeast *was* genuinely different from Vedic Āryāvarta. The question the Śākya-Saka hypothesis poses — whether that difference reflects, in part, Iranian steppe influence absorbed at an early date — becomes a real and well-formed historical question rather than a fringe argument, precisely because Bronkhorst (the hypothesis' critic) has independently established that the region was non-Vedic. One can accept his "Greater Magadha" framing entirely and still ask: *what were the non-Vedic influences?*

There is a further tension in Bronkhorst's own position worth noting: his *Greater Magadha* thesis attributes the non-Vedic character of early Buddhism to an *indigenous* śramaṇic tradition — but the śramaṇic tradition itself (wandering asceticism, karma, rebirth, non-violence, the pursuit of liberation through insight rather than sacrifice) has structural features that several scholars find difficult to explain as purely autochthonous developments. Bronkhorst's framework **raises the question more acutely than his answer closes it**. By establishing that Greater Magadha was a genuinely distinct cultural zone, he makes the search for non-Vedic formative influences more pressing — not less.

**Evidence tier: A** for Bronkhorst's Greater Magadha cultural-zone thesis (mainstream and well-received in Buddhist studies). **Tier B–C** for the subsequent use of that thesis to create space for the Iranian-contact hypothesis.

## 7. Attwood (2012): full annotated evidence

This section annotates Jayarava Attwood, "Possible Iranian Origins for the Śākyas and Aspects of Buddhism," *JOCBS* 3 (2012): 47–69. Attwood frames this as a hypothesis warranting further scholarly attention, not a concluded finding.

> "While this is an argument from circumstantial evidence, the evidence, such as it is, makes a connection between the Śākyas and Iran at least plausible. If this thesis is correct, then some features of Buddhism are actually cultural features of the Śākya tribe preserved from an earlier period of living in Iran." — Attwood (2012: 68)

> "It still allows for the Buddha as visionary and innovator, and it does not deny the influence of Brahmins and Jains, but it broadens the cultural pool from which he might have drawn his ideas." — Attwood (2012: 68)

### 7.1 Sibling marriage traditions

**(Tier B)**

In the **Ambaṭṭha Sutta** (DN i.87), the progenitors of the Śākyas are exiled from their kingdom and, unable to find suitable spouses, marry their own sisters. This incestuous origin is preserved despite the later Buddhist condemnation of incest. Attwood applies the "Principle of Embarrassment" from NT criticism: details unflattering to the group are more likely to be historical precisely because they would not be invented.

Jonathan Silk confirms the Iranian practice: "there is good evidence for this practice called *xvaētuuadaϑa*, so-called next-of-kin or close-kin marriage" (Silk 2008b: 377). This practice (*xvaetvadatha*) was specifically associated with the Zoroastrian religious elite and with Iranian royal dynasties. The Achaemenid kings practiced close-kin marriage; it was theologically valorized in Zoroastrianism as a righteous act.

**If real:** the Śākyas preserved an Iranian marriage custom that was specifically *religious* in Zoroastrian context, suggesting not merely ethnic but cultural-religious continuity with Iranian practice.

**Cross-cousin marriage as additional kinship evidence (Tier C):** Some discussions of Śākya kinship argue that the clan's origin traditions may preserve **cross-cousin marriage** practices — a kinship pattern typically associated with Dravidian or otherwise non-Vedic social organization, and distinct from the *gotra* exogamy system standard in Indo-Aryan Brahmanical custom. This is not a Scythian-specific argument: cross-cousin marriage points toward Dravidian or Austroasiatic rather than Iranian cultural affiliation. But cumulatively it adds another marker of non-standard north Indian kinship organization for the clan — consistent with the *saṃkīrṇa-yonayaḥ* ("mixed origin") designation and the Munda substrate evidence. Multiple non-Vedic kinship features in the same clan increases the prior probability that the Śākyas represent a genuinely heterogeneous ethnic formation, whatever the specific origins.

### 7.2 The Śākyas as described in Buddhist texts

**(Tier B)**

Buddhist texts themselves flag the Śākyas as unusual within the Indian social landscape:

- The **Ambattha Sutta** (DN i.90–91) describes the Śākyas as "fierce, rough-spoken, touchy and violent" — accusing them of not honouring Brahmins "owing to their menial origin." This outsider characterization is internally attested and uses the language of social stigma, not just regional distance.
- The Śākyas are **absent from the earliest Vedic literature**. They do not appear in the Rigveda, the Atharvaveda, or the early Brāhmaṇas — which would be expected if they were a long-established local clan in the Indo-Aryan heartland.
- The Śākyas are characterized as **saṃkīrṇa-yonayaḥ** ("of mixed origin") in Indian literature, and are said not to subscribe to the standard *caturvarṇa* social organization. This "mixed-origin" designation is a technical social category, not a pejorative in isolation — but it marks them as outside the normal Indo-Aryan genealogical grid.
- The **Manusmṛiti** groups the Vaidehas, Māgadhīs, Licchavikas, and Mallakas — neighboring peoples of the Śākyas — as born from "mixed caste marriages" and effectively non-Aryan in social standing. The **Baudhāyana-Dharmaśāstras** go further, requiring travelers who visit these northeastern lands to perform purificatory sacrifices on return — treating the region as ritually contaminating. Attwood reads this brahmanical anxiety as reflecting genuine ethnic and cultural difference in the northeastern borderlands.
- Their **republican (gaṇasaṅgha) political structure** — a warrior oligarchy with assembly governance rather than hereditary monarchy — was unusual for the Gangetic plain and more consistent with steppe confederacy organization than with the established rājasūya-crowned monarchies of the Vedic tradition.
- The **relic-claim episode** after the Buddha's death (Mahāparinibbāna Sutta) is a telling marker of Śākya political identity: the Śākyas of Kapilavatthu mobilized as a distinct polity to claim a share of the Buddha's relics, alongside seven other tribal and royal groups. The fact that they did so — and were awarded their share by arbitration — shows that the Śākyas maintained a recognized collective political identity well into the generation after the Buddha. This does not establish Scythian origin, but it documents a level of cohesive tribal-political organization more consistent with a confederate steppe polity than a small local clan absorbed into a Gangetic monarchy.

**Counter-argument:** the same sources consistently locate the Śākyas in the Himalayan foothills, far from any documented Saka-Scythian presence in this period. Absence from Vedic texts could reflect their northeastern peripheral location, not foreign origin. The "mixed origin" designation may describe local ethnic mixing rather than Central Asian immigration.

### 7.3 Physical descriptions in Buddhist sources

**(Tier C)**

Some Buddhist texts and later Gandharan art describe the Buddha and the Śākyas with physical features atypical for the Gangetic plain:

- Descriptions of unusual height and light complexion appear in some biographical texts.
- Gandharan art (c. 1st–4th centuries CE) depicts the Buddha with clearly Hellenistic / Central Asian features — wavy hair, draped robes, occasionally blue or grey eyes. This is universally attributed to artistic syncretism (Hellenistic influence on Gandharan sculptors) rather than historical memory of the Buddha's appearance.

**Counter-argument:** such descriptions are rare, possibly symbolic (idealization of the *mahāpuruṣa* / great man), and Gandharan art postdates the Buddha by half a millennium. Physical description arguments are not admitted in mainstream historical analysis.

### 7.4 Buddhist stūpas and Scythian kurgan burials

**(Tier B)**

Witzel (2012) notes that Buddhist stūpas are "similar to the kurgan type grave mounds of Southern Russia and Central Asia." H. G. Rawlinson (1916) made the same observation independently:

> "...relic-worship, and its concomitant the stūpa, are quite un-Indian. Gautama belonged to the Śākya clan: were they an early offshoot of the Śakas, the Sacae or Scyths...? The word *stūpa* signifies a 'barrow,' or 'tumulus,' a Sanskrit name for a Scythian object..." (Rawlinson 1916: 15–16)

Przyluski (1935) and Werner (1987, 2002) independently noted the structural similarities: earthen mound over a central chamber, relics of a sacred figure at the core, circumambulation. The Pazyryk and Issyk kurgans show exactly this structure.

**Counter-argument:** stūpa construction has multiple possible antecedents, including pre-Buddhist Indian funerary mounds (the Mahāparinibbāna Sutta describes the Buddha being buried under a stūpa "as befits a cakravartin king," implying the form was already known). Direct derivation is not proven.

### 7.5 Division of actions: body, speech, and mind

**(Tier B)**

Caroline Rhys Davids (1946) notes that the Buddhist division of human action into body, speech, and mind (*kāya, vāca, citta*) parallels the Zoroastrian ethical triad *humata, hūxta, hvaršta* (good thought, good word, good deed). This triad is said to "encapsulate the ethical goals of Zoroastrianism" (Boyce 2007). Rhys Davids notes the triad is not found in other early Indian texts, suggesting it is not autochthonous to the Brahmanical tradition.

The parallel is: both systems divide moral agency into the same three domains — mental, verbal, physical — and both make this tripartite division the core framework for ethical evaluation. The order differs (Buddhist: body first; Zoroastrian: thought first), but the structure is identical.

### 7.6 Karma and the weighing of deeds

**(Tier B)**

Witzel notes that post-mortem judgment by weighing deeds was not a Vedic concept:

> "This was first an Egyptian, then a Zoroastrian and Iranian concept. It is connected with the idea of personal responsibility for one's action (karma)." (Witzel 2012)

In Zoroastrianism, the soul crosses the **Chinvat Bridge** and is judged based on the balance of thoughts, words, and deeds. In early Buddhism, karma operates through individual moral accounting — the weight of actions determining rebirth conditions. The Vedic tradition does not have individual post-mortem moral accounting; Vedic afterlife is collective (the *pitṛloka*) and depends on correct performance of sacrificial ritual, not ethical balance.

**Counter-argument:** Indian traditions developed karma concepts independently from multiple directions (Jain karma theory is possibly older than Buddhist; the Upanishadic concept of action-consequence is indigenous). Direction of influence remains debated.

### 7.7 Linguistic evidence: Śākya and Śaka

**(Tier B)**

Both *Śaka* (Scythian) and *Śākya* appear to derive from the root √*śak*, "to be able, strong, or powerful." *Śākya* is probably a derivative form meaning "related to, or descended from, the Śakas" (cf. Monier-Williams s.v. *Śākya*).

**Levman's competing etymology:** **Bryan Levman** has proposed an independent derivation that entirely severs the Scythian connection. On his reading, "Śākya" derives from *śaka* or *sāka* — the name of a tree (identified variously with teak or the sāla tree), itself related to *śākhā* ("branch"). Levman ties this to the Śākyas' attested practice of tree-worship: the clan name would then mean "people of the śaka tree" or "those connected to the śaka tree," referencing a cultic or totemic practice rather than an ethnic label. Levman explicitly maintains that the Śākyas were a population native to the northeastern Gangetic plain with no Iranian genealogy. This etymology is not the mainstream philological position, but it is the strongest single rebuttal to the Śaka–Śākya name equation.

The **Gaumāta–Gautama** connection (from the Bardiya hypothesis) is **Tier C** and linguistically separate: Gaumāta is Old Iranian (*gau-māta*, cattle + personal suffix); Gautama is Sanskrit (*go-tama*, superlative of "cattle," a common patronymic). Both share the PIE root *\*gʷow-* (cow/cattle), but this is a very common root across all IE languages and the derivational paths differ significantly. No established linguistic pathway from Old Persian *Gaumāta* to Sanskrit *Gautama* has been proposed in peer-reviewed comparative linguistics.

**Standard references:** Mayrhofer, *Etymologisches Wörterbuch des Altindoarischen* (EWAia); Cheung, *Etymological Dictionary of the Iranian Verb* — neither traces the Gaumāta/Gautama connection.

### 7.8 Migration timeline

**(Tier B)**

Two independent lines converge on approximately the same date for Iranian migration into northwest India:

- **Parpola (2008)**: based on textual and archaeological data, hypothesizes Iranians generically called *Dāṇḍu* entered India around **800 BCE** via the Indus Valley.
- **Van Geel et al. (2004)**: climate scientists document an abrupt climate shift around **850 BCE** that caused rapid Scythian culture expansion on the steppe and drought pressure in northwest India — creating conditions for Saka migration southward.

If this migration occurred, it would predate the main Saka incursions into India (c. 2nd century BCE) by over 600 years — placing Iranian-speaking groups in the northwestern frontier at approximately the time when the Śākyas, if of steppe origin, might have been migrating toward the Himalayan foothills.

### 7.9 Achaemenid administrative contact and script transmission

**(Tier A)**

The Achaemenid Empire controlled territory as far east as the Indus River from the late 6th century BCE. The administrative consequences are directly attested:

- **Kharoṣṭhī script**: derived from Aramaic (Achaemenid administrative script) — the script of the earliest Buddhist manuscripts. **Tier A.**
- The word **lipi** ("writing") is a Persian loanword in Indian languages (Salomon 1999: 9). **Tier A.**
- **Babylonian astronomical knowledge** entered India via Iranian intermediaries (Pingree 1963: 246). **Tier B.**

### 7.10 Divination practices

**(Tier B)**

The list of divination techniques prohibited in the Brahmajāla Sutta is "almost identical in form and content to Mesopotamian divination manuals *Šumma ālu* and *Enūma anu Enlil*" (Pingree 1995, 1996). The prohibition implies these practices were actively used in northeast India — meaning Mesopotamian divination knowledge had already arrived in the Buddha's milieu, almost certainly via the Achaemenid intermediary.

### 7.11 Munda substrate: village names and the clan founder's name

**(Tier B)**

The Śākyas appear to have been **at least bilingual**, living under significant linguistic influence from **Munda** (Austroasiatic) languages. Many villages in the Śākya homeland bear Mundari names — a substrate that suggests the area was previously or concurrently populated by non-Indo-Aryan Munda-speaking peoples. This bilingual situation is not unique to the Śākyas in the northeastern borderlands, but its density in their territory is noted by scholars.

More specific is the name of the Śākya clan's legendary founding ancestor. The Sanskrit tradition gives his name as **Ikṣvāku** (lit. "bitter gourd/cucumber" — a folk etymology). The Pāli tradition, however, preserves a different form: **Okkāka**. Scholars have proposed that *Okkāka* is of **Munda origin** — a name that entered the tradition from an Austroasiatic substrate rather than being a native Indo-Aryan formation. If the founding eponym of the clan is a Munda word, this points to the Śākyas as a group formed at or near the Indo-Aryan / Munda ethnic boundary — consistent with the *saṃkīrṇa-yonayaḥ* ("mixed origin") designation.

This does **not** directly support the Saka-Scythian hypothesis, but it is consistent with the broader picture of the Śākyas as a border people — not fully integrated into Brahmanical Indo-Aryan society — whose cultural memory encoded heterogeneous origins. Munda linguistic influence and possible Central Asian Iranian contact are not mutually exclusive; border populations often exhibit multiple strata.

**Counter-argument:** the Munda name interpretation of *Okkāka* is proposed, not established by consensus. Pāli is subject to significant phonological transformation from Sanskrit; the divergence of *Okkāka* from *Ikṣvāku* may reflect internal Pāli phonology rather than an external substrate.

### 7.12 The Śākya solar lineage (*Sūryavaṃśa*) and Saka solar religion

**(Tier B)**

Buddhist texts locate the Śākyas within the **Sūryavaṃśa** — the "solar lineage" or "solar dynasty" — descending from the mythological king **Ikṣvāku**, himself descended from Vivasvat, the Sun. The claim appears in the *Mahāvastu*, Jātaka narratives (e.g., Jātaka 454), and in later Buddhist biographical literature, where the lineage functions as a prestige genealogy for the clan. It is a Brahmanical genealogical framework; many Indian royal dynasties make similar claims.

However, it intersects with one of the most secure aspects of Saka religion: **solar worship as a primary cult**. The relevant data points already in §2 of this article:

- The **Massagetae** (a Saka group) worshipped the Sun as their sole deity, offering horse sacrifices to it (Herodotus 1.216)
- **Goitosyros** (= Apollo in Herodotus's account) was the solar-oath deity of the Pontic Scythians
- **Saka kings functioned as solar representatives** — the Issyk Golden Man's solar regalia embodies exactly this ideology
- The *mithras-timeline.csv* records Saka rulers explicitly tied to "solar symbolism" and "Saka kings as dharma kings" with solar legitimacy

If the Śākyas were of Saka cultural background, the *Sūryavaṃśa* claim could encode a genuine religious identity with a solar deity — one that was subsequently translated into the Brahmanical Ikṣvāku genealogical framework as the clan acquired legitimacy within Indian social systems. A clan with actual solar religious traditions would naturally accept, or be assigned, a solar lineage within the Brahmanical grid.

**Counter-argument:** *Sūryavaṃśa* is the single most common Indian royal legitimation claim; it was applied to dozens of dynasties without any implication of Scythian origin. The Śākyas' claim may be entirely conventional. The overlap with Saka solar worship could be coincidental given the shared Indo-Iranian background of all solar veneration in the region.

### 7.13 Iranian doctrinal parallels: research directions

**(Tier C — areas warranting investigation, not established findings)**

Attwood (2012) surveyed kinship customs, political organization, and select ritual parallels, but several doctrinal areas have not received systematic comparison. These are framed here as **research directions** — genuine questions that have not been resolved — rather than as evidence for the hypothesis.

#### Light and dark as ethical cosmology

Zoroastrianism has explicit cosmic light/dark dualism: Ahura Mazda (the Wise Lord, associated with light and fire) is in eternal opposition to Angra Mainyu (the Destructive Spirit, associated with darkness). Early Buddhism does not replicate this ontological dualism — there is no cosmic evil principle — but the **metaphorical structure** is strikingly similar:

- *Avijjā* ("not-knowing," ignorance, darkness) is the root of *saṃsāra* — the ground state of unredeemed existence
- *Vijjā* (knowledge) and *bodhi* (enlightenment, lit. "awakening," related to *luminosity*) are the liberating counterparts
- The Buddha is persistently described with light imagery: golden body (*suvaṇṇavarṇa*), the *prabhā* (radiant halo), and epithets like *jyotiṣkara* ("light-maker") and *obhāsaka* ("illuminator")

Whether this light/wisdom vs. darkness/ignorance structure entered early Buddhism from an Iranian milieu — as the same Iranian influence demonstrably entered Second Temple Judaism (see *See also*: [Dead Sea Scrolls dualism and Iranian parallels](dead-sea-scrolls-dualism-and-iranian-parallels.md)) — or developed independently within the Śramaṇic tradition, has not been examined by a specialist comparative study. The structural parallel is real; direction of influence is unresolved.

#### *Ṛta / asha* and Buddhist *sacca*

The Zoroastrian ethical framework opposes *asha* ("truth / cosmic order") to *druj* ("the Lie") as the fundamental moral division of the cosmos. In the *Gāthās*, telling the truth and living by *asha* is the highest ethical obligation. Buddhism places similar weight on *sacca* (truth) as a fundamental virtue — Right Speech (*sammā vācā*) is the third factor of the Noble Eightfold Path, and *truthfulness* (*sacca*) is one of the perfections (*pāramitā*). The *Mahāparinibbāna Sutta* preserves the Buddha's last teaching as centered on truth; the *Saccakiriyā* (Act of Truth) tradition treats truthful declaration as a source of apotropaic power.

This structural parallel — truth as a metaphysical force, not just a social norm — is noted in §8 of this article (Bardiya–Gaumāta theory) as "Tier B typology." Whether it represents independent convergence or actual transmission via the Iranian milieu of early Buddhism has not been argued systematically in the literature.

#### Cosmic time and world ages

Buddhist cosmology includes elaborate **cosmic time cycles** (*kalpas*, *mahākalpas*) and world-age succession. Zoroastrian cosmology similarly divides cosmic history into 12,000-year periods, with each 3,000-year age governed by different cosmic forces. The Hindu *yuga* system offers an equally plausible indigenous source for Buddhist cosmic time, and the Indian tradition's independent development of cyclic cosmology is well-attested. This remains the least developed of the three parallels — it would require specialist comparison of the specific numerical and structural features of Buddhist *kalpa* cosmology against Avestan *hazāra* literature before any claim could be responsibly formulated.

## 8. The Bardiya-Gaumāta theory

The **Behistun inscription** (Darius I, ~520 BCE) describes how a Magian named **Gaumāta** seized the throne by impersonating **Bardiya** (Greek: Smerdis), the murdered brother of Cambyses II. This is **Achaemenid political propaganda** — Iranian historians debate whether Darius himself was the usurper and the "real Bardiya" was eliminated by him.

The **speculative hypothesis** (advanced by Attwood and others in extended form): the name *Gaumāta* resembles Sanskrit *Gautama*; the narrative of a prince who renounces or is ejected from royal life, wanders, and founds a counter-establishment has structural parallels to the Buddha story; and the chronological window (6th–5th cent. BCE) overlaps.

| Element | Behistun / Achaemenid | Buddhist tradition | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Name** | Gaumāta (Old Iranian) | Gautama (Sanskrit) | Both share PIE cattle root; derivational paths differ — **not cognate** |
| **Royal displacement** | Bardiya (or impostor) loses throne | Siddhārtha renounces kingship | Generic structural parallel — **Tier C** |
| **"Truth vs. Lie"** | Darius frames coup as *arta* defeating *drauga* | Buddhist *dhamma* vs. ignorance | Zoroastrian ethical dualism as backdrop — **Tier B typology** |
| **Geography** | Eastern Achaemenid satrapies | Gangetic plain (Magadha, Kosala) | Requires non-standard geographical interpretation — **not accepted** |

**Why this remains Tier C:** phonetic resemblance ≠ cognacy; structural parallels are generic; Buddhist geography is internally consistent and detailed; no specialist consensus.

## 9. The Lumbini controversy

The traditional birthplace of the Buddha is **Lumbini** (modern Nepal), anchored by an Aśokan pillar inscription (3rd century BCE). This is the primary archaeological evidence for the traditional site — but its reliability has been contested.

### The Fuhrer fraud

The original 1896 identification of Lumbini was led by **Dr. Alois Anton Fuhrer**, who was later exposed for multiple archaeological frauds, including fabricating inscriptions and artifacts. Terence Alan Phelps and others argue that the site's association with the Buddha is based on dubious evidence and "a gigantic fraud." Critics note that neither Tilaurakot (proposed Kapilavastu) nor Lumbini have yielded Buddhist votive tablets, sealings, or statues from the earliest periods — which is unusual for major pilgrimage sites.

### Robin Coningham's excavations

**Robin Coningham**'s team (UNESCO; University of Durham) found a **wooden shrine** beneath the Maya Devi Temple at Lumbini, radiocarbon-dated to c. **550 BCE** — potentially the oldest Buddhist shrine. The shrine's layout, with an open space for a tree, matches Buddhist birth-legend traditions.

Some archaeologists (Julia Shaw) caution that the wooden structure could have been a non-Buddhist cultic site, and the link to the Buddha's birth is not conclusively proven. A "palatial compound" found at Kapilavastu (Tilaurakot) has been dated to approximately the 6th century BCE, consistent with Buddhist tradition.

### The competing site: Piprahwa

Indian archaeologists have promoted **Piprahwa** (Uttar Pradesh, India) as the location of Kapilavastu, while Nepalese scholars favor Tilaurakot (Nepal). Both claims are disputed, with no academic consensus. The ongoing dispute illustrates the political dimensions of the archaeology: UNESCO, Nepal, and India all have strong economic and cultural incentives.

**What the controversy does and does not establish:** It does not support the Saka-Scythian theory directly. It does establish that the traditional geographic anchoring of the Buddha's life to the Gangetic-plain / Himalayan-foothills region — often cited as the primary refutation of northwest-Indian-origin theories — rests on later and not uncontested archaeological evidence.

**Evidence tier: A** for the existence of the Aśokan pillar at Lumbini; **Tier B** for the identification of the Maya Devi shrine with the exact birth site; **Tier C** for any counter-geography claims.

## 10. The Saka cultural legacy in India

### Gandhāra, Taxila, and Mathura: Saka and Parthian donations to Buddhist communities

**(Tier A)**

Inscriptions recording the establishment of Buddhist relics and donations to monasteries across the northwest — **Gandhāra, Taxila, Mathura**, and western India — document that **Sakas, Parthians, and other Iranians** were among the most active lay supporters of the early Buddhist community. These are not isolated texts: the pattern extends across multiple sites and multiple generations of Iranian rulers and merchants.

The Kharoshthi inscriptions from Gandhāra (collected in Salomon, *Indian Epigraphy*, 1998) include donations by Saka and Parthian patrons whose names carry explicitly Iranian prefixes. The recurrence of Iranian personal names in donors' inscriptions at sites such as **Taxila, Sirkap, Butkara, and the Swat Valley** provides material evidence for the sustained identification of Iranian-origin communities with Buddhist patronage.

The simplest explanation — that Saka and Parthian groups patronized Buddhism because it was the religion of their overlords (Mauryan, then Sungan, then Kushana) — is not wrong, but it does not explain the *intensity* and *geographic distribution* of Iranian Buddhist patronage, which exceeds what one would expect from mere political accommodation. Proponents of the Śākya-Saka hypothesis argue this sustained affinity reflects a deeper cultural continuity.

### Queen Ayasya's Mathura inscription

**(Tier A)**

A lion-capital excavated at **Mathura** in 1869 carries Kharoshthi inscriptions recording donations by the Shaka Queen **Ayasya** (*Aiyasiyā*) to a **Sarvāstivāda** Buddhist monastery (the school central to Gandhāran manuscript transmission). Ayasya expresses the wish that the merit of her donation extend to "**all of Śākastan**" — calling the northwest the Śaka homeland, a name that persisted as "Sistan" (from *Śakastān*) in eastern Iran. This is direct evidence of Saka rulers identifying themselves ethnically and patronizing Buddhism simultaneously. **Reference:** Konow, Sten. *Kharoshthī Inscriptions with the Exception of Those of Aśoka* (CII II.1, 1929).

### Saka dating systems

The Shakas inaugurated two calendar systems still in use:
- **Vikrama Era** (beginning 57 BCE by convention)
- **Śaka Era** (beginning 78 CE) — adopted in 1957 as one of the **official calendars of the Republic of India**

The Śaka Era outlasted Saka political power by two millennia.

### Shakas and Sanskrit as imperial language

**Sheldon Pollock** (*The Language of the Gods in the World of Men*, UC Press, 2006) argues that Saka-era rulers were the **inaugurators of Sanskrit in royal eulogies** (*praśasti* inscriptions). The earliest datable *praśastis* are associated with Kusana and early Saka rulers in the northwestern borderlands — not with the Sanskrit-speaking Brahmanical heartland. The Sakas elevated Sanskrit from a priestly language to an **imperial prestige language**, with consequences for its eastward transmission through the Silk Road. This is the **barbarian inaugurator pattern** documented across the Indo-Greeks (anthropomorphic Buddha image), Kushans (Silk Road infrastructure), and Achaemenid Persians (Kharoṣṭhī script, Hebrew Bible canonization). See [The barbarian inaugurator pattern](barbarian-inaugurator-pattern.md).

## 12. Editorial synthesis

### Tier summary

- **Tier A (established):** Achaemenid and later Saka polities **did** connect Iranian, Indic, and Central Asian cultural space — see [Gandhara and Central Asian transmission](gandhara-central-asian-transmission.md). Kharoṣṭhī script derives from Aramaic. Indian soldiers served in Xerxes' army. Bronkhorst's Greater Magadha was a genuinely non-Vedic cultural zone. Saka rulers patronized Buddhism at Gandhāra, Taxila, and Mathura. The √śak etymological root is shared by Śaka, Śākya, and Śakra/Sakka.
- **Tier B (plausible, not proven):** Northwest Indian Buddhism absorbed Hellenistic, Iranian, and steppe visual and political idioms. The stūpa/kurgan parallel, the body-speech-mind triad, sibling marriage, karma-weighing, the Deva/Daeva inversion, and the solar lineage cluster represent a cumulative circumstantial case that Iranian cultural features are embedded in early Buddhist practice. The chronological gap, if closeable by earlier migration evidence, would raise several of these to Tier A.
- **Tier C (speculative):** The Śākyas *are* genetically or ethnically Saka-Scythian; the Buddha's epithet *Śākyamuni* proves foreign birth; Gaumāta = Gautama biographically; light/dark dualism, cosmic time, and ritual structure represent Iranian transmission.

### The most defensible formulation

The most defensible version of the argument remains Attwood's (2012) framing: not that the Buddha was a Scythian, but that **some features of early Buddhism are cultural features of the Śākya tribe that were inherited from an earlier period of Iranian cultural contact** — which is a much weaker and more plausible claim than biographical identification. This modest claim can absorb the chronological objection (it requires only *cultural contact* before the main Saka migrations, not that the Śākyas themselves were migrants) while still explaining the cumulative evidence.

### Overall verdict

The hypothesis is currently at the level of **"interesting, cumulative, and not yet refuted"** — which is meaningfully different from both "established" and "fringe." No single item in the evidence catalogue is decisive; no single item has been definitively refuted. The case rests on a cluster of observations that, individually, have competing explanations, but whose cumulative weight exceeds what would be expected from coincidence and shared PIE background alone.

Three areas are under-dismissed by critics:

1. **The Deva/Daeva inversion.** Zoroastrianism explicitly demonized Indra; early Buddhism retained him and renamed him Śakra/Sakka — from the same root as both Śaka and Śākya. The Saka Haumavargā sat outside the Zoroastrian reform that produced that demonization. The alignment of early Buddhism with the pre-reform Iranian veneration of Indra is a substantive observation about religious family resemblance that critics have not directly addressed.
2. **Bronkhorst's own framework creates the problem he denies.** By establishing Greater Magadha as a genuinely non-Vedic zone, he makes the search for formative non-Vedic influences more urgent — not less. His answer ("indigenous śramaṇic tradition") presupposes exactly what needs explaining.
3. **The √śak / Sūryavaṃśa / Sakka cluster.** The Śākyas claim a solar lineage, share a name-root with steppe peoples who had prominent solar cults, and their patron deity in Buddhist texts is named Śakra from the same root. As a cluster — not as individual items — this is more than common-root coincidence.

### What it would take to move to Tier B consensus

Two conditions would need to be met simultaneously:

1. **An early (pre-6th century BCE) Iranian-speaking or Iranian-influenced presence in the northeastern frontier**, independently attested archaeologically or genetically. Without this, the chronological gap remains the hypothesis's single most damaging weakness. Parpola's 800 BCE scenario and Van Geel et al.'s climate data suggest a plausible mechanism; direct Indian evidence is lacking.
2. **At least one feature of early Buddhism that has no plausible non-Iranian explanation and is structurally specific.** The sibling marriage parallel comes closest: it is embarrassing in the Buddhist context (invoking the Principle of Embarrassment), rare as a religious custom, and specifically documented as an Iranian royal-religious practice. But it has not been accepted by the mainstream as diagnostic. A further candidate would be an Iranian theological loanword in the oldest Pāli strata — one that could not be attributed to general Achaemenid administrative contact.

## 13. Open questions and research directions

The following evidence types have the highest potential to shift the debate. They are ranked by likely impact and, where relevant, feasibility.

### High-value: potentially decisive

**Ancient DNA from the Terai (6th–5th century BCE sites).** No ancient DNA exists yet from Nepalese lowland or Himalayan-foothill sites dated to the Śākya period. If Śākya-region burials could be genotyped, steppe/Central Asian admixture would substantially support the hypothesis; its absence would be significant evidence against. This is the single test that could shift the debate most conclusively in either direction. The technical obstacle is the humid tropical-to-subtropical burial environment, which degrades ancient DNA rapidly — but permafrost-adjacent Himalayan sites may be more tractable than lowland ones.

**Iranian loanwords in the oldest Pāli strata.** K.R. Norman's philological work identifies the *Sutta Nipāta* (esp. the *Aṭṭhakavagga* and *Pārāyanavagga*) and *Dhammapada* as the oldest recoverable Buddhist textual layers. A dedicated search for Iranian theological vocabulary — beyond the already-known administrative loanwords (*lipi*, etc.) that affect all of north India — would be significant. If the words for karma, specific meditation states, or ethical concepts in these early strata show Iranian cognates that diverge from Sanskrit forms, that would be strong direct evidence. Absence of such loanwords is also informative: it would strengthen the mainstream view that cultural contact was general rather than Śākya-specific.

**Gandhari manuscript linguistic analysis.** The Gāndhārī Buddhist manuscripts (1st–4th c. CE, British Library and Schøyen collections) are written in Kharoṣṭhī from the exact northwest contact zone. Systematic study of Iranian influence on their vocabulary and doctrinal idiom — compared against Pāli Canon parallels — could reveal whether specifically Iranian theological language entered Buddhist transmission in the northwest. This is technically feasible given the ongoing Gandhari manuscript projects (Salomon's group at UW).

### Medium-value: clarifying but not decisive

**The *Okkāka* name: a Munda specialist study.** The claim that the Śākya founding ancestor's Pāli name *Okkāka* is of Munda rather than Indo-Aryan origin (§7.11) is proposed but not established. A dedicated etymological study by a Munda language specialist (not a Pāli scholar working from general principles) could confirm or close this substrate hypothesis. If confirmed, it directly supports the multi-ethnic border-group picture without which the Iranian-contact scenario makes less geographic sense.

**The *Saccakiriyā* tradition and Zoroastrian *asha*: a comparative study.** The Buddhist "Act of Truth" — where a truthful declaration has apotropaic power — is structurally very close to the Zoroastrian *asha* force (§7.13). A dedicated monograph comparing the role of truth in Gāthic Zoroastrianism, early Buddhism (*sacca* as *pāramitā*; the *Saccakiriyā* texts), and Vedic *ṛta* could establish whether the parallel is structural convergence or represents genuine Iranian transmission via a mechanism distinct from the general Achaemenid contact.

**Saka patronage inscriptions: doctrinal content analysis.** Existing scholarship (Konow, Salomon) catalogues *who* donated to Buddhist sites; less attention has been paid to *what doctrinal language* those inscriptions use. If Saka patrons specifically donated to schools with Iranian-inflected doctrines (Sarvāstivāda has certain cosmological features that some scholars find Iranian-adjacent), that would be more than patronage data — it would suggest active doctrinal affinity.

**The *gaṇasaṅgha* structure comparative study.** The republican-oligarchic political form the Śākyas used appears in the same northeast Indian zone among Licchavis and Vajjians — who are not suspected of Saka origin. This is currently used as a refutation: the form was regional, not Saka-specific. However, a systematic comparison of *gaṇasaṅgha* governance structures with steppe confederacy organization (using Scythian and early Central Asian polity records) could either confirm the resemblance is superficial or show it has structural specificity beyond what the "regional coincidence" explanation accounts for.

### Lower-value: speculative but trackable

**The Amitābha / Saka solar religion thread.** Pure Land Buddhism's *Amitābha* ("Infinite Light") has been tentatively connected to Iranian solar religion by a few scholars. If a continuous thread can be traced from Śākya solar lineage (*Sūryavaṃśa*) → solar Buddha iconography → Amitābha's light theology — and if Saka/Kushana patronage of specifically Pure Land traditions is attested — that would be a culturally coherent transmission argument across several centuries. This is Tier C until someone argues it systematically, but it has more substance than most Tier C claims because each link in the chain is individually attested.

**Beckwith's Pyrrho claim: direct engagement with Bett (2000) and Flintoff (1980).** The philosophical parallel between Pyrrho's formula and the Trilakṣaṇa is Beckwith's most specific and testable claim. Richard Bett's (*Pyrrho, His Antecedents and His Legacy*, 2000) engagement is skeptical but not dismissive. A direct response to Bett addressing specifically whether Pyrrho's formula can be distinguished from Jain or Ājīvika dialectic — not just from Brahmanical philosophy — would either strengthen or close Beckwith's Pyrrho argument, which currently stands as the strongest single data point connecting early Buddhism to Central Asian philosophical transmission.
