# Śaka–Scythian hypotheses and Buddhism

The **source compilation** foregrounds **steppe** peoples (**Saka / Śaka**, “**Scythians**”) as carriers of **Iranian**-flavored ideas toward **India** and **Gandhāra**, including conjectures about the **Śākya** clan of the historical **Buddha**. Greeks called some of these groups Scythians; Achaemenid sources use *Saka*; Indic texts use *Śaka*. Scholars such as **Michael Witzel** and **Christopher I. Beckwith** have explored whether the Śākyas should be read against that Central Asian horizon—not as established fact, but as a research program with heavy pushback.

## Indian soldiers in the army of Xerxes: an early documented contact vector

Before Alexander, before Aśoka, and before the Indo-Greek kingdoms, there is one documented and datable mechanism by which Indians received direct information about both the Persian empire and the Greek world: **Indian soldiers served in the Achaemenid army of Xerxes during the second Persian invasion of Greece (480–479 BCE)**.

This is not a hypothesis. Herodotus (*Histories* 7.65, 7.70, 7.86) specifically lists Indian contingents in Xerxes' assembled force, describing their dress, weapons (cane bows and iron-tipped arrows), and commanders. Indian troops therefore participated directly in the battles of Thermopylae and Salamis — in direct military contact with Greek forces.

These soldiers returned to India. The Indian rulers who had supplied them received back men who had spent time inside the world's largest empire (the Achaemenid, stretching over 4,000 miles), had observed Persian administrative infrastructure firsthand, and had fought against the Greeks. By the time they returned, Indians had a name for the Persians — *Kamboja* — and a name for the Greeks — *Yona* or *Yavana* (the latter from *Iāwān*, the Semitic name for Ionians, transmitted through the Achaemenid multilingual bureaucracy). Both names appear in later Indian texts, including Aśokan edicts, where they mark known foreign peoples.

**Significance for this site's framework:** This documented military participation represents one of the earliest concrete transmission vectors for information about the Greek world entering India — predating Alexander's invasion by 150 years, and predating the Indo-Greek kingdoms by over three centuries. It establishes that the cultural contacts between India and the Greek world visible in Gandhāran art, philosophy, and text were not a sudden introduction but the culmination of a contact history that had been building since at least the early 5th century BCE.

**Evidence tier: A** (Herodotus is a primary-source report; Indian contingents are listed specifically). The *interpretation* of how much cultural information was transmitted back remains Tier B.

**References:** Herodotus, *Histories* 7.65, 7.70, 7.86 (Indian contingents in Xerxes' army); Briant, Pierre. *From Cyrus to Alexander* (2002), ch. on military organization; Thapar, Romila. *Early India* (Penguin, 2002), on early Indian knowledge of western peoples.

## What the **hypotheses** claim

| Author / work | Core idea |
| --- | --- |
| **Michael Witzel** (forum precis in compilation) | **Śākya** might reflect **Śaka**-type **incursions**; “**strange**” warrior-ethos passages; burial customs; **oath** culture tied to **Truth / Lie** dualism. |
| **Christopher I. Beckwith** (*Greek Buddha*, 2015; *The Scythian Empire*) | Argues Pyrrho (327–325 BCE in India with Alexander) encountered early Buddhist-like dialectic in Central Asia. Reads **Śākyamuni** as "the Scythian Sage" — marking the Buddha as "a foreigner from northwestern India or Central Asia, regions known to have been ruled by the Scythians, Scytho-Medes, and related peoples." Beckwith notes: "No other Indian philosopher has an epithet marking him as a foreigner." He further argues the **Trilakṣaṇa** (three characteristics: impermanence, imperfection, no-self) is quoted in reverse order by Pyrrho, offering the earliest datable testimony — **controversial**; critics argue he overreads etymology and sidelines Gangetic-plain evidence. |
| **Jayarava Attwood (2012)** | Survey in *Journal of the Oxford Centre for Buddhist Studies* **3**:47–69 — collects **Iranian-origin** proposals for **Śākya** identity **without** endorsing all as **proven**. Later work (e.g. Gaumāta, *Dipankara Jātaka*) is more speculative and not mainstream Iranian history. |

**Empirical limits:** **kurgan** burial mounds ≠ **Buddhist** **stūpas** in **function** or **chronology**; **similarity** of ethonyms **Śaka** / **Śākya** invites **caution** (homonymy, generic “**mighty**” semantics).

### Bardiya-Gaumāta theory of the origin of the Buddha

The **Behistun inscription** (Darius I, ~520 BCE) describes how a Magian named **Gaumāta** seized the throne by impersonating **Bardiya** (Greek: Smerdis), the murdered brother of Cambyses II. Darius claims to have killed the impostor and restored legitimate rule. This is **Achaemenid political propaganda** — Iranian historians debate whether Darius himself was the usurper and the "real Bardiya" was eliminated by him.

The **hypothesis** (advanced in speculative form by Attwood and others): the name **Gaumāta** resembles Sanskrit **Gautama** (the Buddha's clan name / epithet); the narrative of a prince who renounces or is ejected from royal life, wanders, and founds a counter-establishment has structural parallels to the Buddha story; and the chronological window (6th–5th cent. BCE) overlaps.

**What the hypothesis claims:**

| Element | Behistun / Achaemenid | Buddhist tradition | Proposed connection |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Name** | Gaumāta | Gautama (Gotama) | Phonetic resemblance (but different language families: Old Iranian vs. Sanskrit) |
| **Royal displacement** | Bardiya (or impostor) loses throne | Siddhārtha renounces kingship | Structural parallel — prince → outsider |
| **"Truth vs. Lie"** | Darius frames his coup as Truth (*arta*) defeating Lie (*drauga*) | Buddhist emphasis on Truth (*dharma/dhamma*) vs. ignorance | Zoroastrian ethical dualism as backdrop |
| **Geography** | Eastern satrapies overlap with Buddhist heartland? | Traditional setting: Gangetic plain (Magadha, Kosala) | Requires reinterpreting Pāli *Majjhimadesa* ("middle country") as Persian imperial geography — **not accepted** in mainstream Indology |

**Why this remains Tier C (speculative):**

1. **Phonetic resemblance ≠ cognacy.** Gaumāta is Old Iranian; Gautama is Sanskrit/Prakrit. The resemblance is suggestive but does not follow regular sound correspondences between the language families.
2. **Structural parallels are generic.** "Prince loses throne, founds new movement" describes many historical and legendary figures.
3. **Buddhist geography.** The vast majority of early Buddhist texts locate the Buddha in the Gangetic plain with detailed, internally consistent geographical references (Lumbinī, Kapilavastu, Bodh Gaya, Sarnath, Kusinagara) that do not map onto Persian imperial space without heavy special pleading.
4. **No specialist consensus.** The identification is not accepted in mainstream Iranian history, Indology, or Buddhist studies. It is catalogued here as a hypothesis worth knowing about — and as an illustration of how the Achaemenid-era contact zone generates creative speculation.

**What IS established:** Achaemenid and later Saka polities **did** create the contact zone in which Iranian, Indic, and Central Asian cultures interacted — making some form of conceptual exchange plausible even if the specific Gaumāta = Gautama identification is rejected. See [Gandhara and Central Asian transmission](gandhara-central-asian-transmission.md) and [The Parthian corridor](parthian-arsacid-corridor.md).

## The Saka cultural legacy: dating systems, Sanskrit, and Queen Ayasya's inscription

The Śaka rulers of northwestern India left a cultural legacy that extends far beyond their documented Buddhist patronage. Three contributions deserve specific documentation.

**Queen Ayasya's Mathura inscription (excavated 1869, Tier A).** A lion-capital excavated at **Mathura** in 1869 carries Kharoshthi inscriptions recording donations made by the Shaka Queen **Ayasya** (*Aiyasiyā*) to establish the worship of a relic near a **Sarvāstivāda** Buddhist monastery linked to the **Mūlasarvāstivāda** school (the same school discussed in [Gandhāra and Central Asian transmission](gandhara-central-asian-transmission.md) for its role in the Gandhāran manuscript tradition). Ayasya expresses the wish that the merit of her donation should extend to "**all of Śākastan**" — the territory she considered her homeland and the homeland of her people. This toponym is significant: she does not use "India" (*Bhāratavarṣa*) or any Indic regional name. She calls the northwest the **Śaka homeland** — a name that persisted as "Sistan" (from *Śakastān*) for the eastern Iranian region. This reframes the territory not as a province absorbed by outsiders but as a distinct political geography with its own ethnic identity. **Reference:** Konow, Sten. *Kharoshthī Inscriptions with the Exception of Those of Aśoka* (Corpus Inscriptionum Indicarum II.1, 1929).

**Saka dating systems.** The Shakas inaugurated **two calendar systems** that remained in long-term use across South Asia. The **Vikrama Era** (beginning 57 BCE by convention, though its specific attribution to Śaka rulers is debated by some scholars) and the **Śaka Era** (beginning 78 CE) both appear in historical records primarily in association with Saka political contexts. The Śaka Era was adopted in 1957 as one of the **official calendar systems of the Republic of India**, running alongside the Gregorian calendar in government publications. The persistence of Saka-origin dating conventions into modern South Asian administrative practice is a direct measure of cultural depth — a foreign ruling dynasty's temporal framework outlasting their political power by two millennia.

**Shakas and the inauguration of Sanskrit in royal eulogies.** The Sanskrit scholar **Sheldon Pollock** (*The Language of the Gods in the World of Men: Sanskrit, Culture, and Power in Premodern India*, University of California Press, 2006) specifically identifies Saka-era rulers as the **inaugurators of Sanskrit in royal eulogies** — the tradition of *praśasti* (royal panegyric inscriptions in Sanskrit) that subsequently spread across South and Southeast Asia and became a primary mode of royal self-presentation throughout the medieval period. The earliest datable *praśastis* are associated with rulers in the northwestern borderlands — Kusanas and early Sakas — rather than with the Sanskrit-speaking Brahmanical heartland. This is a striking finding: the tradition of Sanskrit as the language of royal power, which became fundamental to the entire medieval Indian political imagination, was inaugurated not by indigenous Indian Brahmin kings but by the foreign Saka and Kusana conquerors of the northwest. Pollock's argument situates the Shakas as the agents who elevated Sanskrit from a priestly and ritual language to an **imperial prestige language** — a transformation with consequences for the transmission of Indian religious culture eastward through the Silk Road into Central and East Asia.

**Significance for this site:** The Saka cultural legacy illustrates the same pattern documented for the Indo-Greeks: "foreign" rulers not merely assimilating into Indian culture but actively reshaping it in ways that defined its later forms. The mechanisms by which Śramaṇic traditions spread through Central Asia into East Asia were in part Saka-mediated — a fact often obscured by later textual traditions' tendency to minimize "barbarian" contributions.

Pollock's *praśasti* finding points toward a broader structural pattern — the **barbarian inaugurator pattern** — in which the canonical and iconic forms of major religious traditions were built on infrastructure provided by foreign conquerors whom those traditions themselves classified as outsiders. This pattern recurs across the Indo-Greeks (anthropomorphic Buddha image), the Kushans (Silk Road transmission infrastructure, Mahāyāna iconography), the Achaemenid Persians (Hebrew Bible canonization, Kharoṣṭhī script), and the Sakas (Sanskrit as imperial language). See [The barbarian inaugurator pattern](barbarian-inaugurator-pattern.md) for the full cross-traditional synthesis.

## Controversies (brief)

- **Beckwith:** critics argue he overreads etymology, conflates Prakrit / Sanskrit forms, or sidelines early Indian Buddhist texts that situate the Buddha in Greater Magadha / the Gangetic plain. See academic reviews (e.g. BMCR) and Indological responses.
- **Attwood (Gaumāta / Achaemenid threads):** depends on interpretations of seals and narrative parallels that many historians of Persia treat as inconclusive for identifying the historical Buddha with specific Achaemenid figures.

**Key evidence cited by Attwood (2012)** for Iranian features in the Śākya tradition:
- Śākya tribes are largely **absent from Vedic literature** and, where noticed, their customs are described as "strange" (*Ambattha Sutta* D.i.90–91).
- **Burial practices** in Magadha similar to Central Asian kurgan traditions (earth mounds around a central wooden pillar, compared to stūpa forms).
- **Incest marriage** among the Śākya founders (Ambattha Sutta): brothers banished from a kingdom take their own sisters as wives — Witzel identifies this as an Iranian trait.
- **Post-mortem judgment** of body, speech, and mind triad — echoing Zoroastrian eschatological patterns.
- Early Buddhist **stūpas** structurally compared to **kurgan** burial mounds of Southern Russia and Central Asia (discussed by Werner 1987, 2002; Rawlinson; Przyluski 1935).

## Editorial synthesis

- **High confidence:** **Achaemenid** and later **Saka** polities **did** connect **Iranian**, **Indic**, and **Central Asian** space — see [Gandhara and Central Asian transmission](gandhara-central-asian-transmission.md).
- **Moderate confidence:** **Northwest** Indian Buddhism absorbed **Hellenistic**, **Iranian**, and **steppe** **visual** and **political** idioms.
- **Low / contested:** **Śākya** **are** “**the Scythians**” who brought **Zoroaster**-style dualism **into** Buddhism; or that the **Buddha**’s **epithet** **Śākyamuni** **proves** **foreign** birth — these require **archaeogenetics**, **onomastics**, and **text-critical** work **beyond** analogy.
