# Method: evidence, influence, and milieu

This page states **editorial rules** used across curated articles. It does **not** replace specialist monographs or primary-source philology; it helps readers judge **what kind of claim** each page is making.

## Typology versus genealogy

- **Typology** (or **family resemblance**): two traditions share **motifs** or **roles**—future restorer, initiator, healer, solar lord—without implying that one **text** or **cult** **fathered** the other.
- **Genealogy** (or **direct diffusion**): a **specific** pathway—translation, migration, institution—is argued with **dated** evidence (names, donors, bilingual texts, archaeological horizon).

Confusing the two is the main way comparative religion **overreaches**. The **goal** here is not to forbid comparison but to **make real connection easier to find**—by separating **look-alike** from **genealogy** and naming **mechanisms**. See [Exploring transmission and deep narrative patterns](exploring-transmission-and-deep-patterns.md).

**Reordering episodes:** **Constructing** a **single** **synthetic** **biography** **by** **splicing** **episodes** **from** **several** **figures** **(e.g.** **to** **mirror** **a** **Buddha**-**shaped** **arc)** **can** **clarify** **which** **motifs** **recur**—but **proves** **nothing** **about** **literary** **dependence** **unless** **independently** **argued**. See [Mythic biography patterns — prophet lives and Buddha-schema analogy](mythic-biography-patterns-prophet-and-buddha-analogy.md). When a page here stresses **milieu**, it means **shared environment** (vocabulary, civic cult, art forms), **not** proven **literary** **dependence**.

## Tiers of claim (informal)

| Tier | Meaning | Example posture |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Secure description** | Widely accepted summary of cult or text | Roman **tauroctony** in *mithraea*; LXX use of **χριστός** in some books |
| **Specialist debate** | Real schools of thought; evidence is partial | Origins of **Roman** Mithraism; Iranian input vs **syncretic** invention |
| **Hypothesis** | **Active research target** — interesting but not consensus; may be strengthened or falsified | Śaka–Śākyan identifications; some **Mithra–Amida** phonological sketches; **transmission** claims with named mechanisms |
| **Analogy / heuristic** | Illuminates **questions** only | PIE twin reconstruction beside **Danubian** reliefs |

Pages should **signal** tier in prose; where they do not, default to treating material as **descriptive** or **debated**, not **proved**. **Hypothesis** tier is where much of this site’s **exploration** lives—see [Exploring transmission and deep narrative patterns](exploring-transmission-and-deep-patterns.md).

## Positive program: exploration vs. overreach

- **Exploration:** Ask **where contact is plausible**, name **pathways** (empire, bilingualism, cult, manuscript networks), and say **what evidence** would **confirm, weaken, or rule out** a transmission story. The dossiers linked from the exploration page do this **on purpose**.
- **Overreach:** Assert **literary** or **doctrinal** dependence from **motif** similarity alone, or treat **analogy** tier as **proof**.

[Typology versus genealogy](#typology-versus-genealogy) is the **rule**; **exploration** is the **aim**.

## Contact mechanisms

When arguing **influence**, it helps to name **how** contact could work:

- **political** empire (administration, army, diaspora)
- **trade** and **urban** **pluralism**
- **literacy** and **bilingual** **texts**
- **ritual** **institutions** (temples, mysteries, courts)

Absent a plausible mechanism, **parallel** development remains on the table.

## Fragmentary archives, victors’ histories, and “retold” heroes

Ancient religion is preserved through **partial** corpora: libraries burn, languages die, and ruling elites shape what is copied. It is legitimate to ask what is missing between texts and to read for silences—provided one does not treat absence of evidence as evidence of a specific hidden plot.

A separate **hypothesis tier** (heuristic) is worth naming explicitly: local figures can acquire new biographical layers after death (hero cult, royal mythmaking, hagiography). When the same motif cluster (exile, revelation, founding a community) attaches to different names in different centuries, chronological alignment can look broken even if story shapes recur. That explains why “universal mythos” reconstruction is tempting—and why it must be checked against dated sources and specialist philology, not only pattern matching.

## Tools: large corpora, indexes, and assisted collation

Search tools and language models can cluster themes, propose etymological leads, and organize timelines—useful for discovery and hypothesis generation. They do not by themselves establish historical genealogy or identity of figures across cultures; human review against editions and peer literature remains necessary. See [About research notes](../research/about-research-notes.md).

## Why this matters for “messiah” language

Israelite **māšîaḥ** and early Jewish **messiah** expectations have **specific** literary and historical settings. **Greek** *christos* and *sōtēr* belong to **wider** **civic** and **ruler** idioms—see [Messiah and Christ language in Greek civic context](messiah-language-greek-civic-context.md) and [Emperor cult and civic *sōtēr*](emperor-cult-and-civic-soter.md). Mapping **similarity** is valid; **equating** institutions without evidence is not.
