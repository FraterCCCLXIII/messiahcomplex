# Paul, Mithraism, and historicity (tiered notes)

This page collects questions that often arise when comparing early Christianity to Mediterranean and Iranian religious contexts—especially Roman Mithraism and Paul. It explores hypotheses fairly: what might be learned, where chronology blocks simple diffusion stories, and why mainstream New Testament scholarship still treats a historical Paul and Jesus as the default working model. See [Method: evidence, influence, and milieu](method-evidence-and-influence.md) for tiers (hypothesis versus consensus description).

Nothing here settles authorship of every letter or every pericope; it orients readers who encounter popular or nineteenth-century parallel arguments.

## 1. Manuscripts, P46, and “no first-century papyrus of Paul”

**Fact:** Papyrus 46 (ca. 175–225 CE) is among the earliest substantial witnesses to a Pauline corpus (Hebrews appears in this codex too). Autographs of Paul’s letters do not survive—nor do most ancient literary originals.

**What follows from that alone:** little. Gaps between composition and our oldest copies are normal; textual critics reconstruct earlier forms using many later manuscripts, citations in Church Fathers, and translations. The absence of a first-century physical copy does not by itself imply that “Paul” was invented in the second century—that would require independent arguments (authorship studies, coherence with first-century context, multiple early attestations of letters, etc.).

**Tier:** manuscript gap = secure description of material evidence; “Paul never existed” = hypothesis (minority in academic NT studies).

## 2. Acts, hagiography, and external mentions of Paul

*Acts of the Apostles* (often dated late first century or later) presents Paul within conventions of Greco-Roman narrative and theological concerns—not modern reportage. Historians dispute details (itineraries, speeches, harmonization with letters) while still using Acts as evidence for early memory and debate.

Early Christian references to Paul include 1 Clement (traditionally ca. 95–110 CE), Ignatius of Antioch (early 2nd c.), and Marcion’s use of Pauline letters (ca. 140 CE). From a skeptical angle, one can ask whether these texts “idealize” Paul for authority; from a mainstream angle, they are also what we would expect if communities were already circulating letters under his name within roughly one lifetime of the traditional chronology.

**Tier:** Acts as shaped literature = specialist debate; whether Paul is a pure literary synthesis = hypothesis requiring positive argument, not only silence in papyrus survival.

## 3. The “posthumous Paul” hypothesis (explored, not endorsed)

Some popular and fringe hypotheses propose that Paul’s biography was retrofitted to link Jesus traditions to later institutional Christianity. Questions such hypotheses raise (even when one does not accept them) include: how collections of letters gained authority, how “apostle” functioned as a claim, and how second-century readers (including Marcion) read Paul against other gospels.

Counterweights often cited in scholarship: linguistic and situational variety across letters treated as authentic (Romans, Galatians, 1–2 Corinthians, etc.); theological tensions that are easier to explain as correspondence than as a single later forger’s masterplan (debated).

**Tier:** hypothesis / minority reading—worth mapping as intellectual history, not as default history without argument.

## 4. Nineteenth-century “mystery religions” and Paul at Tarsus

German and European scholarship (e.g. currents associated with comparing Christianity to “mystery cults”) sometimes speculated that Paul’s language owed something to Mithraic or broader mystery idiom. Eduard Meyer and others worked in that climate of comparison.

**Chronological friction:** Roman *mithraea* and the fully developed Western Mithras cult as known archaeologically belong chiefly to the imperial period (see [Roman Mithras and Manichaean Mithra](mithra-roman-manichaean.md)). There is no secure evidence for a Roman-style Mithraic mystery guild in Paul’s Tarsus in the mid-first century CE as we would need for a simple “Paul copied Roman Mithraism” story. That does not close every question about Iranian or Anatolian religious pluralism in Cilicia—it redirects the debate toward what kind of contact is plausible when.

**Tier:** “Paul derived core theology from Roman Mithraism” = hypothesis with serious chronological problems as usually stated; shared Mediterranean motifs (light, initiation language) = analogy / milieu (compare [Measuring influence](measuring-influence-mitra-maitreya.md)).

## 5. Parallel lists: what is compared, and what to watch for

Popular lists compare Paul to Mithraic themes: blood and sacrifice, spiritual rock and drink, military metaphors, light and darkness, cosmic struggle. Some observations:

- Jewish and Septuagintal background already furnishes sacrificial metaphor, exodus imagery (spiritual rock in 1 Corinthians 10 echoes scripture), and apocalyptic light/dark language—so parallels need not imply Mithraic dependence.
- Hebrews 9:13–14 is sometimes cited in such lists as “Paul.” Hebrews is anonymous and not among the undisputed Paulines—attribute parallels carefully when arguing about “Paul’s” voice.
- Ephesians 6 (armor of God) is disputed Pauline authorship in much scholarship—same caution.
- Roman Mithraic tauroctony (bull-slaying) has no clear counterpart in Paul’s letters; critics of the Mithra–Paul thesis often start there.

**Tier:** motif comparison = analogy heuristic until a specific pathway (text, travel, community) is demonstrated.

## 6. Arabia, Persia, and “three years”

Galatians 1:17–18 places Paul in “Arabia” before Jerusalem—a term whose geography in Paul’s mouth is debated (Nabatea, wilderness, etc.). It is not equivalent to “Persia” in mainstream exegesis. Hypotheses that stress Iranian contact should engage that textual debate explicitly rather than substituting “Persia.”

## 7. Damascus road and Mithras–Sol: typology vs. influence

Acts 9 (and Paul’s own remarks about revelation and visions) can be compared to Mithraic iconography of Mithras and Sol (light, encounter, handshake / *dexiosis* in art). Similarities at the level of symbol (divine light, mission, transformation) are real enough to compare cross-culturally.

Differences matter for history: Paul’s story is framed in Jewish and Christ kerygma; Mithras–Sol scenes belong to polytheist myth and Roman cult settings. Chronology again cautions against assuming Paul imitated Roman mithraeum theology as we know it.

**Optional speculative avenue** (sometimes raised in popular literature): wordplay on Saul / Apollo / Sol / Helios—interesting as a literary or cultural echo hypothesis, not as proof without philological and contextual work. 1 Corinthians 1:12 contrasts factions named for Paul, Apollos, Cephas, and Christ; Apollos is a known teacher in Acts 18—mainstream exegesis does not require equating Apollos with Apollo.

**Tier:** typological parallel = analogy heuristic; literary dependence = hypothesis needing positive evidence.

## 8. Paul’s knowledge of the “historical Jesus”

Critical introductions often note that Paul says relatively little about Jesus’ ministry compared to later gospels, while emphasizing crucifixion, resurrection, and ritual memory (e.g. Last Supper in 1 Corinthians 11). That silence / selectivity can be read as theological focus (kerygma before biography) rather than as proof that Paul knew nothing of traditions later written in gospels—but it does fuel debate about how Jesus tradition developed.

Introductory reading: Bart D. Ehrman on Paul and Jesus; James D. Tabor, *Paul and Jesus* (2012)—theses vary in strength; check reviews and peer responses.

## 9. Canon, Marcion, and second-century collections

Paul’s letters circulated early (Marcion’s collection ca. 140 CE is a famous witness). Formal lists (Muratorian fragment, Athanasius’ Festal Letter 367 CE, later councils) show long processes of recognition and debate over which books counted as authoritative. That history illustrates contestation—it does not by itself demonstrate that Paul was invented at that late stage.

| Approx. | Milestone |
| --- | --- |
| ca. 140 CE | Marcion’s edition of Pauline letters + edited Luke |
| ca. 200 | Muratorian fragment (witness to developing lists) |
| 367 CE | Athanasius lists 27 books (Easter letter) |
| 382+ | Western conciliar / papal lists (traditions vary) |

## 10. Bridge: gospels, mythicism, and non-Christian witnesses (very short)

Debates about Jesus’ historicity (“mythicist” positions vs. mainstream historical Jesus research) use Paul’s letters as early evidence but interpret them differently. Josephus’ *Testimonium Flavianum* is widely held to contain Christian interpolations while possibly resting on an original notice (debated); Tacitus (*Annals* 15.44) mentions Christ’s execution under Pilate but late and not independent in a strong sense. Most historians nevertheless treat Jesus as a first-century figure because they judge the combined case stronger than alternatives—see mainstream introductions (e.g. Ehrman, Meier) and minority critiques (e.g. Carrier, Price, Lataster) as labeled minority positions.

On gospels as literature: many scholars stress theological shaping and disagreement between accounts while still seeking a historical core via criteria (multiple attestation, embarrassment, coherence)—a nuanced stance rather than “all fiction” or “all fact.”
