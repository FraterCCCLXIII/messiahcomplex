# Eschatological vocabulary timeline

This page asks a simple but powerful question: **when** does each major eschatological concept **first appear** in each tradition's textual record, and what does the chronological alignment suggest about transmission?

The argument is not that timing proves borrowing. It is that when multiple concepts cluster in the same period and the same contact zone, the **cumulative case** for influence becomes harder to dismiss as coincidence. See [Method: evidence, influence, and milieu](method-evidence-and-influence.md) for rules on separating typology from genealogy.

---

## 1. Resurrection of the body

| Tradition | Earliest attestation | Source | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoroastrian** | Old Avestan seeds; Younger Avestan explicit | Gathas (debated, ~1000–600 BCE); Yasht 19.89, Bundahišn | *Ristāxēz* ("rising of the dead") tied to Frashokereti; molten metal ordeal (benign for the righteous) |
| **Jewish** | Post-exilic: Isaiah 26:19; Daniel 12:2 (~165 BCE) | Hebrew Bible; 2 Maccabees 7 (Hellenistic) | Pre-exilic texts (Hosea 6:2, Ezekiel 37 "dry bones") use revival language but are debated as true resurrection doctrine vs. national metaphor |
| **Christian** | 1 Corinthians 15 (~55 CE); Gospels | NT | Inherits and intensifies Jewish resurrection; adds Christological framing |
| **Islamic** | Quran (7th cent. CE) | Multiple surahs | Yawm al-Qiyāmah ("Day of Resurrection"); draws on Jewish and Christian antecedents |
| **Hindu** | Bodily resurrection not a central doctrine | Upanishads (~800–200 BCE); Puranas | The eschatological goal is *moksha* (liberation from the cycle of rebirth / *saṃsāra*), not resurrection. However Puranic cosmology describes cosmic dissolution (*pralaya*) followed by full renewal at the end of each age — a structural analogue to Frashokereti. |
| **Buddhist** | Liberation from *saṃsāra* central; "rebirth in the Pure Land" in later traditions | Pali Canon (Theravāda); Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtras (~1st–2nd cent. CE) | The goal is *nirvāṇa* (liberation from rebirth cycles), not bodily resurrection. But the Pure Land tradition offers *upapatti* — "rebirth" in the Sukhāvatī realm — which functions structurally as an eschatological destination parallel to paradise and resurrection hope. See [Amitābha and Maitreya](amitabha-maitreya-gandhara-transmission.md). |

**Chronological observation:** Jewish resurrection language becomes **explicit** during and after the **Persian period**. The Zoroastrian concept is attested (at least in seed form) **earlier**. This does not prove borrowing, but the timing is consistent with Iranian influence via Achaemenid contact.

**The mechanism of resurrection in Zoroastrian eschatology** is elaborated in the Pahlavi Bundahišn (ch. 34): the final Saoshyant (Sōšyāns) performs the **sacrifice of the bull Hadayānš**, from whose fat the **White Hōm** (Haoma) is rendered, making **all** beings immortal. This means the resurrection is accomplished through a **sacrificial act** that produces an **elixir of eternal life** — a pattern with structural parallels to the Christian Eucharist (“drink this and live forever”) and the Indo-Iranian Haoma/Soma tradition (Avestan *haoma* = Vedic *soma*, “pressed juice” conferring immortality). The Zoroastrian tauroctony also parallels the central image of Roman Mithraism (Mithras slaying the cosmic bull), suggesting a shared Indo-Iranian sacrificial substrate. See [Saoshyant: the eschatological tauroctony](../figures/zoroastrian-saoshyant.md#tauroctony) and [Haoma, Soma, and the elixir of immortality](indo-iranian-mitra-contract.md#haoma-soma).

## 2. Cosmic dualism (good vs. evil as cosmic forces)

| Tradition | Earliest attestation | Source | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoroastrian** | Yasna 30 (Gathas) — twin spirits choosing truth or lie | Old Avesta | Foundational; Ahura Mazdā's "spenta mainyu" vs. "angra mainyu" |
| **Hindu / Vedic** | Rig Veda (~1200–900 BCE) | RV 1.32; Atharva Veda; Puranas | The *deva* / *asura* conflict is the structural backbone of Vedic cosmology — order-maintaining gods against chaos-embodying anti-gods. Early Vedic texts use *asura* positively (even of Varuna and Mitra), but the sharp dualism solidifies over time — paralleling the evolution of *ha-Satan* in Judaism. The Indra–Vritra battle (Indra slaying the cosmic dragon who obstructs the waters) is a **direct PIE cognate** of the cosmic combat myth (cf. Greek Zeus–Typhon, Norse Thor–Jörmungandr). |
| **Jewish** | Develops across Second Temple period; peaks at **Qumran** | 1QS 3:13–4:26 (Community Rule: Spirit of Truth vs. Spirit of Falsehood); War Scroll (Sons of Light vs. Sons of Darkness) | Pre-exilic Hebrew Bible has adversaries but not a **cosmic** good/evil dualism |
| **Buddhist** | ~5th–3rd cent. BCE | Pali Canon (Mahāparinibbāna Sutta; Samyutta Nikāya) | *Māra* ("the Bringer of Death," the Tempter) is the personification of cosmic resistance to liberation and the lord of *kāmadhātu* (the realm of desire). In canonical accounts Māra attacks the Buddha on the night of his enlightenment with armies of demons and temptations — structurally parallel to Angra Mainyu and Satan. Note: Buddhist dualism is functional/soteriological rather than ontological — Māra is ultimately subject to the Dharma, not a co-eternal evil principle. |
| **Christian** | Inherits Jewish framework; elaborates Satan, demons, Antichrist | NT (esp. Revelation, Johannine literature) | Dualism via Jewish apocalypticism |
| **Manichaean** | 3rd cent. CE (Mani) | Manichaean scriptures | Radical dualism: two eternal principles (Light and Darkness) |

**Chronological observation:** The **structural** dualism of Qumran's two-spirits doctrine is strikingly close to Yasna 30. The Dead Sea Scrolls community (2nd–1st cent. BCE) postdates Persian rule by only a century or two, and the intellectual milieu of Jewish apocalypticism developed continuously from the Persian period. See [Dead Sea Scrolls dualism and Iranian parallels](dead-sea-scrolls-dualism-and-iranian-parallels.md).

## 3. Final judgment / universal tribunal

| Tradition | Earliest attestation | Source | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoroastrian** | Gathas (seeds); Younger Avesta (explicit) | Yasna 43, 51; Bundahišn | Molten metal ordeal; Saoshyant presides; all are judged |
| **Hindu / Vedic** | Rig Veda *Yama* hymns (~1200–900 BCE) | RV 10.14; Atharva Veda 18; Katha Upanishad | **Yama**, the first man to die, becomes king and judge of the dead. He weighs deeds and assigns the righteous to the blissful realm. *Vedic Yama* and *Avestan Yima* are the **same figure by name** (both from PIE *\*yemo-*, "twin" or "the paired one") — this is not parallel development but **shared Indo-Iranian inheritance**. |
| **Buddhist** | ~5th–3rd cent. BCE | Mahāparinibbāna Sutta; Devadūta Sutta (MN 130) | **Yama** (same name, same Indo-Iranian heritage) persists as judge of the dead in Buddhist cosmology. The deceased appear before Yama and are shown their deeds reflected in a mirror (*karma-darśana*); Yama's emissaries (*devadūta*) bring them to judgment. The *Devadūta Sutta* (MN 130) gives a vivid narrative of this tribunal. |
| **Egyptian** | Old Kingdom onward | Book of the Dead; Osiris weighing of the heart | ANE parallel; **independent** of IE traditions |
| **Jewish** | Post-exilic development; Daniel 7 (~165 BCE) | Daniel 7:9–14 (Ancient of Days, thrones, books); 1 Enoch (Parables); 4 Ezra | Pre-exilic prophets have "Day of the LORD" but universal cosmic tribunal is later |
| **Christian** | NT (Parousia and judgment) | Matthew 25, Revelation 20 | Elaboration of Jewish apocalyptic |

## 4. Paradise / eschatological destination of the righteous

| Tradition | Term | Earliest use | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Persian** | *paridaida-* ("walled garden") | Achaemenid period (attested in Elamite PFT) | Originally a royal park; eschatologized in later Zoroastrian texts |
| **Hindu / Vedic** | *Svarga* ("heaven"); *Vaikunṭha* (Vishnu's realm); *Satya-loka* (Brahma's realm) | Rig Veda *Yama* hymns; elaborated in Upanishads and Puranas | Svarga is the blissful realm of the righteous dead — presided over by Indra, reached by those who have performed good deeds. Not permanent: after exhausting their good *karma*, souls return to rebirth. Vaikuntha and Satya-loka (in Puranic/Vaishnavite theology) are more transcendent destinations. |
| **Buddhist** | *Sukhāvatī* ("the Blissful [realm]"); *Tuṣita* heaven | Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtras (~1st–2nd cent. CE); Pali *Tuṣita* references much earlier | *Sukhāvatī* is the western Pure Land of Amitābha Buddha — an eschatological paradise accessed by faith and contemplative practice, not only by good works. *Tuṣita* is the penultimate heaven where the bodhisattva Maitreya currently resides, awaiting his final birth as the next Buddha. The solar and western-direction symbolism of *Sukhāvatī* is relevant to potential Iranian parallels. See [Amitābha and Maitreya](amitabha-maitreya-gandhara-transmission.md). |
| **Jewish** | *pardēs* ← **Old Persian loanword** | Song of Songs 4:13; Ecclesiastes 2:5; later: LXX Genesis 2:8 (*paradeisos*); 2 Enoch; Luke 23:43 | The word itself is **borrowed from Persian**; see [Persian loanwords](persian-loanwords-in-biblical-tradition.md) |
| **Christian** | *paradeisos* | Luke 23:43; 2 Corinthians 12:4; Revelation 2:7 | Via LXX and Jewish apocalyptic |
| **Islamic** | *Jannah* (garden); *Firdaws* (← *pardēs*/*paradeisos*) | Quran | *Firdaws* preserves the Persian chain |

**Observation:** The vocabulary chain is unbroken: Old Persian *paridaida* → Jewish *pardēs* → LXX *paradeisos* → NT *paradise* → Quranic *Firdaws*. This is not a hypothesis; it is **established lexicography**.

## 5. Cosmic adversary / Satan figure

| Tradition | Figure | Earliest attestation | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoroastrian** | **Angra Mainyu** ("destructive spirit") | Gathas (Yasna 30, 45) | Cosmic adversary from the beginning; later: Ahriman |
| **Hindu / Vedic** | **Vritra** (cosmic dragon); **Asuras** as a class | Rig Veda 1.32 (~1200–900 BCE) | Vritra is the cosmic serpent/dragon who obstructs the waters (= chaos, famine) and is slain by Indra in the foundational creation battle. This is a **direct PIE cognate** of the cosmic combat myth — the same inherited pattern as Greek Zeus vs. Typhon, Norse Thor vs. Jörmungandr, Hittite Tarhunt vs. Illuyankas. The *Asuras* generalize to a class of cosmic adversaries opposing divine order. |
| **Buddhist** | **Māra** ("the Bringer of Death," the Tempter) | Pali Canon; Mahāparinibbāna Sutta (~3rd–1st cent. BCE final redaction) | Māra attacks the Buddha on the night of enlightenment with armies and temptations. In later Mahayana texts Māra is the cosmic lord of *kāmadhātu* (the desire realm) who rules over those not yet liberated. Structurally parallel to Satan (desert temptation of Jesus; cf. Matthew 4) and Angra Mainyu. |
| **Jewish** | **ha-Satan** (הַשָּׂטָן) | Job 1–2 (~6th–5th cent. BCE?); Zechariah 3:1–2; 1 Chronicles 21:1 | Evolves from "the accuser" (a role in the divine court) to cosmic enemy; 1 Chronicles 21:1 replaces "the LORD" (2 Samuel 24:1) with "Satan" — a theological shift toward dualism |
| **Christian** | **Satan** / **Devil** | NT throughout | Cosmic enemy; "prince of this world"; Revelation dragon |

**Chronological observation:** The evolution of ha-Satan from a **prosecutorial role** (Job, Zechariah) to a **cosmic adversary** (Chronicles, intertestamental literature, NT) happens across the Persian and Hellenistic periods. In the **oldest** Hebrew texts, YHWH causes both good and evil (Isaiah 45:7: "I form light and create darkness, I make peace and create evil"). The bifurcation into a separate evil agent is a **structural shift toward Iranian-style dualism**. Whether this is influence or independent development is debated — but the direction of development is consistent with Persian contact.

## 6. Named angelic hierarchy

| Tradition | System | Earliest attestation | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoroastrian** | **Amesha Spentas** + **yazatas** (Mithra, Anahita, Sraosha, etc.) | Gathas (Amesha Spentas); Younger Avesta (expanded) | Named divine beings with cosmic portfolios under Ahura Mazdā |
| **Hindu / Vedic** | **Adityas** (including *Mitra*, *Varuna*, Aryaman, etc.); **Vasus**, **Rudras** | Rig Veda (~1200–900 BCE) | The Adityas are 6–12 named solar/cosmic deities with defined portfolios — Mitra (contract/covenant), Varuna (cosmic law/*ṛta*), Aryaman (hospitality/path of the ancestors). This is a **direct cognate** of the Zoroastrian yazata system; Vedic *Mitra* = Avestan *Mithra* by name, function, and PIE origin. |
| **Buddhist** | **Bodhisattvas** (Avalokiteśvara, Mañjuśrī, Maitreya, etc.); *devas* in Buddhist cosmology | Early Pali Canon (devas); Mahāyāna elaboration (~1st cent. CE onward) | The structured hierarchy of bodhisattvas in Mahāyāna Buddhism functions analogously to a divine court — named cosmic beings with salvific portfolios serving under the cosmic Buddhas. Less directly related to the IE yazata pattern, but shows the consistent human religious impulse toward structured divine intermediaries. |
| **Jewish** | **Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Uriel** | Daniel 8:16, 10:13 (~165 BCE); 1 Enoch; Tobit | Pre-exilic texts have **mal'akhim** ("messengers") but not a named, structured hierarchy |
| **Christian** | Inherits Jewish angelic hierarchy | NT; Pseudo-Dionysius (late systematic) | Elaboration |

See [Jewish angelology and yazata parallels](jewish-angelology-and-yazata-parallels.md) for detailed comparison.

## 7. Future savior / end-of-age restorer

This is the concept where the universality is most striking. Every major tradition in the Indo-Iranian sphere — and traditions that came into contact with it — generates a **named figure** who arrives at the end of a cosmic age to defeat evil, restore righteousness, and inaugurate a new order. This is not accidental family resemblance; it reflects both deep PIE inheritance and documented transmission across the Achaemenid–Hellenistic–Kushan contact zone.

| Tradition | Figure | Expected timing | Earliest source | Core features |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoroastrian** | **Saoshyant** (*Sōšyāns*, "future benefactor") | End of the 12,000-year cosmic age | Younger Avesta (Yasht 13.128–129); Bundahišn ch. 34 | Born of Zarathustra's seed preserved in a lake; performs the eschatological bull-sacrifice; presses the White Hōm; resurrects the dead; defeats Angra Mainyu finally. See [Saoshyant figure page](../figures/zoroastrian-saoshyant.md). |
| **Hindu** | **Kalki** ("the White Horse Rider") | End of the Kali Yuga (current age) | Mahābhārata (*Vanaparva* 188–190); Kalki Purāṇa (~6th–10th cent. CE) | Tenth and final avatar of Vishnu; arrives on a white horse, wielding a blazing sword; destroys evil rulers (*adharma*), restores righteousness (*dharma*), and inaugurates the new golden Satya Yuga. Not identical to Saoshyant in origin, but occupies the same structural slot: divine agent, white/solar horse, end-of-age justice. |
| **Buddhist** | **Maitreya** (Skt., "the Friendly/Loving One") | After the decline of the current Dharma; when human lifespan again reaches 80,000 years | *Cakkavatti-Sīhanāda Sutta* (DN 26, Pali Canon, ~3rd–1st cent. BCE); *Maitreyavyākaraṇa* | Future Buddha, currently dwelling in *Tuṣita* heaven. Will descend when the Dharma has been entirely forgotten, rediscover enlightenment, and teach the path anew to a renewed humanity. The name *Maitreya* derives from *maitri* ("loving kindness") — a possible echo of Vedic/Avestan *Mitra* ("covenant/friendship"); see [Amitābha and Maitreya](amitabha-maitreya-gandhara-transmission.md) for the transmission debate. |
| **Jewish** | **Messiah** (*māšîaḥ*, "anointed one") | End of the present age | Daniel 7 (~165 BCE); 1 Enoch (Parables of Enoch, ~1st cent. BCE); Psalms of Solomon 17 | Anointed king from David's line (pre-exilic template) transformed into a cosmic end-of-age figure. Defeats the nations; gathers the exiles; inaugurates YHWH's kingdom. |
| **Christian** | **Christ at the Parousia** | Return at an unexpected hour | 1 Thessalonians 4:13–17 (~50 CE); Revelation 19–22 | Descends from heaven, defeats the Beast and the False Prophet, inaugurates the millennium and new creation. Inherits and transforms Jewish messianic expectation. |
| **Islamic** | **Mahdi** + **ʿĪsā** (Jesus) | Before the Day of Resurrection | Hadith traditions (varies: *Sunan Abī Dāwūd*, *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim* — not in Quran explicitly) | Two-figure schema: the Mahdi ("the rightly guided one") restores justice; ʿĪsā descends and defeats the Dajjāl (Antichrist). |

**Structural observation:** All six figures share: (a) arrival at the **end of a degenerate age**, (b) **defeat of a cosmic evil** opponent, (c) **restoration of order/law/righteousness**, (d) inauguration of a **renewed golden age**. The Zoroastrian Saoshyant is the earliest explicitly eschatological version of this template. The Hindu Kalki and Buddhist Maitreya preserve the structural slot in traditions that share PIE ancestry with Zoroastrianism but developed it independently. The Jewish, Christian, and Islamic versions develop in traditions with **documented Achaemenid contact**.

**Tier note:** That all these traditions have a future-savior figure is **Tier A (secure)**. That this pattern in Jewish/Christian thought was specifically *transmitted* from Zoroastrianism is **Tier B (probable)** for the post-exilic period based on contact evidence. That the Buddhist Maitreya was specifically influenced by Mithraic or Iranian concepts is **Tier C (hypothesis)** — possible but not demonstrated. See [Measuring influence](measuring-influence-mitra-maitreya.md).

## 8. Cosmic time cycles and eschatological age

The conception of **cosmic history as structured in ages or cycles**, ending in a transformation, is another cross-tradition pattern — and here too the Iranian and Indian traditions share the deepest structural parallels, with the Abrahamic traditions being more strongly linear.

| Tradition | Framework | Climax / transformation | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zoroastrian** | 12,000-year cosmic year divided into four 3,000-year periods | **Frashokereti** (cosmic renovation): evil permanently defeated; world perfected (*frašō-kərəti*, "making excellent") | The most precisely articulated eschatological time-cycle before the Hellenistic period; Younger Avesta and Bundahišn. |
| **Hindu** | Four *Yugas* (Satya → Tretā → Dvāpara → **Kali**), totaling 4,320,000 years; ends in *Pralaya* (dissolution) and renewal | Kalki ends the Kali Yuga → cosmic dissolution → new Satya Yuga begins | The yuga system is elaborated in the Mahābhārata and Puranas. The current Kali Yuga (age of vice and strife) is the lowest point, which Kalki will end. |
| **Buddhist** | Vast *kalpas* (cosmic epochs); within each, the Dharma appears and fades through multiple Buddhas | The **Bhadrakalpa** ("fortunate eon") contains 1,000 Buddhas; we are currently between the fourth (Śākyamuni) and fifth (Maitreya). Each kalpa ends in cosmic dissolution. | *Mahāvastu*; various Mahāyāna sūtras. The Buddhist framework is radically cyclical but each cycle has its own eschatological drama — the rise and fall of the Dharma, the coming of the next Buddha. |
| **Jewish** | "This age" (*ha-ʿolam ha-zeh*) vs. "the age to come" (*ha-ʿolam ha-bāʾ*) | Messianic age → resurrection → final judgment → renewed world | Largely **linear** (one-directional from creation to eschaton), though some apocalyptic texts incorporate cyclical elements. The two-age schema is a major development of the Second Temple period. |
| **Christian** | Inherits Jewish two-age framework; adds Millennium (Revelation 20) | Parousia → Millennium → final judgment → New Creation | The Millennium introduces a quasi-cyclical "golden age" interlude before the final state. |

**Observation:** The Zoroastrian and Hindu time-cycle frameworks show the deepest structural parallels — both involve a precise cosmic clock, a cycle of progressive degeneration through ages, and a climactic renewal. This suggests a common **Indo-Iranian inheritance** rather than later mutual influence. The Buddhist framework is structurally similar (progressive degeneration, future savior/Buddha, renewal) but reaches it through the *kalpas* cosmology. The Jewish/Christian/Islamic traditions are more strongly linear but imported the concept of discrete historical ages and an end-of-age transformation — a development that, again, accelerated under Achaemenid influence.

## 9. The cumulative pattern

### The Iran → Judaism transmission argument

The core argument of this site: six conceptual shifts in Judaism, all in the same direction, all in the same time window (Persian period), all with attested contact mechanisms.

| Concept | Pre-exilic Judaism | Post-exilic / Persian-period Judaism | Zoroastrian tradition |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Resurrection | National metaphor at most | **Explicit** individual resurrection | Attested earlier |
| Cosmic dualism | YHWH causes good and evil | Two-spirit dualism at Qumran | Foundational since Gathas |
| Final judgment | "Day of the LORD" | Universal cosmic tribunal | Attested earlier |
| Paradise | No *pardēs* as eschatological term | **Persian loanword** adopted | Source of the word |
| Satan | Prosecutorial role | Cosmic adversary | Angra Mainyu from the beginning |
| Named angels | Anonymous *mal'akhim* | Michael, Gabriel, Raphael | Named yazata system |

Every one of these developments happens during or shortly after the period when Jews lived under Persian imperial rule. No single datum is conclusive. The **pattern** — six conceptual shifts, all in the same direction, all in the same time window, all with attested contact mechanisms — is the argument.

### The broader Indo-Iranian picture: universality of themes

The Hebrew-to-Iranian argument above addresses one transmission channel. But zooming out reveals that these concepts are not just Iranian exports — they reflect a **deeper Indo-Iranian and Proto-Indo-European substrate** that predates both Zoroastrianism and Vedic Hinduism. When the same structural slots appear in traditions that **share PIE ancestry** (Vedic, Avestan, Greek, Roman) they represent **inheritance**, not borrowing.

| Concept | Zoroastrian | Hindu / Vedic | Buddhist | Jewish | Christian | Islamic |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Future savior** | Saoshyant | Kalki | Maitreya | Messiah | Christ (Parousia) | Mahdi + ʿĪsā |
| **Cosmic adversary** | Angra Mainyu | Vritra / Asuras | Māra | ha-Satan | Satan | Iblīs / Dajjāl |
| **End-of-age judgment** | Saoshyant presides | Yama (judge of dead) | Yama (same figure) | Ancient of Days (Daniel) | Christ judges | Yawm al-Dīn |
| **Eschatological paradise** | House of Song (*Garō-nmāna*) | Svarga / Vaikunṭha | Sukhāvatī / Tuṣita | *Gan Eden* / *pardēs* | *Paradeisos* | *Jannah* / *Firdaws* |
| **Cosmic time cycles** | 12,000-year cosmic year | Four Yugas + Pralaya | Kalpas + Dharma decline | Two ages (*ʿolamot*) | This age / age to come | Eras before the Final Hour |
| **Structured divine hierarchy** | Amesha Spentas + yazatas | Adityas + Vasus + Rudras | Bodhisattvas + Buddhas | Named archangels | Angels + archangels | Angels (*malāʾika*) |

This table is not an argument that all traditions are "the same" — the **content** and **theology** of each differ substantially. It is an argument that the **structural slots** are shared, and that understanding why requires taking the PIE inheritance seriously alongside the documented transmission channels. See [PIE and ANE deep grammar](pie-ane-deep-grammar.md) and [Interpretatio and divine translatability](interpretatio-and-divine-translatability.md).
