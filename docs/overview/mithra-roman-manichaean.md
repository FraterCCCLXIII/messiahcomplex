# Roman Mithras and Manichaean Mithra

Roman **Mithras** is **not** a simple transplantation of Avestan liturgy; it is a **Western mystery cult** (chiefly **1st–4th cent. CE**) that **reused** an Iranian divine **name** and select **motifs** (light, oath, cosmic **bull** drama) inside **Greco-Roman** initiatory practice. **Manichaeism** (3rd cent. CE onward) provides a **separate** Iranian-tinged reception of **Mihr / Mithra** in a **dualistic** cosmos.

## Roman mysteries: what is relatively secure

- **Iconography:** the **tauroctony** (Mithras slaying the bull) dominates **mithraea**; grades of initiation; strong association with **soldiers**, **officials**, and **freedmen** along frontiers and cities.
- **Literary echoes:** e.g. **Statius** (*Thebaid*); **Plutarch** on Cilician pirates (transmission **story**, not **secure** documentary history).
- **Origins debate (still open):** classic **Franz** **Cumont** framed Roman Mithraism as **Iranian** survival; **Roger Beck**, **Manfred** **Clauss**, and others stress **Roman** creative **syncretism** (sometimes via **Asia Minor** elites) rather than a **branch** of **Sasanian** worship; **David** **Ulansey** emphasized **astrological** / **precession** readings. Most scholars today expect a **hybrid** picture ([mithraeum.eu](https://www.mithraeum.eu/introductio/origins-of-mithraism) summarizes positions).

**Spread of the cult:** The legions were the chief documented vector, with mithraea attested at military stations along the *limes* and in frontier settlements. However, **merchants from Asia Minor** (many of Eastern Greek origin) may also have propagated the cult from the 2nd century CE onward, as evidenced by epigraphic dedications with Greek-Eastern names in cities where the military element was weak — ports and trade centers. Freedmen and slaves figure in surviving dedications as well ([mithraeum.eu](https://www.mithraeum.eu/introductio/origins-of-mithraism)).

**Relation to site “messiah map”:** Roman Mithras is primarily an **initiatory savior/mediator** in **this** life and cosmology; he is **not** the future Saoshyant type — compare [Roman Mithraism — figure page](../figures/roman-mithraism.md).

### The tauroctony problem: Roman mystery and Zoroastrian eschatology

The **tauroctony** (bull-slaying) is the defining image of Roman Mithraism — found in virtually every mithraeum. Its meaning remains debated (astrological/precessional reading: Ulansey; cosmic-creative act: Beck; Iranian ritual survival: Cumont). What has received less attention in popular treatments is its **Zoroastrian structural parallel**:

In the Pahlavi **Bundahišn** (ch. 34), the culmination of the Frašegird (cosmic Renovation) is described as the final Saoshyant **Sōšyāns** performing the **sacrifice of the bull Hadayānš**, from whose fat the **White Hōm** (Haoma) is rendered, making **all beings immortal**. This is an **eschatological tauroctony** — a bull-sacrifice that produces the substance of eternal life at the end of time.

| Feature | Roman Mithraic tauroctony | Zoroastrian eschatological tauroctony |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Agent** | Mithras (divine figure) | Sōšyāns (eschatological savior) |
| **Victim** | Cosmic bull | Bull Hadayānš |
| **Product** | Life, vegetation, cosmic renewal (grain from tail, animals from blood in some readings) | White Hōm (Haoma) — elixir of universal immortality |
| **Setting** | Cosmic/mythic time (in mithraeum liturgy) | End of time (Frašegird) |
| **Function** | Initiatory/soteriological — life through sacrifice | Eschatological — resurrection and immortality through sacrifice |

**What this suggests:**
- Both traditions preserve an Iranian-rooted myth in which **a bull-sacrifice is the central act that generates life or immortality**.
- The **Indo-Iranian ritual substrate** includes bull-sacrifice (*gōspand*) and the Haoma/Soma pressing as ancient practices, providing a plausible **common source** layer.
- Whether Roman Mithraism **inherited** this specific myth from Iranian tradition (Cumont’s position, now nuanced) or **independently constructed** it from available Iranian raw materials (Beck, Clauss) remains the core origins debate.
- The Bundahišn is a **late** text (Sasanian/post-Sasanian), but the motifs it elaborates are rooted in Younger Avestan passages (Yasht 19.89; Yasna 9.2) that predate the Roman cult. The question is **how deep** the common tradition runs.

See the [Saoshyant figure page](../figures/zoroastrian-saoshyant.md#tauroctony) for the full Bundahišn passage and cross-tradition comparison table.

### Vedic parallel: Mitra, Soma, and the reluctant sacrifice

A striking comparative detail from Indic sources reinforces the antiquity of the tauroctony motif. In a Vedic text, **Mitra reluctantly participates in the sacrifice of a god named Soma**, who often appears in the shape of a **white bull** or of the moon. On Roman monuments Mithras **equally reluctantly** sacrifices the white bull, which is then transformed into the moon (noted by A. A. Macdonell, *A Vedic Reader for Students*, 1917; discussed in Encyclopaedia Iranica). This detailed parallel — the same reluctance, the same lunar transformation — suggests the sacrificial motif is **pre-Zoroastrian**, rooted in a shared Indo-Iranian ritual substrate predating both the Roman mysteries and the Avestan reform. Treaties in ancient times were sanctioned by a **common sacrificial meal**, so the link between contract-keeping (Mithra's core function) and the bull sacrifice has a deep cultic logic.

## The archaeology of mithraea: what the evidence actually shows

Because there are **no written texts** from Mithraic worshippers — no mythologies, origin stories, or theological treatises from Roman Mithraists — all our direct knowledge of the cult's internal life comes from archaeology. This makes the archaeological record unusually important and its constraints unusually constraining.

### What mithraea look like

Mithraea are architecturally consistent across the entire Roman world — from Britain to North Africa to the Black Sea to Syria — which itself implies a degree of organizational coherence and deliberate practice:

- **Shape**: narrow, rectangular meeting rooms, often (though not always) built underground or in basements
- **Size**: most measure only about **10 meters long** — intentionally small, accommodating perhaps 30–40 people at most
- **Interior**: vaulted ceilings; stone or masonry benches along the long walls where initiates reclined; a central aisle; the tauroctony image displayed at the far end, positioning Mithras as the symbolic host

The small size is significant: these were not mass congregations but tight, closed fellowship groups. Manfred Clauss interprets the tauroctony at the head of the room as **Mithras presiding as host** over the communal meal that was the central communal ritual.

**Not all mithraea were underground or hidden.** The site of ritual was not always an underground cave — that is partly a modern misperception. Notable examples in completely public settings:

- The **Mithraeum of the Baths of Caracalla** (Rome) — located inside a major public bathhouse. Neighbors would have known exactly what was going on.
- The **Mithraeum in Crypta Balbi** (Rome) — in a large apartment complex
- Numerous mithraea in frontier garrison towns where the local military community was openly identifiable

The "mystery" in "mystery cult" refers to secret initiatory rituals, not to a hidden social presence. The cult operated openly enough that Christians knew about it and wrote polemics against it.

### The tauroctony: iconography and interpretation

The tauroctony is the single most universal image of the Roman Mithras cult — found in virtually every mithraeum without significant stylistic deviation across the entire Mediterranean. Its universality suggests it represented a foundational theological statement, even though we cannot reconstruct the myth it illustrated.

Standard features of the tauroctony scene:
- **Mithras** dressed in Persian garb (Phrygian cap, tunic, trousers), kneeling on the back of a bull, driving a dagger into its neck
- His head **turned to look over his shoulder** — away from the act, as though reluctant or directed by an outside force
- The bull accompanied by: a **snake** crawling toward the wound, a **dog** leaping toward the wound, a **raven** perched near Mithras's shoulder, a **scorpion** clasping the bull's testicles
- Often framed by the **zodiac** and figures of **Sol** (sun) and **Luna** (moon) at the upper corners

There is no archaeological evidence that actual bull-slaying was practiced. The scene is symbolic, not liturgical in that sense.

**Interpretive debate:** The zodiacal framing and the accompanying animals (all of which correspond to constellations or celestial features) have led **Roger Beck** and **David Ulansey** to propose primarily **astrological interpretations** — the tauroctony encodes a celestial event, possibly the precession of the equinoxes (Ulansey), or a map of the soul's descent and ascent through the planetary spheres (Beck). **Franz Cumont's** earlier reading (an Iranian cosmological myth directly transplanted) is now largely rejected because its specific Iranian antecedents cannot be demonstrated. The **Mithraeum of the Seven Spheres** at Ostia depicts zodiac symbols along the benches, strongly supporting an astral/cosmic reading of the initiatory experience.

### The seven grades and social structure

Ancient sources (principally **Jerome**, confirmed by floor mosaics at the Mithraeum of Felicissimus at Ostia) identify **seven grades of initiation**, each associated with a planet:

| Grade | Latin/Greek name | Planet | Symbol |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Corax** (Raven) | Mercury | Caduceus |
| 2 | **Nymphus** (Bridegroom) | Venus | Lamp |
| 3 | **Miles** (Soldier) | Mars | Helmet/sword |
| 4 | **Leo** (Lion) | Jupiter | Fire shovel |
| 5 | **Perses** (Persian) | Moon | Curved sword |
| 6 | **Heliodromus** (Runner of the Sun) | Sun | Torch |
| 7 | **Pater** (Father) | Saturn | Staff |

Ascending through these grades mapped onto the soul's journey through the planetary spheres — the Neoplatonic cosmological framework (see [Neoplatonism and theurgy](neoplatonism-and-theurgy.md)) applied to initiatory practice.

**Social composition based on inscriptions:**

- **Male-only**: only male names appear in dedications — women were excluded
- **Military concentration**: a high proportion of inscriptions come from soldiers and officers along the *limes* (frontier garrisons); the cult seems to have been particularly popular in the Roman military
- **Broad social range**: inscriptions also record Roman politicians, merchants, freedmen, and slaves — suggesting the cult crossed class lines
- **But stratified in practice**: the social hierarchy of Roman life was reproduced inside the cult. A centurion would not allow a lowly soldier to outrank him in the Mithraic grades. Slaves could join but rarely advanced beyond lower grades. The **Lion grade** appears to be the most commonly attested rank, suggesting relatively few initiates advanced beyond the middle grades
- **Small, intimate cells**: given the 10-meter mithraeum size, each cell was a close-knit group of perhaps 30–40 men, with intense personal bonds reinforced by shared secrets, ritual meals, and the grade hierarchy

## Christianity and Mithraism: the archaeological relationship

### Shared practices in shared space

Both Christianity and Roman Mithraism were products of the same late Roman religious environment and shared some of its generic features:

- **Ritual communal meal**: a central practice in both. But as the podcast host notes, shared meals were a **common feature of Roman religious life** — not specific to either cult. The **theological meaning** of the meals was almost certainly distinct: Paul's Eucharist encodes a sacrificial death-and-resurrection theology; the Mithraic meal's significance, while unclear (no texts), was likely cosmic/astral.
- **Meeting in small, closed groups**: again, a feature of many Roman associations (*collegia*) and religious movements
- **Dura-Europos** (Syria, ~3rd cent. CE): a Christian house church and a Mithraeum were **found on the same street** in this garrison town — direct archaeological evidence of literal co-existence. The two communities shared a neighborhood, not a theology.

### Evidence of antagonism and destruction

The relationship was not only parallel but **competitive and sometimes violent**:

**Justin Martyr** (~150 CE) and **Tertullian** (~160–220 CE) explicitly identified Mithraic practices as diabolical parodies of Christian ones — Justin compares the Mithraic bread-and-water rite to the Eucharist (saying the devil imitated it; *First Apology* 66). This is the earliest datable evidence that Christians recognized the parallel and felt threatened enough to explain it.

Beyond polemic, **physical destruction** is archaeologically documented:

- **Hawarte, Syria**: A Mithraeum with **Christian graffiti scratched into the walls** — and a **Christian church built directly on top of the Mithraeum** after it went out of use. The sequence indicates deliberate supersession.
- **Santa Prisca, Rome** (under the church of Santa Prisca on the Aventine Hill): A Mithraeum with **defaced Mithraic images** — systematic obliteration of the iconography before or concurrent with the church's construction above it.
- **San Stefano Rotondo, Rome**: Another case where a church was built over or adjacent to a destroyed Mithraeum.

These sites are not isolated anomalies. The pattern — Mithraeum → defacement → church construction — is documented at multiple locations across the empire and represents a **physical archaeology of religious competition** in the 4th–5th centuries.

## Why the Roman Mithras cult disappeared: Walsh's multi-factor thesis

The Roman Mithras cult effectively disappeared by the **late 4th–early 5th century CE**. The archaeologist **David Walsh** (whose dissertation surveyed the abandonment of mithraea) argues there is **no single explanation** — rather a combination of factors:

1. **Constantine's patronage of Christianity** (from 312 CE onward): Christianity gained powerful, wealthy patrons in the imperial government that Mithras worshippers could not match. State resources, imperial prestige, and eventual legal pressure all shifted against non-Christian cults.

2. **Anti-Persian sentiment**: Some Mithraea were in areas where the Roman Empire was actively at war with Persia (Sasanian dynasty). Worshipping a god identified as "Persian" (*Mithras/Mitra*, explicitly a Persian name in Roman consciousness) was potentially problematic during military conflict on the eastern frontier.

3. **Barbarian destruction of frontier Mithraea**: A large proportion of mithraea were located in garrison towns and frontier settlements along the *limes* — precisely the areas most exposed to barbarian incursions in the 3rd–5th centuries. Physical destruction accounts for some of the archaeological abandonment.

4. **Loss of interest**: Some Mithraea show **no evidence of destruction at all** — the ritual equipment is intact, the images undamaged, the buildings simply abandoned. This suggests that for at least some communities, the cult ended not with external violence but with internal disinterest, possibly as members converted to Christianity voluntarily.

**Walsh's conclusion**: the cult's disappearance was **overdetermined** — multiple pressures converged simultaneously, and different Mithraea ended by different routes. This cautions against simple narratives of "Christianity destroyed Mithraism" (true in some cases) or "Mithraism naturally faded" (true in others).

## “Contract” in the West

Even when Roman initiates swore **oaths** and underwent **ordeal-like** progression, it is **analogy**, not **proof**, to collapse Roman, Zoroastrian, and Buddhist **figures** into one **historical person** or one **pure** Iranian rite.
