# The shared Greek translation lens: how Middle Platonic vocabulary restructured Buddhism and Christianity simultaneously

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers who have encountered the Greek-Buddhist philosophical parallels in [Alternative transmission theories §8](alternative-buddha-jesus-transmission-theories.md) and want to investigate a structural explanation that does not require direct Buddhist–Christian contact.
- **Question:** How much of the apparent "convergence" between Mahāyāna Buddhism and early Christianity is a property of the *traditions themselves* — and how much is a property of the Greek philosophical vocabulary that both traditions were translated *through* by overlapping intellectual communities in the same 3rd BCE–3rd CE window?
- **Scope:** The Greek philosophical vocabulary applied to Buddhist, Jewish, and Christian sacred corpora roughly 250 BCE–300 CE, with focus on Middle Platonic Greek (~100 BCE–250 CE) as the peak translation moment.
- **Epistemic status:** The individual translation events documented here are **Tier A** (e.g., Aśoka's Greek edicts, the LXX, Philo's Logos theology). The claim that these translation events *explain* apparent Buddhist–Christian convergence — rather than direct contact or independent structural convergence — is **Tier B–C** and is explicitly framed as a research hypothesis.

---

## Consensus snapshot

No unified scholarly synthesis exists for the specific thesis presented here. What is independently established:

- Aśoka's Greek edicts render *dharma* as *eusebeia* (~250 BCE) — documented in the bilingual Kandahar rock edicts (Fussman; Schlumberger et al.).
- The LXX translators imported Aristotelian and Platonic vocabulary into Torah (~250–50 BCE) — extensively documented in Septuagint scholarship (Rajak; Hengel; Pietersma).
- Philo of Alexandria systematically reconciled Jewish theology with Middle Platonic philosophy using Greek vocabulary that was then available to early Christian writers — documented in Runia's *Philo of Alexandria and the Timaeus of Plato* (1986) and extensive subsequent scholarship.
- John 1:1 deploys the *Logos* concept with a background that presupposes the Philonic synthesis — argued by Dodd, Brown, and most NT scholars.
- The Gandhāran Buddhist tradition produced bilingual Greek-Kharoṣṭhī texts and Greek-vocabulary Aśokan edicts at exactly the nodes where Buddhist-Greek philosophical exchange is most densely documented.

The synthesis — that this shared translation vocabulary explains apparent Buddhist–Christian convergence — is the hypothesis this page proposes.

---

## The thesis

In the 3rd century BCE through the 2nd century CE, the same Greek philosophical vocabulary — drawn from Stoic physics, Middle Platonic theology, and Aristotelian logic — was applied by bilingual intellectual communities to translate, interpret, and systematize at least four different sacred corpora:

1. **Jewish scripture** (in Alexandria, producing the LXX and Philo's allegorical commentary tradition)
2. **Buddhist Dharma** (in Gandhāra and Bactria, producing Greek-language Aśokan edicts, Milindapañha dialogue, and Greek Buddhist philosophical synthesis)
3. **Nascent Christian theology** (in Antioch, Ephesus, and Alexandria, producing the Pauline and Johannine texts and their early commentarial tradition)
4. **Iranian Zoroastrian thought** (in the Hellenistic courts of the Seleucids and later in Greek-speaking Magi communities)

These four translation projects were not coordinated. But they were using the *same vocabulary*, drawn from the same Greek philosophical tradition, under similar imperial pressures (the need to make local traditions legible in a *koinē* Greek administrative culture), at the same nodes (Alexandria, Antioch, Taxila/Gandhāra).

**The structural implication:** traditions that appear to converge may be converging not *toward each other* but *toward the shared Greek vocabulary they were both translated through*. What looks like "Buddhist influence on Christianity" or "Mahāyāna bodhisattva theology paralleling Pauline Christology" may, in several specific cases, be the *same Greek philosophical lens* applied to two different traditions by translators working in similar intellectual environments.

---

## Mechanism: who were the translators?

The translation events were not performed by theologians with deep knowledge of both traditions simultaneously. They were performed by:

- **Bilingual educated administrators and court intellectuals** trained in Greek *paideia* (philosophical education) who were tasked with making local religious traditions comprehensible to Greek-speaking rulers and communities
- **Diaspora community members** (Jewish intellectuals in Alexandria; Greek Buddhist monks in Gandhāra) who were personally bicultural and whose theological synthesis naturally used Greek vocabulary to articulate inherited religious concepts
- **Philosophical school members** (Pythagorean, Stoic, Middle Platonic) whose categories were the common intellectual vocabulary of educated Greek speakers across the Mediterranean and Near East

None of these communities needed to know about *each other's* translation projects. They needed only to share the same Greek philosophical vocabulary and face the same basic problem: how do you articulate a non-Greek religious concept to a Greek-speaking audience?

## The systematic term survey

The following table maps key Greek philosophical terms across Buddhist and Christian applications, with dates and a comparison column indicating what the timing reveals. Absences are as informative as presences.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Logos** (λόγος) | Cosmic rational principle (Heraclitus); active agent of divine creation (Stoics); mediating divine mind (Middle Platonism) | No direct Buddhist equivalent. The *absence* is informative: Buddhist philosophy had no need for a *Logos*-type cosmic-rational-principle because its cosmology is non-theistic. The closest structural parallel — *dharma* as the cosmic principle of order — was rendered as *eusebeia*, not *logos*. | John 1:1 (~95 CE); Philo (~25 CE); Colossians 1:15–17 (disputed; ~60–80 CE); Justin Martyr (~150 CE) systematizes. | The *Logos* is a Christian-Jewish creation; it has no Buddhist application. This term's *absence* from Buddhist Greek texts undermines any claim of direct Buddhist–Christian doctrinal exchange on this specific concept. |
| **Eusebeia** (εὐσέβεια) | Piety, reverence toward the divine; Platonic civic virtue meaning proper relationship to gods and parents | **Aśoka's Greek edicts** (~250 BCE, Kandahar): *dharma* rendered as *eusebeia* — the earliest and most significant rendering. The edict at Kandahar reads: "Ten years having passed, King Piodasses (Aśoka) showed *eusebeia* to men." *Eusebeia* is chosen because it is the Greek virtue closest to *dharma*'s civic-ethical dimension. | LXX uses *eusebeia* for Hebrew concepts of piety; Pastoral Epistles deploy it as a key virtue term (1 Tim 2:2; 4:7; 6:3). | **Buddhist use is earlier** (~250 BCE) than most NT uses (~50–100 CE). This reveals that the Greek vocabulary for Buddhist ethics was already established before the Christian texts were composed. The convergence of *eusebeia* in both traditions reflects a shared Greek vocabulary for "proper conduct," not Buddhist influence on Christianity. |
| **Kenōsis** (κένωσις) | "Self-emptying"; the act of emptying oneself of content. However: *kenōsis* as a theological term is a Christian coinage from Phil 2:7 ("he emptied himself"). *Śūnyatā* is rendered in Pali/Sanskrit, not through Greek *kenōsis*. The Gandhāran scrolls use Gāndhārī, not Greek, for *śūnyatā*. Whether any Greek Buddhist translator used *kenōsis* for *śūnyatā* requires primary-source verification (see [R21 in the Research backlog](research-backlog.md)). | Philippians 2:7 (~55 CE): "ἑαυτὸν ἐκένωσεν" ("he emptied himself"). Becomes a theological term for the divine self-limitation in the incarnation. | **The parallel is structural, not translational.** Both traditions developed an "emptying" concept independently using their own vocabularies. The apparent convergence (*kenōsis* = *śūnyatā*) is a typological analogy, not evidence of shared translation vocabulary. |

The Mahāyāna *tathāgatagarbha* tradition develops independently in India in the 1st–3rd cents. CE. Both may reflect the influence of Middle Platonic elevation-of-the-divine-potential ideas, but through different translation channels. |

The structural parallel to *apatheia* is real. Both concepts describe a state of emotional non-reactivity achieved through detachment from outcomes and desires. The Pyrrhonist *ataraxia* (un-disturbedness) is closely related. | Desert fathers and Evagrius Ponticus (4th cent. CE) deployed *apatheia* as a central ascetic goal. John Cassian (5th cent.) transmitted *apatheia* to the Latin West as *tranquillitas*. | The Buddhist and Stoic concepts are genuinely parallel and may both derive from pre-Alexandrian contemplative practice. Pyrrho's contact with Indian ascetics (~327–325 BCE) is one documented transmission vector. But Evagrius's *apatheia* derives from Stoic philosophy through Clement and Origen, not directly from Buddhism. The *convergence* is real; the *mechanism* is Stoicism applied to both, not Buddhist→Christian borrowing. |
| **Ataraxia** (ἀταραξία) | Un-disturbedness; Pyrrhonist goal: the state produced by suspension of all judgments. The Pyrrhonist case is the most significant: Pyrrho's *ataraxia* was produced by his contact with Indian ascetics at Taxila, making this the one Greek term most likely to have *direct* Buddhist input into its formation (Tier B). The concept enters Christian monasticism through Stoic-derived *apatheia*, not directly. | Pyrrho is the **hinge figure**: the most plausible single case where a Greek philosophical concept was directly shaped by Indian Buddhist or proto-Buddhist practice. *Ataraxia* is therefore the term most likely to represent genuine direct influence — but in the *Greek←Indian* direction, not Christian←Buddhist. |
| **Sōtēr / sōtēria** (σωτήρ / σωτηρία) | Savior / salvation; Greek civic term for a benefactor who delivers a community from danger. The *sōtēr* concept entered Christianity via the LXX (Jewish-Greek) and Hellenistic ruler cult, not via Buddhism. |
| **Pneuma** (πνεῦμα) | Breath, spirit; Stoic *pneuma* = the divine rational fire pervading all things; imparts life and rationality. | *Prāṇa* (Sanskrit): breath, life-force, vital energy. However, they develop in very different theological directions and the parallel is **structural and etymological, not a translation event**. | Paul's *pneuma* as the divine Spirit active in the community (1 Cor 12–14; Gal 5:22–25; Rom 8) draws on Stoic *pneuma* theology via Jewish usage. | **Independent tracks with a shared IE root.** The convergence is older than the translation moment — it reflects PIE inheritance, not Greek translation of Buddhism into Christian vocabulary. |
| **Anastasis** (ἀνάστασις) | Standing up, rising; in the Septuagint and NT, bodily resurrection of the dead. | **No Buddhist parallel.** Buddhism explicitly denies the permanent existence of a bodily self that could be resurrected. The Buddhist rejection of a permanent *ātman* is precisely what makes *anastasis*-type resurrection theology impossible within Buddhist metaphysics. | LXX: *anastasis nekrōn* appears in Daniel 12:2 (~165 BCE) and 2 Maccabees 7. Paul: *anastasis* is a core concept (1 Cor 15); the resurrection of Christ is "firstfruits" of universal resurrection. | **Complete absence from Buddhism.** This absence is arguably the most important single piece of evidence against Buddhist origin for Pauline resurrection theology. The *anastasis* concept has Iranian (Zoroastrian *ristākhēz*, "rising of the dead") roots that entered Judaism during the Achaemenid period — a completely different transmission route. |
| **Hypostasis** (ὑπόστασις) | Foundation, substance; in Neoplatonism (Plotinus), the three *hypostaseis* are the One, Intellect, and Soul — the three levels of divine emanation. | **No direct Buddhist application in the Gandhāran period.** The Mahāyāna *trikāya* doctrine (three bodies of the Buddha: *dharmakāya*, *sambhogakāya*, *nirmāṇakāya*) has been compared to the Neoplatonic three *hypostaseis* — both describe a supreme principle, an intermediary luminous level, and a manifest level. This is a structural parallel (Tier C); no evidence that the *trikāya* doctrine was formulated with reference to Plotinus. | Christian Trinitarian theology uses *hypostasis* after Nicaea (325 CE) to describe the three "persons" of the Trinity. Origen had already used the three *hypostaseis* framework. | The parallel between *trikāya* and the Neoplatonic/Trinitarian three-level structure is one of the clearest examples of the "shared translation lens" thesis: both doctrines emerged in the 3rd–4th cents. CE in communities steeped in Middle Platonic philosophy, using the same structural vocabulary to articulate a three-level cosmic hierarchy. Neither tradition needed to know about the other; the vocabulary itself provided the template. |

## What this table reveals

### 1. The asymmetry of absence

The terms most central to Christian soteriology — *sōtēr*, *anastasis*, *hypostasis* in the Trinitarian sense — are either completely absent from Buddhist Greek usage or reflect entirely different transmission routes (Iranian for *anastasis*; internal Christian development for Trinitarian *hypostasis*). This asymmetry argues strongly against direct Buddhist→Christian borrowing for these concepts.

### 2. The priority of Buddhist translation

*Eusebeia* as a translation of *dharma* predates most NT uses by 300 years. This suggests that the Greek-Buddhist translation tradition was **pioneering** the use of Greek virtue vocabulary for non-Greek religious concepts, and that the Christian translators worked *after* this vocabulary had already been applied to Buddhism — not before. If there is an influence direction for specific Greek vocabulary choices, it may run *Buddhist Greek → LXX → early Christian* in some cases, not Christian → Buddhist.

### 3. The *trikāya*–Trinity convergence: the clearest case

The parallel between the Buddhist *trikāya* doctrine and the Christian Trinity (or the Neoplatonic three *hypostaseis*) is the strongest structural case for the shared-vocabulary thesis. Both are:
- Three-level hierarchical structures
- Articulated in the 3rd–4th centuries CE
- In communities that used Middle Platonic Greek as their philosophical vocabulary
- Describing a cosmic principle, an intermediate luminous level, and a manifest/embodied level

Neither tradition needed to read the other's texts to arrive at this structure. The Middle Platonic three-*hypostaseis* framework was the available conceptual template for *anyone* in the educated Greek-speaking world who wanted to describe a hierarchical cosmic divine structure. Both Buddhist and Christian theologians used it.

### 4. Pyrrho as the genuine hinge

The one case where direct Buddhist→Greek philosophical influence is most plausible (*ataraxia*, the tetralemma, the trilakṣaṇa) is also the one case with documented biographical contact (Pyrrho at Taxila). But Pyrrho's influence runs into *Greek philosophy*, not into *Christianity*. Pyrrhonism influenced Neoplatonism; Neoplatonism influenced Christian theology. The Buddhist element — if present — entered Christianity through this long indirect chain, not through direct Christian knowledge of Buddhist texts.

## The indirect influence model

The following flow chart represents the most defensible version of indirect Buddhist–Christian mutual influence:

```
Proto-Indian contemplative traditions (~600–400 BCE)
    ↓
Pyrrho contacts Indian ascetics at Taxila (~327–325 BCE)
    ↓
Greek Pyrrhonism: epochē, ataraxia, negative vocabulary
    ↓
Greek Skepticism → Middle Platonism (~100 BCE–200 CE)
    ↓
Neoplatonism (Plotinus, Porphyry, ~250–300 CE)
    ↓
Christian Neoplatonism (Origen, Clement, ~185–254 CE)
    ↓
Apophatic Christian theology (Pseudo-Dionysius, ~500 CE)

Buddhist Gandhāran synthesis (Mahāyāna, trikāya, ~50–250 CE)
    ↓
Buddhist Middle Platonic vocabulary (hypostasis-type structure)
    ↓
Buddhist theology East (Central Asia, China, Japan)
```

This model does not require a single moment of Buddhist→Christian contact. It requires only:
- Indian contemplative practice influencing early Greek Pyrrhonism
- Pyrrhonism influencing Neoplatonism
- Neoplatonism providing the shared vocabulary that both Christian and Mahāyāna theologians used independently

The **convergence between Christianity and Mahāyāna Buddhism** that modern scholars observe is therefore real — but it is mediated by a shared philosophical vocabulary, not by direct reading of each other's texts.

---

## What the thesis does not explain

The shared-vocabulary thesis handles some apparent convergences well. It does not explain:

- **Biographical motif parallels** (miraculous birth, desert temptation/Tree of Bodhi temptation, twelve disciples/followers, death/parinirvāṇa, physical ascension): these structural parallels predate the Greek translation layer and belong to the PIE mythic biography pattern — see [Mythic biography patterns](mythic-biography-patterns-prophet-and-buddha-analogy.md).
- **The cosmic mediator slot** shared by Maitreya and Christ: this reflects PIE inheritance — see [PIE origin of the messianic slot](pie-mediator-slot-origins.md).
- **The tomb-relic parallel** between Buddhist stupa veneration and Christian martyr cult: this may reflect either IE inherited practices or genuinely independent convergent responses to the same cultic need — see [Research backlog §R20](research-backlog.md).
