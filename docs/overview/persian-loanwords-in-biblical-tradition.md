# Persian loanwords in Biblical Hebrew and Aramaic

Loanwords are among the **hardest** evidence for cultural contact: a community does not borrow a foreign word without **sustained exposure** to the culture that uses it. The Hebrew Bible and Imperial Aramaic documents contain a significant layer of **Old Persian** (and sometimes **Median**) vocabulary — concentrated in texts composed or edited during or after the **Achaemenid period** (c. 550–330 BCE).

This page catalogs the most important examples, distinguishes **secure** from **debated** etymologies, and connects them to the site's broader argument about **Persian imperial corridors** as vectors for eschatological concepts.

---

## 1. The key loanwords

### pardes (פַּרְדֵּס) — "paradise"

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Hebrew** | *pardēs* — "enclosed garden, park" (Song of Songs 4:13; Ecclesiastes 2:5; Nehemiah 2:8) |
| **Old Persian** | *paridaida-* — "walled enclosure, royal park" (attested in Elamite PFT transcriptions as *par-te-taš*) |
| **Greek** | *paradeisos* (παράδεισος) — Xenophon *Anabasis* 1.2.7 popularizes the term for Persian royal gardens |
| **Later theological use** | LXX uses *paradeisos* for the Garden of Eden (Genesis 2:8 LXX); becomes "Paradise" in Christian and Islamic eschatology |
| **Confidence** | **Secure** — universally accepted Persian loanword |

**Significance:** The word that all three Abrahamic traditions use for the **eschatological destination of the righteous** is a Persian administrative term for a royal garden. This does not prove that the *concept* of afterlife reward came from Persia, but it shows that the **vocabulary** through which Jews, Christians, and Muslims imagine paradise was shaped by Persian material culture.

### dat (דָּת) — "law, decree, religion"

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Hebrew / Aramaic** | *dāt* — "royal decree, law" (Esther 1:8, 3:8; Ezra 7:12, 7:26; Daniel 2:9, 6:6) |
| **Old Persian** | *dāta-* — "law" (from PIE \*dʰeh₁- "to place, establish"; cognate with Latin *datum*) |
| **Modern Persian** | *dād* — "justice, law" |
| **Confidence** | **Secure** — standard lexical borrowing; concentrated in Esther, Ezra, Daniel (Persian-period settings) |

**Significance:** The legal vocabulary of post-exilic Judaism was partly shaped by Persian administrative language. When Ezra promulgates "the law of God and the law of the king" (Ezra 7:26), the word for royal law is Persian.

### raz (רָז) — "mystery, secret"

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Aramaic** | *rāz* — "mystery, secret" (Daniel 2:18, 2:19, 2:27–30, 4:6; prominent in Dead Sea Scrolls: 1QpHab, 1Q27 *Mysteries*) |
| **Iranian** | *rāz* — "mystery, secret" (Middle Persian; possibly from Old Iranian \*rāza-) |
| **Greek** | *mystērion* (μυστήριον) is the LXX/NT translation — itself from mystery-cult vocabulary |
| **Confidence** | **Secure** — widely accepted Iranian loanword in Aramaic |

**Significance:** The word Daniel uses when interpreting Nebuchadnezzar's dream — the eschatological "mystery" of history's plan — is Iranian. The Dead Sea Scrolls use *rāz nihyeh* ("mystery of what is to come") as a technical term for apocalyptic revelation. The **conceptual vocabulary** of Jewish apocalypticism partly borrows its key term from Persian.

### genaz (גְּנַז) — "treasure, treasury, archive"

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Aramaic / Hebrew** | *gənaz* / *ganzak* — "treasury, stored treasure" (Ezra 5:17, 6:1; Esther 3:9, 4:7; Ezekiel 27:24) |
| **Old Persian** | *ganza-* — "treasure, treasury" |
| **Modern forms** | Persian *ganj*; cf. Gazna / Ghazni (city name) |
| **Confidence** | **Secure** — standard loanword |

### apadana (אפדנו) — "palace, pavilion"

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Hebrew** | *'appaden* — "palace" (Daniel 11:45, a hapax legomenon) |
| **Old Persian** | *apadāna-* — the great audience hall at Persepolis and Susa |
| **Confidence** | **Secure** — architectural borrowing |

### pitgam (פִּתְגָם) — "decree, sentence, word"

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Aramaic** | *pitgām* — "word, decree, sentence" (Ezra 4:17, 5:7; Esther 1:20; Ecclesiastes 8:11) |
| **Old Persian** | *patigāma-* — "message, reply" |
| **Confidence** | **Secure** |

## 2. What this evidence does and does not show

### What it shows (strong claims)

- **Sustained cultural contact:** Jewish scribes and administrators worked inside a Persian-language bureaucratic world for over two centuries. The borrowings are not random tourist words; they are **legal**, **administrative**, **architectural**, and **conceptual** vocabulary.
- **Direction of borrowing is clear:** Old Persian → Aramaic / Hebrew (not the reverse). The Persian empire was the prestige culture; Jewish communities were embedded within it.
- **Concentration in specific books:** Esther, Ezra-Nehemiah, Daniel, and late Psalms/Wisdom literature — precisely the texts where Persian-period composition or editing is expected.

### What it does not show (limits)

- Vocabulary borrowing does not automatically prove **doctrinal** borrowing. Jews could adopt *pardes* without adopting Zoroastrian eschatology.
- However: when the **vocabulary** for paradise, mystery, and law comes from Persian, and the **concepts** of paradise, apocalyptic mystery, and divine decree also develop or intensify in the same period, the convergence is suggestive. It shifts the burden of proof toward explaining why the conceptual borrowing would **not** follow the linguistic borrowing.

## 3. The eschatological implications

| Concept | Persian vocabulary in Jewish texts | Conceptual development in same period |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Paradise** | *pardēs* (< *paridaida*) | "Garden" becomes eschatological destination (LXX, 1 Enoch, Lukan paradise) |
| **Mystery of history** | *rāz* (< *rāza*) | Daniel's apocalyptic visions; Qumran *rāz nihyeh* |
| **Divine law/decree** | *dāt* (< *dāta*) | Post-exilic emphasis on Torah as cosmic law |
| **Royal judgment** | *pitgām*, *dātābār* | Eschatological "verdict" language |

The pattern is consistent: **Persian administrative and conceptual vocabulary** entered Jewish literary culture during the same centuries when **Jewish eschatology** became more elaborate, more cosmic, and more dualistic.
