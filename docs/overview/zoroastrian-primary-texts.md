# Zoroastrian primary texts — the source side

The site argues constantly *about* Zoroastrian eschatology but rarely presents the texts themselves. This page corrects that by laying out the primary Avestan and Pahlavi witnesses — the source side of the Iran→Judaism transmission argument. Knowing what these texts actually say, when they were composed, and how scholars date them is essential for evaluating every claim on the site.

---

## How to read Zoroastrian texts

The textual tradition has two major layers:

- **Old Avestan** (*Gāthās* of Zarathustra): the oldest stratum, linguistically related to Vedic Sanskrit, composed by or attributed to Zarathustra himself. Approximate date is disputed: conservative mainstream scholarship places the Gāthās between ~1000–600 BCE; some scholars argue for an earlier horizon (~1500–1200 BCE) based on linguistic and archaeological considerations.
- **Younger Avestan** (Yashts, later Gathas, Vidēvdāt): a later compositional layer, preserving older material in a newer linguistic register. Approximate date: ~700–300 BCE for the core, though continued oral transmission is assumed.
- **Pahlavi books** (Middle Persian): the systematic elaborations written down in Sasanian and post-Sasanian centuries (3rd–10th cent. CE). They preserve much older oral tradition but their written form is late.

**Critical implication for the site's thesis**: the transmission argument requires demonstrating that the *concepts*, not necessarily the specific written texts, were available during the Achaemenid period (550–330 BCE). The Gāthic seeds of resurrection and dualism are attested before the Achaemenid period; the Pahlavi elaborations are late but encode tradition traceable to the Younger Avesta.

## 1. Yasna 30 and 45 — the Gāthic dualism (Old Avestan)

**Composition layer**: Old Avestan; attributed to Zarathustra. Approximate date: 1000–600 BCE (debated).

**What it says**: Yasna 30 contains the foundational statement of Zoroastrian cosmic dualism — the doctrine of the **twin spirits** (*mąθra* of the *spənta mainyu* and *angra mainyu*) who exist from the beginning and whose choice defines the moral structure of the cosmos.

> "Now I will speak of the two primal Spirits of existence, of whom the holier thus addressed the evil one: 'Neither our thoughts, nor teachings, nor wills, nor choices, nor words, nor deeds, nor inner selves, nor souls agree.'" — *Yasna 45.2* (tr. after Skjærvø)

> "In the beginning, those two Spirits who are twins were known as the one good and the other bad, in thought, in word, and in deed. Between these two, the wise chose correctly; fools did not." — *Yasna 30.3* (paraphrase after Boyce/Insler)

> "And when these two Spirits first met, they established life and not-life; and that at the end the worst existence shall be for the followers of the Lie, but the best dwelling for those of Righteousness." — *Yasna 30.4*

**Yasna 43 and 51** elaborate: Ahura Mazdā will preside over a final judgment; the righteous will receive the "best existence" (*vahišta ahu*); the followers of the Lie will be punished. The cosmic fire ordeal (*ātar*) will separate righteous from wicked at the end.

**Tier assessment**: Tier A — this is a primary text in the standard scholarly canon; the dualism is authentically old Avestan and predates Achaemenid rule. Whether this specific Gāthic formulation was known to Jewish scribes during the Achaemenid period is a separate (Tier B) question.

**Relevance**: this is the oldest clear statement of the cosmic good/evil dualism the site traces into the Dead Sea Scrolls' "Spirit of Truth vs. Spirit of Falsehood" (1QS 3:13–4:26). See [Dead Sea Scrolls dualism](dead-sea-scrolls-dualism-and-iranian-parallels.md).

## 2. Zamyad Yasht (Yasht 19) — the Saoshyant and the *xwarnah* (Younger Avestan)

**Composition layer**: Younger Avestan; core material older, final form ~700–300 BCE.

**What it says**: Yasht 19 is one of the longest and most important Younger Avestan hymns. Its central subjects are *xwarnah* (divine radiance/glory, sometimes translated "fortune" or "kingly glory") and the future saviors — the **Saoshyants**.

> "We sacrifice unto the awful Kingly Glory … that will cleave to the victorious Saoshyant [the holy Saoshyant] and his helpers, when he shall restore the world, which will (thenceforth) never grow old and never die, never decaying and never rotting, ever living and ever increasing, and master of its wish, when the dead will rise, when life and immortality will come, and the world will be restored at (God's) wish." — *Yasht 19.89* (tr. Darmesteter, lightly revised)

**Key concepts introduced here**:
- The Saoshyant (*saošyant*, "one who will bring benefit") as a future eschatological figure born of Zarathustra's preserved seed
- Universal resurrection (*ristāxēz*) — "the dead will rise"
- Cosmic renovation — the world becomes undecaying, undying, ever-living
- The *xwarnah* as the divine radiance that accompanies and validates righteous kingship and the final savior

**Tier assessment**: Tier A for the text's existence; Tier B for its influence on Second Temple Jewish resurrection concepts (contact is plausible; specific dependence requires case-by-case argument).

**Relevance**: the Saoshyant passage is the clearest Zoroastrian precursor to the "universal resurrection at the end of time" concept that appears in Daniel 12:2 (~165 BCE). See [Saoshyant influence hypotheses](saoshyant-influence-hypotheses.md).

## 3. Bundahišn, chapter 34 — the eschatological tauroctony and the White Hōm (Pahlavi)

**Composition layer**: Pahlavi; the *Greater Bundahišn* was compiled c. 9th–10th cent. CE from older Iranian oral and written tradition. The specific cosmological content reflects pre-Islamic Zoroastrian thought with Younger Avestan roots.

**What it says**: The culminating act of the Frašegird (cosmic Renovation) is described in chapter 34 as a **sacrificial act** by the Saoshyant Sōšyāns:

> Sōšyāns with his helpers will perform a *yazišn* [worship/sacrifice]. For that *yazišn*, they will slaughter the bull Hadayānš. From the fat of Hadayānš and from the White *Hōm* they will prepare the drink of immortality (*anōšag*) and give it to all the righteous. All the righteous become immortal thereby; the resurrection of the body is accomplished.

**Key concepts**:
- The **eschatological tauroctony** — a bull sacrifice at the end of time that generates immortality. This parallels the Roman Mithraic *tauroctony* iconographically, and both may reflect a shared Indo-Iranian sacrificial substrate.
- The **White Hōm** — eschatological Haoma/Soma pressed at the end of time to produce the elixir of immortality. Connects to the Vedic Soma complex and the Indo-Iranian ritual origin of "elixir of life" theology.
- **Universal resurrection through sacrificial elixir** — all bodies are raised by drinking this substance, completing the cosmic moral order.

**Tier assessment**: Tier A for the text; Tier B for its relationship to Roman Mithraism (structural parallel with the tauroctony); Tier B/C for its relationship to Christian Eucharistic theology ("drink this and live forever" — structural parallel, not documented dependence).

**Relevance**: cross-links to [Roman Mithras and Manichaean Mithra](mithra-roman-manichaean.md) for the tauroctony comparison; to [Saoshyant figure page](../figures/zoroastrian-saoshyant.md) for the full passage and elaboration.

## 4. Zand-i Wahman Yasn (Bahman Yasht) — the Zoroastrian apocalypse (Pahlavi)

**Composition layer**: Pahlavi, probably composed in early Islamic period (~7th–9th cent. CE) but preserving much older traditions, possibly rooted in a now-lost Avestan original (*Wahman Yasht*). The core of the "metals vision" may date to the Achaemenid or Hellenistic period.

**Why it matters for this site**: The *Zand-i Wahman Yasn* is the **closest Zoroastrian parallel to Daniel and Revelation** — a prophetic apocalypse in which Zarathustra receives a vision of future world ages, tribulation, and final cosmic renovation. It is **almost entirely absent from popular comparative religion discussions** and represents one of the most important under-used sources on the site.

**The metals vision (chs. 1–3)**: Zarathustra sees a tree with four branches — of gold, silver, steel, and iron — representing successive world ages of increasing corruption, corresponding to the four metals vision of Daniel 2 (gold head, silver chest, bronze belly, iron legs). The structural parallel is exact:

| Feature | Zand-i Wahman Yasn | Daniel 2 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Visionary framing** | Zarathustra receives the vision from Ahura Mazdā | Daniel interprets Nebuchadnezzar's dream |
| **Four ages / materials** | Gold, silver, steel, iron tree | Gold head, silver chest, bronze belly, iron legs |
| **Progressive corruption** | Each age worse than the last | Each material weaker/baser than the last |
| **Final destruction** | The iron age culminates in demonic rule and tribulation | The iron kingdom is shattered by a stone not cut by human hands |
| **Renovation** | Frashokereti follows the tribulation | God establishes an eternal kingdom |

**Key passages**:
> "In the reign of Kay Wištāsp [a great affliction will come] … demons will be manifest; the Lie will be strong; the Renovation will be near." — *ZWY* 3.25 (summarized)

> "After them will come the most evil sovereignty … those who are most sinful will be their leaders. Then Sōšyāns, the world-renovator, will arise with his helpers, and the Renovation of the world will come." — *ZWY* ch. 7 (paraphrase after West)

**The tribulation schema** in the *Zand-i Wahman Yasn* — progressive ages, demonic rule, final savior, cosmic renovation — is structurally identical to the schema of Daniel 7–12, Revelation, and 4 Ezra. This is not an accident. Whether it reflects:
(a) Iranian influence *on* Jewish/Christian apocalyptic, or
(b) parallel development from a shared ancient Near Eastern "oracle of ages" form, or
(c) *late* Zoroastrian adaptation *from* Jewish/Christian apocalyptic

…is a genuinely contested scholarly question. The structural parallel itself is **Tier A** (secure); the direction of influence is **Tier B** (probable Iranian priority given the metals vision's likely antiquity, but not certain given the Pahlavi redaction date).

**Tier assessment**: Tier A for the structural parallel; Tier B for Iranian priority in the metals-vision schema; Tier C for specific literary dependence either direction.

## 5. Vidēvdāt (Vendidad) — demons, purity, and death (Younger Avestan)

**Composition layer**: Younger Avestan; date debated, possibly late Achaemenid or early Hellenistic final form.

Less directly relevant to eschatology than the texts above, but important for the **demonology** parallel. The Vidēvdāt systematically catalogs the *daēvas* (demons) that Ahura Mazdā opposes, including **Aēšma** (Fury/Violence) — a demon whose name appears in the Book of Tobit as *Asmodeus* (*Ashmedai* in the Aramaic text). This is one of the most direct **onomastic transmissions** from Zoroastrian demonology into Jewish intertestamental literature:

> "Aēšma of the bloody mace" (*Vidēvdāt* 10.9, 19.6) → Tobit's *Asmodeus* (Tob. 3:8)

**Tier**: Tier A/B — the name equation is philologically secure; whether Tobit's author was drawing on Iranian demonological tradition (via Mesopotamian contact) or knew the specific Avestan text is less clear.

## The dating problem — a summary

| Text | Language | Approximate composition | Available during Achaemenid contact? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Gāthās (Yasna 28–34, 43–51, 53) | Old Avestan | ~1000–600 BCE (debated) | Yes — predates Achaemenid rule |
| Younger Avesta (Yashts, Vidēvdāt) | Younger Avestan | ~700–300 BCE | Partially — core material predates, final form contemporary |
| Bundahišn | Middle Persian (Pahlavi) | Compiled 9th–10th cent. CE | No in written form — but encodes traditions with Younger Avestan roots |
| Zand-i Wahman Yasn | Middle Persian (Pahlavi) | 7th–9th cent. CE | No in written form — but the metals vision may have Achaemenid-era antecedents |

**The methodological point**: the transmission argument does not require that Jewish scribes read the specific Pahlavi books. It requires that the *concepts* — cosmic dualism, future savior, universal resurrection, cosmic renovation — were orally and institutionally present in Iranian religious practice during the Achaemenid period. The Gāthic and Younger Avestan texts establish that these concepts were indeed pre-Achaemenid. The Pahlavi books preserve elaborations of them, not their invention.
