# Neoplatonism, theurgy, and solar theology (2nd–4th cent. CE)

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers who want to understand the philosophical-religious framework within which Mithraism, Roman solar religion, and early Christianity competed for educated adherents in the 2nd–4th centuries CE, and who want to understand why late antique intellectuals took mystery cult ritual theologically seriously.
- **Question:** What is Neoplatonism, what is theurgy, and how do they relate to the site's themes of solar religion, initiatory ritual, and imperial theology?
- **Scope:** Plotinus, Porphyry, Iamblichus, Julian the Apostate; Sol Invictus; the Christmas date question; Constantine's solar background.

---

## 1. The Neoplatonic framework

### Plotinus (204–270 CE) — the founding synthesis

**Plotinus** was an Egyptian-born philosopher who studied under Ammonius Saccas in Alexandria and then taught in Rome (~244–270 CE). His lectures were collected and edited by his student Porphyry into the ***Enneads*** (*Enneas* = group of nine; six sets of nine treatises each). The *Enneads* are the primary Neoplatonic text.

**The three hypostases (foundational realities):**

1. **The One** (*to hen*): Absolute unity; beyond all predication, description, or thought. The One does not "think" or "be" in any ordinary sense — it simply is, as the source of all being and all intelligence. The One "overflows" or "emanates" (not by choice or action — this is a necessary, eternal process) to produce the next level.

2. **Nous (Intellect)** (*Nous*): The first emanation from the One; eternal self-thinking thought — the divine mind contemplating the Platonic Forms. Nous is simultaneously thinker and thought, the realm of eternal truths. The Plotinian Nous is structurally close to both the Stoic Logos and Philo's Logos — a divine intellect that is the active principle of order in reality.

3. **World-Soul** (*Psychē*): The second emanation; the soul of the universe, which mediates between Nous and the material world. Individual human souls are parts of or derive from the World-Soul.

**Matter** is not a fourth hypostasis but a kind of non-being — the furthest point of emanation from the One, where the creative power of the One is effectively exhausted.

**The mystic return (*epistrophē*):** The point of Plotinian philosophy is not merely to describe this structure but to enable the human soul to reverse the emanation process — to ascend through contemplation from the engagement with matter, through purification and philosophical practice, back to the One. Plotinus himself reportedly had several experiences of mystical union with the One (*Porphyry, Life of Plotinus* 23).

**Relevance for this site:** Neoplatonism provides the **intellectual grammar** for understanding why late antique educated people could take mystery cult initiation, solar religion, and sacramental rites as philosophically serious — not merely as popular superstition. The mystery rites, in a Neoplatonic reading, are *enacted philosophy* — the practical counterpart to contemplative ascent. See also [Baptism and initiation](baptism-initiation-ritual-bridge.md).

### Porphyry (~234–305 CE) — Mithras and the cave

**Porphyry of Tyre** was Plotinus's student and literary executor. He edited the *Enneads*, wrote a *Life of Plotinus*, and produced a large corpus of Neoplatonic, anti-Christian, and religious texts.

**For this site, two works are central:**

***De Abstinentia*** (*On Abstinence from Animal Food*, 4 books): An argument for vegetarianism and philosophical purity; includes comparative religious material about priestly abstinence practices across various traditions.

***De antro nympharum*** (*The Cave of the Nymphs*): An allegorical commentary on Odyssey 13:102–112 (the cave of the Nymphs on Ithaca). Porphyry reads the cave as a cosmic symbol — the world as the cave into which souls descend and from which they return. In this context, he makes the most important ancient explicit connection between Neoplatonic philosophy and Mithraism:

> "Eubulus tells us that Zoroaster was the first to consecrate a natural cave in honour of Mithra, the creator and father of all; it was located in the mountains near Persia and had flowers and springs. This cave bore for him the image of the Cosmos which Mithra had created, and the things within it, by their proportionate arrangement, provided him with symbols of the elements and climates of the Cosmos."
>
> — Porphyry, *De antro nympharum* 6 (Arethusa edition, adapted)

Porphyry continues: Mithraists use the cave as a symbol of the cosmos; the grades of initiation represent the soul's passage through planetary spheres as it descends into incarnation and ascends back to the divine origin. This is the **primary ancient text** making the Mithraeum a cosmic-philosophical symbol rather than merely a cult site.

Porphyry also wrote the most sustained ancient philosophical critique of Christianity, ***Adversus Christianos*** (*Against the Christians*, 15 books) — now lost but partially reconstructed from quotations in Christian refutations. His critique included textual arguments against the authenticity of Daniel and careful source analysis — making him arguably the first historical-critical Bible scholar in antiquity.

### Iamblichus (~245–325 CE) — theurgy as divine practice

**Iamblichus of Chalcis** (Syria) was the most influential figure in later Neoplatonism. His most significant work for this site is ***De Mysteriis*** (*On the Mysteries*, ~300 CE) — a defense of **theurgy** (*theourgia*, "divine work") against Porphyry's purely contemplative Neoplatonism.

**The Porphyry-Iamblichus debate:**

Porphyry had written to an Egyptian priest named Anebo asking whether rituals, prayers, and material symbols could really elevate the soul toward the divine — or whether only philosophical contemplation could do this. *De Mysteriis* is the (fictional) response of the Egyptian divine Abammon.

Iamblichus's argument: **ritual works by divine grace, not human effort**. The "symbols" (*symbola*) and "tokens" (*synthēmata*) used in ritual are not mere signs of the divine — they *are* divine, because the gods themselves placed these tokens in matter. When a theurgist activates these symbols (through invocation, physical materials, and ritual action), the gods *respond* because these are their own tokens.

**Why this matters for the site:** Iamblichus's theurgy provides the philosophical framework for understanding why a late antique intelligent person would take a Mithraic initiation, an Eleusinian initiation, or even a Christian Eucharist as *actually* transformative — not as psychological reinforcement or social ritual but as genuine divine action in the material world. The debates between Porphyry and Iamblichus are the philosophical background for the same debates that split early Christianity between Donatists (sacraments work by the minister's holiness) and their opponents (the sacrament works *ex opere operato*, by the act itself).

## 2. Julian the Apostate and solar theology

**Julian** (Flavius Claudius Iulianus, ~331–363 CE), known as "the Apostate" in Christian tradition, was Roman Emperor from 361 to 363 CE. He is the last pagan Roman emperor. His philosophical theology is the clearest surviving statement of late antique solar religion.

### *Hymn to King Helios* (*Eis ton basiléa Hélion*, 362 CE)

Written on the occasion of the festival of Sol Invictus in December 362, the *Hymn to King Helios* is Julian's systematic solar theology:

- **Helios** is not merely the physical sun but the **visible image of the intelligible Sun** — the Neoplatonic One as expressed in the sensory world
- Helios is the **second one** (after the transcendent One / the Good) in the Neoplatonic hierarchy — identified with Nous, the divine intellect
- **Mithra** is identified with Helios: Julian writes that Helios-Mithra was revealed to Julian by his tutor Mardonius and later through dreams and contemplation — for Julian, Mithras *is* Helios
- **Apollo** is another aspect of the same solar divine principle
- The **Sol Invictus** cult is the Roman civic expression of this universal solar theology

Julian's text is the most philosophically sophisticated ancient statement of the solar-religion synthesis that the Kushan coins document in material form (MIIRO + BODDO + HELIOS on the same coinage). It shows that educated late antique thinkers were aware of and explicitly articulating this synthesis.

**The key passage:**

> "Helios, the king of all that is beautiful, who is the father and lord of me, of the whole of this visible world, and of the entire universe — he from the first, from everlasting, who is the cause of all things good … Mithra is in the midst, between the Father of all things and our world, not in place but in virtue."
>
> — Julian, *Hymn to King Helios* 130B-C (Wright translation, adapted)

## 3. Sol Invictus and the imperial solar cult

**Sol Invictus** ("Unconquered Sun") became the official state cult of Rome under the emperor **Aurelian** (r. 270–275 CE) after his conquest of the Palmyrene empire in Syria (272 CE). The solar deity he promoted was associated with the Syrian sun god **Elagabalus** (from whom a previous emperor, Caracalla/Elagabalus, had taken his name) and with the broader Syrian-Iranian solar tradition.

**Aurelian's specific acts:**
- Built a grand Temple of Sol Invictus in Rome (dedicated 274 CE, near the modern Piazza Venezia)
- Instituted a festival of Sol Invictus on **December 25** — the day after the winter solstice on the Julian calendar (*natalis Solis Invicti*, "birthday of the Unconquered Sun")
- Created a special college of priests for Sol Invictus
- Used Sol Invictus imagery on coinage throughout the empire

### The December 25 problem

The date of Christmas (December 25) has been extensively debated in relation to Sol Invictus. There are two main scholarly positions:

**History-of-religions (solar influence) view:** Christmas was deliberately placed on December 25 to compete with or replace the Sol Invictus festival — a pagan feast Christianized by the church.

**Calculation hypothesis (Talley and others):** Early Christians calculated December 25 independently from the assumption that Jesus died on March 25 (the same date as his conception — the "integral age" or *computus* theory); 9 months later = December 25. This calculation appears in Christian sources independently of any reference to Sol Invictus.

**Current scholarly status:** The calculation hypothesis has gained ground and is now preferred by many liturgical historians (Talley, *The Origins of the Liturgical Year*, 1986; Bradshaw and Johnson, *The Origins of Feasts, Fasts and Seasons*). The popular claim that Christmas was "stolen from Sol Invictus" is likely an oversimplification, though the cultural resonance of December 25 as a solar date is not accidental.

## 4. Constantine — solar symbolism before and during conversion

**Constantine I** (r. 306–337 CE) is traditionally the first Christian emperor — the emperor who issued the Edict of Milan (313 CE) granting religious tolerance and who presided over the Council of Nicaea (325 CE). But his relationship with solar religion before and during his conversion complicates the picture.

### Solar imagery in pre-conversion sources

- **Panegyric of 310 CE**: delivered in honor of Constantine at Trier, this official oration describes a vision Constantine had of Apollo/Sol Invictus offering him laurel crowns (symbols of victory) and promising 30 years of rule. This is a documented public vision of a solar deity — before the Christian tradition of the vision before the Battle of Milvian Bridge (312 CE).
- **Coinage**: Sol Invictus appears on Constantine's coinage until approximately 325 CE — well after his supposed conversion in 312 CE. The gradual phasing-out of Sol imagery on Constantinian coinage is itself evidence of a slow transition rather than an abrupt conversion.

### The Chi-Rho ambiguity

The **Chi-Rho** (☧) — the Christogram used by Constantine after the Battle of Milvian Bridge — combines the Greek letters Chi (X) and Rho (P), the first two letters of *Christos*. But the same symbol also appeared in pre-Christian contexts as an astronomical abbreviation for the Greek word *chrēston* ("auspicious") and is sometimes interpreted as a solar symbol.

The ambiguity is not proof that Constantine was "really" a solar rather than a Christian, but it illustrates how thoroughly solar and Christian iconography overlapped in the early 4th century — a fact that Neoplatonic solar theology had been building for decades.

## 5. Why this zone matters for the site's research program

The Neoplatonic-theurgy-solar zone (2nd–4th cent. CE) is the **intellectual and theological context** for:

1. Understanding why Mithraism attracted educated adherents (it was philosophically respectable, connected to Neoplatonic solar metaphysics)
2. Understanding why the Roman emperor cult blended so easily with solar religion and eventually with Christianity (all three were articulations of the same underlying theology of the divine sun-king)
3. Understanding the competition between mystery religions and early Christianity as a competition within a shared philosophical framework — not between "rational" Christianity and "irrational" paganism, but between competing articulations of the same late antique religious sensibility
4. The Kushan coin evidence (MIIRO + BODDO + HELIOS) connects eastern solar-Iranian theology to this western synthesis on the same Central Asian horizon
