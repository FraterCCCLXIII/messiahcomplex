# Nestorian Christianity and the Silk Road

The history of Christianity's eastward spread — from the Council of Ephesus (431 CE) through Sasanian Persia, along the Silk Road into Central Asia, and finally into Tang-dynasty China — is less well-known than its western expansion but equally important for understanding how religions moved through the late antique and early medieval world. This page covers the Church of the East's theological origins, the Syriac translation centers that made eastward mission possible, the communities that left traces along the Silk Road, and the Xi'an Stele of 781 CE as the most important surviving primary evidence.

---

## 1. Theological background: the Council of Ephesus and the split

**Nestorius** (~381–451 CE) was Patriarch of Constantinople from 428 CE. His teaching — that Mary should be called *Christotokos* ("bearer of Christ") rather than *Theotokos* ("bearer of God") because the divine nature of Christ could not be born or suffer — was condemned at the **Council of Ephesus** (431 CE) on charges of dividing Christ into two persons.

The condemnation is more politically complex than it appears: Nestorius was supported by the **school of Antioch** with its *dyophysite* ("two natures") Christology (associated with Theodore of Mopsuestia), while Cyril of Alexandria promoted a *miaphysite* Christology ("one nature" of the incarnate Christ). The politics of Constantinople, Rome, Alexandria, and Antioch were all entangled.

### The "Nestorian" label

**Important:** "Nestorian" is largely a polemical label applied by opponents. The Church of the East itself prefers names like *Suraya* (Syriac Christians) or **Church of the East**. Nestorius himself was condemned; his followers disputed that they were following Nestorius specifically. The theology they actually held was that of **Theodore of Mopsuestia**, the revered teacher of the Antiochene school.

The **theological position** of the Church of the East:
- Two *qnōmē* (Syriac: "individual subsistences" or "natures") in Christ: divine and human
- Opposed to both Chalcedonian (two natures, one *hypostasis*) and Miaphysite (one nature) positions
- More emphasis on the full humanity of Christ — theologically relevant to their Silk Road encounters with Buddhists and Zoroastrians, who could engage the human Jesus without committing to divine incarnation

### The split and its consequences

After Ephesus, the Antiochene Christians increasingly separated themselves from the Byzantine church. By 484 CE, the **Council of Beth Lapat** (Sasanian Persia) formally declared the Persian church's independence from Byzantine Christianity.

Key centers:
- **School of Edessa** (now Urfa, Turkey): originally the premier theological school; expelled from the Byzantine empire in 489 CE by Emperor Zeno, who closed the school
- **School of Nisibis** (now Nusaybin, Turkey/Syria border): the expelled teachers of Edessa reconstituted the school here, inside Sasanian territory — where it operated freely under Persian protection until the Islamic conquest

## 2. Syriac translation centers

The Schools of Edessa and Nisibis were not merely theological seminaries; they were **translation and intellectual centers** of enormous importance for the transmission of Greek learning.

**What was translated:** The curriculum at Nisibis included:
- Biblical texts (Syriac *Peshitta* version of the Bible)
- Greek theological texts (Theodore of Mopsuestia, Diodore of Tarsus)
- **Aristotle's *Organon*** (logical works) in Syriac translation — this is the critical transmission vector for Aristotelian logic into the Islamic world
- Medical texts (Galen, Hippocrates)
- Astronomical texts

**The translation chain:** Greek originals → Syriac translations at Edessa/Nisibis → absorbed into Gondishapur Academy (Sasanian) → Arabic translations at the Abbasid House of Wisdom → Islamic and eventually medieval European philosophy

Without the Syriac Christian translators of Edessa and Nisibis, the Islamic philosophical tradition (al-Kindi, al-Farabi, Ibn Sina / Avicenna, Ibn Rushd / Averroes) and through it medieval European scholasticism (Thomas Aquinas) would lack Aristotle. The chain runs directly through Nestorian Christianity.

## 3. Sasanian Persia: refuge and mission base

The Church of the East was paradoxically **better protected** under Sasanian Zoroastrian rule than under Byzantine Christian rule — because the Sasanian kings found Nestorian Christians useful:
- They were enemies of the Byzantine emperor (the Nestorians' theological opponents), making them politically trustworthy
- They brought medical, technical, and scholarly expertise (the Gondishapur connection)
- They were economically valuable as merchants and administrators

The **Patriarch of the Church of the East** was based at **Seleucia-Ctesiphon** (the Sasanian capital) and recognized by the Sasanian court. Church of the East bishops operated across Sasanian territory — Mesopotamia, Persia, Khorasan — and increasingly eastward toward Central Asia.

### Martyrdom under Shapur II

The major exception to Sasanian tolerance was the persecution under **Shapur II** (309–379 CE), during his prolonged wars with Christian Rome. Tens of thousands of Syriac Christians were reportedly killed (~340–379 CE) as suspected Roman sympathizers. The Syriac martyrology literature (*Acts of the Persian Martyrs*) documents this period extensively.

This persecution paradoxically accelerated eastward migration of Syriac Christian communities further into Central Asia — away from the Persian-Roman war zone.

## 4. The Silk Road mission

From approximately the 5th century CE, Church of the East bishops and monks were active across the Silk Road network:

### Central Asia

Documented Church of the East presence:
- **Merv** (modern Turkmenistan): bishop attested from the 4th century CE; a major Church of the East center on the eastern Iranian plateau
- **Samarkand** (Uzbekistan): bishop attested from 6th–7th century CE
- **Herat** (Afghanistan): Church presence attested

**The oasis cities:** The Silk Road did not run across open steppe but through a chain of oasis cities — Merv, Samarkand, Tashkent, Kashgar — each of which was a commercial and cultural node. In these cities, Nestorian Christians, Zoroastrians, Manichaeans, Buddhists, and eventually Muslims operated in proximity, sharing commercial networks and sometimes sharing institutions (shared monasteries in some Turfan evidence).

### Turfan (Tarim Basin)

The **Turfan oasis** in western China (modern Xinjiang) yielded extraordinary manuscript finds in the early 20th century. The collections include:
- Manichaean texts in Sogdian, Old Turkish, and Middle Persian
- Buddhist texts in multiple languages
- **Syriac Christian texts**: fragments of biblical texts, liturgical texts, and devotional poetry
- **Hybrid texts**: documents showing Christian-Buddhist and Christian-Manichaean vocabulary overlap

The Turfan evidence shows multiple religious traditions in direct contact — not merely coexistence but occasional textual mixing and vocabulary borrowing.

## 5. China: the Xi'an Stele (781 CE)

The **Xi'an Stele** (*Da Qin Jingjiao Liuxing Zhongguo Bei*, "Stele Commemorating the Spread of the Luminous Religion of Da Qin in China") is the most important primary source for the history of the Church of the East in China.

**Physical description:** A limestone stele approximately 3 meters high; found buried in 1625 CE near Chang'an (modern Xi'an, Shaanxi province). Currently in the Xi'an Beilin (Forest of Steles) Museum.

**Date:** Inscribed in 781 CE during the Tang dynasty.

**Languages:** Bilingual: Chinese on the front face; Syriac inscription around the base and sides.

**Content:**

The Chinese text (composed by Adam / Jing Jing, a Nestorian monk) narrates:
- A theological introduction to the "Luminous Religion" (*Jingjiao*, literally "shining/luminous teaching") — using Daoist and Buddhist-sounding vocabulary to describe Christian theology
- The arrival in China in **635 CE** of the monk **Alopen** (*Ālèběn* in Chinese; probably *Abraham* in Syriac) from "Da Qin" (the Roman/Byzantine west), bringing scriptures
- The reception of Alopen by Emperor Taizong (r. 626–649 CE), who ordered the scriptures translated
- A list of Church of the East clergy who served in China across 150 years

**Key passage on the 635 CE arrival:**

> "In the time of the accomplished Emperor Taizong, the brilliant and accomplished Tang Emperor, among the enlightened and holy men who came was the most virtuous Alopen, from the country of Da Qin. Guided by the azure clouds, he bore the true scriptures; observing the direction of the winds, he braved difficulties and dangers. In the ninth year of the period Zhenguan [635 CE] he arrived at Chang'an. The Emperor sent the Duke Fang Xuanling, the left-wing general of the Imperial Guard, with a guard of honor, to the western suburbs to meet the visitor."
>
> — Xi'an Stele, face 1 (Saeki translation, adapted)

**The Syriac cross:** The stele is surmounted by a **cross growing from a lotus flower** — a visual synthesis of Christian and Buddhist symbolism. Below it is a Chinese cloud pattern. This iconography is itself evidence of deliberate **cross-cultural accommodation** in Nestorian missionary strategy.

**The theological language:** The stele uses *Daoist* vocabulary (*wuwei*, non-action; *yuanshi*, primordial beginning), *Buddhist* vocabulary (*fa*, dharma; *sanzang*, three baskets/scriptures), and Chinese cosmological vocabulary to describe Christian concepts. This is the same "translation" strategy used by Jewish writers of the Sibylline Oracles: repackaging one tradition's content in another tradition's authoritative vocabulary.

## 6. The Thomas Christians (Malabar Church)

A separate stream of early Eastern Christianity reached India through the **Indian Ocean trade routes** rather than the overland Silk Road.

The **Thomas Christians** (also: *Nasrani*, *Saint Thomas Christians*, or *Malabar Christians*) are communities in modern Kerala (southwestern India) who claim that the Apostle Thomas brought Christianity to India in 52 CE. The historicity of this origin claim is debated, but:
- The community is undeniably ancient — documented by the Persian monk Cosmas Indicopleustes (~550 CE, *Christian Topography*) who mentions a bishop, presbyters, and laity in India at Malabar
- The **Copper Plates of Quilon** (9th cent. CE) document community rights granted by the local Hindu king — physical evidence of an established community
- The liturgy used by the Thomas Christians is **Syriac** (*East Syriac* rite) — confirming the connection to the Church of the East rather than to Western or Byzantine Christianity
- The earliest undisputed evidence points to a community established by the 4th–5th centuries CE through the trading networks connecting the Persian Gulf and the Malabar coast

The Thomas Christian tradition shows that the Church of the East's reach through Indian Ocean trade routes paralleled its Silk Road expansion.

## 7. Interaction with Buddhism and Manichaeism

The Nestorian presence in Central Asia brought Christian communities into direct contact with Buddhist and Manichaean institutions at the same oasis cities.

**Shared vocabulary:** Turfan texts show that Syriac Christians borrowed Buddhist and Manichaean terms for key concepts: *bnay qyama* (Syriac: "sons of the covenant," a term for church members) appears alongside Buddhist *sangha* vocabulary in multilingual contexts.

**Shared institutional structures:** Some scholars (Tardieu on Manichaeism; Tang Xijian on Tang-dynasty religion) have argued that Nestorian monastery structures in Central Asia were influenced by Buddhist monastery organization (the *vihāra* format of cells around a central courtyard).

**The Jingjiao texts:** A set of Chinese-language Nestorian texts from Dunhuang (the Pelliot and Stein collections) show syncretic tendencies — the *Sūtra of Jesus the Messiah* (*Yishen Messiah jing*) and the *Discourse on Almsgiving* use Buddhist vocabulary (*Buddha-dharma*, *brahmacārin*, *bodhisattva*) to describe Christian concepts. Whether these represent genuine synthesis or merely translation accommodation is debated.
