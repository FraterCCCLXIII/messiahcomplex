## 1. The Sasanian empire: overview

The **Sasanian (Sassanid) empire** (Persian: *Sāsāniyān*) was founded by **Ardashir I** in 224 CE when he defeated and killed the last Parthian king, Artabanus IV. The Sasanians ruled from 224 to 651 CE, when the last emperor Yazdegerd III fled before the Arab-Islamic armies and was murdered in Merv.

The Sasanians were:
- **Iranian** (from Fārs province in southwestern Iran) and **explicitly Zoroastrian** — the first Persian dynasty to make Zoroastrianism a state religion with institutional apparatus
- In permanent **conflict with Rome/Byzantium** in the west (a series of wars over Mesopotamia, Syria, and Armenia)
- **Tolerant of religious minorities** to varying degrees — Jews, Christians (Nestorian), Buddhists, and Manichaeans all had communities in Sasanian territory at various points, though the degree of tolerance fluctuated

The Sasanian capital was **Ctesiphon** (near modern Baghdad), on the Tigris river — which meant that the empire's heartland was in Mesopotamia, not just Iran.

## 2. Zoroastrianism institutionalized

The Sasanian period is when Zoroastrianism was first **organized as a formal state religion** with a priesthood (*mobad* hierarchy), fire temple network, and canonical texts.

### Kartir and the Magian church

**Kartir** (also: Kerdir, Karder) was a Zoroastrian high priest who served under multiple Sasanian kings from Shapur I to Narseh (~240–293 CE). He left a series of rock inscriptions — the most extensive personal inscriptions from any Sasanian figure — describing his religious reforms:

> "And I made the Mazdayasnan religion and the Zoroastrian priests prosperous and honorable throughout all the regions. And I distressed and punished Ahreman and the demons … I distressed and punished Jews, Buddhists, Hindus, Nazoreans (*nāzarēans*, Christians), Christians (*kristiyāns*), Baptists, and Manichaeans."
>
> — Kartir inscription at Ka'ba-ye Zartosht (summary)

Kartir's inscriptions are the **first documentation of an organized Zoroastrian clerical establishment** with:
- A hierarchy of priests (*mobadān mobad*, chief Magian, down to local priests)
- A system of fire temples (*ātašgāh*) throughout the empire
- State support for the Zoroastrian establishment
- Active suppression of competing religions

### The Avesta compiled

The **Avesta** — the sacred scripture of Zoroastrianism — was compiled in its surviving form during the Sasanian period, particularly under **Shapur II** (309–379 CE) and **Khosrow I** (531–579 CE), according to traditional accounts preserved in the Denkard.

**What survives:**
- The **Yasna** (72 chapters; includes the Old Avestan Gathas of Zarathustra and the Younger Avestan liturgy)
- The **Yashts** (hymns to individual yazatas; including Yasht 10 to Mithra)
- The **Vendidad** (*Vī-dāevo-dāta*, "Law against the demons"): purity codes and demonology
- The **Khorde Avesta**: liturgical compilation for daily prayers

Only about **one-quarter** of the original Avesta survived the disruptions of Alexander's conquest and subsequent centuries. The Sasanians were reportedly attempting to reconstruct the whole from memory, oral tradition, and scattered copies.

### The Pahlavi literature

The **Pahlavi** (Middle Persian) religious literature — Bundahishn, Denkard, Selections of Zādspram, Mēnōg ī Xrad — was compiled primarily in the 9th–12th centuries CE after the Islamic conquest, representing Zoroastrian scholars' efforts to preserve and systematize their tradition. These texts contain:
- **Cosmogony and eschatology** (Bundahishn): the full account of creation, the cosmic struggle, and the Frashokereti
- **Zarathustra's life and teachings** (Denkard, Books 5–7)
- **Ethics and wisdom** (Mēnōg ī Xrad)

The late compilation date of the Pahlavi texts creates a persistent methodological challenge: how much of their eschatological content is authentically pre-Islamic Zoroastrian, and how much reflects post-Islamic-conquest elaboration or response to Islamic influence?

## 3. Manichaeism: the Sasanian synthesis religion

**Mani** (born 216 CE in Babylonia, probably of Jewish-Christian *Elchasaite* background; died 274–277 CE) founded the religion that bears his name. Manichaeism is explicitly syncretic by design — Mani claimed to be the Seal of the Prophets, the final and complete revelation that synthesized and surpassed the teachings of Zarathustra, the Buddha, and Jesus.

### Mani's synthesis

Mani's cosmology drew on:
- **Zoroastrian dualism**: cosmic struggle of Light (Ahura Mazda / the Father of Greatness) and Darkness (Ahriman / the Prince of Darkness)
- **Christian soteriology**: a divine figure (Jesus the Luminous / Jesus Patibilis, "Suffering Jesus") who is crucified on the cross of light in every living thing; redemption through his light-sparks
- **Buddhist psychology and ethics**: the soul's journey through reincarnations; detailed ethical codes for the Manichaean community (*Electi* = monks and nuns; *Auditores* = lay followers); vegetarianism; non-violence

### Sasanian reception

The Sasanian kings **alternately patronized and persecuted** Manichaeism:
- **Shapur I** (r. 240–270 CE) befriended Mani and allowed his mission; Mani may have accompanied Shapur on his campaigns
- **Bahram I** (r. 273–276 CE) had Mani arrested and executed under pressure from the Zoroastrian priesthood (led by Kartir)
- Manichaeism was officially condemned and periodically persecuted in Sasanian Persia but survived by spreading outward through trade networks — westward to the Roman empire (where Augustine of Hippo was a Manichaean for nine years before his conversion to Christianity) and eastward through Sogdian merchants into Central Asia and China

### Geographic spread

| Direction | Key period | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Roman empire | 3rd–5th cent. CE | Augustine's *Confessions*; North African Manichaean communities; Roman imperial edicts against Manichaeism |
| Sogdia and Central Asia | 4th–10th cent. CE | Sogdian Manichaean texts from Turfan; Manichaean communities at major Silk Road oases |
| China | 694 CE onward | Official Manichaean mission to the Tang court; Manichaean temples in Fujian surviving into the Ming dynasty |
| Uighur Empire | 762–840 CE | State religion of the Uighur Khaganate — the only time Manichaeism became a state religion |

Manichaeism's spread eastward through the Silk Road is one of the clearest documented cases of Iranian-tinged religious transmission in the site's framework (see [Manichaean Mihr and soteriology](manichaean-mihr-soteriology.md)).

## 4. The Babylonian Talmud under Sasanian rule

The **Babylonian Talmud** (*Talmud Bavli*) was compiled at the Rabbinic academies of **Sura** (founded ~219 CE) and **Pumbedita** (founded ~259 CE) in Babylonia — under Sasanian imperial rule. The redaction is conventionally dated to around **500–600 CE**.

This means that the most authoritative text of Rabbinic Judaism was produced in a Persian-Zoroastrian imperial context, where:
- Jewish scholars had centuries of contact with Zoroastrian priests, scholars, and legal institutions
- The Aramaic language shared by Jewish and Zoroastrian literati facilitated intellectual exchange
- Jewish communities were legally autonomous (*dhimmi*-style status under Sasanian law) but embedded in a Zoroastrian imperial culture

### Potential Zoroastrian influences on the Talmud

**Yaakov Elman** (*Between Worlds: Duality and Transcendence in Rabbinic Judaism*, and multiple articles) has conducted the most systematic study of potential Zoroastrian influence on the Babylonian Talmud:

- **Purity laws (*ṭaharāh*)**: Talmudic expansion of purity categories (corpse impurity, menstrual purity codes, food laws) shows structural parallels to Zoroastrian purity legislation (*Vendidad*). The direction of influence is debated; both may draw on common ANE priestly traditions.
- **Demonology**: The Babylonian Talmud is more elaborated in its demonology than the Palestinian Talmud — a difference possibly reflecting the Zoroastrian-Babylonian cultural environment.
- **Legal forms**: Some Babylonian Talmudic legal formulas show resemblances to Sasanian administrative legal forms — suggesting that Jewish legal thinking was partially shaped by the ambient legal culture.

Elman's conclusion is cautious: the influence is real but **diffuse and structural** rather than specific doctrinal borrowing — the kind of ambient cultural influence that permeates a community living inside a dominant culture for centuries.

## 5. The Academy of Gondishapur

**Gondishapur** (Jundishapur, in Khuzestan province of southwestern Iran) was a major intellectual center in the Sasanian period. Under **Khosrow I "the Great"** (r. 531–579 CE), the Academy of Gondishapur became one of the most cosmopolitan intellectual institutions in the pre-Islamic world:

- **Nestorian Christian scholars** who had been expelled from Edessa (489 CE) by Byzantine emperor Zeno brought with them Syriac translations of Aristotle and Greek medical texts (Galen, Hippocrates)
- **Indian scholars** brought mathematical and astronomical texts — including the decimal number system and early algebra
- **Greek philosophical texts** (Platonic, Aristotelian, Neoplatonic) were translated into Syriac and Pahlavi

When the Islamic caliphate conquered Persia, the intellectual heritage of Gondishapur did not disappear — it was actively sought out. The Abbasid caliph **al-Mansur** (r. 754–775 CE) and especially **Hārūn al-Rashīd** (r. 786–809 CE) and **al-Maʾmūn** (r. 813–833 CE) sponsored the **Translation Movement** (*Bayt al-Ḥikma*, House of Wisdom, Baghdad), which translated the Gondishapur scholarly heritage — Aristotle, Galen, Euclid, Ptolemy, Indian mathematics — into Arabic.

**The chain:** Greek philosophy → Syriac translation at Edessa and Nisibis → Gondishapur under Sasanians → Arabic translation in Baghdad → medieval Islamic philosophy → transmission to medieval Europe via Toledo and Sicily.

This is perhaps the single most important intellectual transmission chain in world history, and it runs directly through the Sasanian period.

## 6. The Islamic conquest and what was transmitted (651 CE onward)

The Arab-Islamic conquest of Persia (636–651 CE) did not erase the Sasanian cultural heritage — it absorbed and transformed it.

### What survived the conquest

**Administrative culture**: The Islamic caliphate inherited Sasanian administrative structures, particularly under the Umayyads and Abbasids. Persian administrators (*dihqāns*, landed nobility) served in the caliphal bureaucracy and transmitted Iranian cultural norms and practices.

**Persian language**: The Arabic conquest did not extinguish Persian (*Fārsī*); Persian survived and eventually re-emerged as the language of Islamic high culture in the 9th–10th centuries (the **Samanid renaissance**). Persian became the literary language of Islamic courts from Spain to India — and Persian literature carried Zoroastrian and pre-Islamic Iranian themes (the Shahnameh of Firdausi, ~1010 CE, is explicitly a Zoroastrian epic).

**Eschatological vocabulary**: Persian converts (*mawālī*) brought Iranian eschatological concepts into early Islamic discourse. The word *firdaws* (paradise, from Avestan *pairidaeza*) appears in the Quran (18:107, 23:11). Iranian cosmological frameworks influenced the *Isrāʾ and Miʿrāj* (the Prophet's Night Journey and Ascension) literature.

**Shia Islam**: The strong Iranian component in early Shia communities — particularly the claim that the Prophet's daughter Fatima and ʿAlī's line merged with the Sasanian royal house through the marriage of al-Ḥusayn and the princess Shahrbānū — gave Iranian Zoroastrian eschatological concepts a specific channel into Shia theology. See [Shia Imamology and Zoroastrian parallels](shia-imamology-zoroastrian-parallels.md).
