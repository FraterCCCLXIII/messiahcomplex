# Dead Sea Scrolls dualism and Iranian parallels

The **Dead Sea Scrolls** (DSS), discovered near **Qumran** and dated primarily to the **2nd cent. BCE – 1st cent. CE**, contain some of the most striking parallels to **Zoroastrian** theology in all of Jewish literature. This page examines the key texts, the scholarly arguments for and against Iranian influence, and what this means for the site's broader investigation of transmission.

---

## 1. The Treatise on the Two Spirits (1QS 3:13–4:26)

The **Community Rule** (1QS) contains a passage known as the "Treatise on the Two Spirits" that describes a **cosmic dualism**:

> "He created man to rule the world and placed within him **two spirits** so that he would walk with them until the appointed time of his visitation: they are the **spirits of truth and of falsehood**. In the abode of light are the origins of truth, and from the source of darkness are the origins of falsehood. In the hand of the **Prince of Lights** is dominion over all sons of righteousness … and in the hand of the **Angel of Darkness** is total dominion over the sons of falsehood." (1QS 3:17–21, adapted)

### Structural parallel to Yasna 30

| Feature | Qumran (1QS 3–4) | Zoroastrian (Yasna 30) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Two spirits** | Spirit of Truth / Spirit of Falsehood | Spenta Mainyu / Angra Mainyu |
| **Cosmic scope** | Govern all humanity until the End | Choose truth (*aša*) or lie (*druj*) |
| **Ethical dualism** | Sons of Light vs. Sons of Darkness | Followers of Truth vs. Lie |
| **Eschatological resolution** | God destroys falsehood at the "appointed time" | Frashokereti: Ahura Mazdā defeats Angra Mainyu |
| **Divine sovereignty** | God created **both** spirits | Debated — Gathas may imply Ahura Mazdā's priority over both |

The parallel is **structural**, not verbal — the Qumran text is in Hebrew, the Gathas in Old Avestan. But the **architecture** (two cosmic spirits, ethical choice, light/dark mapping, eschatological destruction of the evil principle) is remarkably close.

---

## 2. The War Scroll (1QM)

The **War Scroll** describes a final eschatological battle between the "**Sons of Light**" and the "**Sons of Darkness**" — a 40-year cosmic war culminating in divine intervention and the defeat of Belial (the evil principle).

**Iranian parallel:** The Bundahišn and related Pahlavi texts describe the final battle at Frashokereti, where the forces of Ahura Mazdā defeat Ahriman (Angra Mainyu). While the War Scroll's military detail reflects **Hellenistic** and **Roman** military practice, the **cosmic war** framework is closer to Iranian eschatology than to anything in pre-exilic Hebrew prophecy.

---

## 3. The Messianic Apocalypse (4Q521)

**4Q521** describes a messiah figure who will: "**heal the wounded, revive the dead, and proclaim good news to the poor**" — language strikingly close to Isaiah 61 and to the Jesus tradition (Luke 4:18, Matthew 11:5). The text explicitly attributes **resurrection of the dead** to the messianic age.

**Significance:** This shows that by the 2nd–1st century BCE, some Jewish groups had developed a **messiah who raises the dead** — a concept not found in pre-exilic Hebrew texts but present in Zoroastrian eschatology (Saoshyant presides over universal resurrection).

---

## 4. *Rāz nihyeh* — the "mystery of what is to come"

The DSS use the term **rāz nihyeh** (רז נהיה) as a technical term for the divine plan of history revealed to the elect. The word *rāz* is an **Iranian loanword** (see [Persian loanwords](persian-loanwords-in-biblical-tradition.md)). The concept — a **mystery** hidden in heaven, revealed through apocalyptic vision, governing the entire course of history from creation to end — has no clear pre-exilic Hebrew antecedent. It appears fully formed in Daniel (also using *rāz*) and at Qumran.

## 5. The scholarly debate

### Arguments for Iranian influence

- **Shaked, Shaul** ("Iranian Influence on Judaism," *CHJ*): Argues that the structural parallels are too close and too concentrated in the Persian/post-Persian period to be coincidental. The Qumran community's dualism is the strongest single case for Iranian → Jewish conceptual transmission.
- **Hultgård, Anders** ("Persian Apocalypticism," in Collins, ed., *Encyclopedia of Apocalypticism*, vol. 1, Continuum, 1998, pp. 39–83): Documents specific parallels between Qumran eschatology and Zoroastrian apocalyptic texts.
- **Widengren, Geo; Hultgård, Anders; Philonenko, Marc** (eds.). *Apocalyptique iranienne et Dualisme Qoumrânien*. Paris: Maisonneuve, 1995 — the essential European collection making the positive case; Philonenko's contribution ("La Doctrine Qoumrânienne des Deux Esprits: Ses origines iraniennes," pp. 163–211) is specifically on the two-spirits doctrine and its Iranian roots in the Essene and early Christian traditions.
- **García Martínez, Florentino.** "Iranian Influences in Qumran?" In M. McNamara (ed.), *Apocalyptic and Eschatological Heritage: The Middle East and Celtic Realms*. Dublin: Four Courts, 2003, pp. 37–49 — recent focused treatment; concludes Iranian influence is "undeniable" for the dualist framework.
- **Collins, John J.** (*Apocalypticism and the Dead Sea Scrolls*, Routledge, 1997, pp. 41–43): In this focused book, Collins states directly: "It is widely recognized that the clear dualism of light and darkness in these scrolls reflects Zoroastrian influence."

### Arguments against or for caution

- **Collins, John J.** — Collins's position is nuanced rather than dismissive. In his *Oxford Handbook* survey (ch. 2, 2007), he writes: "While the importance of Persian dualism was exaggerated in the past, it is undeniably relevant to the discussion of the Dead Sea Scrolls." He maintains that Jewish apocalypticism also has **indigenous** roots in prophetic and wisdom traditions, and that Iranian influence is "one factor among several" rather than the whole explanation. The two positions — Zoroastrian influence is real, and Jewish apocalypticism is not simply imported — are compatible. *(Collins, *The Apocalyptic Imagination*, 3rd ed., Eerdmans, 2016.)*
- **Barr, James**: Emphasizes the difficulty of dating Zoroastrian texts — if the Gathas are late (some minimalists argue ~600 BCE or later), the direction of influence becomes less clear.
- **Hellenistic mediation**: Some scholars suggest Greek philosophical dualism (Platonic, Pythagorean) as an alternative or supplementary source for Qumran thinking.

### The dating problem

The strongest objection to Iranian influence is the **dating of Zoroastrian texts**. The Gathas are composed in Old Avestan but were transmitted orally for centuries before being written down in the Sasanian period. If the *ideas* are old but the *texts* are late, how do we know the direction of influence?

**Response:** (1) The linguistic archaism of the Gathas is independently established by comparative IE philology — they are genuinely old compositions even if written down late. (2) The Achaemenid inscriptions and the PFT confirm that Iranian religion was a living, practiced system in the 6th–5th centuries BCE. (3) The **loanword evidence** confirms the direction of cultural flow: Persian → Hebrew/Aramaic, not the reverse.

---

## 6. What this means for the site's thesis

The Qumran evidence does not prove that Judaism "copied" Zoroastrianism. It shows that:

1. The **most dualistic** Jewish community in the ancient world developed its theology during and after the period of **sustained Iranian contact**.
2. The **vocabulary** of their apocalyptic revelation (*rāz*) is an Iranian loanword.
3. The **structure** of their cosmic dualism (two spirits, light/dark, eschatological defeat of evil) closely parallels Iranian models.
4. The **concepts** that emerged (resurrection, cosmic war, messiah who raises the dead) are precisely those that later Christianity would inherit and elaborate.

The Qumran community is a **waypoint** on the road from Iranian eschatology to Christian soteriology — not the only road, but a documented one.
