# 1 Enoch and the Son of Man

## Context

- **Audience**: readers who want to understand how the "Son of Man" figure travels from Daniel through intertestamental Jewish literature into the New Testament — and what Iranian eschatological material is embedded in that transmission chain.
- **Question**: does the Enochic literature preserve evidence of ongoing Iranian eschatological influence in Second Temple Judaism, and does the "Son of Man" of 1 Enoch explain New Testament Christology better than the Gospels alone?
- **Scope**: the five books of 1 Enoch with primary focus on the *Book of Parables* (1 En. 37–71); the dating controversy; the Iranian substrate; the bridge to Paul and the Synoptics.

---

## 1. The Enochic literature: five books in one

"1 Enoch" is not a single unified composition. It is an **anthology of five originally independent works** attributed to the antediluvian patriarch Enoch (*Genesis 5:21–24*: "Enoch walked with God; then he was no more, because God took him" — the textual seed for an entire literary tradition of heavenly journeys by the one man who never died).

| Book | Chapters | Approximate date | Language of origin | Key content |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Book of Watchers** | 1–36 | ~3rd cent. BCE | Aramaic | Angels (*watchers*) descend, take human wives, produce giants; Enoch's heavenly tour; the binding of the watchers |
| **Book of Parables** (Similitudes) | 37–71 | ~1st cent. BCE–1st cent. CE (debated) | Probably Aramaic/Hebrew | The **Son of Man** / Elect One enthroned in heaven; universal judgment |
| **Book of Astronomical Writings** | 72–82 | ~3rd cent. BCE | Aramaic | Solar and lunar calendrical calculations; 364-day year |
| **Book of Dream Visions** | 83–90 | ~165 BCE | Aramaic | Animal Apocalypse: symbolic history from creation to Maccabean period |
| **Epistle of Enoch** | 91–108 | ~2nd–1st cent. BCE | Aramaic | Woes against the wicked rich; Apocalypse of Weeks |

Four of the five books are attested in **Aramaic at Qumran** (Dead Sea Scrolls). The **Book of Parables is absent from Qumran** — which is the central fact driving the dating controversy (see §4).

## 2. The Book of Parables (1 Enoch 37–71): what it says

The Parables are three visionary discourses in which Enoch is transported to the heavenly realm and sees:

- **The Elect One / Son of Man** seated on a throne of glory beside "the Head of Days" (= Ancient of Days of Daniel 7)
- This figure presiding over **universal judgment** of "the kings and the mighty" of the earth
- The **righteous** being vindicated and gathered
- The **wicked** — kings and rulers who exploited the poor — being overthrown and destroyed

### Key passages

> "And there I saw One who had a head of days, and His head was white like wool, and with Him was another being whose countenance had the appearance of a man, and his face was full of graciousness, like one of the holy angels. And I asked the angel who went with me and showed me all the hidden things, concerning that Son of Man, who he was, and whence he was, and why he went with the Head of Days?" — *1 Enoch 46:1–2* (tr. Charles)

> "And at that hour that Son of Man was named in the presence of the Lord of Spirits, and his name before the Head of Days. Yea, before the sun and the signs were created, before the stars of the heaven were made, his name was named before the Lord of Spirits. He shall be a staff to the righteous whereon to stay themselves and not fall, and he shall be the light of the Gentiles, and the hope of those who are troubled of heart." — *1 Enoch 48:2–4*

> "And he sat on the throne of his glory, and the sum of judgment was given unto the Son of Man, and he caused the sinners to pass away and be destroyed from off the face of the earth … And the righteous and elect shall be saved on that day, and they shall never thenceforward see the face of the sinners and unrighteous." — *1 Enoch 69:27–29*

### What the Parables add beyond Daniel

Daniel 7:13–14 introduces a figure "like a son of man" who *receives* dominion from the Ancient of Days — but this figure is not named, not described in detail, and not clearly pre-existent.

The Parables substantially develop this figure:

| Feature | Daniel 7 | 1 Enoch Parables |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-existence** | Implied at most | Explicitly pre-existent ("named before the sun and signs were created") |
| **Personal designation** | "One like a son of man" | "Son of Man" / "Elect One" / "Anointed One" (*meshiha*) — all three titles used |
| **Function** | Receives dominion from the Ancient of Days | Actively presides over universal judgment; destroys the wicked |
| **Relationship to the righteous** | Not specified | "Staff to the righteous," "hope of those troubled of heart," "light of the Gentiles" |
| **Identification with Enoch** | None | In 1 Enoch 71:14 Enoch himself is identified as the Son of Man — the visionary *becomes* the figure |

The Parables thus contain a **pre-existent, enthroned, judging Son of Man who is also the messianic Anointed One** — a christological profile that is, in several key respects, more developed than what the Synoptic Gospels present and strikingly close to Paul's cosmic Christ.

## 3. The Iranian substrate in 1 Enoch

The Enochic literature did not develop in an Iranian-free environment. Multiple features of the corpus reflect ongoing engagement with Iranian eschatological tradition:

### The Book of Watchers (1 En. 1–36)

The structural geography of the Watchers tradition — angels descend, corrupt the earth, are punished and bound; Enoch intercedes and tours the heavenly realm — has parallels in Zoroastrian angelological and cosmological traditions. More specifically:

- The **heavenly tour** genre (*Hekhalot*-style ascent through celestial levels) reflects a shared ancient Near Eastern and Iranian tradition of heavenly journeys and celestial geography
- The binding of the **Watchers** in a cosmic prison (*Tartaros*, 1 En. 10) parallels the binding of demonic forces in Zoroastrian cosmology

### The Parables' cosmic geography

The Parables describe a **multi-level heaven** with specific regions for the righteous dead, for cosmic winds and meteorological forces, and for the enthroned divine beings. This layered cosmic geography closely parallels both the Iranian concept of the *hamēstagān* (the zone between heaven and hell) and the seven-sphere cosmology that underlies both Iranian and later Jewish/Christian heavenly ascent traditions.

### Iranian eschatological vocabulary in the Parables

The figure of the **Elect One** (*behīdag* in later Pahlavi = "chosen one") is a title also applied to figures in the Zoroastrian eschatological tradition. The concept of a **pre-existent figure chosen by God before creation** to execute the final judgment resonates with the Saoshyant's pre-determination in the Avestan tradition (Yasht 13 and 19: the Saoshyant's seed is preserved since the beginning).

## 4. The dating controversy: why the Parables matter and why they are contested

The dating of the Parables of Enoch is one of the most consequential questions in the study of early Judaism and Christian origins.

### The Qumran problem

**The Parables are absent from the Dead Sea Scrolls.** At Qumran, Aramaic fragments of all four other books of 1 Enoch were found (Books of Watchers, Astronomical Writings, Dream Visions, Epistle). The Parables were not. This absence is significant because:

- If the Parables pre-date the Qumran community (~2nd cent. BCE), their absence from the library is puzzling and requires explanation
- Alternatively, they may have been composed *after* the Qumran library was sealed (after ~68 CE when the site was destroyed by Rome)

### The main scholarly positions

**Position A — pre-Christian composition (~1st cent. BCE)**:
- The Parables must predate Herod the Great (37–4 BCE) because they reflect conditions that do not map neatly onto the Herodian period
- The absence from Qumran may reflect the sectarian community's specific library choices rather than the text's non-existence
- J. T. Milik (who first noted the Qumran absence) argued for a late date, but his position has not achieved consensus
- Scholars including James VanderKam, Sabino Chialà, and George Nickelsburg argue for pre-Christian dating

**Position B — post-Christian composition (~1st cent. CE)**:
- If the Parables were composed *after* the Jesus movement, the Son of Man Christology in the NT may have *influenced* the Parables rather than the reverse
- The absence from Qumran is taken as strong evidence that the text was not available in the 1st cent. BCE

**Current consensus** (tentative): most scholars today favor a **1st century BCE–1st century CE** date, making the relationship with the NT Son of Man tradition ambiguous. The direction of influence cannot be definitively established. What *can* be established is that both the Parables and the Synoptic Gospels draw on a **shared Daniel-rooted tradition** of the heavenly Son of Man, regardless of which came first.

**For the site's argument**: the dating uncertainty weakens the claim that "1 Enoch's Son of Man is the specific source of NT Christology." It does not weaken the claim that "both texts draw on Iranian-influenced Second Temple Jewish eschatological tradition." The Parables are evidence of the *tradition pool*, not necessarily the *specific source*.

## 5. The bridge to Paul and the Synoptics

### The Synoptic Son of Man

Jesus in the Synoptic Gospels uses "Son of Man" (*huios tou anthrōpou*) as his primary self-designation in ~80 occurrences. Three distinct uses:
1. Earthly authority ("the Son of Man has authority to forgive sins" — Mark 2:10)
2. Suffering and death ("the Son of Man must suffer" — Mark 8:31)
3. **Eschatological coming** ("they will see the Son of Man coming in clouds with great power and glory" — Mark 13:26)

The eschatological use is a **near-verbatim echo of Daniel 7:13**: "one like a son of man coming with the clouds." Whether Jesus's use goes through the Enochic tradition specifically or draws directly on Daniel is debated. What is not debated: the Danielic tradition is the source.

### Paul's cosmic Christ

Paul does not use "Son of Man" as a title. But his *concept* of Christ maps closely onto the Enochic figure:
- Pre-existent before creation (Phil. 2:6; Col. 1:15–17: "firstborn of all creation ... all things were created through him")
- Enthroned at the right hand of God after ascension (Phil. 2:9–11; 1 Cor. 15:25)
- Agent of universal final judgment (2 Cor. 5:10: "we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ")
- "Light of the world" / solar associations (2 Cor. 4:6; see [Paul's celestial Christology](paul-celestial-christology.md))

The trajectory **Daniel 7 → Enochic Son of Man/Parables → Paul's cosmic Christ** is the most coherent single chain explaining the development of high Christology without invoking direct Hellenistic borrowing. Each step in the chain is rooted in Jewish apocalyptic tradition that itself reflects ongoing engagement with Iranian eschatological concepts.

### The *Logos* connection

John's Gospel replaces "Son of Man" with "Logos" — a Greek philosophical term that does different work but maps onto the same pre-existent, cosmologically active figure (see [Logos doctrine and Neoplatonic Christology](logos-doctrine-neoplatonic-christology.md)). The fact that the same *functional* profile (pre-existent, mediating, judging, light-bringing) can be expressed in both Aramaic apocalyptic *and* Platonic philosophical vocabulary demonstrates that it was a **conceptual inheritance** flexible enough to be translated across cultural registers — which is exactly what the [Septuagint transmission mechanism](septuagint-transmission-mechanism.md) made possible.
