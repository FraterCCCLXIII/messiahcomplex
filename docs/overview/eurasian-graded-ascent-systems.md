# Eurasian graded-ascent systems: a comparative map

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers tracing the structural parallel between the Mithraic seven grades, the Hekhalot seven halls, the Indian seven chakras, and other multi-level traversal systems across the Eurasian world.
- **Question:** Are these systems related historically, or do they converge independently? And crucially: are they all doing the *same thing*? Distinguishing the kinds of practice encoded in each system is as important as noting their shared meta-structure.
- **Scope:** ~25 traditions arranged by practice type. Each entry includes the system, its practice type, level-count, obstacle per level, method, goal, earliest surviving textual witness, source corpus, and first attested date. The page concludes with an argument for why both documented diffusion and convergent development are operative and need to be distinguished. Tier labels follow the site's [standard method](method-evidence-and-influence.md).

---

## The universal traversal meta-structure

Before surveying individual systems, the shared architecture they all instantiate:

| Archetype | Levels | Obstacles | Method | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Universal traversal technology | Layered cosmos or body | Guardians, knots, moral failures, ignorance | Ritual / discipline / meditation / knowledge | Unity with source / liberation / deification |

Four invariants appear across every tradition surveyed here:

1. **Cosmic stratification** — reality is envisioned as layered, with discrete levels that are qualitatively different, not merely distant
2. **Obstacle typology** — each layer has a characteristic threshold or guardian that the traverser must pass
3. **Traversal protocol** — a specific disciplined technology (ritual, meditation, name-recitation, moral development) is required at each stage
4. **Transformative teleology** — the traverser becomes divine, liberated, or reborn; the journey changes the subject, not merely the location

The crucial observation is that these four invariants appear in traditions with no documented contact (e.g., Egyptian Amduat and Tibetan *bhumi* path), which requires either a deep common cultural substrate or genuine cross-cultural convergence arising from the structure of human contemplative experience.

---

## Practice-type categories

The systems surveyed here are not all doing the same thing. Collapsing them into a single "graded ascent" category obscures important distinctions. This page uses five practice-type categories:

- **Ritual / initiatory** — graded initiation by external rites, oaths, and ordeal; progress measured by the community (Mithraic, Eleusinian, Isiac, Orphic, Samothracian, Pythagorean, Essene, Dionysian, Manichaean, Valentinian)
- **Meditative / yogic / psycho-physical** — internal practice on the body-as-cosmos; progress measured by inner states (agnicayana, chakra/kundalini, Daoist neidan, Tibetan *bhumi*)
- **Visionary / cosmological** — ascent through cosmological levels in trance, death, or divine journey; progress measured by where you go (Hekhalot, Hermetic, Egyptian Amduat, Shamanic world-tree, Mandaean *matarta*, Muhammad's Mi'raj, Zoroastrian Arda Viraf)
- **Moral / ascetic progression** — advance through purification of character; progress measured by virtue (Sufi *maqamat*, Christian Ladder of Divine Ascent, Platonic ascent, Kabbalistic *devekut*)
- **Allegorical / literary** — narrative encoding of the ascent archetype; progress is the reader's interpretation (Dante's *Commedia*)

### Egyptian Amduat

**Practice type:** Visionary / cosmological

**Levels:** 12 hours of the night (12 sequential chambers of the underworld)

**Obstacle per level:** Serpents, hostile beings, and gated passages at each hour; the sun-god Ra encounters guardians who must be correctly named

**Method:** Correct recitation of spells and ritual formulae; royal knowledge of the guardians' names grants passage

**Goal:** The sun-god Ra's regeneration at dawn; the deceased king's rebirth with Ra; solar renewal of the cosmos

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** *Amduat* ("That Which is in the Underworld"), tomb of Thutmose III, Valley of the Kings, Egypt

**Source corpus:** Royal funerary literature; New Kingdom *Amduat* (Hornung ed.; Abitz and Elias eds.)

**Genre:** Illustrated cosmological narrative in a royal tomb; secret knowledge; priestly/royal transmission

**First attested date:** c. 1479–1458 BCE (Thutmose III's reign; earliest complete *Amduat* copy)

**Notes on certainty:** The oldest structured gated-passage + hostile-being + correct-speech system on record. The twelve-hour structure is well-dated. Tier A for the Egyptian context.

---

### Vedic agnicayana

**Practice type:** Ritual / meditative (boundary case — external ritual with internal cosmological logic)

**Levels:** 5 altar layers built from earth upward, each corresponding to a cosmic level; 365 bricks = days of the year; 10,800 bricks = muhurtas in a year

**Obstacle per level:** Ritual constraints and required knowledge; incorrect brick-laying is cosmologically dangerous

**Method:** Precise brick-by-brick fire-altar construction, replicating the dismembered body of Prajāpati; recitation of mantras and geometry from the *Śulba Sūtras*

**Goal:** Ritual reconstitution of the creator Prajāpati / becoming Prajāpati / cosmic totality; fire ritual as the mechanism for transformation

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Baudhāyana *Śulba Sūtras* and Āpastamba *Śulba Sūtras*; *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* 10.4.2–3

**Source corpus:** Vedic śrauta ritual literature / ritual geometry

**Genre:** Ritual manual with theological commentary

**First attested date:** c. 800 BCE for extant *Śulba Sūtras*; ritual system c. 10th cent. BCE (practice predates surviving texts)

**Notes on certainty:** Directly documented five-layer cosmological structure. The body-reconstitution theology is explicit in the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa*. Tier A.

---

### Orphic / Bacchic mysteries

**Practice type:** Ritual / initiatory

**Levels:** Three tiers: uninitiated → *mystēs* (initiated) → full *bakkhos* (completely initiated)

**Obstacle per level:** Guardians at the underworld threshold; gold tablets served as afterlife-passport scripts, with specific statements required for passage; the lake of memory vs. the lake of forgetfulness as the key threshold test

**Method:** Initiation ritual; fasting; possession of correctly worded gold tablet; knowledge of the correct afterlife-path

**Goal:** Escape the cycle of rebirth; become divine; reunite with Dionysus or the pure divine realm

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Derveni Papyrus (c. 4th cent. BCE; oldest surviving European book); Pelinna, Thurii, Hipponion gold tablets (5th–3rd cent. BCE)

**Source corpus:** Orphic gold tablets (*lamellae aureae*); Derveni Papyrus; Pindar; Plato *Meno* 81b

**Genre:** Ritual instruction texts (gold tablets carried by the initiate into burial); philosophical commentary (Derveni)

**First attested date:** c. 450–250 BCE for existing tablets; Orphic tradition older (Pythagoras encountered Orphism in the 6th cent. BCE)

**Notes on certainty:** The gold tablets are physical objects with datable archaeological contexts. Their text is the actual afterlife-passport script. Tier A.

---

### Eleusinian Mysteries

**Practice type:** Ritual / initiatory

**Levels:** Three tiers: Lesser Mysteries (*myesis*) → Greater Mysteries (*myesis* second level) → *epopteia* (vision-seeing); a full year's wait was required between the latter two stages

**Obstacle per level:** Fasting (*nesteia*); consumption of the *kykeon* (barley-water drink, possibly with psychoactive additives); nocturnal revelation in the Telesterion at Eleusis; the content of the final *epopteia* was never directly disclosed in surviving texts

**Method:** Ritual purity; participation in the sacred drama (the search of Demeter for Persephone); ultimately a visual revelation (*deiknymena*, "the things shown")

**Goal:** Blessedness in the afterlife; union with Demeter and Persephone; escape from the ordinary human condition

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Homeric Hymn to Demeter (c. 650 BCE); mysteries independently attested from c. 1450 BCE at Eleusis

**Source corpus:** Homeric Hymn to Demeter; Plato *Meno* 81b; *Phaedrus* 249c; Pausanias 1.38; Cicero *De Legibus* 2.14.36

**Genre:** Hymn; philosophical dialogue; travel writing; legal/religious commentary

**First attested date:** c. 1450 BCE (Mycenaean-era attestation at Eleusis); major classical sources c. 650–380 BCE

**Notes on certainty:** The content of *epopteia* was never disclosed; all sources are external or indirect. The graded structure is well-documented. Tier A for the structure; Tier B for the content of the final vision.

---

### Pythagorean brotherhood

**Practice type:** Ritual / moral (boundary case)

**Levels:** 2–3 grades: *akousmatikoi* (hearers) → *mathematikoi* (learners / mathematicians) [→ *physikoi* (natural philosophers) in some sources]

**Obstacle per level:** A five-year silence vow at entry; strict dietary prohibitions; communal meals as purity boundaries; mathematical and philosophical competence thresholds

**Method:** Ascetic discipline; mathematical study; communal life under strict rules; contemplation of number as divine reality

**Goal:** Purification and transmigration guidance; escape from rebirth through alignment with divine mathematical order

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Iamblichus, *De Vita Pythagorica* 72–80; Porphyry, *Life of Pythagoras* 37

**Source corpus:** Neopythagorean biographical literature

**Genre:** Philosophical biography / hagiography

**First attested date:** c. 300 CE for Iamblichus and Porphyry; subject matter c. 530 BCE (Pythagoras's community at Croton)

**Notes on certainty:** The grading details may reflect later Neopythagorean elaboration rather than 6th-cent. practice. The broad outlines (novitiate period, silence vow, dietary rules) are plausibly historical. Tier B.

---

### Platonic ascent

**Practice type:** Moral / contemplative

**Levels:** Successive stages from particular beautiful things → all beautiful bodies → beautiful souls → activities and sciences → Beauty Itself; the *Republic* encodes 10 stages of political-philosophical education

**Obstacle per level:** Ignorance; attachment to appearances (the cave allegory); inability to withstand the higher light; failure of dialectical reasoning

**Method:** Dialectic; contemplation of the Forms; directed erotic ascent (*eros* toward Beauty); eventually philosophical argument that goes beyond argument

**Goal:** Vision of the Good (*Republic*) / vision of Beauty Itself (*Symposium* 210a–212a); becoming akin to the divine through *theoria*

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Plato, *Republic* 514a–521b (cave allegory; philosopher's return); *Symposium* 210a–212a (Diotima's ladder)

**Source corpus:** Platonic dialogues

**Genre:** Philosophical dialogue

**First attested date:** c. 380–370 BCE

**Notes on certainty:** Philosophical rather than ritual; became the template for Neoplatonic theurgy and Hermetic ascent. The stages are described differently in different dialogues. Tier A for the texts; the precise stage-count varies by dialogue.

---

### Zoroastrian soul journey (Chinvat Bridge / Ardā Wirāz)

**Practice type:** Visionary / cosmological

**Levels:** Judgment at the Chinvat Bridge + heavenly levels (three heavens: good thoughts, good words, good deeds → Garothman paradise) or hellish descent (three hells) + limbo (*Misvan Gatu*)

**Obstacle per level:** Demons at the Chinvat Bridge; moral judgment; the bridge widens for the righteous and narrows to a blade for the unrighteous

**Method:** Righteous conduct, ritual purity, good thoughts/words/deeds in life; correct priestly knowledge

**Goal:** Garothman (highest heaven); union with Ahura Mazda in paradise; cosmic resurrection at the Frashkart (final renovation)

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** *Ardā Wirāz Nāmag* (Book of the Righteous Viraf); Pahlavi visionary narrative, c. 9th–10th cent. CE in current form

**Source corpus:** Avestan Gathas (Yasna 46 — Chinvat Bridge mentioned); *Ardā Wirāz Nāmag*; *Dinkard*; Vendidad

**Genre:** Avestan liturgical poetry (earliest Chinvat reference); Pahlavi visionary narrative (fully developed account)

**First attested date:** Avestan roots c. 1000–500 BCE (Gathas); fully developed visionary account c. 9th–10th cent. CE

**Notes on certainty:** The textual layers must be dated carefully. The Chinvat Bridge concept is Avestan and much older; the visionary narrative of Ardā Wirāz is Sasanian/early Islamic era. The basic judgment-and-heaven/hell structure is among the most ancient in Eurasia. Tier A for the Avestan bridge motif; Tier B for the full seven-heaven narrative.

### Samothracian Mysteries

**Practice type:** Ritual / initiatory

**Levels:** Two grades: *myesis* (initiation) → *epopteia* (vision-seeing)

**Obstacle per level:** Oath of secrecy; confession of the worst deed committed (unique among mystery cults)

**Method:** Purification through confession; nocturnal initiation ritual on the island of Samothrace; identity of the Kabeiroi (the gods of the mysteries) kept secret

**Goal:** Protection from shipwreck; special afterlife status; divine protection

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Diodorus Siculus 5.48–49; Livy 45.5 (describing Philip V's initiation)

**Source corpus:** Greek historians

**Genre:** Historical narrative

**First attested date:** Mysteries attested from c. 700 BCE; Diodorus writing c. 60–30 BCE

**Notes on certainty:** The content of the mysteries is poorly documented. The confession requirement is unusual and well-attested. Tier A for the structure; Tier B for the content.

---

### Dionysian *thiasoi*

**Practice type:** Ritual / initiatory (ecstatic)

**Levels:** Loose progression: neophyte → *mystēs* → ecstatic *bakkhos*; communal meals and shared nocturnal rites as thresholds

**Obstacle per level:** Communal meal; nocturnal dancing; possession-ecstasy; formal oath of membership in some contexts

**Method:** Communal feast (possibly including raw meat consumption symbolically); wine; nocturnal dance; music; mask-wearing as transformation

**Goal:** Union with Dionysus; escape from ordinary consciousness; afterlife blessing

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Euripides, *Bacchae* (405 BCE); Livy 39.8–19 (Bacchanalia scandal in Rome, 186 BCE)

**Source corpus:** Greek tragedy; Roman historical narrative; Orphic gold tablets (overlap with Orphic Dionysian tradition)

**Genre:** Tragic drama; Roman historical narrative

**First attested date:** c. 405 BCE (Euripides); the cult practice is older

**Notes on certainty:** The Roman Senate's suppression of the Bacchanalia (186 BCE) is the best-documented historical episode; Livy's account is a primary source. Tier A for the general structure; Tier C for any specific staged-initiation sequence.

### Essene community

**Practice type:** Ritual / moral (structured community)

**Levels:** Four formal grades; probationary periods of one year (entry) and two years (before water-purification); formal admission at each stage

**Obstacle per level:** Communal meals as purity thresholds (admission to the communal meal is a staged honor); solemn oaths at each formal admission; behavioral assessment by the community; graduated access to the community's books and secrets

**Method:** Communal purity, dietary discipline, study, shared property; ritual immersion (*masbuta*-type bathing)

**Goal:** Holiness; covenant with the Teacher of Righteousness / the Sons of Light; final eschatological battle and reward

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Josephus, *Jewish War* 2.137–142; Dead Sea Scrolls *Community Rule* (1QS)

**Source corpus:** 1QS (c. 1st cent. BCE physical copy; composition earlier); Josephus *Jewish War* (c. 75 CE); Philo *Every Good Man is Free* 75–91

**Genre:** Community rule / legal text; Hellenistic Jewish apologetic; scroll fragments

**First attested date:** 1QS c. 1st cent. BCE; subject matter c. 2nd cent. BCE

**Notes on certainty:** Josephus's four-grade account closely matches 1QS's staged admission process. This is among the best-documented multi-grade initiation systems in Second Temple Judaism. Tier A.

### Shamanic world-tree ascent

**Practice type:** Visionary / cosmological

**Levels:** 3 or 7 cosmic worlds (lower, middle, upper; or seven-level variants in Turkic/Mongolian traditions)

**Obstacle per level:** Spirits and guardians at each level; hostile beings in the lower world; the shaman must know the correct spirit allies and ritual protocols

**Method:** Ecstatic trance induced by drumming, chanting, or plant medicines; spirit flight; the shaman's soul travels while the body remains

**Goal:** Healing; retrieval of lost souls; access to cosmic information; contact with the Sky Father or cosmic center

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** No single text; pan-Eurasian comparative ethnography documented systematically by Mircea Eliade (*Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, 1951/1964)

**Source corpus:** Comparative ethnography; Upper Paleolithic rock art (Lascaux, c. 15,000 BCE — possibly consistent with shamanic practice)

**Genre:** Ethnographic comparative study; archaeological inference

**First attested date:** Pan-Eurasian; Upper Paleolithic archaeological context is plausible for some elements; documented historical ethnography from c. 16th cent. CE onward

**Notes on certainty:** "Shamanism" is a typological category, not a single tradition. The structural elements (three/seven-world cosmos, ecstatic travel, spirit guardians) are attested across Eurasia from Siberia to the Americas. Eliade's framework has been critiqued for over-generalization. Tier B for the broad structural pattern; Tier A for specific documented traditions.

### Mithraic Mysteries

**Practice type:** Ritual / initiatory

**Levels:** Seven grades: *Corax* (Raven) → *Nymphus* (Male Bride) → *Miles* (Soldier) → *Leo* (Lion) → *Perses* (Persian) → *Heliodromus* (Sun-runner) → *Pater* (Father)

**Obstacle per level:** Grade-specific initiation ordeal; oaths; the grade corresponds to a planetary sphere (Corax=Mercury, Nymphus=Venus, Miles=Mars, Leo=Jupiter, Perses=Moon, Heliodromus=Sun, Pater=Saturn); the initiate underwent ritual ordeals corresponding to the nature of that planet

**Method:** Tauroctony (the sacred bull-slaying as the initiatory cosmological act); sacred meal of bread and wine; initiation ordeal; planetary-sphere cosmological knowledge

**Goal:** Cosmic ascent through the seven planetary spheres; identification with the *Pater*; post-mortem celestial ascent through all seven spheres to the eighth sphere

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Porphyry, *De Abstinentia* 4.16; Origen, *Contra Celsum* 6.22; Felicissimus mithraeum mosaic (Ostia, c. 3rd cent. CE); Celsus's ladder diagram (reported by Origen, c. 178 CE)

**Source corpus:** Anti-pagan apologetic (Origen, Tertullian); Neoplatonic philosophical writing (Porphyry); mithraeum inscriptions and mosaics

**Genre:** External polemical description; philosophical writing; material evidence (mithraea)

**First attested date:** Grade system clearest from 3rd-cent. CE monuments; cult active c. 1st–4th cent. CE

**Notes on certainty:** No Mithraic internal text describes the grades from within; all grade descriptions are external. The Felicissimus mosaic is the primary physical evidence. Roger Beck (*The Religion of the Mithras Cult in the Roman Empire*, Oxford 2006) and Manfred Clauss (*The Roman Cult of Mithras*, 2000) are the standard modern references. Tier A for the seven-grade structure; Tier B for the specific initiation ordeals.

The Mithraic system is the closest structural match to Hekhalot: both have exactly **7 levels** with a **planetary-sphere cosmology**, and both require the initiate to demonstrate correct knowledge at each stage (passwords / grade ordeal).

### Hermetic planetary ascent

**Practice type:** Visionary / cosmological

**Levels:** Seven planetary spheres (*Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn*) + the Ogdoad (eighth sphere of fixed stars) + the Ennead (ninth, divine sphere)

**Obstacle per level:** At each sphere, the soul on its descent acquired a characteristic vice from the planetary archon; on the soul's post-mortem ascent, it surrenders each vice back to the sphere that gave it (Moon: capacity for growth/decrease; Mercury: evil cunning; Venus: concupiscence; Sun: ruling impulse; Mars: rashness and foolhardiness; Jupiter: greed for wealth; Saturn: falsehood and guile)

**Method:** *Gnosis* — self-knowledge as divine intellect; the soul's recognition of its divine origin enables the ascent; knowledge of the planetary archons' names and nature

**Goal:** Union with the *Nous* (divine mind); becoming divine; the soul absorbed into the God of the Ogdoad

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** *Corpus Hermeticum* I (*Poimandres*), XIII; Nag Hammadi Codex VI

**Source corpus:** Greco-Egyptian philosophical-religious treatises (*Corpus Hermeticum*); Nag Hammadi library

**Genre:** Philosophical-religious revelation dialogue; wisdom literature

**First attested date:** c. 1st–3rd cent. CE for *Corpus Hermeticum* tractates; individual dates within the corpus are disputed

**Notes on certainty:** Individual dates within the Corpus are debated; the *Poimandres* (*CH* I) may be as early as the 1st cent. CE. The seven-sphere descent/ascent is the most explicit ancient statement of the idea that the soul acquires and must surrender its defects to planetary powers. Tier A for the Hermetic context.

### Merkabah / Hekhalot

**Practice type:** Visionary / cosmological

**Levels:** 7 heavenly *hekhalot* (palaces / halls)

**Obstacle per level:** Angelic gatekeepers at each hall requiring the correct *ḥotam* (seal) and divine names; failure means destruction or expulsion

**Method:** Divine name recitation; fasting; liturgical hymns (*piyyutim*); the practice of "descending to the chariot" (*yored ba-merkavah*) — paradoxically named, since the practitioner ascends

**Goal:** Throne-chariot vision of the divine in the seventh hall; the *kavod* (divine glory); access to divine secrets

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** *Hekhalot Zutarti* (earlier stratum, Schäfer *Synopse* §335–426); *Hekhalot Rabbati* (Schäfer *Synopse* §81–306); *3 Enoch / Sefer Hekhalot* (Schäfer *Synopse* §1–80); *Ma'aseh Merkavah*

**Source corpus:** Jewish mystical treatises (Hekhalot literature); foundational vision in Ezekiel 1 (c. 593 BCE)

**Genre:** Jewish mystical treatises with liturgical sections

**First attested date:** c. 3rd–7th cent. CE for extant texts; Ezekiel 1 the foundational vision (c. 593 BCE); *Songs of the Sabbath Sacrifice* (4Q400–407, c. 200–50 BCE) as earliest liturgical Merkabah practice

**Notes on certainty:** Antecedent texts (Ezekiel, 1 Enoch, Songs of the Sabbath Sacrifice) push the practice earlier than the Hekhalot texts proper. Scholem (*Jewish Gnosticism, Merkabah Mysticism, and Talmudic Tradition*, 1960) and Halperin (*The Faces of the Chariot*, 1988) are the standard references. Tier A.

See also: [Merkabah mysticism](merkabah-mysticism.md) and [Mandaean stauros and the pre-Christian seven-heaven system](mandaean-stauros-and-merkabah-origins.md).

### Isiac Mysteries

**Practice type:** Ritual / initiatory

**Levels:** Three sequential initiations: Isis → Osiris → unnamed third deity; each separated by a period of preparation

**Obstacle per level:** Fasting; shaving of the head; nocturnal vigil; symbolic death-and-rebirth enacted between each major initiation

**Method:** Ritual purification; symbolic immersion; processing through stages of divine identification; the final initiation involved seeing the sun shining at midnight — a vision of divine light

**Goal:** Rebirth as Osiris; deification; post-mortem survival through divine identification

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Apuleius, *Metamorphoses* (= *The Golden Ass*), Book 11.21–28

**Source corpus:** Ancient novel / religious narrative (Apuleius)

**Genre:** Novel with embedded religious initiation narrative; the most detailed ancient description of mystery-cult initiation

**First attested date:** c. 160 CE (Apuleius); cult practice from c. 3rd cent. BCE in Hellenistic Egypt

**Notes on certainty:** Apuleius is the only detailed account. He deliberately withholds the content of the visions. The three-initiation structure is well-described. Tier A for the structure; Tier B for the specific ritual content.

### Valentinian Gnostic church

**Practice type:** Moral / ontological

**Levels:** Three grades: *hylics* (material people, capable only of matter) → *psychics* (soul-people, capable of some spiritual growth) → *pneumatics* (spiritual people, carrying a divine spark)

**Obstacle per level:** Not a passage to be traversed but an ontological category of being, though *pneumatics* still require *gnosis* to achieve full realization

**Method:** *Gnosis* — saving knowledge of the divine Pleroma, one's divine origin, the cosmic fall, and the Savior's redemptive action; participation in the sacramental life of the Valentinian church

**Goal:** The *pneumatics*' return to the divine Pleroma; the *psychics*' lower salvation; the *hylics*' dissolution into matter

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Irenaeus of Lyon, *Against Heresies* 1.6.1–4 (c. 180 CE); Nag Hammadi *Gospel of Philip*; *Gospel of Truth*

**Source corpus:** Polemical Christian heresiological writing; Nag Hammadi library (Valentinian self-description)

**Genre:** Theological polemic (external); mystical speculation and Gospel commentary (internal)

**First attested date:** c. 180 CE (Irenaeus); Valentinus active c. 140–160 CE in Rome

**Notes on certainty:** The ontological nature of the grades makes this different from initiatory systems: you do not progress through the grades by effort alone. Tier A for the three-category structure.

### Manichaean community

**Practice type:** Moral / ritual

**Levels:** Two grades: *Hearers* (*auditores* / *katechoumeni*) → *Elect* (*electi* / *perfecti*)

**Obstacle per level:** A strict ethical and ascetic threshold: the Elect ate no meat, had no sexual relations, owned nothing; the Hearers provided for the Elect in hopes of future promotion

**Method:** Ethical purity; asceticism; service to the Elect; knowledge of Manichaean cosmology and light-particle theology

**Goal:** The Elect: liberation of the divine light-particles in this life; immediate post-mortem return to the realm of light. The Hearers: eventual rebirth as Elect members in a future life

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Augustine of Hippo, *Confessions* 3.6 (c. 400 CE, on his own Manichean period); Coptic *Kephalaia* (sayings of Mani); Al-Biruni's account

**Source corpus:** Patristic polemic; Coptic Manichaean texts; Islamic scholarly accounts

**Genre:** Autobiography (Augustine); dogmatic instruction text (*Kephalaia*); Islamic encyclopedism

**First attested date:** c. 400 CE (Augustine); subject matter c. 240–276 CE (Mani's lifetime)

**Notes on certainty:** Mani (c. 216–276 CE) founded the religion in Babylonia. The two-tier structure is among the most clearly documented in any ancient religious tradition. Tier A.

### Mandaean matarta ascent

**Practice type:** Visionary / cosmological

**Levels:** Seven *matartā* (watch-houses / guard-stations) through which the soul passes in the post-death ascent

**Obstacle per level:** Named angelic wardens (*Uthra*) requiring passwords demonstrating the soul's purity and knowledge; each watch-house corresponds to a planetary sphere

**Method:** *Masbuta* (baptism in flowing water, called *yardna* = "Jordan") as repeated in-life rehearsal of the post-death ascent; recitation of the correct liturgical texts; moral purity

**Goal:** The Lightworld (*Alma d-Nhura*) — the divine realm of pure light from which souls originate and to which they return

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** *Ginzā Rabbā* (The Great Treasure; Left Ginza = the soul's ascent), *Qolasta* (liturgical hymns), *Drāšā d-Yahyā* (Book of John the Baptist)

**Source corpus:** Mandaic Aramaic liturgical-cosmological texts

**Genre:** Cosmological treatise; liturgical hymn-book; apostolic discourse

**First attested date:** Manuscripts medieval; community origins debated (possibly pre-70 CE in Jordan Valley / lower Mesopotamia)

**Notes on certainty:** The structural parallel with Hekhalot is the closest outside Jewish tradition: both have seven levels with named angelic gatekeepers requiring correct demonstration at each stage. Direct Hekhalot influence OR common Babylonian source are both plausible. See: [Mandaean stauros and the pre-Christian seven-heaven system](mandaean-stauros-and-merkabah-origins.md). Tier A for the Mandaean structure; Tier B for dating.

### Kabbalistic ascent (Sefirotic / Four Worlds)

**Practice type:** Moral / meditative

**Levels:** 10 *sefirot* in 4 worlds (*Atzilut* = Emanation; *Beriah* = Creation; *Yetzirah* = Formation; *Assiyah* = Action); the soul ascends through all four worlds toward Ein Sof

**Obstacle per level:** *Qliphoth* (shells of impurity); spiritual distraction; moral impurity; the three "veils" that precede Keter

**Method:** Divine name meditation; *teshuvah* (repentance/return); study of Torah; prayer with intention (*kavvanah*)

**Goal:** *Devekut* — clinging-union with God; the soul's ascent to its divine source in Ein Sof

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** *Sefer Yetzirah* (c. 3rd–6th cent. CE); *Sefer Bahir* (c. 12th cent.); *Zohar* (c. 1280 CE); Lurianic *Etz Chayyim* (c. 1572 CE)

**Source corpus:** Kabbalistic mystical literature; Lurianic system

**Genre:** Speculative mystical treatise; mystical Bible commentary

**First attested date:** *Sefer Yetzirah* c. 3rd–6th cent. CE; *Bahir* c. 1150–1200 CE; *Zohar* c. 1280 CE; Lurianic system c. 1570s

**Notes on certainty:** Distinct from the Hekhalot palace-ascent: theosophical/structural rather than visionary. The Sefirotic diagram's Mesopotamian antecedent is argued by Parpola — see [Parpola page](assyrian-tree-sefirot-parpola.md). Tier A for the Kabbalistic context.

### Tibetan ten *bhumi* (bodhisattva path)

**Practice type:** Meditative / moral

**Levels:** Ten stages of the bodhisattva path, from the first *bhumi* (Joyful) to the tenth (Dharma-Cloud), each with increasing degrees of realization and compassionate activity

**Obstacle per level:** Subtle *āvaraṇa* (cognitive and affective obscurations) at each stage; the bodhisattva must penetrate deeper levels of emptiness at each *bhumi*

**Method:** Combined cultivation of wisdom (*prajñā*) and compassion (*karuṇā*); the *pāramitā* (perfection) specific to each stage; sustained meditative practice across many lifetimes

**Goal:** Full Buddhahood; liberation for all sentient beings

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** *Daśabhūmika Sūtra* (Ten-Stage Sutra)

**Source corpus:** Mahāyāna sūtra literature

**Genre:** Sūtra (buddhavacana / "word of the Buddha" in the Mahāyāna tradition)

**First attested date:** Sanskrit composition c. 2nd–4th cent. CE; Kumārajīva's Chinese translation c. 406 CE

**Notes on certainty:** Well-documented. The ten-stage structure became standard in East Asian and Tibetan Buddhism. Tier A.

### Chakra / kundalini system

**Practice type:** Meditative / psycho-physical

**Levels:** 7 chakras from *mūlādhāra* (base/earth) to *sahasrāra* (crown/space); 3 *granthis* (psychic knots at root, heart, and third-eye that must be pierced)

**Obstacle per level:** Specific energetic blockages, patterns, and obscurations at each chakra; the three *granthis* are the major obstacles

**Method:** *Prāṇāyāma* (breath control); *bandhas* (physical locks: mūla-bandha, uḍḍīyāna-bandha, jālandhara-bandha); visualization; mantra; *śaktipāta* (transmission from guru)

**Goal:** Kundalini Shakti rising from the base to meet Shiva at the *sahasrāra*; non-dual awareness; liberation (*moksha*)

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Explicit 7-chakra maps: *Gorakshashatakam* (Gorakshanatha, c. 11th–12th cent. CE); *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* (Svātmārāma, c. 1450 CE)

**Source corpus:** Nātha siddha tradition; Hatha Yoga literature; Kashmir Śaivism (*Tantrāloka*, Abhinavagupta, c. 975–1025 CE)

**Genre:** Yogic manual; mystical-philosophical synthesis

**First attested date:** Chakra precursors in Tantric texts from c. 6th–10th cent. CE; full 7-chakra system c. 11th–15th cent. CE

**Notes on certainty:** The *Katha Upanishad* (c. 400 BCE) names the central channel (sushumna); the five-tattva architectural stupa (c. 300 BCE) encodes the five lower chakras. The explicit seven-chakra system is medieval. Tier A for the Tantric context.

### Muhammad's Mi'raj

**Practice type:** Visionary / cosmological

**Levels:** 7 heavens, each with a different prophet-greeter: Adam (1st), Jesus and John (2nd), Joseph (3rd), Idris/Enoch (4th), Aaron (5th), Moses (6th), Abraham (7th) + the Lote Tree of the uttermost boundary (*Sidrat al-Muntahā*)

**Obstacle per level:** Angelic gatekeepers; angelic vetting of Muhammad's identity and mission at each level; Moses negotiates the reduction of daily prayers from 50 to 5

**Method:** Gabriel as guide; the *burāq* (heavenly steed) as transport; divine selection

**Goal:** Receiving the divine commandment (five daily prayers); divine encounter at the Lote Tree; confirmation of prophetic mission

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Quran 17:1 (*isrā'* — the night journey from Mecca to Jerusalem); *Ṣaḥīḥ Bukhārī* 5.58.227 (Mi'raj narrative)

**Source corpus:** Quran; *hadīth* (Bukhārī, Muslim); Ibn Isḥāq *Sīra* (c. 767 CE)

**Genre:** Quranic verse (isrā'); hadith narrative (mi'raj); prophetic biography

**First attested date:** Quran 7th cent. CE; seven-heaven itinerary is hadith-level tradition, not Quranic; written narrative layers in the 2nd cent. AH / 8th cent. CE

**Notes on certainty:** The seven-heaven cosmological structure reflects the same Jewish/Zoroastrian/Mesopotamian framework used in Hekhalot and Mandaean sources. Structural parallels strong. Tier B for direct Hekhalot influence; Tier A for the common apocalyptic Jewish-cosmological vocabulary.

### Sufi *maqāmāt* (stations)

**Practice type:** Moral / ascetic

**Levels:** 7 stations: *tawba* (repentance) → *zuhd* (abstinence) → *tawakkul* (trust in God) → *qanā'a* (contentment) → *'uzla* (withdrawal) → *dhikr* (remembrance) → *fanā'* (annihilation in God)

**Obstacle per level:** The *nafs* (self/ego) in its characteristic manifestation at each station; desire and attachment that must be surrendered

**Method:** *Dhikr* (repeated divine name invocation); *murāqaba* (meditation/watchfulness); obedience to the *shaykh*; service

**Goal:** *Fanā' fī Allāh* — annihilation of the ego in God; divine union; *baqā'* (subsistence in God after fanā')

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Al-Sarrāj, *Kitāb al-Luma'* (c. 988 CE); Al-Qushayrī, *Risāla* (c. 1046 CE)

**Source corpus:** Classical Sufi manuals; institutional path (*tariqa*) literature

**Genre:** Mystical-ethical treatise

**First attested date:** Concept of stations attested in 8th-cent. figures (Hasan al-Basri, Rābi'a al-'Adawiyya); explicit seven-station systematization 9th–11th cent. CE

**Notes on certainty:** The stations system is one of the most thoroughly documented medieval Islamic mystical structures. Tier A.

### Daoist neidan (internal alchemy / microcosmic orbit)

**Practice type:** Meditative / psycho-physical

**Levels:** Three *dantian* (cinnabar fields: lower/belly, middle/heart, upper/crown) through which *qi* is circulated; the full microcosmic orbit traverses the *Du Mai* (ascending spine) and *Ren Mai* (descending front)

**Obstacle per level:** Internal alchemical blockages; stagnant *qi*; the refining of coarse *jing* (essence) into *qi* into *shen* (spirit) is the three-stage internal alchemy

**Method:** Meditative *qi* circulation; *qigong*; visualization of the inner channels; *neidan* (internal alchemy) techniques

**Goal:** Crystallization of the "immortal embryo" (*yangsheng*); reunion with the Dao; immortality

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Wei Boyang, *Cantong Qi* (c. 150 CE); *Huangdi Neijing* (c. 200 BCE for outer alchemy context); *The Secret of the Golden Flower* (*Taiyi Jinhua Zongzhi*, c. 17th–18th cent. CE in current form)

**Source corpus:** Daoist alchemical literature; inner cultivation (*neiye*) tradition

**Genre:** Alchemical treatise; contemplative manual

**First attested date:** *Cantong Qi* c. 150 CE; microcosmic orbit practice older than surviving text evidence; roots in *Huangdi Neijing* tradition (c. 200 BCE)

**Notes on certainty:** The *Du Mai* (spinal ascent channel = kundalini structural equivalent) is the most direct non-Indian parallel to the kundalini system. Independent development is Tier A; causal connection to Indian systems is Tier C. Tier A for the Chinese context.

### Christian Ladder of Divine Ascent

**Practice type:** Moral / ascetic

**Levels:** 30 rungs organized in three groups: renunciation of the world (1–4) → struggle with passions (5–23) → acquisition of virtues (24–30)

**Obstacle per level:** The characteristic passion (*pathos*) or vice at each rung: anger, gluttony, lust, avarice, sloth, pride, and their subdivisions

**Method:** Prayer; fasting; humility; obedience to an elder; *hesychia* (stillness); tears of compunction

**Goal:** *Theosis* — deification; union with God through conformity to Christ; "becoming by grace what God is by nature"

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** John Climacus (John of Sinai), *Klimax tou Paradeisou* (The Ladder of Divine Ascent)

**Source corpus:** Monastic-ascetical treatise; Eastern Christian spirituality

**Genre:** Spiritual instruction for monks

**First attested date:** c. 600–649 CE (John Climacus's dates); tradition of virtue-stages goes back to Evagrius Ponticus (c. 345–399 CE) and the eight *logismoi* (eight troubling thoughts)

**Notes on certainty:** Well-documented. The 30-rung structure is explicit and canonical in Eastern Christian monasticism. Tier A.

### Dante's Paradiso

**Practice type:** Allegorical / literary

**Levels:** Nine celestial spheres (Moon → Mercury → Venus → Sun → Mars → Jupiter → Saturn → Fixed Stars → Primum Mobile) + the Empyrean (infinite divine light)

**Obstacle per level:** Moral residue (addressed in Purgatorio); misaligned desire; the incapacity to receive higher divine light without being prepared by love and intellect

**Method:** Beatrice as guide; philosophical instruction at each sphere; the purification already accomplished in *Purgatorio*; love and intellect moving together

**Goal:** The Beatific Vision — direct contemplation of God in the form of the threefold white light (Trinity); the love "that moves the sun and the other stars"

**Earliest surviving textual witness:** Dante Alighieri, *Divina Commedia* — *Paradiso*

**Source corpus:** Theological epic poem

**Genre:** Vernacular theological poetry

**First attested date:** c. 1308–1321 CE (composition; Dante died 1321)

**Notes on certainty:** The planetary-sphere model draws on Ptolemaic astronomy and Neoplatonic cosmology (Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite's angelic hierarchy). Dante is encoding the full Neoplatonic-Christian synthesis of the ascent tradition in vernacular Italian. Tier A.

## The two-cause argument

The structural convergences surveyed above arise from two distinct causes, both of which are real and need to be distinguished.

### 1. Documented diffusion

Some of these systems are historically connected:

- The **Egyptian gated-passage** (Amduat, c. 1479 BCE) → **Mesopotamian seven-heaven** (attested in Babylonian cosmology) → **Iranian seven-sphere** (Zoroastrian Chinvat/Arda Viraf) → **Jewish apocalyptic seven heavens** (2 Enoch, c. 100 BCE–100 CE) → **Hekhalot seven halls** (c. 3rd–7th cent. CE) is a traceable genealogy along the corridor of empire, exile, and cultural exchange.
- **Mithraic grades** use the same seven planetary-sphere cosmology as the Hermetic *Corpus* and the Mandaean *matartā*; all three are products of the late Hellenistic and Roman-era synthetic milieu in which Babylonian astronomy, Iranian religious tradition, and Greek philosophy converged.
- **Platonic ascent** → Neoplatonic theurgy → Hermetic planetary ascent is a documented philosophical lineage.
- **Muhammad's Mi'raj** inherits seven-heaven vocabulary directly from the Jewish and Zoroastrian traditions present in 7th-century Arabia.
- **Sufi *maqāmāt*** → Kabbalistic *devekut* → Christian Ladder share a common late antique vocabulary of graded virtue that was transmitted through translation movements and cultural contact in the medieval Mediterranean.

### 2. Cognitive / phenomenological convergence

Some of the parallels — especially between traditions with no documented contact — are better explained by convergence arising from the structure of human contemplative experience. Documented altered-state experiences (sustained meditation, trance, near-death, entheogens) consistently produce:

- A sense of **vertical movement** through qualitatively distinct zones
- Encounters with **threshold experiences** that feel like guardians, tests, or obstacles
- A **terminal encounter with light** — radiant, oceanic, sourceless

This predisposes contemplative cultures everywhere toward layered-ascent frameworks. The seven-level variant may be preferred because of the astronomical availability of seven visible planets, which provided a ready-made cosmological map onto which inner experience could be projected. This is not the same as saying the traditions are independent — it is saying that the convergence arises partly from phenomenology, not purely from textual transmission.

**The art of using this page** is to assess each parallel individually: Does the evidence support documented contact, or is convergence the better explanation? Both answers are legitimate; the problem is treating all convergences as evidence of diffusion (which inflates the transmission claim) or all contacts as coincidence (which deflates it).
