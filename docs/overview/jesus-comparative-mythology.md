# Jesus in Comparative Mythology

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers familiar with [Historical-Critical Scholarship on the NT](/docs/overview/historical-jesus-critical-scholarship) and [Christ Myth Theory and the Historical Jesus Debate](/docs/overview/christ-myth-theory), who want a detailed, tier-labeled survey of specific parallels between Jesus traditions and Mediterranean mythology.
- **Question:** Which specific features of the Gospel narratives and early Christian tradition show demonstrable shaping by Jewish, Greek, Roman, Egyptian, or Near Eastern religious backgrounds — and what is the scholarly status of each comparison?
- **Scope:** This page covers *influence and shaping* of the Jesus tradition by mythological contexts, not *mythicism* (the fringe claim that Jesus never existed). Both topics are distinct: influence on the narrative is Tier A–B scholarship; denial of a historical person is a fringe position. See [Christ Myth Theory](/docs/overview/christ-myth-theory) for the latter.
- **Epistemic status:** The existence of these comparisons is not in dispute; what varies is the *mechanism* (deliberate literary modelling, shared cultural environment, or unconscious assimilation). Tiers are applied per comparison below.

## 1. Legendary material in the Synoptic Gospels

### 1.1 Jesus as "new Moses" (Matthew's deliberate literary shaping)

The Gospel of Matthew intentionally portrays Jesus as a "new Moses." This is not a matter of subtle parallel—it is a sustained literary programme throughout the text.

Key instances:
- Herod's attempt to kill the infant Jesus, the family's flight into Egypt, and their return to Judaea (Matt 2) is a mythical narrative modelled on the Exodus narrative in the Torah (Eddy & Boyd 2007; Sanders 1993).
- Jesus delivers his first public sermon on a mountain (the Sermon on the Mount, Matt 5–7) in deliberate imitation of Moses receiving the Law on Sinai. According to Theissen and Merz (*The Historical Jesus*, 1998), the individual teachings preserved in the sermon may represent authentic sayings of Jesus from different occasions, but the author of Matthew compiled them into an organized lecture and invented the literary context to fit his "new Moses" portrayal.

**Tier A:** The deliberate Mosaic typology in Matthew is not disputed among NT scholars. The question of which elements within that frame may preserve historical kernels is a separate, harder question.

### 1.2 Birth narratives as legendary invention

E. P. Sanders, a leading historical Jesus scholar, describes the birth narratives in Matthew and Luke as the "clearest examples of legends in the Synoptic Gospels" (Sanders 1993, pp. 85–88). Several features underscore this:

- Both accounts have Jesus born in Bethlehem (in conformity with Micah 5:2) and growing up in Nazareth—but give two contradictory explanations for how that happened.
- Matthew quotes the Septuagint translation of Isaiah 7:14 ("Behold, the virgin shall conceive..."), but the Hebrew text uses *ha'almāh* ("young woman"), not the Greek *parthenos* ("virgin"). Most secular historians see the virgin birth accounts in both Matthew and Luke as independent legendary constructions designed to fulfil this mistranslated passage (Sanders 1993, pp. 88–91).
- The Annunciation accounts in Matthew 1:18–22 and Luke 1:26–38 are modelled on the OT annunciations of Ishmael, Isaac, and Samson (Litwak 2005; Farris 2015).
- Sanders notes: "No other part of the gospels relies so heavily on Old Testament parallels" as the birth narratives; they are "an extreme case resulting from the gospel authors' lack of knowledge about Jesus's birth and childhood" (Sanders 1993, p. 88).

**Tier A:** Birth narratives as legendary construction = mainstream secular scholarly consensus.

### 1.3 Healing miracles and Asclepius

Several of Jesus's healing miracles in the Synoptic Gospels bear structural similarities to stories about Asclepius, the Greco-Roman god of healing:

- Brennan R. Hill (2004) notes that the Synoptic authors may have "subtly borrowed from Greek literary models," while emphasizing that Jesus's miracles are primarily told within a Jewish theology of Yahweh's healing power.
- The key difference between Jesus and Asclepius identified by Edelstein and Edelstein (1998): Jesus extended healing to "sinners and publicans"; Asclepius, as a god, confined his healing to those who were ritually pure.
- Hill further notes that Jesus's miracles differ from those of Asclepius in that they are attributed to a *human being on earth*, while Asclepius's miracles originate from a distant deity.

**Tier B:** Structural echoes plausible and noted by multiple scholars; direct literary borrowing by Synoptic authors disputed but possible.

The Lucan parable of the rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19–31) bears strong resemblances to folktales told throughout the Near East (van Eck 2016), though scholars disagree whether it originates with the historical Jesus or is a later Christian addition.

### 1.4 The Barabbas release

Bart Ehrman identifies the Barabbas release episode (Mark 15:6–15 and parallels) as one of the "genuinely legendary episodes" in the Gospels (Ehrman 2012, pp. 184–185). No evidence from Roman legal practice or Jewish sources corroborates the existence of a Passover amnesty custom. This does not imply that Jesus did not exist, but that specific narrative details were shaped by theological and dramatic purposes rather than historical reporting.

**Tier A:** The legendary character of the Barabbas episode = accepted in mainstream critical scholarship. Its historicity = minority position.

### 1.5 The Prometheus parallel and the crucifixion

Although the crucifixion of Jesus is one of the most historically secure events in his life—accepted by virtually all scholars (Ehrman, Crossan, Dunn)—the German historian of religion Martin Hengel has examined how non-Christians would have perceived crucifixion accounts.

Hengel (*Crucifixion in the Ancient World*, 1977) identifies the *only* instance in all classical literature of a god being apparently crucified: Lucian of Samosata's comic dialogue *Prometheus* (2nd century AD), in which Prometheus is fastened to two rocks using all the terminology of a Roman crucifixion—"nailed through the hands" producing "a most serviceable cross." Lucian's intent is *deliberate mockery* of the gods, showing Zeus as a cruel tyrant. This is not a predecessor tradition for the Christian crucifixion, but it does illuminate why Greco-Roman culture could only conceive of divine crucifixion as "malicious parody"—and how shocking the Christian kerygma would have been in that context.

**Tier B:** Hengel's comparative observation = accepted; this parallel clarifies reception history, not origin.

### 1.6 MacDonald's Homeric thesis: the Gospel of Mark as Jewish Odyssey

American theologian Dennis R. MacDonald (*Mythologizing Jesus*, 2013) has argued that the Gospel of Mark is a Jewish retelling of the *Odyssey* (with its ending derived from the *Iliad*), using Jesus as its central character. According to MacDonald, the Gospels are primarily intended to show Jesus as superior to Greek heroes.

This thesis has been met with intense scepticism in scholarly circles:
- Sandnes (2005): relies on "extremely vague and subjective parallels."
- Critics note the Gospel of Mark never directly quotes from either Homeric epic and uses completely dissimilar language (Watts 2013; Perkins 2007).
- Perkins further notes that many of the Marcan incidents MacDonald derives from the *Odyssey* have much closer parallels in the Old Testament.
- MacDonald's argument has become popular in non-scholarly internet circles as supposed support for the Christ myth theory; MacDonald himself rejects this interpretation as "too drastic."

**Tier C/Fringe:** The Homeric thesis as stated is rejected by specialists. The broader point that Gospel-writers used literary conventions familiar in the Greco-Roman world is unremarkable.

## 2. Gospel of John: Platonism and Dionysian influence

### 2.1 The Logos and Platonic philosophy

The "Logos" theology in the Prologue of John (1:1–18) derives from a long philosophical tradition:
- The concept was devised by the Pre-Socratic philosopher Heraclitus and adapted to Judaism by the Jewish Middle Platonist Philo of Alexandria.
- The author of the Gospel of John was **not personally familiar with Greek philosophy** and probably did not borrow Logos theology directly from Platonic texts (Hurtado 2003; Finkelstein & Davies). Instead, the concept entered via Hellenistic Jewish deuterocanonical texts (Wisdom literature), which John inherited and expanded.
- In Platonic terminology, Logos was a universal rational principle. In its Jewish adaptation, as Davies and Finkelstein write, it became "a mediating divine figure between God and man" connected specifically with Israel, God's chosen people, and his sanctuary.

**Tier A:** John's Logos theology draws on Hellenistic Jewish Wisdom tradition, not directly from Platonic texts.

### 2.2 Possible Dionysian influence on the Gospel of John

Scholars have long suspected that the Gospel of John was influenced by symbolism associated with the cult of Dionysus. This question is *hotly disputed*, with reputable scholars defending both sides:

**Arguments for Dionysian influence:**

1. **Water into wine at Cana** (John 2:1–11): The miracle bears similarity to several Dionysian stories. Pausanias (*Description of Greece*, 2nd century AD) describes a ritual in Elis where empty barrels locked inside a temple overnight were found filled with wine the following morning. The Greek novel *Leucippe and Clitophon* (Achilles Tatius, 1st–2nd century AD) describes Dionysus miraculously turning a drink into wine at a herdsman's table. The Cana miracle does not appear in any of the Synoptic Gospels, suggesting it may have been a Johannine invention (Salier 2004).
2. **The True Vine** (John 15:1–17): Jesus declares himself the "True Vine," a title reminiscent of Dionysus, who was said to have discovered the first grape vine (Shorrock 2011).
3. **Structural parallels with Euripides's *Bacchae*** (first performed 405 BC): Mark W. G. Stibbe (1994) has argued that both works portray an incarnate deity who arrives in a country where he should be recognized and worshipped, but is disguised as a mortal, is not recognized, and is persecuted by the ruling party. In both, the deity is supported by female followers. Stibbe emphasizes that the accounts are also *radically different*: in *The Bacchae*, Dionysus advocates wine and hedonism and is portrayed as aggressive and violent; Jesus offers salvation from sin and is portrayed as peaceful.

**Arguments against:**
- The devout Christian author of the Gospel of John would have been unlikely to deliberately incorporate Dionysian imagery.
- The wine symbolism in John is more plausibly rooted in the extensive references to wine in the Old Testament (Kierspel 2006).
- Proponents counter that the author may have used Dionysian imagery to show Jesus as *superior* to Dionysus (Salier 2004; Stibbe 1994).

**Tier B:** Possible Dionysian shaping of specific Johannine episodes (especially Cana and the True Vine). *The Bacchae* parallel = structural/thematic (not genealogical). The issue is genuinely disputed.

## 3. Mithraism and Christianity

### 3.1 What we know and don't know

Around the same time Christianity was expanding (1st–3rd centuries CE), the cult of Mithras was also spreading throughout the Roman Empire. Key facts:
- Mithraism was a Mystery Cult: members were forbidden from disclosing beliefs to outsiders. **No Mithraic sacred texts have survived**, if any such writings ever existed.
- Most knowledge comes from archaeological excavation of *mithraea* (underground sanctuaries found across the Roman world).
- Like Jesus, Mithras was seen as a divine saviour — but unlike Jesus, Mithras was **not** believed to have brought salvation by suffering and dying (Patella 2006; Clauss 2001).

**On the false parallels:** The popular list of Mithras–Christ parallels — virgin birth, December 25 birthday, twelve disciples, crucifixion, a last supper — **has no ancient basis** and originates with 19th-century popular writers (Higgins 1836, Doane 1882) who cited no primary sources. See [Paul, Mithraism, and historicity (tiered notes)](/docs/overview/paul-mithraism-and-historicity-notes) for full documentation.

### 3.2 What archaeology does show: the tauroctony and ritual meal

The centre of every *mithraeum* was the **tauroctony**: a painting or sculpture showing Mithras as a young man (wearing a cape and Phrygian cap) plunging a knife into the neck or shoulder of a bull. A dog laps the blood, from which emerges an ear of corn; a scorpion stings the bull's scrotum; human torchbearers stand on either side; a serpent is also present. The figures appear to correspond to signs of the zodiac (Ulansey 1989).

The **closest parallel between Jesus and Mithras** is a ritual meal. After slaying the bull, Mithras was believed to have shared the bull's meat with the sun-god Sol Invictus — a meal shown in Mithraic iconography and ritually reenacted by Mithraists as part of their liturgy (Clauss 2001, pp. 112–116).

Manfred Clauss speculates that the similarities between Christianity and Mithraism may have made it easier for Mithraic cult members to convert to Christianity without abandoning their ritual meal, sun-imagery, candles, incense, or bells — "a trend which might explain why, as late as the sixth century, the Christian Church was still trying to stamp out the *stulti homines* who still paid obeisance to the sun every morning on the steps of the church itself" (Clauss 2001, p. 172).

Additionally, in Mithraic cults from the Rhine-Danube region, there are representations of a myth in which **Mithras shoots an arrow at a rock face, causing water to gush forth**. Both Christians and Mithraists used water as a symbol for their saviours: Jesus is called the "water of life" in the NT; a votive altar to Mithras from Poetovio proclaims him the *fons perennis* ("the ever-flowing stream") (Clauss 2001, pp. 62–66).

**Tier A:** The tauroctony, ritual meal, and water symbolism are archaeologically well-attested features of Mithraism. The parallels with Christianity are *real* but better explained by shared cultural environment than direct borrowing.

### 3.3 Early Christian apologists on Mithraism

Justin Martyr and Tertullian — early Christian apologists who had never been members of the Mithraic cult and had never spoken to its members — claimed that Mithraic practices were copied *from* Christianity:

- **Justin Martyr** (*First Apology*, ch. 66): after describing the Christian Eucharist, he claimed "the wicked devils have imitated [this] in the mysteries of Mithras."
- **Tertullian** (*De praescriptione haereticorum*): attributed Mithraic baptism, bread offering, resurrection symbol, and celibacy practices to the devil's counterfeiting of Christian sacraments.

According to Ehrman (2012, pp. 213–214), these writers were ideologically motivated: they wanted to persuade pagan officials that Christianity was not uniquely subversive — by showing it had analogues in other accepted religions — and therefore intentionally *exaggerated* similarities. "Scholars are generally wary of trusting anything these sources have to say about the Mithraic cult's alleged practices" (Ehrman 2012, p. 214).

**Tier A:** These Christian apologetic sources are motivated, ideologically-shaped witnesses. They are evidence for 2nd–3rd century *perception* of similarity, not for actual Mithraic borrowing from Christianity or vice versa.

## 4. Christian iconographic syncretism

Early Christians in late antiquity frequently adapted pagan iconography to suit Christian purposes. As Joan E. Taylor (2018, pp. 96–97) and Robin Jensen (2000, pp. 37–38) note, this does not indicate that Christianity was "derived from" paganism — only that early Christians made use of pre-existing symbols already available in their visual culture. Sometimes Christians deliberately used pagan imagery to show Jesus as *superior* to the pagan gods.

### 4.1 Hermes Kriophoros → the Good Shepherd

In classical iconography, Hermes (*kriophoros*, "ram-bearer") was sometimes shown as a handsome, beardless youth bearing a sheep over his shoulders. In late antiquity this image developed a generic association with philanthropy. Early Christians adapted it as a representation of Jesus as the "Good Shepherd" (Taylor 1993, pp. 96–97; Jensen 2000).

### 4.2 Orpheus

Early Christians identified Jesus with Orpheus, who tamed wild beasts with his lyre. The Church Father Clement of Alexandria compared Orpheus and Jesus as both revered for their "songs," but insisted Orpheus misused eloquence to bind people to the temporal world, while Jesus, the singer of the "New Song," brings peace and frees men from the flesh. A hematite gem inscribed with a crucified man identified as *ΟΡΦΕΩΣ ΒΑΚΧΙΚΟΣ* (Orpheos Bacchikos) may date to the late 2nd–3rd century AD, though it has long been suspected a forgery from the 17th–18th century; it was formerly in the Altes Museum in Berlin before being lost or destroyed in WWII (Carotta & Eickenberg 2009).

### 4.3 Asclepius → Jesus as healer

Justin Martyr argued that believing in Jesus's divinity should not be hard for pagans, since it was no different from believing in the divinity of Asclepius. Over time, Christians adapted much of Asclepius's iconography: images of Jesus as healer replaced images of Asclepius and Hippocrates as the ideal physician (Ferngren 2009). Jesus, originally shown clean-shaven, may first have been shown as bearded partly through this syncretism with Asclepius, and with other bearded deities (Zeus, Serapis) (Taylor 2018, pp. 118–122). A 2nd-century AD head of Asclepius was discovered underneath a 4th-century AD Christian church in Gerasa, Jordan.

### 4.4 Sol Invictus, Zeus, and the halo

- In some depictions from late antiquity, Jesus was shown with the **halo of Sol Invictus** (Taylor 2018, p. 121).
- Images of "Christ in Majesty" seated on a throne were inspired by classical depictions of Zeus and other chief deities (Taylor 2018, p. 122).
- The title *kosmokrateros* ("Ruler of the Cosmos"), eventually applied to Jesus, had previously been borne by Serapis (Jefferson 2014, pp. 6–7).
- By the 4th century CE, the recognizable image of Jesus as long-haired, bearded, and clad in long, baggy-sleeved clothing had fully emerged.

A fragment of a lost work by Theodor Lector preserves a story (ca. 465 AD) in which bishop Gennadius of Constantinople was said to have healed an artist who lost all strength in his hand after painting Christ with long, curly hair *parted in the same manner as traditional representations of Zeus* — showing that this adaptation did not sit well with all Christians (Taylor 2018).

### 4.5 Isis Lactans → the Virgin Mary

Early Christians may have adapted the iconography of the Egyptian goddess **Isis nursing her son Horus** (*Isis Lactans*) and applied it to the Virgin Mary nursing Jesus (Baring & Cashford 1991; Warner 2016, pp. 210–212).

### 4.6 Tammuz/Adonis and the Bethlehem cave

Jerome (letter, 395 AD) records that the cave in Bethlehem that became the site of the Church of the Nativity "was overshadowed by a grove of Tammuz, that is to say, Adonis." Peter Welten has argued the cave was never dedicated to Tammuz and that Jerome misidentified Christian mourning as a pagan ritual; Joan E. Taylor counters that Jerome, as an educated man, could not have made so naive an error. During the 6th century AD, some Christians in the Middle East borrowed elements from Tammuz/Ishtar mourning poetry into retellings of the Virgin Mary at the cross — Syrian writers Jacob of Serugh and Romanos the Melodist both wrote laments in which Mary's grief at the cross closely resembles Ishtar's laments over Tammuz's death (Aldrete & Aldrete 2012, pp. 82–84; Struthers 2012).

**Tier A across this section:** The art-historical facts of iconographic borrowing are well-documented. These represent cultural assimilation, not doctrinal derivation.

## 5. The December 25 birthdate

The Bible never states when Jesus was born. By late antiquity, Christians had begun celebrating his birth on 25 December. The scholarly debate concerns which pagan observance influenced this choice:

- **Sol Invictus:** In 274 CE, the emperor Aurelian declared 25 December the birthday of Sol Invictus — a sun god of Syrian origin vigorously promoted by Elagabalus. Some scholars argue Christians may have adopted the date to attract converts (John 2005, p. 112; Struthers 2012).
- **Saturnalia:** 25 December also falls around the time of the Roman festival of Saturnalia, which was much older and more widely celebrated. Many Saturnalia customs eventually became associated with Christmas (Aldrete & Aldrete 2012).
- **Conception calculation:** Early Christians may also have been influenced by the idea that Jesus died on the anniversary of his conception (a belief found in Tertullian and others). Because Jesus died during Passover, and in the 3rd century AD Passover was celebrated on 25 March, they may have reasoned that his birthday fell nine months later — on 25 December (Johnson & Johnson 2007).

**Tier A/B:** The Sol Invictus and Saturnalia connections are widely accepted; the exact mechanism and relative weighting remain debated. What is certain is that 25 December was not designated as Jesus's birthday until the 5th century, and was not a Christian practice during the earliest centuries.

## 6. Miraculous birth parallels

Classical mythology is filled with miraculous birth stories, but most differ structurally from the Christian virgin birth account: in most classical parallels, a god engages in *literal sexual intercourse* with a mortal woman, producing a son who is half-god, half-man. The Christian account of the virgin birth deliberately avoids this pattern (Litwa 2014, pp. 62–64).

**Specific comparisons:**

- **Erichthonius (Athena/Gaia):** In an Athenian legend, Hephaestus attempted to rape the virgin goddess Athena; she deflected him, his semen fell on the ground, impregnating Gaia, who gave birth to Erichthonius, whom Athena adopted. This allowed Athena to produce a "son" without losing her virginity — a structural parallel to the Christian account (Deacy 2008, pp. 82–83).
- **Danaë/Perseus:** Zeus came to Danaë in the form of a shower of gold and impregnated her. No surviving Greek text calls this a "virgin birth," but Justin Martyr's Jewish speaker Trypho refers to it as such in *Dialogue with Trypho* (Litwa 2014, p. 42).
- **Dionysus:** In most versions, Zeus came to Semele disguised as a mortal and had sex with her; when he revealed his divine form she was incinerated; Zeus rescued the unborn Dionysus and sewed him in his own thigh until birth. In an alternative version (Hyginus), Dionysus was born of Zeus and Persephone, torn apart by the Titans; Zeus mixed Dionysus's heart into a potion, which Semele drank, conceiving the child (Kerényi 1974).
- **Plato's virgin conception:** Origen (*3rd century*) retells a legend that Plato's mother Perictione virginally conceived him after Apollo appeared to her husband and told him not to consummate their marriage — a scene closely paralleling the Annunciation to Joseph. Origen interpreted this and similar stories as *prefiguring* the reality manifest in Jesus's birth (Warner 2016, pp. 35–38).

M. David Litwa (2014) argues that the Gospel authors *consciously* tried to avoid portraying Jesus's conception as resembling pagan accounts of divine parentage — for example, Luke tells a parallel story about the conception of John the Baptist to emphasize the *Jewish* character of Jesus's birth — but that their accounts were nonetheless *unconsciously* influenced by the broader cultural discourse about divine men.

**Tier B:** Structural parallels exist and are acknowledged by scholars. The mechanisms (deliberate avoidance, unconscious influence, or convergent mythologizing) are debated. The differences are as significant as the similarities.

## 7. The Rank-Raglan mythotype

Folklorist Alan Dundes (*Interpreting Folklore*, 1980) argued that Jesus fits all but five of the 22 narrative patterns in the Rank-Raglan mythotype — better than many traditionally cited heroes (Jason, Bellerophon, Asclepius, etc.). Dundes views Jesus as a historical "miracle-worker" or "religious teacher" whose life was told and retold through oral tradition until it became legend; he does not deny that Jesus was a real person.

Dundes's application of the Rank-Raglan mythotype to Jesus has been strongly criticized:
- **Eddy & Boyd (2007):** Dundes draws patterns from different texts written centuries apart without differentiating between them.
- **Multiple scholars:** the mythotype is artificial in nature and lacks specificity to Hellenistic culture.
- Lawrence M. Wills (2005) acknowledges that "the hero paradigm in some form does apply to the earliest lives of Jesus," but not to the extreme extent Dundes argues.

The Christ myth theory makes use of the Rank-Raglan framework as supposed evidence that Jesus is entirely mythological. Mainstream scholars, including Dundes himself, reject this conclusion: the framework identifies *narrative patterns*, not *evidence of non-existence*.

**Tier C:** The Rank-Raglan mythotype identifies folkloric patterns in the Gospel narratives; its value as a historical-critical tool is limited.

## 8. The "dying-and-rising god" archetype: Frazer and the scholarly critique

### 8.1 Frazer's original claims

The late 19th-century Scottish anthropologist Sir James George Frazer (*The Golden Bough*, 1890; later works) wrote extensively about a "dying-and-rising god" archetype in comparative religion. Frazer's main intention was to prove that all religions are fundamentally the same and that all essential features of Christianity could be found in earlier religions. He strongly *implied*, though did not state explicitly, that Jesus was a "dying-and-rising god" of the typical Near Eastern variety. Frazer's claims were widely influential in late 19th- and early 20th-century scholarship.

**They are now mostly rejected by modern scholars** (Smith 1987; Mettinger 2004; Eddy & Boyd 2007).

### 8.2 The specific cases: what the evidence shows

**Dumuzid/Tammuz** (Mesopotamian): A Sumerian god of vegetation associated with springtime fertility. Women mourned his death when crops withered. The category "dying-and-rising god" was based on the *abbreviated Akkadian redaction* of Inanna's Descent, which was missing its ending — scholars assumed it ended with Tammuz's resurrection. When the complete, unabridged Sumerian text was finally translated in the mid-20th century, it *ended with Tammuz's death*, not his resurrection. The *Return of Dumuzid* text (discovered 1963) briefly revived hope, but it does not describe a triumph over death: Dumuzid can only leave the Underworld when his *sister* takes his place — the text "affirms the inalterable power of the realm of the dead" (Jacobsen 2008, pp. 83–84).

**Adonis** (Greek equivalent of Tammuz): He is never described as rising from the dead in any extant Greco-Roman writings. The only possible allusions to a resurrection come from late, highly ambiguous statements made by Christian authors (notably Origen, *Selecta in Ezechielem*) (Barstad 1984; Mettinger 2004, p. 379).

**Attis** (Phrygian): He is never described as being resurrected in any surviving text. Many myths surround his death (self-castration, etc.) but none claim he returned from death (Eddy & Boyd 2007, pp. 143–144).

**Osiris** (Egyptian): Osiris was *not* truly resurrected in the way the Christian resurrection is understood. In the Egyptian myth, Set murdered Osiris, dismembered him, and scattered the pieces. Isis reassembled them, reviving Osiris in the *Duat* (the Egyptian afterlife), where he became *king of the dead* — not a living person who returned to the world of the living. Jan Assmann (2001, pp. 129–130) and others draw a sharp distinction between Osiris's status as ruler of the dead and the Christian (or Zoroastrian) concept of resurrection.

### 8.3 Jonathan Z. Smith's decisive critique (1987)

In 1987, Jonathan Z. Smith concluded in Mircea Eliade's *Encyclopedia of Religion* that:

> "The category of dying and rising gods, once a major topic of scholarly investigation, must now be understood to have been largely a misnomer based on imaginative reconstructions and exceedingly late or highly ambiguous texts."

Smith proposed that the deities previously called "dying-and-rising" should be separated into "dying gods" and "disappearing gods" — two distinct categories that, before Christianity, did not overlap: gods who "died" did not return; those who returned never truly "died." By the end of the 20th century, most scholars accepted that the "dying-and-rising god" as a unified archetype was an invention, and that the term "is not a useful scholarly designation" (Eddy & Boyd 2007, pp. 140–143).

**Tier A:** Jonathan Z. Smith's critique of the "dying-and-rising god" concept is the current scholarly consensus. The specific parallels to Tammuz, Adonis, Attis, and Osiris are based on misreading of sources, incomplete texts, or texts post-dating Christianity.

## 9. Maurice Casey's synthesis: not perfectly unique, but not a myth

Maurice Casey, Emeritus Professor of New Testament Languages and Literature at the University of Nottingham, offers a useful framing: these parallels "do not in any way indicate that Jesus was invented based on pagan 'divine men'" — but rather that Jesus was "simply not as perfectly unique as many evangelical Christians frequently claim he was" (Casey 2014, pp. 203–204).

This matches the site's own tier framework: **motif similarity and cultural shaping** (Tier B–C) must not be collapsed into **genealogical dependence** (requires evidence of specific contact, mechanism, and chronology).
