# Indo-Aryan migration and Vedic religion

The **Vedic religion** that forms the oldest surviving textual layer of South Asian religious history is a synthesis: a PIE-derived tradition carried into the Indian subcontinent by **Indo-Aryan-speaking populations** from the Central Asian steppe, reshaped by contact with the **Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex (BMAC)** in the intermediate zone, and then further transformed through encounter with the remnants and descendants of the **Indus Valley Civilization (IVC)**. The result — documented in the **Rigveda** (~1500–900 BCE) and the subsequent Vedic corpus — is both the direct continuation of Proto-Indo-European religious grammar and the beginning of everything that would later become Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism.

> **Scope note.** This page covers the migration, the founding theological structure, and the IVC encounter. For the PIE origins and expansion *before* the Indian entry, see [PIE origins, the Yamnaya expansion, and mechanisms of religious transmission](pie-origins-yamnaya-expansion.md). For what the Śramaṇa movements did to this inheritance, see [The Śramaṇa revolution and the Axial Age in India](sramana-revolution-axial-india.md).

## Context

**Question:** What do we actually know about how Indo-Aryan populations entered South Asia, what religion they brought, and how it interacted with what was already there?

**Tier situation:** The ancient DNA and archaeological evidence for migration is **Tier A**. The linguistic identification of the Rigveda as Indo-Aryan composition on or shortly after the migration is **Tier A**. Claims about what IVC populations *believed* (since they left no translated texts) are **Tier B–C**, based on archaeology and structural inference. Claims about deliberate "synthesis" between incoming and indigenous traditions are **Tier C** — the synthesis happened; calling it deliberate is interpretive.

---

## Consensus snapshot

- Indo-Aryan-speaking populations entered South Asia from the northwest, c. 1500–1200 BCE, bringing an IE language and a religious tradition derived from the Proto-Indo-Iranian branch. *(Tier A: linguistic and genomic evidence.)*
- The **Rigveda** is the oldest surviving textual product of this tradition — composed orally in the Punjab region, preserved with extraordinary fidelity through a specialist oral tradition. *(Tier A.)*
- The BMAC (c. 2200–1700 BCE) was an intermediate cultural zone between the steppe homeland and South Asia, and Indo-Iranian contact with BMAC left detectable traces in the Vedic ritual tradition. *(Tier B: Parpola 2015, Witzel 2003.)*
- The Indus Valley Civilization (IVC, c. 3300–1300 BCE) was a major urban culture contemporary with the earliest steppe expansions. Its script is undeciphered; claims about IVC religion are archaeological inference. *(Tier A for the archaeological record; Tier B–C for religious reconstruction.)*
- Vedic religion over time absorbed non-IE elements, including some that have no PIE etymology and are more plausibly attributed to indigenous South Asian sources. *(Tier B: Witzel 1999 on "substrates" in Rigvedic Sanskrit.)*

## The migration: routes and evidence

### The Sintashta–Andronovo trajectory

The ancestral Indo-Iranian population is associated with the **Sintashta culture** (c. 2100–1800 BCE) on the eastern Eurasian steppe — a culture notable for its **spoke-wheeled chariots** (the earliest known anywhere), horse-warrior burials, and fort-like settlements (*gorodishcha*). From Sintashta, the **Andronovo cultural horizon** (c. 2000–900 BCE) spread east and southeast into Central Asia.

The split between the **Avestan (Iranian)** branch moving southwest and the **Vedic (Indo-Aryan)** branch moving southeast is not directly observable but can be inferred from the early divergence between the two traditions: Vedic *deva* (the gods) = Avestan *daeva* (the demons); Vedic *asura* (power, sometimes pejorative) = Avestan *ahura* (the Lord, the highest good). The **inversion of the divine hierarchy** between the two sister traditions is one of the most striking features of their early split and has been interpreted as evidence of a **religious reform** that occurred *after* the Indo-Iranian common period but *before* the full Vedic and Avestan textual records begin.

### The BMAC encounter

Between the Sintashta homeland and the Indian subcontinent lay the **Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex** — an urban, sedentary culture in modern Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan, and northern Afghanistan (c. 2200–1700 BCE), centered on oasis agriculture. BMAC sites include Gonur Tepe, Togolok 21, and Dashly.

The BMAC encounter is critical because:
- Archaeological evidence shows **steppe populations moving into BMAC settlements** in the late BMAC period (~1800 BCE)
- **Ephedra seeds and poppy remains** have been found in what appear to be ritual contexts at BMAC sites — raising the possibility that the *haoma* / *soma* preparation tradition (a central feature of both Vedic and Avestan ritual) was learned at or developed through this contact zone
- **BMAC material culture** (distinctive pottery, fire altars, architecture) appears in later sites associated with Indo-Aryan populations in South Asia, suggesting the BMAC was not bypassed but assimilated
- Certain deity names in the Rigveda (including possibly **Indra's** epithet *vṛtra-han*, "slayer of Vṛtra") may reflect an older stratum of the tradition that crystallized during the BMAC contact period

*(Tier B: the contact is archaeologically documented; the specific religious transmissions from BMAC are inferential.)*

### Entry into South Asia

The Indo-Aryan entry into South Asia probably occurred through **multiple waves** over several centuries rather than a single migration event. Ancient DNA from early Rigvedic-period sites in South Asia shows increasing steppe ancestry contribution from the northwest, consistent with an ongoing influx. The direction was the **Khyber Pass corridor** and the upper Indus valley.

The **Mitanni treaties** (c. 1380–1340 BCE, northern Syria — see [PIE origins](pie-origins-yamnaya-expansion.md)) demonstrate that a closely related Indo-Iranian religious vocabulary was in use in the Near East at approximately the same period as the Rigvedic compositions, confirming the mid-2nd millennium BCE as the window for this branch's dispersal.

## The Rigveda: structure and content

### What it is

The **Rigveda** is a collection of 1,028 hymns in 10 books (*maṇḍalas*), composed orally in a specialized archaic Sanskrit. It is the oldest surviving text of the Indo-European language family in terms of *linguistic archaism* — closer to reconstructed PIE in many respects than Homer or the Avesta. The hymns are addressed to the Vedic gods and were used in ritual performance, particularly the *soma* sacrifice.

The **oral transmission** of the Rigveda, sustained through specialist Brahmin lineages for over three millennia, is one of the most remarkable feats of human memory in any culture. The text was not committed to writing until the early medieval period — yet the transmitted text shows almost no linguistic corruption, verified by internal metrical analysis.

### The core Vedic pantheon

| Deity | Function | PIE connection |
|---|---|---|
| **Indra** | King of the gods; storm and thunder; slayer of Vṛtra | Fills the PIE *\*Perkʷunos* (thunder-god) function-slot; his combat with Vṛtra is the direct reflex of the PIE cosmic combat myth |
| **Agni** | Fire deity; priest of the gods; intermediary between humans and gods | Direct cognate: Latin *ignis*, Lithuanian *ugnis*; the sacrificial fire is the oldest demonstrably shared PIE ritual technology |
| **Varuṇa** | Cosmic sovereign; guardian of *ṛtá* (cosmic order); punisher of oath-breaking | Paired with Mitra; together they represent the PIE sovereignty function (Dumézil); Varuṇa's omniscience and moral authority anticipate later monotheistic attributes |
| **Mitra** | Covenant, contract, alliance; solar associations; paired with Varuṇa | Direct IE cognate: Avestan *Mithra*; the *\*mitra-* root ("contract, friendship") is among the most important PIE religious reconstructions for this site |
| **Soma** | Sacred plant/drink; ritual intoxicant; deity of ecstasy and immortality | Exact cognate: Avestan *haoma*; the *soma* sacrifice is the central Rigvedic ritual; its plant identity is debated (ephedra, fly agaric, harmaline, others) |
| **Sūrya** | Sun deity | Cognate: Latin *sol*, Greek *hḗlios* |
| **Uṣas** | Dawn goddess; associated with renewal and light | Cognate: Greek *Eos*, Roman *Aurora*, Lithuanian *Aušra* — one of the most secure PIE goddess reconstructions |
| **Aśvins** | Divine twin horsemen; healers; rescuers; appear at dawn | Cognate: Greek *Dioskouroi* (Castor and Pollux); Lithuanian *Ašvieniai*; this is the PIE **divine twins** archetype |
| **Vāyu** | Wind deity | Cognate: Avestan *Vayu*; the wind that carries sacred breath |

Notably absent from the mature Vedic pantheon — compared to later Hinduism — are **Viṣṇu** (present in the Rigveda but minor: three strides of the cosmos) and **Śiva** (not named as such in the Rigveda, though the Rudra figure is a precursor). Both rise to dominance later, which is part of the story of how Vedic religion transformed into Hinduism.

### The *ṛtá* concept: cosmic order and moral law

The most theologically important concept in Vedic religion — and the one most directly relevant to this site's comparative argument — is **ṛtá** (Sanskrit, cognate with Avestan *asha*, "truth/order," and Latin *ritus*, "ritual").

*Ṛtá* is the cosmic principle of truth, order, and right functioning — maintained by Varuṇa and Mitra, sustained by the sacrificial ritual, and violated by falsehood, oath-breaking, and impiety. It is simultaneously:
- A cosmological principle (the order of the universe)
- A moral principle (the right conduct of human beings)
- A ritual principle (the correct performance of sacrifice)

The trajectory from Vedic *ṛtá* to later Buddhist/Hindu *dharma* is not direct borrowing (the terms are different) but structural inheritance: *dharma* fills the same cosmic-order/right-conduct function-slot as *ṛtá*, and the Vedic *ṛtá* concept is the ancestral form. See [The Śramaṇa revolution](sramana-revolution-axial-india.md#deep-structural-continuities-pie-to-buddhism) for the full trajectory.

### The Vedic ritual system

The Vedic ritual system centers on **fire sacrifice (yajña)**:
- **Agni** (fire) receives the offerings and carries them to the gods
- The **Brahmin priest class** (*brāhmaṇa*) is the essential intermediary — without correct ritual performance by trained specialists, the cosmic order (*ṛtá*) cannot be maintained
- **Soma** sacrifice is the peak ritual: the pressed juice of the soma plant is offered to the gods and consumed by priests, producing altered states described in the hymns as divine insight and immortality

The **Aśvamedha** (royal horse sacrifice) is the most elaborate Vedic ritual and almost certainly preserves the PIE royal horse sacrifice (see [PIE origins](pie-origins-yamnaya-expansion.md#the-horse-sacrifice-prestige-and-cosmological-vehicle)). It was performed by victorious kings to establish sovereignty and cosmic legitimacy — a year-long ceremony involving the ritual wandering of a consecrated stallion, followed by its sacrifice and a complex set of rites. The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa provides the fullest description.

## The Indus Valley Civilization: what was there before

### What we know

The **Indus Valley Civilization** (IVC, c. 3300–1300 BCE) was one of the world's first urban cultures — contemporary with Early Dynastic Sumer and Old Kingdom Egypt — centered on cities like **Mohenjo-daro** and **Harappa** in modern Pakistan. At its height (~2600–1900 BCE) it supported a population of perhaps 5 million people across cities of 40,000+ inhabitants, with standardized weights, sophisticated sanitation, and long-distance trade networks reaching Mesopotamia.

Its script remains **undeciphered**. Without readable texts, claims about IVC religion are entirely archaeological:
- **Great Bath at Mohenjo-daro**: a large, precisely sealed tank that many archaeologists interpret as a **ritual purification structure**, anticipating the importance of sacred bathing in later South Asian tradition
- **Fire altars** in residential areas at some IVC sites (Kalibangan), suggesting domestic fire ritual — though whether this is connected to later Vedic *yajna* is debated
- **Female figurines** in abundance — widely but cautiously interpreted as evidence of a **goddess or fertility cult**
- **Standardized weights and measures** across the entire civilization — suggesting a complex administrative and possibly religious authority

### The Pashupati seal and proto-Śiva

The most debated IVC religious artifact is the **Pashupati seal** (Mohenjo-daro, c. 2350–2000 BCE): a steatite seal showing a **seated, possibly ithyphallic figure in a yoga-like posture**, surrounded by animals (elephants, tiger, rhinoceros, buffalo, deer). The figure has three faces (or one face visible in profile from three angles) and appears to wear a horned headdress.

Scholars since John Marshall (1931) have identified this figure with a **proto-Śiva** — a precursor to the Hindu god Śiva in his aspect as *Paśupati* ("Lord of Animals") and as a meditating ascetic associated with animals and yogic discipline. This identification is:
- **Tier B**: Attractive because of the structural similarities (seated posture, multiple faces, animal lord), and supported by the fact that later Śiva iconography explicitly recalls these elements
- **Challenged** because: the figure may not be ithyphallic (the interpretation depends on damaged stone); the yoga-posture interpretation is contested; the identification is reading backward from later tradition

What can be said more carefully: certain elements of later Hindu Śiva theology — the ascetic/meditating god, the lord of animals, multiple faces, the *liṅga* as sacred object — appear in IVC material culture in some form. Whether these survived the IVC collapse and were directly transmitted to the later tradition, or whether they represent independent parallel development in South Asian religious culture, is an open question.

### Yogic/ascetic practices and their possible IVC origin

The **meditative and ascetic traditions** that are so central to the Śramaṇa movements (Buddhism, Jainism) and that have no clear PIE origin are frequently attributed to an **indigenous South Asian substrate**:

- PIE religion centers on **ritual action** (sacrifice, fire, poetry) performed by a specialist priest class — not on individual meditation or asceticism
- The **vrātya** brotherhoods of early Vedic India (extra-Vedic wandering warrior-ascetics, described in the Atharvaveda) may represent an early synthesis between incoming IE martial tradition and indigenous ascetic practice
- The core **yoga postures** depicted in later Upanishadic and Buddhist contexts — seated cross-legged meditation — have no clear PIE precedent
- The **renunciation ethic** of the Śramaṇa movements (world-rejection, begging bowl, wandering homeless) is structurally at odds with the Vedic *householder* ideal (married, performing domestic fire rites, maintaining family)

The most defensible position is that **yogic and ascetic practice was already present in the pre-Aryan South Asian cultural world** and was gradually absorbed into and eventually transformed the Vedic tradition from within. The Upanishads (c. 800–200 BCE) represent the point where the internal critique — "what is the inner self?" — began transforming the outer ritual system into a philosophical tradition.

*(Tier B–C: plausible attribution of yoga/asceticism to a non-IE substrate; direct proof requires deciphering the IVC script.)*

## The transformation of Vedic religion toward Hinduism

### The Upanishadic turn (~800–200 BCE)

The **Upanishads** represent an **internal reform** of Vedic religion — not a rejection (as the Śramaṇa movements were) but a philosophical reinterpretation. The Upanishadic move is to ask: what is the *inner reality* of which the outer ritual is a symbol?

- The outer sacrifice → inner self-knowledge (*jñāna*)
- **Ātman** (the individual self) = **Brahman** (the universal cosmic ground) — the core Upanishadic equation (*tat tvam asi*, "that thou art")
- The goal shifts from post-mortem bliss through correct ritual to *mokṣa* — liberation from the cycle of rebirth through realization of the self's true nature

This move happens *within* the Brahmin intellectual class, not against it. The Upanishads are formally part of the Vedic corpus (*śruti*, "heard/revealed"). But they effectively dissolve the centrality of ritual action — since if Ātman = Brahman, the self is already the divine; ritual is either pedagogically useful or ultimately dispensable.

### The rise of Viṣṇu and Śiva

The two dominant deities of later Hinduism — **Viṣṇu** and **Śiva** — represent different trajectories from the Vedic pantheon:

- **Viṣṇu** in the Rigveda is a solar deity known for his three strides (spanning earth, atmosphere, and heaven). He rises to prominence as a cosmic sustainer and the deity of *avatāra* (divine descent) — the concept that Viṣṇu incarnates as **Kṛṣṇa**, **Rāma**, and the future **Kalki** to restore cosmic order at critical moments. This is the Hindu expression of the **cosmic mediator slot** — the deity who descends from the divine realm to human history to defeat cosmic evil and restore *dharma*.
- **Śiva** absorbs the Vedic **Rudra** (a marginal, fearsome deity of storms and destruction), adds the IVC Pashupati substrate (lord of animals, meditating ascetic), and eventually becomes the supreme deity of numerous Hindu traditions — the destroyer, the dancer (*Naṭarāja*), the meditating yogi, and the cosmic phallus (*liṅga*). Śiva synthesizes Vedic, possibly IVC, and Dravidian elements more visibly than any other Hindu deity.

### The decline of Indra

One of the most striking changes from Vedic to classical Hinduism is the **decline of Indra**. In the Rigveda, Indra receives more hymns than any other deity (~250 of 1028). In classical Hinduism he is the king of the lesser gods, repeatedly humiliated by Viṣṇu, Śiva, and even human sages. He becomes a stock comic figure rather than the cosmic warrior.

This shift reflects the transformation of the Vedic religion's priorities: from the **warrior-deity who defeats chaos through combat** (the PIE storm-god function) toward the **cosmic sustainer who maintains order through dharma** (Viṣṇu) and the **ascetic yogi who transcends the dualities of cosmic conflict** (Śiva). The Śramaṇa revolution accelerated this shift by demonstrating that the most sophisticated spiritual figures were meditating renouncers, not warriors or ritual specialists.

## Continuities with PIE that pass through Vedic into later traditions

| PIE structure | Vedic expression | Later Indian elaboration |
|---|---|---|
| *ṛtá* (cosmic order maintained by divine contract) | Varuṇa/Mitra guardians of *ṛtá*; ritual as cosmic maintenance | *Dharma* as both cosmic law and individual right conduct; the Bodhisattva path as cosmic-order maintenance |
| Thunder-god vs. serpent (cosmic combat) | Indra defeats Vṛtra (Rig Veda 1.32) | Viṣṇu defeats Śeṣa/Ananta; Kṛṣṇa defeats Kāliya; persistent nāga (serpent) combat motifs |
| Horse sacrifice (royal legitimation) | Aśvamedha (Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa 13) | Survives through classical period; echoes in Kṛṣṇa's horse imagery |
| Divine twins (Aśvins) | Aśvins as healers, dawn figures, twin horsemen | Śiva's twin sons (Skanda, Gaṇeśa); twin themes in epic literature |
| Priestly sacrificial mediation | Brahmin class as cosmic intermediary | Guru as intermediary in later bhakti and tantric traditions |
| Solar mediator (*mitra-* slot) | Mitra (with Varuṇa); Sūrya | Viṣṇu's solar associations; Amitābha ("infinite light") in Buddhist cosmology |

## Sources & dating

### Tier A (primary evidence)

- **Rigveda** (~1500–900 BCE; oral composition; written manuscripts from ~10th century CE): 1,028 hymns; the primary source for early Vedic religion. Standard edition: Max Müller (1849–74); modern: Jamison & Brereton translation (Oxford UP, 2014).
- **Atharvaveda** (~1200–900 BCE): fourth Veda; contains vrātya texts, early magical/medical hymns.
- **Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa** (~800–600 BCE): prose ritual manual; contains the Aśvamedha description and early Upanishadic material.
- **Mitanni treaties** (c. 1380–1340 BCE): Vedic divine names as oath witnesses; published in Beckman, *Hittite Diplomatic Texts* (1999).
- **Kikkuli horse-training text** (c. 1350 BCE): Indo-Iranian vocabulary in Hittite administrative context.
- **Pashupati seal**, Mohenjo-daro (c. 2350–2000 BCE): National Museum of India, New Delhi. Published in Marshall, *Mohenjo-daro and the Indus Civilization* (1931).
