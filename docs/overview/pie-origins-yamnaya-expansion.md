# PIE origins, the Yamnaya expansion, and mechanisms of religious transmission

**Proto-Indo-European (PIE) religion** is a scholarly reconstruction — not a surviving text or an observed practice, but an inference from the systematic comparison of languages and mythologies across the IE family. It represents the beliefs of pastoralist societies on the Pontic–Caspian steppe before their dispersal across Eurasia, roughly 4500–2500 BCE. Understanding its archaeology, reconstructed content, and transmission mechanisms is the necessary foundation for every argument the site makes about why Vedic, Zoroastrian, Greek, Roman, Norse, and later traditions share so many structural features.

> **Scope note.** This page covers the *origins and dispersal* of PIE religion — the Yamnaya homeland, the expansion timeline, the core features, and the mechanisms by which religious material traveled. For what happened to the tradition as it entered India specifically, see [Indo-Aryan migration and Vedic religion](vedic-religion-indo-aryan-migration.md). For the specific cognate myths (storm deity vs. serpent, primal twins), see [The PIE cosmic combat myth](pie-cosmic-combat-myth.md) and [PIE twin and brother archetypes](pie-twin-and-brother-archetypes.md).

## Context

**Question:** What can we reconstruct about the religion of the PIE-speaking communities before their dispersal, and how confident can we be?

**Who the PIE speakers were:** Philologist Martin L. West's formulation is the most precise available:

> "If there was an Indo-European language, it follows that there was a people who spoke it: not a people in the sense of a nation, for they may never have formed a political unity, and not a people in any racial sense... We should probably think of them as a loose network of clans and tribes, inhabiting a coherent territory of limited size." (*Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, 2007)

This framing matters for the site's comparative argument: the PIE speakers were a **linguistic community**, not an ethnic bloc or a unified political entity. Their religion was carried not by state institutions but by the shared vocabulary and oral-poetic tradition of dispersed pastoral clans. This is why religious parallels survive across 3,000 years of separation — and why they should not be read as evidence of ethnic or racial continuity between modern populations.

**Method:** PIE religion is recovered through two converging disciplines:
- **Comparative linguistics** — reconstructing shared vocabulary for deities, rituals, and cosmological concepts from cognate words across IE daughter languages
- **Comparative mythology** — identifying narrative patterns that recur across geographically separated traditions and cannot be explained by later contact

**Tier situation:** The individual linguistic cognates (deity names, ritual vocabulary) are **Tier A** — established philological results. The narrative reconstructions (which myth sat behind the cognate words) are **Tier B** — scholarly hypothesis checked against the linguistic evidence. Any claim about what PIE *people believed* as a matter of lived religion is **Tier C**. The site uses these tools to ask what structural slots were available in the inherited religious grammar before any historically attested tradition elaborated it.

## The homeland and the Yamnaya culture

### The Pontic–Caspian steppe

The consensus homeland for PIE-speaking populations is the **Pontic–Caspian steppe** — the grassland zone stretching from modern Ukraine through Kazakhstan, north of the Black and Caspian Seas. This is David Anthony's (*The Horse, the Wheel, and Language*, 2007) and Wolfgang Haak et al.'s (2015 ancient DNA study) position, which currently dominates the field. The competing "Anatolian hypothesis" (Renfrew 1987, Bouckaert et al. 2012) — which places PIE origins in Neolithic Anatolia — has lost ground as ancient genomics has confirmed a major steppe ancestry contribution to European populations that is absent in Anatolian Neolithic genomes.

The steppe homeland matters for religious reconstruction because it was a **pastoralist, mobile environment**:
- Horses were domesticated here (Botai culture, c. 3500 BCE)
- Wheeled vehicles appear on the steppe before anywhere else (c. 3300 BCE)
- Cattle were the primary source of prestige and wealth
- Burial practices — the **kurgan** (mounded burial) — provide the primary archaeological evidence for social hierarchy and ritual

### The Yamnaya culture (c. 3300–2600 BCE)

The **Yamnaya** ("Pit Grave") culture is the archaeological horizon most consistently associated with early PIE or late-PIE dispersal. It is characterized by:
- **Kurgan burial mounds** with single inhumations, often in ochre-stained pit graves
- **High individual mobility** (strontium isotope analysis shows individuals moved widely)
- **Cattle and horse herding** as the economic base
- **Social hierarchy** — elite burials with prestige goods (weapons, ornaments, wheeled vehicles)
- **Wide geographic range** — from the Danube to the Ural River

The massive Yamnaya expansion after ~3300 BCE is now confirmed by ancient genomics: Yamnaya-related ancestry contributed approximately **75% of the genetic ancestry of Corded Ware populations** in northern Europe (Haak et al. 2015), and Yamnaya-related populations moved east toward Central Asia (the Afanasievo culture in Siberia and the Tarim Basin).

### The genetic composition of the Yamnaya

A key finding from Jones et al. (2015) and Haak et al. (2015) is that the Yamnaya themselves were a **secondary admixture population** — not an original steppe lineage but a blend of two earlier groups:

- **Eastern European Hunter-Gatherers (EHG)** — the mesolithic population of the Russian steppe, themselves partly related to Ancient North Eurasian (ANE) populations from Siberia (~50% of Yamnaya ancestry)
- **Caucasus Hunter-Gatherers (CHG)** — a distinct population from the South Caucasus who had been isolated during much of the last Ice Age (~50% of Yamnaya ancestry)

As Andrea Manica (Cambridge) summarized the finding: "their genetic make-up is a mix of Eastern European hunter-gatherers and a population from this pocket of Caucasus hunter-gatherers who weathered much of the last Ice Age in apparent isolation." All Yamnaya individuals sampled by Haak et al. (2015) belonged to Y-haplogroup **R1b**; R1a (the other major IE marker, prominent in Eastern Europe and northern South Asia today) derives from the EHG component.

The presence of a significant **Caucasus/Near Eastern genetic component** in the Yamnaya — which connects them distantly to the populations that introduced farming into Europe from the Middle East — is the basis for a minority "south of the Caucasus" PIE homeland hypothesis advocated by David Reich and Kristian Kristiansen. Anthony (2019) argues against this reading, noting that the Caucasian contribution in Yamnaya derives from an *earlier* hunter-gatherer mixture, not from a Bronze Age or late-Neolithic Caucasian population.

**Downstream genetic evidence:**
- **Bronze Age Greece** (Lazaridis et al. 2017): Mycenaean Greeks had **13–18% steppe ancestry** not present in the earlier Minoan population — confirming IE migration into Greece during the 2nd millennium BCE
- **Ireland and Iberia**: ancient DNA studies confirm R1b introduction from the steppe in the same period, replacing most prior Neolithic ancestry in Britain within a few generations
- **South Asia**: ancient DNA from the Swat Valley and Haryana shows increasing steppe ancestry contributions consistent with Indo-Aryan migration c. 1500 BCE

*(Tier A for the archaeological and genetic evidence. Tier B for the equation "Yamnaya = PIE speakers" — the genetic and cultural continuity is strong but not directly proven by text.)*

## History of research and competing hypotheses

The question of where PIE speakers originated — the *Urheimat* ("original homeland") question — has a contested two-century history that matters for how we evaluate the current consensus.

### The 19th-century foundations

Scholars of the 1800s who first recognized the IE language family had essentially only linguistic evidence. They attempted localization by reconstructing PIE vocabulary for plants, animals, and technology (the beech tree, the salmon, cattle, the wheel). This produced two competing camps: a **European hypothesis** (migration from Europe to Asia) and an **Asian hypothesis** (migration in the opposite direction). The field was then severely contaminated in the early 20th century when the question became associated with a hypothesized "Aryan race" — a now-discredited theory that was weaponized to justify European colonialism and, catastrophically, Nazi racial ideology. The term "Aryan" as a general label for PIE speakers has largely been abandoned in scholarship as a result.

### The Kurgan hypothesis (Gimbutas; consensus since ~1956)

**Marija Gimbutas** systematized the **Kurgan hypothesis** from 1956 onward — named for the kurgan burial mounds of the Eurasian steppes. The hypothesis places PIE origins in the Pontic–Caspian steppe and proposes that PIE-speaking populations expanded into Europe through several waves of migration beginning ~3500–3000 BCE, bringing a patriarchal, patrilinear, and mobile pastoral culture into contact with the previously egalitarian Neolithic farming cultures of "Old Europe." J. P. Mallory later modified the hypothesis to de-emphasize its earlier militaristic framing while preserving the steppe origin and migration chronology. The Kurgan hypothesis is "the most widely accepted theory" (Mallory 1989) and since the 2015 ancient DNA studies has moved from dominant hypothesis to near-consensus.

### The Anatolian hypothesis (Renfrew; minority since 2015)

**Colin Renfrew** (1987 onward) proposed that IE languages spread peacefully into Europe from Anatolia around 7000 BCE, with the Neolithic agricultural revolution. This would push the PIE period far earlier and attribute the spread to farming populations, not steppe pastoralists. The hypothesis faces a serious internal problem: PIE vocabulary securely reconstructs the horse, the wheel, and metal — all absent from Early Neolithic cultures. Renfrew disputed this, but after the 2015 ancient DNA papers demonstrated a massive steppe ancestry contribution to European Bronze Age populations that is absent from Anatolian Neolithic genomes, he acknowledged that "the DNA evidence had completely rejuvenated Marija Gimbutas' kurgan hypothesis." The Anatolian hypothesis in its original form is no longer the mainstream position, though a modified "Indo-Hittite" version (arguing for a very early Anatolia split before the main IE expansion) has some support.

### The Armenian/Iranian hypothesis (Reich, Wang, Kristiansen; active minority debate)

Following the 2015 ancient DNA findings, a more geographically nuanced proposal emerged. **David Reich** (2018) argued that because some IE languages (Hittite) existed in ancient Anatolia, "the most likely location of the population that first spoke an Indo-European language was south of the Caucasus Mountains, perhaps in present-day Iran or Armenia." **Kristian Kristiansen** suggested the Yamnaya culture may have had a predecessor in the Caucasus where "proto-proto-Indo-European" was spoken. Wang et al. (2018) noted that the Caucasus served as a corridor for gene flow between the steppe and southern populations, which "opens up the possibility of a homeland of PIE south of the Caucasus."

**David Anthony (2019)** offers the most detailed rebuttal: the Yamnaya's Caucasian genetic component derives from an **earlier hunter-gatherer admixture** (pre-4000 BCE), not from a Bronze Age or late-Neolithic Caucasian population; the Yamnaya paternal lineages derive from the Eastern European steppe hunter-gatherers, not from the south; and the Anatolian farmer admixture common in the Caucasus by 5000 BCE is scarce in the Yamnaya. Anthony argues for a PIE origin in the Eastern European steppe north of the Caucasus, from the EHG + CHG admixture event. This remains the majority position.

### The terminology problem: "Aryan"

The PIE root *\*aryo-* (→ Sanskrit *ārya*, Avestan *airya*, Greek forms, Germanic names) originally designated what appears to have been a **socio-cultural concept of nobility** — plausibly an in-group designation used by IE-speaking elites. It was explicitly used as a self-designation for all of their people only in the Indo-Iranian branch (hence "Indo-Aryan" is still the correct technical term for the South Asian branch of PIE). Outside Indo-Iranian, the cognate forms seem to designate only a ruling elite, not an entire ethnic group.

As a consequence:
- **"Aryan"** as a general term for PIE speakers = **abandoned** in mainstream scholarship due to Nazi contamination
- **"Indo-Aryan"** = still used specifically for the South Asian branch that settled the Indian subcontinent
- **"Indo-European" / "IE"** = the standard neutral term for the language family and its speakers

## Core features of reconstructed PIE religion

### The sky father: *\*Dyēus ph₂tḗr*

The most securely reconstructed PIE deity is the **sky father**, whose name is reconstructable from cognates in multiple branches:

| Language | Deity | Etymology |
|---|---|---|
| Vedic Sanskrit | **Dyaus Pitṛ** | *dyaus* "sky" + *pitṛ* "father" |
| Greek | **Zeus** (genitive *Dios*) | from *\*Dyēus* |
| Latin | **Jupiter** (Iuppiter) | from *\*Dyēu-pater* |
| Illyrian / Paleo-Balkan | **Dei-paturos** | direct cognate |
| Proto-Germanic | **\*Tīwaz** (→ Norse Týr) | related root |

The phonological correspondence is exact across branches — this is among the most robust reconstructions in comparative IE linguistics. The deity was associated with the **bright daytime sky**, oaths and contracts, and fatherly authority over the divine assembly. He is not a storm or thunder deity; the sky-father and the storm-god are separate figures in the PIE pantheon who are often confused in later popular accounts. *(Tier A: linguistic reconstruction.)*

### The storm / thunder god: *\*Perkʷunos*

Separately from the sky father, PIE religion included a **thunder/storm deity** whose name is reconstructable from:

- Slavic **Perun** (thunder god)
- Baltic **Perkūnas** / **Pērkons** (Lithuanian/Latvian thunder god)
- Norse ***Fjörgyn*** (mother of Thor; cognate root)
- Germanic **Þórr** (partially cognate)
- Vedic **Parjanya** (rain/storm deity; cognate, though Indra fills the primary combat slot)

This deity's signature myth is the **cosmic combat**: the thunder god defeats a monstrous **serpentine adversary** who obstructs the cosmic waters or cattle. See [The PIE cosmic combat myth](pie-cosmic-combat-myth.md) for the full comparative inventory.

### The solar/mediator deity: the *mitra-* slot

The PIE pantheon included a deity associated with the **sun, contracts, and mediation between heaven and humanity**. This "contract-god" or "covenant-deity" function survives as:

- Vedic **Mitra** (alongside Varuṇa, keeper of *ṛtá*, cosmic order)
- Avestan **Mithra** (cosmic covenant-enforcer, judge, light-deity)
- Greek **Apollo** / **Helios** (solar mediator)
- Roman solar cult figures leading to **Sol Invictus**

The *\*mitra-* root reconstructed by comparative Indo-Iranian linguistics means "contract, covenant, friendship." This is the most important PIE deity for the site's comparative argument — see [Indo-Iranian Mitra and the contract complex](indo-iranian-mitra-contract.md) for detailed treatment.

### Dumézil's tripartite ideology

The French comparative mythologist **Georges Dumézil** (1898–1986) proposed that PIE religion was organized around a **tripartite ideological structure**:

1. **Sovereignty** — priests and kings; represented by paired deities (Mitra/Varuṇa in Vedic; Jupiter/Dius Fidius in Roman)
2. **Warfare** — warriors and the martial function; Indra, Mars, Thor
3. **Fertility and production** — agricultural and pastoral abundance; the Aśvins, Quirinus, the Vanir

Dumézil found this tripartite pattern in Vedic, Roman, Norse, Celtic, and other IE mythologies and argued it reflected a PIE social ideology. His framework has been criticized (too schematic; counterexamples exist; "finding" structure where it may not be intended) but also substantially confirmed for specific cases by later scholars including Bruce Lincoln (*Priests, Warriors, and Cattle*, 1981) and Calvert Watkins (*How to Kill a Dragon*, 1995). It is a **Tier B scholarly framework** — useful for identifying structural slots, not to be applied mechanically.

### Ritual vocabulary: what the cognates reconstruct

Comparative linguistics reconstructs shared PIE ritual vocabulary with confidence:

| Reconstructed PIE root | Meaning | Cognates |
|---|---|---|
| *\*yaǵ-* | to sacrifice, to honor the gods | Vedic *yaj-* (yajna), Avestan *yaz-* (yasna), Greek *hagiós* (sacred) |
| *\*seh₂wl̥* | sun | Latin *sol*, Gothic *sauil*, Vedic *sūrya*, Greek *hḗlios* |
| *\*h₁ngʷnis* | fire | Latin *ignis*, Vedic *agni*, Lithuanian *ugnis* |
| *\*médʰu* | mead / honey drink | Greek *méthu*, Vedic *mádhu*, Old English *meodu* |
| *\*ǵʰew-* | to pour (libation) | Latin *fundere*, Greek *khéō*, Sanskrit *juhoti* |
| *\*weydh-* | to bind, unite in contract | Vedic *Varuṇa* (possibly), various contract/oath vocabulary |

The fire deity **\*H₁ngʷnis** → Vedic **Agni** → Latin **ignis** is particularly significant: both the Vedic and Roman ritual traditions center on a fire deity who receives sacrificial offerings. The cognate vocabulary suggests this is not parallel development but inherited practice.

Two **formulaic phrases** reconstruct with particular clarity across branches (Watkins 1995; Beekes 2011):
- ***\*ḱléwos n̥dʰgʷʰítom*** — "imperishable fame": Homer's *kléos áphthiton* (Iliad) and Vedic *śrávas... ákṣitam* (Rigveda) preserve the identical phrase in the same meter. This formula was probably the core purpose of the oral heroic poetry tradition — composing *kléos* for warriors was the primary social function of the IE poet class.
- ***\*sh₂wéns kʷékʷlos*** — "wheel of the sun": a reconstructed PIE solar-wheel formula. The sun as a wheel rolling across the sky is a consistent image in Vedic, Greek, and Germanic traditions, and PIE wheel vocabulary (*\*kʷékʷlos*) allows the cosmological metaphor to be reconstructed to the steppe period.

### Mythological evidence: the "Smith and the Devil" folktale

A 2016 phylogenetic analysis of Indo-European folktales (da Silva and Tehrani, *Royal Society Open Science*) used computational phylogenetic methods to trace the distribution and evolutionary history of folktale types across the IE language family. Their most striking finding: the folktale type known as **"The Smith and the Devil"** can be reconstructed to the **Proto-Indo-European period**.

The tale appears across modern IE folklore from Scandinavia to India: a blacksmith offers his soul to a malevolent being (devil, demon, death) in exchange for superhuman metalworking skill; he then uses his new ability to trap the devil (sticking him to an immovable object), avoiding the bargain. Da Silva and Tehrani argue that the reconstruction of this tale to PIE implies that **the Proto-Indo-Europeans had metallurgy** — since a tale about a smith requires a metalworking culture — which in turn provides a cultural context for the tale's origin.

This is a modest but genuinely independent line of evidence: computational folkloristics reconstructing PIE culture from narrative distribution rather than from linguistic or archaeological data. It is consistent with the Bronze Age dates for the PIE expansion and with the metallurgical activity visible at Sintashta sites. *(Tier B: the phylogenetic method is contested for deep-time reconstruction; the result is plausible but not certain.)*

### The horse: sacrifice, prestige, and cosmological vehicle

The horse occupies a structurally unique position in PIE religion:

- **Horse sacrifice** (*\*h₁eḱwos*-related sacrifice): the **Vedic Aśvamedha** (royal horse sacrifice) and the **Irish Feis Temro** (a royal inauguration involving ritual horse mating, documented by Giraldus Cambrensis for Ireland) are both interpreted as reflexes of a PIE royal horse sacrifice. The Vedic ritual is explicitly described in the White Yajurveda (Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa 13). Lincoln (*Priests, Warriors, and Cattle*, 1981) gives the comparative analysis.
- **Kurgan burials** frequently include horse remains — sometimes whole horses, sometimes sacrificed at the burial
- The horse as **vehicle of shamanic journey**: the link between horse and the shaman's spirit journey appears in Siberian/Mongolian shamanic traditions, early Vedic imagery (the Rigveda's horse poems), and Norse mythology (Odin's eight-legged horse Sleipnir as the shamanic steed between worlds)

### The shamanic substrate

Before and alongside the structured PIE theological system, steppe societies almost certainly retained **shamanic practices** that predate the PIE expansion and continued alongside later elaborations. The evidence is indirect (archaeology, comparative ethnography) but convergent:

- **Kurgan burial goods** include items associated with ritual specialists: animal teeth, ochre, unusual artifact assemblages suggesting spiritual role
- **Anthropomorphic stelae** (stone figures at kurgan sites) suggest mediation between the living and supernatural powers
- **Steppe shamanism** is documented across Siberia, Mongolia, and Turkic cultures — traditions that share steppe geography with the PIE world but are linguistically distinct
- **Animal symbolism** — the bear, horse, and bird as spiritual vehicles — persists in IE traditions from Vedic Ashvamedha to Norse Odin's ravens and ravens in Celtic tradition

The shamanic substrate does not disappear in later IE traditions; it persists most visibly in:
- **Indo-Iranian**: the *vrātya* brotherhoods (warrior-ecstatic groups in early Vedic tradition); the *haoma* / *soma* ritual altered-state tradition
- **Celtic/Germanic**: the *seiðr* shamanic practice; the *fili* (Irish poet-seer) as spiritual intermediary
- **Greek**: the Dionysiac ecstatic tradition; the *mantis* (seer) class; Pythagoras-related shamanic biography

*(Tier B–C: the shamanic substrate is reconstructed from later attestations and comparative ethnography. The specific practices are inferred, not directly documented.)*

## Timeline of Indo-European expansion

The IE dispersal was not a single event but a **series of migrations and cultural diffusions** over two millennia:

### Phase 1: Yamnaya expansion (c. 3300–2500 BCE)

The earliest archaeologically documented expansion moves in two directions:
- **Westward**: into the European steppe and then across the Carpathians into central and western Europe. Yamnaya-related populations appear in the Danube basin by c. 3000 BCE
- **Eastward**: into Central Asia — the **Afanasievo culture** of the Minusinsk Basin (Siberia) and Tarim Basin (western China) is genetically and archaeologically Yamnaya-related and represents the earliest known eastern expansion

Crucially, this expansion was enabled by two Yamnaya innovations: **wheeled wagons** (allowing mobile family units to sustain themselves on the steppe for extended periods) and **horse-based herding** (allowing larger territories to be managed). The Yamnaya were not cavalry raiders but mobile pastoralists whose economic advantages allowed them to expand into thinly populated territories.

### Phase 2: Corded Ware and Bell Beaker cultures (c. 2800–2000 BCE)

The second wave transforms European prehistory:
- **Corded Ware** (~2900–2350 BCE): covers northern and central Europe from the Rhine to the Volga. The population carries ~75% Yamnaya-related ancestry. This culture is ancestral to **Proto-Germanic, Proto-Baltic, Proto-Slavic, and Proto-Celtic** branches
- **Bell Beaker** (~2800–1800 BCE): spreads along Atlantic Europe, ultimately replacing up to 90% of the ancestry of Neolithic Britain within a few generations. Ancestral to later **Celtic and Italic** populations

### Phase 3: Indo-Iranian formation (c. 2200–1500 BCE)

- The **Sintashta culture** (c. 2100–1800 BCE, Ural steppe) represents the ancestral **Indo-Iranian** population — and is the probable origin of both the Vedic and Avestan branches. Sintashta is notable for the **earliest evidence of spoke-wheeled chariots** and for horse burials in warrior graves, suggesting the martial and equine aspects of Indo-Iranian religion were already developed
- The **Andronovo cultural horizon** (~2000–900 BCE) represents the Indo-Iranian expansion east and southeast into Central Asia — into the BMAC (Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex) zone, and eventually toward South Asia and Iran
- Contact with **BMAC** (c. 2200–1700 BCE) is critical: BMAC was an urban, sedentary culture in modern Turkmenistan/Afghanistan with its own religious practices. The synthesis between incoming steppe pastoralists and BMAC urban culture may account for elements of early Vedic religion that lack clear PIE etymology — including possibly some aspects of the *haoma/soma* ritual and certain deity names

### Phase 4: Indo-Aryan entry into South Asia (c. 1500–1200 BCE)

The specific path and mechanics of Indo-Aryan entry into South Asia remain debated, but the **consensus** is that by ~1500 BCE, Indo-Aryan-speaking groups were established in the Punjab region of northwest India and composing the earliest hymns of the **Rigveda**. See [Indo-Aryan migration and Vedic religion](vedic-religion-indo-aryan-migration.md) for detailed treatment.

A key piece of **external evidence** is the **Mitanni treaties** (c. 1380–1340 BCE, northern Syria): Hittite–Mitanni peace treaties invoke Vedic gods — **Mitra, Varuṇa, Indra, and the Nāsatya (Aśvins)** — as oath-witnesses. This places Indo-Iranian religious vocabulary in the Near East at the same period as the earliest Rigvedic composition, demonstrating that the Indo-Iranian branch had already spread far west before or during the period of Rigvedic composition, and that its religious terminology was known in the diplomatic world of the Near East. *(Tier A: the tablets are published; the divine names are unambiguous.)*

## Mechanisms of religious transmission

PIE religion did not spread as a fixed system. It traveled through distinct mechanisms that shaped it differently in each region:

### 1. Elite dominance and prestige diffusion

Small **warrior-elite groups** imposed (or were admired for) their language and ritual practices on larger indigenous populations. The evidence:
- Yamnaya expansion involved small, mobile groups whose genetics quickly dominated local populations — suggesting social dominance rather than simple demographic replacement in many cases
- The **Mitanni elite** in Syria spoke Indo-Iranian but governed a non-IE-speaking population; their religion influenced but did not replace the local Hurrian tradition
- Indo-Aryan elite vocabulary appears in **horse-training manuals** (the Kikkuli text, ~1350 BCE, Hittite archive) before Indo-Aryan languages appear widely in the archaeological record — suggesting prestige association with horse technology drove language adoption

### 2. Syncretism with local traditions

Incoming IE groups regularly **merged their deities with local ones** rather than replacing them. This is the *interpretatio* process at its earliest archaeological scale:
- In India: Vedic religion absorbed elements from **Indus Valley Civilization** traditions (see [Indo-Aryan migration and Vedic religion](vedic-religion-indo-aryan-migration.md))
- In Greece: the Greek Olympians absorbed pre-Greek deity functions (Poseidon's relationship to the pre-Greek "Earth-Shaker")
- In Iran: Zoroaster's reform retained Iranian linguistic material while restructuring the cosmological hierarchy
- With **BMAC**: elements of the Bactrian urban culture's religion (including possibly the *haoma* preparation ritual) entered the proto-Indo-Iranian synthesis in Central Asia before the split between Vedic and Avestan branches

### 3. Oral tradition: formula, meter, and the poet-class

PIE religion was transmitted in **formulaic oral verse** — a fact recoverable from comparative poetics. Calvert Watkins' *How to Kill a Dragon* (1995) demonstrated that cognate formulaic phrases appear in Vedic, Greek, Italic, Celtic, and Hittite — e.g., the phrase "imperishable fame" (*kléos áphthiton* in Homer; *śrávas... ákṣitam* in the Rigveda) is a reconstructed PIE formula preserving exactly the same meter and meaning.

This means PIE religion was not transmitted by text or temple but by a **specialist oral poet class** (*\*wekʷom teks-*, "text-weaver" → Vedic *vipra*, Greek *aoidos*, Celtic *fili*, Germanic *skald*) whose social function was to preserve and perform the sacred narratives. The fidelity of this oral transmission across 3,000+ years of separation (demonstrable from the Vedic–Greek correspondences) is remarkable and explains why structural parallels survive in traditions that lost contact millennia before their earliest texts.

### 4. Trade and intercultural contact

The steppe was not isolated. The **Royal Road** of the Achaemenid period (5th century BCE) ran along corridors that had been trade routes for millennia. Before historical documentation:
- **Obsidian** from Anatolia appears at steppe sites; **Baltic amber** reaches the Aegean
- **Tin** for bronze production traveled east–west across Central Asia (the BMAC was a tin-source hub)
- The **Silk Road** of later antiquity follows corridors already established in the Bronze Age

Religious material traveled on these trade routes. The BMAC contact with proto-Indo-Iranian communities is the clearest Bronze Age example: botanical analysis of BMAC sites has found evidence of **ephedra** (the probable *haoma* plant) in ritual contexts at Gonur Tepe (Sarianidi 2003) — suggesting that whatever the *haoma* ritual was, it was already present in the BMAC before full Indo-Iranian arrival, and was adopted into the incoming tradition.

## Regional outcomes: how PIE religion transformed across Eurasia

| Region | Core transformation | Key surviving features |
|---|---|---|
| **India (Vedic)** | PIE ritual elaborated into a complex sacrificial system; interaction with IVC and Dravidian traditions; Śramaṇa revolution | Agni, Varuna-Mitra pairing, horse sacrifice (Aśvamedha), tripartite social structure (varna) |
| **Iran (Zoroastrian)** | Ethical reform by Zarathustra; dualism sharpens; the adversary made cosmic | Ahura Mazdā vs. Angra Mainyu; Mithra/Verethragna preserved; *asha* (= Vedic *ṛtá*) as cosmic truth |
| **Greece** | Sky-father preserved strongly (Zeus); myth becomes anthropomorphic, narrative-rich; syncretism with pre-Greek traditions | Zeus / Dios; Apollo as solar mediator; hero cult as localized veneration of powerful dead |
| **Rome** | Sky-father (Jupiter); tripartite system most fully institutionalized (Dumézil's Roman case); martial emphasis | Jupiter, Mars, Quirinus triad; pontifex ritual class; *fides* (faith/contract) as civic virtue |
| **Northern Europe** | Sky-father fades; thunder-god becomes primary (Thor/Óðinn system); cosmic cycle mythology (Ragnarök) most elaborated | Thor vs. Jörmungandr; the Vanir/Aesir synthesis; the einherjar (warrior-dead) |
| **Slavic / Baltic** | Perun/Veles combat preserved; strong continuity with PIE thunder-vs-serpent | Perun, Perkūnas; harvest and agricultural ritual; ancestor cult |

The most sophisticated religious elaboration of the PIE substrate occurred in **India** and **Iran**, for different reasons: India because the tradition encountered the richest indigenous religious environment (IVC, Dravidian traditions, the Śramaṇa philosophical movements); Iran because Zoroaster's radical reform systematized the ethics and cosmology into the world's first coherent theological dualism. See [The Śramaṇa revolution and the Axial Age in India](sramana-revolution-axial-india.md) and [Zoroastrian primary texts](zoroastrian-primary-texts.md).

### Tier A (primary evidence)

- **Ancient DNA studies**: Haak et al. (2015), "Massive migration from the steppe was a source for Indo-European languages in Europe," *Nature* 522; Jones et al. - **Sintashta culture** (c. 2100–1800 BCE): archaeological evidence for earliest spoke-wheeled chariots and horse-warrior burials. Reviewed in Anthony, *The Horse, the Wheel, and Language* (2007).
