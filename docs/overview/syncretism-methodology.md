# Syncretism — concepts and methodology

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers who encounter the word "syncretism" and want to know what scholars actually mean by it, how the concept has evolved, and how to apply it carefully without either dismissing all parallels or over-asserting borrowing.
- **Question:** When religious traditions share vocabulary, images, or narrative patterns, does this constitute "syncretism"? Is syncretism a contamination, a creative achievement, or a methodological category that needs replacing?
- **Scope:** The history of the concept, major scholarly debates, and its direct application to the transmission questions on this site.

Syncretism is one of the most overused and underexamined words in comparative religious studies. It appears routinely in popular writing about ancient religion ("Christianity is just syncretized paganism") and just as routinely draws methodological fire from specialists. Understanding what is at stake sharpens every claim on this site.

---

## 1. Etymology and original usage

The word derives from Greek *synkrētismos*, used by Plutarch (*Moralia* 490B) to describe how Cretans would temporarily suspend their mutual feuding to unite against a common enemy — *"syncretism"* as opportunistic alliance, not ideological merger. The original sense is political, not religious.

The term entered theological vocabulary in the 17th century as a polemical label. In the **Syncretist controversy** of Lutheran theology (1640s–1660s), Georg Calixtus proposed reuniting Protestant, Catholic, and Eastern Orthodox traditions by focusing on shared fundamentals; his opponents — including Abraham Calovius — denounced this as *syncretism*, meaning unprincipled mixing that abandoned doctrinal integrity.

From this Protestant origin, the word carried a **negative valence** — syncretism as intellectual or spiritual compromise — well into the 20th century.

---

## 2. The 19th-century contamination model

The dominant 19th-century use of syncretism in religious history treated it as the **corruption of an originally pure tradition** by foreign elements. This framework was driven by:

1. **Romantic nationalism**: each tradition had an "authentic" ethnic-national core; mixing was degradation
2. **Protestant historiography**: early Christianity was seen as more "pure" and "original" before Hellenistic contamination
3. **Evolutionist assumptions**: religions evolved from simple to complex; borrowings introduced from outside disturbed that natural trajectory

The classic 19th-century comparative project was finding **origins**: which tradition *originally* had each motif, who *borrowed* from whom, and who therefore deserved priority. The implicit hierarchy rated traditions by purity.

This model dominated work on mystery religions and early Christianity, producing studies that argued the New Testament "borrowed" dying-and-rising imagery from Osiris, Adonis, or Mithras — often with more polemical intent than historical evidence.

---

## 3. The pagan-background school

In the early 20th century, the **History of Religions school** (*Religionsgeschichtliche Schule*) — particularly German scholars at Göttingen — pushed syncretism from polemics into mainstream historical scholarship. Key figures:

**Richard Reitzenstein** (*Hellenistic Mystery-Religions*, 1910; ET 1978): argued that early Christianity's language of death-rebirth, pneuma, gnosis, and divine mediator derived directly from a reconstructed "Oriental mystery religion" complex — a Hellenistic κοινή of mystery-cult language that permeated the Roman world. His *Poimandres* (1904) argued for a common Gnostic-Hermetic source underlying much of Paul's theology.

**Franz Cumont** (*The Mysteries of Mithra*, 1903; *Oriental Religions in Roman Paganism*, 1911): argued that Oriental mystery religions, especially Mithraism, were the main conduit through which Iranian solar and eschatological theology reached the Roman world and shaped early Christianity. Cumont's Iranian-continuity thesis for Mithraism was highly influential and later substantially revised (see [Roman Mithras and Manichaean Mithra](mithra-roman-manichaean.md)).

**Wilhelm Bousset** (*Kyrios Christos*, 1913): argued that the title *Kyrios* ("Lord") applied to Jesus derived from the Hellenistic mystery-cult use of *kyrios* for divine figures, not primarily from Jewish sources.

**Assessment:** The pagan-background school generated important hypotheses and drew genuine attention to the Hellenistic milieu. Its weaknesses were (a) reconstructing hypothetical shared "sources" that are not directly attested, (b) treating Paul's letters as straightforward windows onto Hellenistic mystery religion without attending to their Jewish rhetorical context, and (c) sometimes overconfident dating and provenance claims.

---

## 4. The corrective critique: Jonathan Z. Smith

The most influential methodological critique of the comparative tradition came from Jonathan Z. Smith, particularly in *Drudgery Divine: On the Comparison of Early Christianities and the Religions of Late Antiquity* (1990).

Smith's central arguments:

1. **The "uniqueness" apologetic and the "similarity" counter-apologetic** are mirror images of each other. Protestant apologists insist on Christianity's uniqueness; rationalist critics insist on pagan precedent. Neither is doing rigorous history.

2. **"Parallelomania"** (the term is Samuel Sandmel's, 1962, but Smith develops it systematically): the tendency to treat superficial verbal or structural similarities as genealogical connections without asking whether the terms have the same meaning in context, whether the dates allow for transmission, and what the mechanism would be.

3. **"The dying and rising gods" category is a scholarly construction**, not an ancient one. No ancient source groups Osiris, Adonis, Tammuz, Baal, and Attis together as a type. The category was assembled by 19th-century scholars and read back onto ancient sources.

4. **Comparison must be self-conscious and methodologically explicit**: it requires stating *why* these two things are being compared, *what* about them is being compared, and *what conclusion* the comparison is meant to support.

Smith's critique does not eliminate comparison; it demands that comparison be done rigorously. His constructive method involves identifying specific, documentable similarities while accounting for differences and being explicit about what counts as evidence of genealogy vs. structural resemblance.

## 5. Creative synthesis: Fowden and beyond

Against both the contamination model and Smith's somewhat minimalist critique, several scholars have argued that syncretism is better understood as a **creative and intentional practice** by ancient actors.

**Garth Fowden**, *Empire to Commonwealth: Consequences of Monotheism in Late Antiquity* (1993): In the Roman imperial period, syncretism was not contamination but **deliberate philosophical and political project**. The Syrian, Egyptian, and Anatolian religious intellectuals who synthesized Plato, Hermetic texts, Chaldean Oracles, and oriental mystery content were not confused or corrupted; they were engaging in a sophisticated project of finding the universal behind the particular. Julian's *Hymn to King Helios* is a prime example: a philosophically literate construction, not a muddle.

**Walter Burkert**, *Ancient Mystery Cults* (1987): More cautious than Fowden, Burkert argues that structural comparison of mystery cults is legitimate and informative, but each cult's integrity must be respected. The Eleusinian, Dionysiac, Orphic, Isis, Mithraic, and Samothracian mysteries shared **functional similarities** (initiation, secrecy, afterlife promise) without constituting a single unified movement. Burkert's approach allows genuine comparison while resisting the collapse of distinct traditions into each other.

**Peter Schäfer**, *Judeophobia* (1997) and related work on Jewish-Hellenistic interface: shows how Jews in the Hellenistic Diaspora deliberately adopted Greek forms (LXX translation, philosophical apologetics, synagogue architecture) while maintaining identity markers — **selective appropriation** rather than passive absorption.

## 6. Current alternatives to "syncretism"

Several scholars have proposed replacing or supplementing "syncretism" with more precise vocabulary:

| Term | Proposed by | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Religious transfer** | Stephan Krech (2012) | Emphasizes the active agency of borrowers; a thing transferred changes in the process |
| **Bricolage** | Lévi-Strauss → applied to religion by scholars like Robert Orsi | Working with "what is at hand" — selecting from available symbolic resources without a master plan |
| **Lived religion** | Robert Orsi, Meredith McGuire | Focus on practice rather than theology; religious actors mix traditions without necessarily being aware they are "syncretizing" |
| **Hybridity** | Post-colonial theory (Bhabha, Spivak) | Emphasizes power dynamics in contact situations; the hybrid is not a neutral mix but a product of colonial or imperial encounter |
| **Interpretatio graeca / romana** | Ancient term | The Greek or Roman practice of identifying foreign gods with their own (*Zeus = Jupiter = Amun*); an ancient form of comparative theology |
| **Translation** | Various (Assmann on *translatability*) | Every contact involves "translation" — putting one tradition's concepts into another's vocabulary, which always involves change |

None of these terms fully replaces "syncretism," but each captures something the broad term misses.

## 7. Application to this site

This site uses four tiers of claim (see [Method: evidence, influence, and milieu](method-evidence-and-influence.md)):

- **Secure description** (mainstream scholarly summary)
- **Specialist debate** (real schools of thought; partial evidence)
- **Hypothesis** (active research target with named mechanism and falsifier)
- **Analogy/heuristic** (illuminates questions only)

Syncretism-related claims on this site require the following methodological controls:

**1. Distinguish structural similarity from genealogical connection.** The fact that two traditions have a dying-and-rising deity does not mean one borrowed the concept from the other. Both may reflect common ANE agricultural theology, independent responses to the same agricultural conditions, or genuine contact — the evidence decides which.

**2. Name the mechanism.** "Syncretism" as a blanket explanation is not a mechanism. The mechanism is: trade routes, imperial administration, bilingual scribal communities, translation projects, military contact, diaspora communities, converted intellectuals. Identify which mechanism is plausible, on what timeline, in what direction.

**3. Watch for anachronism.** A motif in Source B that resembles Source A but post-dates Source A's attestation by 300 years cannot be used as evidence for A's influence on B without a datable transmission path.

**4. Respect the meaning in context.** The same word or image can mean different things in different traditions. *Kyrios* for Jesus and *kyrios* for Mithras are not straightforwardly the same claim if the theological contexts differ — the comparison must specify what precisely is similar and why that matters.

**5. Allow for genuine contact.** Smith's corrective goes too far if it produces a blanket skepticism about all connections. The Achaemenid empire, the Silk Road, the Mediterranean trade network, and the Hellenistic Diaspora were real historical structures that created real opportunities for religious transfer. Documented mechanisms justify documented claims.
