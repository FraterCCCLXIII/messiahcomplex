# The identical algorithm: skepticism, mysticism, and the apophatic constraint

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers who have encountered the Pyrrho–Buddhist parallel ([Alternative transmission theories §8](alternative-buddha-jesus-transmission-theories.md)) and the R23 investigation notes in the [Research backlog](research-backlog.md), and want a curated synthesis.
- **Question:** Is the apophatic move — defining ultimate reality exclusively through negation — a culturally specific theological choice, a property of literate philosophical discourse, or a deeper cognitive and linguistic constraint?
- **Scope:** Greek philosophy (Pyrrho, Neoplatonism, Pseudo-Dionysius), Buddhist philosophy (Nāgārjuna, the Pali canon), Jewish mysticism (Maimonides), Islamic mysticism (Ibn Arabī, Sufi *fanāʾ*), Christian mysticism (Meister Eckhart), Taoist metaphysics.
- **Epistemic status:** Part 1 (universal negative grammar) is **Tier B** — convergent structure documented across multiple independent primary sources; the proposed mechanism (cognitive/linguistic constraint) is **Tier C**. Part 2 (skepticism and mysticism as formally identical) is **Tier B** — structural argument well-documented in comparative philosophy literature (McEvilley, Sells). Part 3 (cognitive science extension) is **Tier C** — a methodological opening.

---

## Consensus snapshot

There is no single scholarly consensus for the unified claim presented here. What is established:

- The apophatic tradition within individual traditions is extensively documented: Carabine (Neoplatonic apophasis), Turner (Christian mystical darkness), Murti (Buddhist *śūnyatā* and negation), Sells (comparative apophatic structure across Neoplatonism, Sufism, and Christian mysticism).
- The structural parallel between Pyrrhonism and Buddhist *madhyamaka* logic has been argued by McEvilley (*The Shape of Ancient Thought*, 2002) and Kuzminski (*Pyrrhonism*, 2008) at Tier B.
- The claim that this pattern reflects a **universal cognitive constraint** rather than cultural diffusion is argued here as a hypothesis; cognitive science of religion has not yet systematically addressed it for this specific pattern.

## Part 1: The universal negative grammar

Every tradition surveyed here defines its ultimate principle — the highest thing it can point to — **exclusively through negation**. This is not a marginal eccentricity of mystical subcultures; it appears in the central philosophical vocabulary of each tradition when pressed to its furthest claim.

### The Greek evidence

**Pyrrho of Elis** (c. 360–270 BCE) built his entire philosophical vocabulary on the Greek *a-* privative — the negation prefix:

- *adiaphora* — **un**differentiated (things cannot be distinguished as better or worse)
- *astathmēta* — **un**balanced (no stable position is available)
- *anepikrita* — **un**decidable (no judgment can be made)
- *adoxastos* — **without** beliefs (the sage holds no opinions)
- *aklinēs* — **un**inclined (the sage leans toward nothing)
- *akradantos* — **un**wavering (immune to disturbance)
- *ataraxia* — **un**disturbedness (the goal: absence of all disturbance)
- *apateia* — **un**suffering (freedom from passion through absence of investment)

The entire Pyrrhonian vocabulary is a grammar of negation. There is no positive description of what the sage is, has, or knows — only descriptions of what they lack. This is not accidental word choice. It reflects a structural commitment: the moment you describe the ultimate state positively, you have made a claim about it, and claims are what disturb tranquility.

**The Neoplatonic One** (Plotinus, *Enneads* V.3.13–14; VI.9) cannot be predicated with any attribute:

> "We must not even say 'it is' of it. We can say nothing of it that would make it definite... We cannot say 'good'; we are only driven to say 'good' because we have no other way of indicating it."

Plotinus's One is beyond Being, beyond intellect, beyond unity itself. Any positive predicate would place the One within a category, and the One is the source of all categories. Every description falsifies it. The ascent to the One is therefore a progressive stripping away of all positive descriptions — the *via negativa*.

### The Buddhist evidence

The **Pali canon** records the Buddha's consistent refusal to characterize nirvāṇa positively. When asked whether the Tathāgata exists after death, the Buddha returns the four-cornered deflection (*catuṣkoṭi*): "The Tathāgata exists after death — this I have not declared. The Tathāgata does not exist after death — this I have not declared. The Tathāgata both exists and does not exist — this I have not declared. The Tathāgata neither exists nor does not exist — this I have not declared." (*Majjhima Nikāya* 72; *Aggivacchagotta Sutta*)

**Nāgārjuna** (*Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, c. 150–250 CE) made this refusal into a systematic philosophical method. For every positive proposition P, Nāgārjuna shows that P leads to contradiction, not-P leads to contradiction, both P and not-P lead to contradiction, and neither P nor not-P leads to contradiction. All four positions are equally untenable. What remains is *śūnyatā* — **emptiness** — which is itself not a positive quality but the absence of inherent existence.

**Nirvāṇa** is defined throughout the Pali canon in negatives: *nib-bāna* = extinction (the blowing-out of a flame); *a-saṃkhata* = unconditioned; *a-natta* = non-self; *a-nicca* = impermanent (what passes away); *dukkha* = un-ease. The only positive characterization the Buddha occasionally offers is *sukha* (bliss) — and the commentarial tradition immediately qualifies this as the absence of suffering, not the presence of a positive content.

### Jewish and Islamic evidence

**Maimonides** (*Guide for the Perplexed* I.50–60, 12th cent. CE) argued that every positive attribute applied to God limits and therefore falsifies the divine nature. God is not "good" in the way we are good; God is not "knowing" in the way we know. The only genuine predications are **negative**: God is not evil, not ignorant, not composite, not finite. This is not rhetorical modesty; it is a strict logical argument that positive divine attributes are impossible.

**Ibn Arabī** (Sufi philosopher, 1165–1240 CE) developed the concept of *tanzīh* — divine incomparability, the refusal of all positive likenings of God. The ultimate divine reality (*al-Ḥaqq*) is beyond all names, attributes, and determinations. The Sufi path toward *fanāʾ* (annihilation) is precisely the annihilation of the seeker's own positive identity until only what cannot be named remains.

**The Tao** (*Tao Te Ching* 1): "The Tao that can be named is not the eternal Tao." The first word of the Tao Te Ching establishes that its subject cannot be its subject — the moment you name it, it is already not it.

**YHWH's name** in rabbinic tradition cannot be spoken except by the High Priest in the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur. The divine name *YHWH* — the four consonants with vowels withheld — is technically unpronounceable in ordinary speech; substitute names (*Adonai*, *HaShem*) are used in worship. The divine name cannot be articulated because the divine reality it names exceeds any articulable content.

## Part 2: Skepticism and mysticism are formally identical

The most striking structural discovery in this survey is that **Pyrrhonian skepticism and apophatic mysticism execute the same algorithm**:

```
Step 1: Take any proposition about ultimate reality
Step 2: Apply it positively  → contradiction or inadequacy
Step 3: Apply its negation  → contradiction or inadequacy
Step 4: Apply both together → contradiction
Step 5: Apply neither        → contradiction
Step 6: Suspend judgment (or: arrive at what cannot be spoken)
Step 7: Result: silence
```

This is **Pyrrho's epochē** (suspension of judgment). It is also **Nāgārjuna's prasaṅga** (reductio ad absurdum applied to all four positions). It is also **Plotinus's ascent** (stripping away all predicates from the One). It is also the **Sufi dissolution** in *fanāʾ*. It is also **Meister Eckhart's** *Abgeschiedenheit* (detachment) in which the intellect empties itself of all images, including images of God.

The only difference between the skeptical and the mystical outcomes is the **interpretation placed on the silence**:

| Tradition | The algorithm | What fills the silence | Interpretation of result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pyrrhonism** | Epochē — suspend all judgments | *Ataraxia* (non-disturbance) | The silence is a *dead end* — the only peace available when nothing can be known |
| **Madhyamaka Buddhism** | *Prasaṅga* — reduce all four positions to absurdity | *Śūnyatā* / liberation | The silence is *fullness* — what remains when inherent existence is seen through |
| **Neoplatonism** | Strip all predicates from the One | Union with the One | The silence is *excess of being* — the One overflows all categories |
| **Christian apophasis** | Negate all divine attributes progressively | *Unio mystica* / *theōsis* | The silence is *union with God* — what language fails is where presence begins |
| **Sufism** | *Tanazzulāt* / progressive dissolution | *Fanāʾ* (annihilation) and *baqāʾ* (subsistence in God) | The silence is *absorption* — the self dissolves into what cannot be named |
| **Zen Buddhism** | *Mu* / kōan — refuse all conceptual positions | *Satori* / direct experience | The silence is *wakefulness* — what the conceptual mind cannot reach |

The algorithm is identical. The metaphysics of what fills the silence — whether it is a dead end, liberation, an overflow of being, divine presence, annihilation, or wakefulness — differs by tradition. But **the method** is indistinguishable.

### What this means for classification

This finding has a direct implication for how we classify these traditions. If Pyrrhonism and apophatic mysticism are formally identical practices, then:

- Pyrrhonism may not be a "philosophical" school in contrast to "religious" mysticism. It may be an **early form of the same practice without the metaphysical superstructure** that later traditions added.
- The "philosophical vs. religious" distinction that modern scholars apply to Pyrrho and Nāgārjuna respectively may reflect modern disciplinary boundaries, not a genuine difference in what the practices were doing.
- Pyrrho's documented contact with Indian ascetics at Taxila (~327–325 BCE) may represent not mere "influence" but an encounter between practitioners of essentially the same contemplative method operating within different vocabulary systems.

## Part 3: Why does every tradition do this?

Three hypotheses are worth distinguishing:

**Hypothesis A: Cultural diffusion.** The apophatic pattern traveled from one tradition to another. The most plausible direction would be: Indian philosophical practice → Pyrrho → Greek philosophy → Neoplatonism → Christian and Islamic mysticism. This is the genealogical hypothesis, and it has some support: Pyrrho's contact with Indian ascetics is documented, and the Neoplatonic–Christian transmission is well-documented. But it cannot explain the Taoist case (*Tao Te Ching* is pre-Alexandrian) or the appearance of negative-definition language in traditions with no plausible contact (e.g., ancient Andean or African religious thought, if it appears there).

**Hypothesis B: Independent convergence driven by the same philosophical pressure.** Any sufficiently rigorous philosophical tradition, when it pushes its central concept far enough, hits the same structural wall: language predicates finite, bounded, relational properties. The *absolute* or *ultimate* — by definition — has no finite boundary, is not bounded relative to anything else, and cannot be the relatum of a relation. Therefore no predicate can apply to it without falsifying it. The apophatic move is what any tradition discovers when it drives its concept to its limit. On this hypothesis, the convergence is **not a property of human culture but of human language applied to the concept of the absolute**.

**Hypothesis C: Cognitive constraint.** The apophatic pattern may reflect a deeper limit in how human minds process concepts at their extreme. The cognitive science of religion has documented that concepts of "supernatural agents" — gods and spirits — typically violate one or two intuitive ontological expectations (a ghost walks through walls) while conforming to all others (it still wants things). But the concept of an *absolute ultimate* violates *all* expectations simultaneously, which may trigger a specific cognitive response: withdrawal of all categorization. This would predict that the apophatic pattern appears cross-culturally wherever sufficiently abstract theological reflection occurs, regardless of cultural contact.

**Working conclusion:** Hypothesis B is the most parsimonious. It explains the convergence without requiring either universal diffusion or a specific cognitive module. The pattern is a structural property of language and predication when pushed toward the absolute. What differs between traditions is not the logical structure of the move but the spiritual and institutional framing placed around it.
