# Amitābha, Maitreya, and Gandhāra–Mahāyāna evidence

**Mithra dossier** sources link **Maitreya**, **Amitābha** (**Amida**), Gāndhārī **phonology**, and **Mithraic** light imagery. This page **separates** **documented** Buddhist textual history from **speculative** equations (**Mithras = Maitreya = Amida**).

## Maitreya: canonical anchor

- **Early** attestation: **Dīgha Nikāya** 26 (*Cakkavatti-Sīhanāda*) — **Metteyya** as **future Buddha**.
- **Name:** dominant philology derives **Maitreya** from **maitrī** / **mettā** (“loving-kindness”).
- **Art:** **Gandhāran** images often give **Maitreya** **radiant** haloes; **interpretation** ranges from **Hellenistic** solar style to **local** prestige — not automatic proof of **Iranian** god-swap.

## Amitābha / Amida and Gāndhārī spellings

**Seishi Karashima** tracks **Gāndhārī**–Prakrit **sound change** chains among **Amitābha** / **Amitāyus** forms (e.g. **Amidāha → … → Amidāyu**), tying **script** and **manuscript** spellings to **developing** Pure Land literature — this is evidence for **internal** Buddhist linguistic history (**Karashima** *Studies in Gāndhārī and Buddhist Sanskrit*, etc.; see compilation references).

## Pure Land’s **Indian** roots (counterweight to “Mithra → Amida”)

**Jan Nattier**’s *The Indian Roots of Pure Land Buddhism* argues **against** exotic **origin** stories: earliest **Mahāyāna** “buddha-field” devotion in her reconstruction clusters around **Akṣobhya** before **Amitābha**’s **systematization**. **Reddit**-quoted summaries in the raw compilation point to the same **consensus trend** — use **Nattier** and **Gāndhārī** manuscript studies, not **forum** posts, as **citations**.

**Akṣobhya** fragments in **Gāndhārī** (Bajaur-style finds; **Ingo Strauch** reports) reinforce that **pure land**-like discourse was **already** Buddhist **before** **speculative** Hellenic-origin tales.

## What remains **moderate–low** confidence

| Claim | Verdict |
| --- | --- |
| **Shared** light / vow / contract **motifs** across Eurasia | **Plausible** as **areal** and **typological** overlap |
| **Phonological** chain **Amida** ⇄ **Mithra** | **Not** accepted as regular historical linguistics in mainstream sources in the compilation |
| **“Same figure”** (Azimia abstract) | **Hypothesis**; needs **primary-text** proof |

## The emergence of Mahāyāna: Persian, Greek, and Silk Road influences

Mahāyāna Buddhism did not spring from a single source. Its emergence (c. 1st cent. BCE – 2nd cent. CE) coincides with Gandhāra's position at the intersection of multiple imperial and cultural corridors:

| Influence vector | Evidence | What it contributed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Hellenistic philosophical culture** | Greco-Bactrian kingdoms; Milindapañha (dialogue between Greek king Menander and monk Nāgasena) | Dialectical method, universalizing discourse, concept of *cosmopolis* |
| **Iranian religious culture** | Kushan coins depicting MIIRO alongside BODDO; Zoroastrian eschatology available via Achaemenid–Saka–Parthian continuum | Light-symbolism, cosmic dualism as backdrop, possible influence on Pure Land "radiance" imagery |
| **Steppe / nomadic patronage** | Kushan state support for Buddhist monasteries and Silk Road caravans | Infrastructure for text transmission; imperial prestige backing Buddhist missions |
| **Roman trade networks** | Finds of Roman coins and goods at Gandhāran sites; Periplus of the Erythraean Sea routes | Mediterranean visual vocabulary (drapery, halos, narrative relief) entering Buddhist art |
| **Internal Buddhist dynamics** | Stūpa devotion, merit transfer, cult of relics, *buddhānusmṛti* (recollection of the Buddha) | Indigenous devotional logic that needed no foreign stimulus |

The scholarly consensus (Nattier, Schopen, Harrison) increasingly treats Mahāyāna as a **gradual, polycentric development** — not a single "reformation" — in which Gandhāran and Central Asian nodes played an outsized role partly because of their exposure to multiple traditions.

## Amitābha and the celestial savior motif: a cross-tradition comparison

Amitābha / Amida represents a distinctive type: the **celestial savior** who operates from a transcendent realm and guarantees salvation (rebirth in a Pure Land) to devotees through faith, vow, or invocation. This motif has structural parallels across traditions:

| Tradition | Figure | Mechanism | Transcendent realm | Key period |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Mahāyāna Buddhism** | Amitābha / Amida | 48 vows; invocation (*nianfo / nembutsu*) | Sukhāvatī (Western Pure Land) | 1st cent. CE onward |
| **Zoroastrianism** | Mithra (as judge + helper) | Righteous action + Mithra's covenant protection | Garōdmāna (House of Song) | Achaemenid+ |
| **Christianity** | Christ at the Parousia | Faith in Christ's atoning sacrifice | Kingdom of Heaven / Paradise | 1st cent. CE |
| **Egyptian religion** | Osiris | Proper burial rites + moral judgment (weighing of the heart) | Field of Reeds (*Aaru*) | 3rd millennium BCE+ |
| **Hinduism (Vaishnava)** | Viṣṇu / Nārāyaṇa | *Prapatti* (surrender); Viṣṇu rescues devotees | Vaikuṇṭha | Post-classical (Āḻvārs, c. 6th cent. CE+) |

**Structural convergences:**
- A **named divine being** in a **specific transcendent location** guarantees post-mortem salvation.
- **Faith, devotion, or invocation** replaces (or supplements) ritual performance or personal merit.
- The savior figure's **vow or covenant** precedes and underwrites individual salvation.
- **Light and radiance** are central symbols (Amitābha = "Infinite Light"; Christ as "Light of the World"; Mithra as light-judge; Osiris as radiant in the afterlife).

**Caution:** These parallels are **typological** — they show that multiple traditions independently or semi-independently developed "celestial savior + transcendent realm + faith" packages. Direct borrowing (e.g., "Amitābha IS Mithra") requires historical-linguistic evidence that has not been established; the Karashima phonological work addresses internal Buddhist development, not Iranian derivation. The simultaneous presence of MIIRO and BODDO on Kushan coins demonstrates **co-existence** in the same cultural field, which is the necessary precondition for influence, but not proof of it.
