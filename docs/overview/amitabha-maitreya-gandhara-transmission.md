# Amitābha, Maitreya, and Gandhāra–Mahāyāna evidence

**Mithra dossier** sources link **Maitreya**, **Amitābha** (**Amida**), Gāndhārī **phonology**, and **Mithraic** light imagery. This page **separates** **documented** Buddhist textual history from **speculative** equations (**Mithras = Maitreya = Amida**).

## Maitreya: canonical anchor

- **Early** attestation: **Dīgha Nikāya** 26 (*Cakkavatti-Sīhanāda*) — **Metteyya** as **future Buddha**.
- **Name:** dominant philology derives **Maitreya** from **maitrī** / **mettā** (“loving-kindness”).
- **Art:** **Gandhāran** images often give **Maitreya** **radiant** haloes; **interpretation** ranges from **Hellenistic** solar style to **local** prestige — not automatic proof of **Iranian** god-swap.

## Amitābha / Amida and Gāndhārī spellings

**Seishi Karashima** tracks **Gāndhārī**–Prakrit **sound change** chains among **Amitābha** / **Amitāyus** forms (e.g. **Amidāha → … → Amidāyu**), tying **script** and **manuscript** spellings to **developing** Pure Land literature — this is evidence for **internal** Buddhist linguistic history (**Karashima** *Studies in Gāndhārī and Buddhist Sanskrit*, etc.; see compilation references).

## Pure Land’s **Indian** roots (counterweight to “Mithra → Amida”)

**Jan Nattier**’s *The Indian Roots of Pure Land Buddhism* argues **against** exotic **origin** stories: earliest **Mahāyāna** “buddha-field” devotion in her reconstruction clusters around **Akṣobhya** before **Amitābha**’s **systematization**. Use **Nattier** and **Gāndhārī** manuscript studies, not forum posts, as citations.

### Akṣobhya: the earliest attested Pure Land focus

The *Akṣobhyatathāgatasyavyūha Sūtra* — translated into Chinese in the **2nd century CE** — is among the oldest known Mahāyāna or Pure Land texts. Its protagonist is a monk who vows not to harbor anger toward any being until enlightenment, proves "immovable" (*Akṣobhya*), and thereby becomes a celestial Buddha. Key evidence:

- **Bajaur Collection Gāndhārī texts** (Pakistan): newly discovered manuscripts contain fragments of an early Mahāyāna sūtra mentioning Akṣobhya; palaeographic dating suggests **late 1st to early 2nd century CE** (preliminary report: Ingo Strauch, 2010).
- **Mahāvastu** of the Mahāsāṃghikas: mentions Akṣobhya as one of Śākyamuni's teachers when the Bodhisattva was on the **8th bhūmi** — attesting early recognition as a celestial Buddha even before formal Mahāyāna texts crystallized.
- The *Dīrgha Āgama* of the Dharmaguptakas already references buddha-fields in a form suggesting the concept is **early and Indian**, not imported.

This positions **Akṣobhya — not Amitābha — as the likely earliest celestial Pure Land focus**, reinforcing Nattier's argument for an internal Indian Buddhist origin rather than an Iranian or Hellenic import. Amitābha practice emerged somewhat later within the same developmental logic.

## What remains **moderate–low** confidence

| Claim | Verdict |
| --- | --- |
| **Shared** light / vow / contract **motifs** across Eurasia | **Plausible** as **areal** and **typological** overlap |
| **Phonological** chain **Amida** ⇄ **Mithra** | **Not** accepted as regular historical linguistics in mainstream sources in the compilation |
| **“Same figure”** (Azimia abstract) | **Hypothesis**; needs **primary-text** proof |

:::note Azimia (2023)

**Moein Azimia**, "Mithra in Light of Buddhism and Manichaean: Savior Figure" (2023) proposes that **Maitreya and Mithra are the same figure**, drawing on historical parallels, linguistic similarities, and the Manichaean Mihr as a light-bearer, judge, and cosmic guide. The strong identity claim — same figure, genealogical derivation — is not accepted by the specialist consensus in Buddhist or Iranian studies, and should not be presented as settled. But the evidence Azimia marshals — the shared *mitra-* root, the milieu convergences, the Manichaean identification — points at a real circumstantial case that deserves to be engaged directly rather than dismissed by asserting that internal Buddhist development is "adequate." The stronger claim (identity) is Tier C at best; the weaker claim (milieu influence on Maitreya's cosmic elaboration) is Tier B. Treat Azimia (2023) as a useful orientation to the circumstantial evidence cluster, with the identity conclusion held at arm's length.

:::

## The emergence of Mahāyāna: Persian, Greek, and Silk Road influences

Mahāyāna Buddhism did not spring from a single source. Its emergence (c. 1st cent. BCE – 2nd cent. CE) coincides with Gandhāra's position at the intersection of multiple imperial and cultural corridors:

| Influence vector | Evidence | What it contributed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Hellenistic philosophical culture** | Greco-Bactrian kingdoms; Milindapañha (dialogue between Greek king Menander and monk Nāgasena) | Dialectical method, universalizing discourse, concept of *cosmopolis* |
| **Iranian religious culture** | Kushan coins depicting MIIRO alongside BODDO; Zoroastrian eschatology available via Achaemenid–Saka–Parthian continuum | Light-symbolism, cosmic dualism as backdrop, possible influence on Pure Land "radiance" imagery |
| **Steppe / nomadic patronage** | Kushan state support for Buddhist monasteries and Silk Road caravans | Infrastructure for text transmission; imperial prestige backing Buddhist missions |
| **Roman trade networks** | Finds of Roman coins and goods at Gandhāran sites; Periplus of the Erythraean Sea routes | Mediterranean visual vocabulary (drapery, halos, narrative relief) entering Buddhist art |
| **Internal Buddhist dynamics** | Stūpa devotion, merit transfer, cult of relics, *buddhānusmṛti* (recollection of the Buddha) | Indigenous devotional logic that needed no foreign stimulus |

The scholarly consensus (Nattier, Schopen, Harrison) increasingly treats Mahāyāna as a **gradual, polycentric development** — not a single "reformation" — in which Gandhāran and Central Asian nodes played an outsized role partly because of their exposure to multiple traditions.

## Amitābha and the celestial savior motif: a cross-tradition comparison

Amitābha / Amida represents a distinctive type: the **celestial savior** who operates from a transcendent realm and guarantees salvation (rebirth in a Pure Land) to devotees through faith, vow, or invocation. This motif has structural parallels across traditions:

| Tradition | Figure | Mechanism | Transcendent realm | Key period |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Mahāyāna Buddhism** | Amitābha / Amida | 48 vows; invocation (*nianfo / nembutsu*) | Sukhāvatī (Western Pure Land) | 1st cent. CE onward |
| **Zoroastrianism** | Mithra (as judge + helper) | Righteous action + Mithra's covenant protection | Garōdmāna (House of Song) | Achaemenid+ |
| **Christianity** | Christ at the Parousia | Faith in Christ's atoning sacrifice | Kingdom of Heaven / Paradise | 1st cent. CE |
| **Egyptian religion** | Osiris | Proper burial rites + moral judgment (weighing of the heart) | Field of Reeds (*Aaru*) | 3rd millennium BCE+ |
| **Hinduism (Vaishnava)** | Viṣṇu / Nārāyaṇa | *Prapatti* (surrender); Viṣṇu rescues devotees | Vaikuṇṭha | Post-classical (Āḻvārs, c. 6th cent. CE+) |

**Structural convergences:**
- A **named divine being** in a **specific transcendent location** guarantees post-mortem salvation.
- **Faith, devotion, or invocation** replaces (or supplements) ritual performance or personal merit.
- The savior figure's **vow or covenant** precedes and underwrites individual salvation.
- **Light and radiance** are central symbols (Amitābha = "Infinite Light"; Christ as "Light of the World"; Mithra as light-judge; Osiris as radiant in the afterlife).

**Caution:** These parallels are **typological** — they show that multiple traditions independently or semi-independently developed "celestial savior + transcendent realm + faith" packages. Direct borrowing (e.g., "Amitābha IS Mithra") requires historical-linguistic evidence that has not been established; the Karashima phonological work addresses internal Buddhist development, not Iranian derivation. The simultaneous presence of MIIRO and BODDO on Kushan coins demonstrates **co-existence** in the same cultural field, which is the necessary precondition for influence, but not proof of it.

## The *Amitāyurdhyāna Sūtra* and the sun contemplation

The claim that Amitābha worship has solar origins — specifically connected to Iranian Mitra/Mithra — rests partly on the first of sixteen visualizations in the ***Amitāyurdhyāna Sūtra*** (観無量寿佛経, *Guan Wuliangshou Fo Jing*, "Meditation Sutra"), a text composed in Central Asia and translated into Chinese by Kālayaśas in **424 CE**.

### The first visualization: 日想観 (*rìxiǎng guān*, "Sun Contemplation")

The sūtra instructs the practitioner as follows:

> "Sit properly, facing west. Contemplate the sun as it sets. With eyes open or closed, fix the mind on the sun as it descends toward the horizon, establishing a firm mental image of it like a drum suspended in the sky. Having seen the sun, maintain that image whether your eyes are open or shut."
> — *Amitāyurdhyāna Sūtra*, T.12 no. 365, 341c–342a (summary)

This establishes the **west** as the direction of Sukhāvatī (the Pure Land) and makes the **setting sun** the perceptual anchor for the entire sixteen-visualization sequence. The subsequent visualizations move through water, ice, jeweled earth, trees of jewels, and ultimately to the forms of Amitābha himself and his two attendant bodhisattvas.

### What this solar orientation may and may not represent

The sun contemplation is frequently cited as evidence of Iranian solar influence on Pure Land Buddhism. The argument runs: Amitābha = "Infinite Light"; the west = the direction of sunset and Pure Land; the sun as the first visualization object; → Iranian/Mithraic solar theology shaped the Amitābha concept in Gandhāra.

This argument deserves careful calibration:

**What supports a genuine solar dimension:**

1. **Amitābha means "Infinite Light"** (*amita* = measureless; *ābhā* = light/radiance). Karashima's phonological chain confirms that the light-name is **prior** to the life-name (Amitāyus). The concept of immeasurable luminosity is foundational, not derivative.

2. **The Gandhāran double-halo** (*nimbus + mandorla*): Art-historical analysis (Tanabe 2020; see below) identifies the presence of a full-body aureole (*prabhāvalī*) as the iconographic marker specifically for Amitābha images at Gandhāra — a visible translation of the Buddha's *vyāmaprabhā* (six-foot radiance), one of the Thirty-Two Great Marks of the Mahāyāna Buddha. The earliest Amitābha images at Gandhāra are therefore **defined by light-emission** as their primary characteristic.

3. **The ambient Kushan cultural context**: Kushan coins depict both MIIRO (Mithra) and BODDO (Buddha) on the same series, demonstrating that solar-Iranian and Buddhist religiosity operated in the same cultural field. Iranian solar vocabulary — divine radiance (*xwarnah*), the solar yazata Mithra as covenant judge — was ambient in the milieu within which Amitābha iconography crystallized.

4. **The sun contemplation as ritual orientation**: Sitting facing west and meditating on the setting sun as the threshold between the living world and the Pure Land — like the Chinvat Bridge as threshold between the living world and Ahura Mazda's realm in Zoroastrian eschatology — is a structural parallel real enough to have prompted comparison.

**What limits the Iranian-origin argument:**

1. **The western direction is not uniquely Iranian.** In Vedic-Indian cosmology, the west is associated with Varuṇa and with the realm of death (Yama governs the south; the sun sets in the west). The western orientation of the Pure Land may reflect an **Indian cosmological convention** independently, not Iranian borrowing.

2. **The sun contemplation is a visualization technique, not solar worship.** The goal is not to venerate the sun but to use the visible sun as an accessible object of concentration (*samādhi*) before progressing to increasingly subtle visualizations. Buddhist meditation manuals frequently use visible objects as anchors. The sun is chosen because it is universally available and directionally fixed at sunset. This is pedagogically motivated.

3. **The "drum suspended in the sky" (*懸鼓*) metaphor** appears to be a Chinese literary elaboration without parallel in Sanskrit or Gāndhārī fragments, suggesting adaptation in the Central Asian/Chinese transmission stage.

4. **The Amitāyurdhyāna Sūtra is not a Gandhāran text.** It was composed in Central Asia (~early 5th cent. CE), after the Gandhāran Amida images Tanabe analyzes. It may reflect a later, more syncretically developed stage than the Gandhāran iconography it incompletely describes (Tanabe 2020 notes the sūtra's iconographic inaccuracies about Gandhāran Mahāsthāmaprāpta).

5. **The Fujita-Tanabe rejection of the specific equations** (see next section) applies equally here: even if the sun contemplation reflects solar-religious vocabulary in its Gandhāran/Central Asian environment, this does not establish Mithra as the genealogical ancestor of Amitābha.

### Tiered assessment of the solar connection

| Claim | Tier | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Amitābha's name means "Infinite Light" and this is the earliest concept (prior to Amitāyus) | A — Secure | Karashima phonological analysis; confirmed by Tanabe's iconographic typology |
| The sun contemplation in the *Amitāyurdhyāna Sūtra* uses the setting sun as the first visualization object, orienting the practitioner westward | A — Secure | Textual evidence |
| Gandhāran Amitābha images are iconographically distinguished by double halos (light-body) | A — Secure | Tanabe 2020; Kurita; Marshall |
| The ambient Kushan milieu (MIIRO + BODDO coins) shows co-existence of Iranian solar and Buddhist religiosity | A — Secure | Numismatic evidence |
| Iranian solar vocabulary and imagery colored the Gandhāran Buddhist milieu within which Amitābha iconography crystallized | B — Specialist debate | Plausible cultural influence; mechanism unspecified; no direct textual evidence |
| The sun contemplation was deliberately modeled on Iranian solar cult practice | C — Hypothesis | No textual evidence; structural parallel only |
| Amitābha derives genealogically from Mithra/Mitra as a figure | D — Rejected by specialists for the specific equation | Fujita 1970; Tanabe 2020 reject specific triad equations; these rejections target structural mappings (Avalokiteśvara = Mithra, Amitāyus = Zurvan) using evidence shown to be wrong, not the broader milieu-influence question |

## Tanabe (2020): the Gandhāran Amida triad's iconographic origins

Katsumi Tanabe's "Origin of the Amida Triad (阿弥陀三尊) in Gandhāra" (*Japan Society for Hellenistic-Islam Archaeological Studies*, 2020) is the most recent systematic art-historical treatment, published in an interdisciplinary journal specifically bridging Hellenistic, Iranian, and Buddhist studies. Its findings are directly relevant to the solar and Zoroastrian influence questions.

### Amitābha vs. Amitāyus: the double-halo distinction

Tanabe argues that the two terms correspond to two iconographic types in Gandhāran sculpture:

- **Amitābha (Infinite Light)** type: the central preaching Buddha is adorned with **double halos** — a round nimbus (*prabhā*) around the head *and* a full-body mandorla/aureole (*prabhāvalī*) surrounding the entire figure. The mandorla is the visualization of the *vyāmaprabhā* (six-foot rays of light), one of the Thirty-Two Great Marks of the Mahāyāna Buddha.

- **Amitāyus (Infinite Life)** type: the central Buddha has only a round nimbus, no mandorla. "The infinite life cannot be visualized by any means because it is an invisible notion, while the infinite radiance of Amitābha can easily be visualized by the figurative form of mandorla/aureole" (Tanabe 2020: 3).

This typological argument supports Karashima's phonological conclusion: Amitābha (light) preceded Amitāyus (life). The earliest identifiable Gandhāran Amida images are light-type — the concept of immeasurable radiance is foundational.

### The origin of the two attendant bodhisattvas: an internal Buddhist development

Tanabe's central argument is that the Amida triad (Amitābha flanked by Avalokiteśvara and Mahāsthāmaprāpta) evolved from **Gandhāran Buddhist preaching scenes**:

1. The prototype was an elaborate "complex stele" depicting the preaching Buddha Amida surrounded by twelve bodhisattvas arranged in tiers
2. Two bodhisattvas were progressively isolated from this audience and elevated to flanking positions:
   - **Avalokiteśvara** prototype: the pensive bodhisattva seated to the Buddha's left, contemplating the teaching — derived from the posture of prince Siddhārtha in Gandhāran life-story reliefs
   - **Mahāsthānaprāpta** prototype: the bodhisattva seated to the Buddha's right, occupying the most honored position (*mahāsthāna* = "great position/rank") — identified not by function but by positional importance
3. Mahāsthānaprāpta's positional nature explains why the right flanking position in Gandhāran Amida triads was frequently occupied by Maitreya or Mañjuśrī — the seat was "open" to any bodhisattva before iconographic crystallization

**The triad is dated to approximately mid-2nd to 3rd century CE** at Gandhāra, associated with the reign of Kanishka I (127–151 CE) on the basis of the BODDO image on Kushan gold coins.

### Explicit rejection of Zoroastrian triad equations

A note on scope before reading these rejections: Tanabe's target is the specific claim that the *three-figure Amida triad* maps onto a Zoroastrian divine triad. That is a structural equation using specific figures (Avalokiteśvara = Mithra, Amitāyus = Zurvan, etc.). His demolition of that equation is well-grounded. It does not address — and is not intended to address — the broader questions of whether the *mitra-* etymological root is relevant, whether Iranian solar vocabulary shaped the Gandhāran milieu, or whether the Manichaean Mitri-Burkhan identification reflects a real conceptual overlap. Those questions remain open even after the triad equations are disposed of.

Tanabe directly addresses and rejects the three main proposals for Zoroastrian origin of the Amida triad:

**De Mallmann (1948):** proposed the equation Avalokiteśvara = Mithra (sun god), Amitāyus = Zurvan akarna (Endless Time), Mahāsthāmaprāpta = Verethragna (war god).

*Tanabe's rejection:* (1) Zurvan akarna is **not attested** in Kushan Zoroastrianism — the Kushan coin evidence shows Miiro (Mithra), Oshlagno (Verethragna), and Ooromozdo (Ahura Mazdah), but not Zurvan; Zurvan worship became prominent only in later Sasanian Iran and Sogdiana (6th–7th cent. CE), well after the Gandhāran triad crystallized. (2) Fujita (1970) had already "perfectly condemned" all Zoroastrian-influence views because "their comparative method does not prove anything but simple and partial resemblance."

**Hayashi (1986):** proposed Sraosha (Zoroastrian yazata) as the origin of Avalokiteśvara, because Sraosha listens to voices — paralleling Avalokiteśvara's compassionate hearing of suffering beings.

*Tanabe's rejection:* Sraosha's actual function in Kushan Zoroastrianism (confirmed by the **Rabatak inscription** of Kanishka I) was as a **guardian of the soul of the dead** at the Chinvat Bridge judgment — not as a general compassionate listener. The functions do not align sufficiently. Moreover, Sraosha was assimilated with the Indian Skanda/Kārttikeya on Kushan coins, showing his religious identity was already being recast in Hindu terms, not Buddhist.

**Imoto (2009) and Mori (2010):** more recent proposals for general Zoroastrian influence on the Amida concept.

*Tanabe's assessment:* All these views are "beyond doubt beside the mark" (Tanabe 2020: 6, citing Fujita 1970 and Fussman 1999).

### The Rabatak inscription and actual Kushan Zoroastrianism

Tanabe draws on the **Rabatak inscription** (North Afghanistan, Bactrian language, reign of Kanishka I) as evidence for what Kushan Zoroastrianism actually looked like. The inscription names the three Zoroastrian psychopomps associated with Kanishka's afterlife protection: **Srošardo** (Sraosha), **Narasao** (Nairiosangha), and **Miiro** (Mithra) — these are the guides and judges of souls at the Chinvat Bridge.

This is the actual Kushan Zoroastrian triad of relevance. It does not map onto the Amida triad. The three Buddhist figures are distinct in function: the central Buddha is a teacher and savior (not a judge); Avalokiteśvara is the bodhisattva of compassion; Mahāsthāmaprāpta is associated with wisdom-light. The functional and structural parallels claimed by de Mallmann are artifacts of superficial comparison, not of genuine doctrinal mapping.

### What Tanabe's paper supports (and does not support) for the Iranian-influence question

| Conclusion Tanabe supports | Conclusion Tanabe does not support |
| --- | --- |
| The double-halo is the iconographic marker for Amitābha (light) vs. Amitāyus (life) | The Amida triad is a transformation of a Zoroastrian divine triad |
| The Amida triad evolved from internal Gandhāran Buddhist preaching scenes | Avalokiteśvara derives from Sraosha, Mithra, or any Iranian deity |
| The Amitāyurdhyāna Sūtra was composed in Central Asia (~5th cent. CE) and contains iconographic inaccuracies | Zurvan akarna was a Kushan religious concept that entered Buddhism |
| Kushan Zoroastrianism's actual triad (Sraosha, Nairiosangha, Mithra) is documented in the Rabatak inscription | The sun contemplation practice was borrowed from Iranian solar religion |
