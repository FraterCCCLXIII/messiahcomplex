# Menorah as encoded Tree of Life: the Asherah survival inside monotheism

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers tracking the cosmic-axis / Tree of Life complex and its survival in Jewish ritual after the suppression of explicit goddess-worship.
- **Question:** Is the Temple menorah a cosmologically encoded form of the Asherah — the sacred tree-pole of Canaanite religion suppressed by the Deuteronomistic reform — and if so, what does this tell us about the deep grammar of tree-of-life theology inside Jewish monotheism?
- **Scope:** The botanical language of Exodus 25, the Asherah as cosmic tree in Canaanite/pre-reform Israelite religion, the menorah in Philo and Josephus, and the continuity between Mesopotamian sacred-tree ideology and Jewish liturgical practice. Tier labels follow [standard method](method-evidence-and-influence.md).

## The botanical description in Exodus

The Torah's instructions for the menorah (Exodus 25:31–40) are botanical with unusual specificity. The Hebrew describes:

- *qāneh* (קָנֶה) — "stalk" or "reed/cane" — the central shaft
- *qᵉnēh menorah* (קְנֵה מְנוֹרָה) — "branches of the menorah" — the lateral arms (six of them)
- *gāvîaʿ* (גָּבִיעַ) — "cups" shaped like *pereḥ šāqēd* (פֶּרַח שָׁקֵד) — "almond blossom flowers" (Exodus 25:33–34)
- *kaptōr* (כַּפְתֹּר) — "bulbs" or "knobs" (the nodes between flowers)
- *pereḥ* (פֶּרַח) — "flower" or "blossom"

The menorah is an **almond tree** — specifically the *šāqēd* (שָׁקֵד), which in Hebrew is also the root of the word for "to watch" / "to be wakeful" (a pun exploited in Jeremiah 1:11–12 where YHWH shows Jeremiah an almond branch as a sign that "I am watching"). The seven branches with twelve sets of botanical elements (three on each of six arms, plus four on the central shaft) encode an almond tree in stylized metal form.

The almond tree (*Amygdalus communis*) is the first tree to flower in the spring in the Levant — it blooms in late winter (January–February) before its leaves emerge, making it a natural symbol of divine vigilance and the first light of renewal. This association is explicit in Jeremiah 1:12 and Numbers 17:8 (Aaron's rod — an almond branch — buds, blossoms, and yields almonds overnight as proof of divine selection). (Tier A: the botanical language of Exodus is textually documented.)

## The Asherah and the sacred tree-pole

The **Asherah** (אֲשֵׁרָה) is attested in Ugaritic, Phoenician, Hebrew, and Aramaic sources as both:

1. A Canaanite goddess — the consort of El ("Asherah of the Sea" in the Baal Cycle) and, controversially, a consort of YHWH in some pre-reform Israelite contexts
2. A **cultic object** — a stylized wooden pole or carved tree set up beside altars (Deuteronomy 16:21: *"you shall not plant an Asherah of any tree beside the altar of the LORD"*)

The **Kuntillet Ajrud inscription** (c. 800 BCE, in the Sinai) reads: *"I bless you by YHWH of Samaria and by his Asherah."* Two storage jars from this site also bear drawings of a sacred tree flanked by ibexes — the canonical Near Eastern "Tree of Life" iconographic form, identical to the Assyrian sacred tree. This is the same stylized-tree form that Simo Parpola traces from the Neo-Assyrian royal reliefs to the Kabbalistic Sefirot (see [Parpola and the Assyrian roots of the Sefirot](assyrian-tree-sefirot-parpola.md)).

Additional evidence:
- **Lachish ewer** (c. 1200 BCE): painted tree flanked by ibexes with the inscription "Elat" (goddess)
- **Taanach cult stand** (10th century BCE): four levels of iconography, including a sacred tree flanked by animals at the top
- **Biblical polemics**: the Deuteronomistic History repeatedly describes kings erecting or maintaining Asherahs inside or near YHWH's Temple (1 Kings 14:23; 2 Kings 21:3, 7; 2 Chronicles 33:19). Josiah's reform (c. 621 BCE) specifically includes the removal and burning of the Asherah from the Temple (2 Kings 23:6)

The Asherah pole is the Levantine form of the Mesopotamian *kigkanu* / sacred tree complex — a cosmic axis marker set up beside the altar to indicate the presence of the divine. See [The cosmic axis: world tree, pillar, and stupa](cosmic-axis-world-tree-stupa.md).

(Tier A: the Kuntillet Ajrud and Lachish evidence is archaeological fact; Tier B: the interpretation of what the Asherah's relationship to YHWH was.)

## The menorah as encoded Asherah

The convergence between the Asherah and the menorah becomes structurally explicit when the following parallels are noted:

| Feature | Asherah pole | Temple menorah |
|---|---|---|
| **Form** | Stylized tree or wooden pole | Seven-branched botanical tree in gold |
| **Material** | Wood (living or carved) | Hammered gold (Exodus 25:31) |
| **Location** | Beside the altar, in high places, near YHWH's Temple | Inside the Temple, before the inner veil |
| **Light** | None intrinsic; associated with fertility/life | Bears the *ner tamid* ("eternal light"), the perpetual flame |
| **Number** | Seven described as the "complete" sacred number in related contexts | Seven branches explicitly |
| **Botanical** | Sacred tree associated with fertility and divine presence | Almond tree with flowers and buds |
| **Axis-mundi function** | Marks the divine presence-point | The lamp whose light = divine presence before the Holy of Holies |

The most compelling argument is the **spatial substitution**: the Asherah pole stood *outside* the Temple, beside the altar. After Josiah's reform, the pole was removed and burned. The menorah stood *inside* the Temple, before the veil. The external sacred-tree marker was suppressed; the internal botanical lamp-tree was retained. The Asherah's life-force function (fertility, divine presence, cosmic tree) was transferred to the menorah's light-giving function (YHWH's presence as light, the *Shekhinah*). The tree became a lamp because fire/light is the acceptable theological mode for divine presence in a monotheistic framework that has eliminated the goddess-association of the physical tree.

This is not a coincidence of form — it is a documented theological move. The Josian reform *preserved* the cosmic-tree structure inside the Temple while *suppressing* the goddess-identified external form. The menorah is the Asherah after theological sanitization. (Tier B: the structural argument is strong; specific deliberate substitution is not documented in any text.)

## What Philo, Josephus, and rabbinic sources say

The menorah's cosmic significance was explicit in Second Temple period interpretation:

**Philo of Alexandria** (*Life of Moses* 2.102–103; *Questions on Exodus* 2.73–82): The menorah represents the seven planets; the central branch is the sun. The lamp-light is the logos (divine word/reason) that illuminates the cosmos. Philo is applying Babylonian planetary theology (the seven celestial bodies = seven lamps) to the Temple object — but the underlying intuition that the menorah encodes the structure of the cosmos is clear.

**Josephus** (*Jewish War* 5.217; *Antiquities* 3.144–146): The menorah's seven lamps represent the seven planets; the twelve loaves of the bread of presence represent the twelve zodiac signs; the altar incense represents the four elements. Josephus is mapping the Babylonian cosmological framework explicitly onto the Temple furniture.

**Zechariah 4:1–14**: In the post-exilic prophetic text, Zechariah's vision of the menorah flanked by two olive trees is interpreted as YHWH's spirit (*rûaḥ*) and the two "anointed ones" (the governor Zerubbabel and the high priest Joshua). The menorah is *still* flanked by trees in the prophetic imagination — the Asherah's flanking animal or tree guardians have been replaced by olive trees, but the three-part structure (central lamp-tree flanked by two subsidiary presences) is preserved.

The **Dead Sea Scrolls Temple Scroll** (11QT) describes the menorah with elaborate botanical precision, consistent with the Exodus text. The Qumran community clearly understood the menorah as a cosmological object.

(Tier A for the Philo, Josephus, and Zechariah evidence; Tier B for the interpretation that these readings encode pre-reform tree-goddess theology.)

## Connection to the seven-lamp = seven-planet = seven-heaven structure

The menorah's seven branches map onto the same numerical structure as:

- The **seven planets** of Babylonian astrology (Sin/Moon, Nabu/Mercury, Inanna/Venus, Shamash/Sun, Nergal/Mars, Marduk/Jupiter, Ninurta/Saturn)
- The **seven heavens** of Jewish Merkabah mysticism and the Mandaean seven-*matartā*
- The **seven Hekhalot** palaces through which the mystic ascends
- The **seven grades** of Mithraic initiation
- The **seven-branched candelabrum** in the Manichaean Tree of Life (attested in Bezeklik cave paintings)

The menorah is therefore not just an Asherah survival — it is the Israelite form of the *same seven-level cosmic structure* that appears across Babylonian astronomy, Iranian religion, and the mystery cults. The persistent seven-fold structure reflects the Babylonian cosmological framework that the Jewish exile transmitted into the Temple cult. The menorah literalizes the seven-sphere cosmos as a lamp-tree standing in the divine presence. See [The cosmic axis: world tree, pillar, and stupa](cosmic-axis-world-tree-stupa.md) and [Babylonian astral religion and the universal transmission language](babylonian-astral-religion.md).

## The *ner tamid* as the kigkanu

The **eternal light** (*ner tamid*) — the perpetual flame that burns before the Ark or in front of the Temple veil — is the specific liturgical form in which the Sumerian *kigkanu* (the eternal sacred tree at Eridu, "whose sheen is pure lapis lazuli," drawing from the Apsu) survives in Jewish practice:

- The *kigkanu* is described as glowing / luminous ("its sheen is pure lapis lazuli")
- It stands at the cosmic center (*Dur-an-ki*, "Bond of Heaven and Earth")
- It connects the cosmic deep-water wisdom (Apsu) to the heavens
- It cannot be cut or destroyed — it is eternal and permanent

The *ner tamid* is:
- Luminous (literally: perpetual light)
- Stands at the cosmic center (before the Holy of Holies)
- Represents the unbroken connection between YHWH and the community
- Cannot be extinguished — it is permanent and eternal

The transformation: cosmic tree → eternal lamp. The Sumerian kigkanu was already described as luminous; the transition to a flame encoding the same "permanent divine presence" is semantically continuous. The menorah as *ner tamid*-bearer is the oldest Near Eastern sacred tree tradition expressed through the new medium of fire/light that was theologically acceptable after the Josian monotheistic reform. (Tier B for the kigkanu connection; Tier A for the *ner tamid* as permanent divine presence marker.)
