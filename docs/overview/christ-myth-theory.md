# Christ Myth Theory and the Historical Jesus Debate

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers who have worked through [Historical-Critical Scholarship on the NT](/docs/overview/historical-jesus-critical-scholarship) and want to understand the outer boundary of the debate—the fringe position that Jesus never existed as a historical person—alongside the mainstream scholarly response.
- **Question:** What is the Christ myth theory, how does it differ from mainstream NT critical scholarship, and why does the scholarly consensus reject it while acknowledging that much about Jesus remains uncertain?
- **Scope:** The history of the "quests for the historical Jesus," the Christ myth theory (mythicism) and its main proponents, the mainstream counter-arguments, and the emerging "social memory" approach that has partially superseded earlier criteria-based methods.
- **Epistemic status:** The distinction between mainstream critical scholarship and mythicism is important to preserve: critical scholarship accepts that a historical Jesus existed while questioning the details of his biography. Mythicism denies his existence entirely. These are not simply different points on the same spectrum—they represent fundamentally different positions with vastly different levels of scholarly support.

## The three quests for the historical Jesus

Scholarly effort to recover the historical Jesus behind the theological overlay began in the 18th century and has gone through three distinct phases.

### The First Quest (19th century–1906)

The First Quest emerged from Enlightenment historical consciousness. Hundreds of "lives of Jesus" were written, each attempting to reconstruct the historical figure using rational criteria. Key landmarks:

- **David Friedrich Strauss**, *Das Leben Jesu* (1835): Rejected supernatural events as mythical elaborations developed by the early church to present Jesus as fulfilling Jewish prophecy. Strauss did not deny that Jesus existed, but argued that the miraculous content was mythological overlay.
- **The *Religionsgeschichtliche Schule*** (History of Religions School, 1890s onward): Used higher criticism to compare Christianity to other ancient religions, arguing it was syncretistic—influenced by Hellenistic Judaism and mystery cults—rather than simply the continuation of the Hebrew Bible.
- **Bruno Bauer** (1841–1852): Went further than Strauss, arguing that Jesus was primarily a literary figure. His work was heavily criticized and largely ignored.
- **Albert Schweitzer**, *The Quest of the Historical Jesus* (1906): Closed the First Quest by demonstrating that scholars had projected their own era's values onto Jesus. Schweitzer's own conclusion was that the historical Jesus was an apocalyptic prophet who expected an imminent divine intervention—and was wrong. This portrait made the historical Jesus useless as a modern ethical teacher, which disturbed many readers.

Martin Kähler's influential distinction (*The So-Called Historical Jesus and the Historic Biblical Christ*, 1892) ran through this period: faith does not depend on the historian's Jesus; the Christ encountered in scripture and proclamation is the proper object of Christian faith. This framing—**"the Jesus of history" vs. "the Christ of faith"**—has structured NT scholarship ever since.

### The Second Quest (1953–1970s)

Initiated in 1953 in reaction to Rudolf Bultmann's skepticism about the historical Jesus, the Second Quest introduced formal **criteria of authenticity** for evaluating Gospel material:

- **Criterion of dissimilarity**: Material that could not be derived from Judaism or early Christianity is most likely authentic to Jesus.
- **Criterion of embarrassment**: Material that embarrassed the early church (e.g., Jesus' baptism by John, suggesting subordination) is more likely historical because they would not have invented it.

This quest declined in the 1970s partly due to the waning influence of Bultmann. Its results are now considered largely superseded, though not disproven.

### The Third Quest (1980s–present)

The Third Quest introduced new criteria—**historical plausibility**, **criterion of rejection and execution**, and **cumulative circumstantial evidence**—and is interdisciplinary and global. It has incorporated archaeological research and Second Temple Jewish studies.

Its primary contribution has been to firmly situate Jesus *within* first-century Palestinian Judaism rather than over against it. The dominant portrait to emerge: Jesus was an **apocalyptic prophet** (Ehrman, Sanders) who expected divine intervention and whose crucifixion for political reasons launched a diverse movement.

The Third Quest also clarified a structural problem: all material about Jesus was preserved and transmitted by the emerging church, making it impossible to access a Jesus independent of early Christian interpretation.

## The demise of authenticity criteria and the social memory approach

Since the late 2000s, scholars including Chris Keith, Anthony Le Donne, and James D. G. Dunn have questioned the criteria of authenticity themselves. According to Keith, the criteria are **literary tools indebted to form criticism**, not genuine historiographic tools—they were designed to identify pre-Gospel traditions, not historical facts, but have been misused to claim access to the "real" Jesus.

Le Donne and Dunn propose a **"social memory" approach**:

- Memories are not static; they are shaped by the present needs of the community that holds them.
- The Jesus tradition is not a theological invention of the early church, but neither is it unmediated biographical data.
- Memories are refracted through "types"—pre-existing narrative templates that shape how experiences are retained and narrated.

Dunn (*Jesus Remembered*, 2003) argues: "The only realistic objective for any 'quest of the historical Jesus' is Jesus *remembered*." The goal shifts from recovering the "real Jesus" to understanding how the memory of Jesus was formed and how it shaped early Christian identity.

According to Keith, there may be a historical Jesus who is "ultimately unattainable but can be hypothesized on the basis of the interpretations of the early Christians." This is not mythicism—it accepts that a historical figure existed—but it substantially qualifies what historical method can claim to know about him.

## What the Christ myth theory claims

The **Christ myth theory** (also: Jesus mythicism, Jesus myth theory, Jesus ahistoricity theory) is the fringe position that Jesus of Nazareth never existed as a historical person. The story of Jesus, in this view, is entirely or substantially mythological.

This is categorically different from mainstream critical scholarship, which accepts that Jesus existed while questioning the biographical details in the Gospels.

Most mythicists follow a threefold argument first articulated by Bruno Bauer and systematized by later proponents:

1. **Pauline silence**: The authentic Pauline letters contain almost no biographical information about Jesus—no parables, no Sermon on the Mount, no named miracles. This is taken to suggest Paul's "Jesus" was a celestial or mythological being, not a recent historical figure.
2. **Gospels as non-historical**: The Gospels are theological writings resembling the "mythic hero archetype," not historical biography. They imposed a fictitious historical narrative on a mythological savior figure.
3. **Absence of contemporary sources**: No non-Christian sources from Jesus' lifetime mention him. This silence is taken as evidence against historical existence.

Additionally, many mythicists argue that early Christianity had **syncretistic origins**, drawing on Jewish Wisdom literature, Hellenistic mystery cults, Zoroastrian dualism, and other traditions to construct a mythological savior figure.

## Key mythicist positions

**"Jesus atheism"** (strongest form): There was never a historical Jesus at all. Notable proponents: Paul-Louis Couchoud, Earl Doherty, Thomas L. Brodie, Richard Carrier.

- **Earl Doherty** (*The Jesus Puzzle*, 1999; *Jesus: Neither God nor Man*, 2009): Paul's Christ originated as a myth derived from Middle Platonism with Jewish mystical influence. A historical Jesus emerged only among Christian communities in the second century. Q's Jesus and Paul's Christ were combined in the Gospel of Mark by a predominantly Gentile community.
- **Richard Carrier** (*On the Historicity of Jesus*, 2014): Originally Jesus was a "celestial deity" — an angelic figure in a supernatural realm who was crucified by Satan and his demons in the heavens, not on earth. The Gospels later historicized this mythological figure.

**"Jesus agnosticism"** (weaker form): Whether there was a historical Jesus is unknowable. Notable proponents: Robert M. Price, Thomas L. Thompson.

- **Robert M. Price** (*Deconstructing Jesus*, 2000): The Jesus of the NT is a "composite figure" assembled from various traditions; if a historical individual existed, their story was completely assimilated into the "Mythic Hero Archetype."
- **Thomas L. Thompson** (*The Messiah Myth*, 2007): Biblical accounts of Jesus are mythical in nature, based on Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Greek literary models. Thompson: "A negative statement, however, that such a figure did not exist, cannot be reached: only that we have no warrant for making such a figure part of our history."

**George Albert Wells** (evolutionist position): In his later work, Wells accepted that a historical Galilean preacher existed whose teachings were preserved in Q, and that Paul's mythical Christ was a separate tradition later fused with this historical figure in the Gospel of Mark. This is not full mythicism but a "two-stream" hypothesis.

## Beyond mythicism: conspiracy theories of Gospel authorship

At the outermost fringe — beyond even mythicism — are theories that claim to identify *who specifically* fabricated the Gospels and for what political purpose. The most prominent example is Joseph Atwill's *Caesar's Messiah* (2005).

**Atwill's thesis:** The New Testament Gospels were composed by a group connected to the Flavian imperial dynasty — Vespasian, Titus, and Domitian — primarily through Flavius Josephus, the Herodian princess Berenice, and the general Tiberius Julius Alexander. Having defeated the Jewish Zealots in the First Jewish-Roman War (66–73 CE), the Flavians needed a pacifist, pro-Roman Jewish religion to neutralize continued Jewish resistance. Christianity, in this theory, was their solution — a deliberate propaganda construct.

**Why this is rejected — including by mythicists:** Richard Carrier, the most academically credentialed proponent of mythicism, analyzed Atwill's alleged typological parallels between the Gospels and Josephus and concluded that they are "either coincidences, mistranslations, or references to Old Testament sources or tropes." Carrier explicitly distanced himself from Atwill's thesis. Robert Price, another mythicist, called one of Atwill's specific suggestions "attractive" but described his methodology as demonstrating "the limitless possibilities of perverse and gratuitous interpretations of the text." The mainstream scholarly reception has been dismissal; the theory has no traction in academic NT studies, ancient history, or Roman imperial history.

**Tier: Rejected fringe — not accepted by mainstream scholars or by mainstream mythicists.**

### The real Flavian-Christian connection (what the evidence does show)

Separate from Atwill's theory, there is genuine historical evidence that members of the Flavian family had personal connections to early Christianity, which is significant for understanding how Christianity penetrated Roman aristocratic circles:

- **Titus Flavius Clemens** (nephew of Vespasian) and his wife **Flavia Domitilla** were likely converts to Christianity or Judaism in the Domitianic period (81–96 CE). Domitian executed Clemens and exiled Domitilla, allegedly for "atheism" (*atheotes*) — the Roman charge typically levelled at those who rejected the imperial cult, which included both Jews and Christians (Cassius Dio, *Roman History* 67.14).
- **Pope Clement I**, traditionally the third or fourth bishop of Rome (fl. late 1st century CE), has a name that clearly references the Flavian family. The legendary *Acts of Saints Nereus and Achilleus* (6th century) identifies him as the son of Titus Flavius Sabinus. The historical relationship between Clement and the Flavian household is uncertain, but the onomastic connection (his name *Clemens* was a cognomen of the Flavian family) is real.
- These connections are evidence for the early penetration of Christianity into Roman aristocratic networks — part of the sociology of how a Judean movement became a Mediterranean-wide phenomenon within a few generations. They are *not* evidence that Flavians invented Christianity.

**Tier B — Historical footnote:** Flavian family members had documented connections to early Christian (or Jewish-Christian) communities; this is real evidence for the social history of Christianity's spread. It does not support Atwill's authorship claims.

## The mainstream scholarly response

The Christ myth theory finds virtually no support among academic specialists in NT, ancient history, early Christianity, and archaeology. The relevant scholarly communities—secular historians as well as confessional scholars—reject it.

**Why mainstream scholarship accepts a historical Jesus:**

- **Multiple independent sources**: Bart Ehrman estimates approximately 30 surviving independent sources written by 25 authors who attest to Jesus, including non-Christians. Archaeologist Ken Dark identifies at least 14 independent sources from seven authors within a century of the crucifixion (2023). More evidence exists for Jesus than for any other notable individual from first-century Galilee.
- **Paul knew eyewitnesses**: Paul explicitly states he met Peter (Jesus' closest disciple) and James (Jesus' brother) in Jerusalem approximately 36 CE, within a few years of the crucifixion (Galatians 1:18–20). Paul was a contemporary of Jesus—even if he never met him in life.
- **What Paul implies about Jesus**: Even without narrating his life, Paul implies that Jesus was human ("born of a woman," Gal 4:4), Jewish ("born under the law"), had brothers, and was crucified by his contemporaries (1 Thess 2:14–16). According to Christopher Tuckett: "Even if we had no other sources, we could still infer some things about Jesus from Paul's letters."
- **Josephus and Tacitus**: The partially authentic *Testimonium Flavianum* (Josephus, *Antiquities* 18) and the reference in Tacitus (*Annals* 15.44) provide non-Christian confirmation that Jesus was crucified under Pilate. From these sources alone scholars can establish: he existed, was named Jesus, was called messiah, had a brother named James, and was executed by Pontius Pilate.
- **No ancient denial**: No ancient enemy of Christianity—no Jewish, Greek, or Roman polemicist—ever claimed Jesus had not existed. The claim that Jesus never existed is not attested before the Enlightenment (Whealey 2015).

**The "dying-and-rising god" parallel rejected:**

A major plank of older mythicism—that Jesus was modeled on dying-and-rising gods like Osiris, Attis, or Mithras—has been rejected by mainstream historians of religion. Per Jonathan Z. Smith (*Encyclopedia of Religion*, 2005): "There is no unambiguous instance in the history of religions of a dying and rising deity... The majority of evidence for Near Eastern dying and rising deities occurs in Greek and Latin texts of late antiquity, usually post-Christian in date." Most perceived parallels are either chronologically later than Christianity, based on misreading of sources, or reflect the kind of "parallelomania" (Evans) that finds structural similarities without genealogical dependence.

For this site's approach to these parallels, see [Method: evidence, influence, and milieu](/docs/overview/method-evidence-and-influence) and [Paul, Mithraism, and historicity (tiered notes)](/docs/overview/paul-mithraism-and-historicity-notes).

**Ehrman's framing (most influential scholarly critique):**

Bart Ehrman (*Did Jesus Exist?*, 2012) makes two complementary points: (1) mythicist writings are almost entirely the work of non-specialists without credentials in NT, ancient history, or related fields; (2) the real problem with Jesus is not that he is a myth, but that he was "far *too* historical"—a first-century Judean Jew whose apocalyptic worldview is utterly foreign to modern sensibilities. Attempts to make Jesus into a contemporary moral teacher, Ehrman argues, distort him just as much as mythicism does—only in the opposite direction.

## Why this matters for this site's comparative project

Mythicism is relevant to this site's scope in two ways:

**1. The dying-and-rising god template (rejected):** Mythicist arguments frequently claim that Jesus was modeled on Osiris, Mithras, Dionysus, Adonis, or other figures. This site's comparative project takes these parallels seriously as motifs and possible transmission evidence—but the methodology of the site's [tier framework](/docs/overview/method-evidence-and-influence) requires distinguishing **motif similarity** (Tier B–C) from **genealogical dependence** (requires evidence of contact, mechanism, chronology). The mythicist use of these parallels typically skips this step. The specific cases — Tammuz, Adonis, Attis, Osiris — are examined in detail in [Jesus in Comparative Mythology](/docs/overview/jesus-comparative-mythology), which documents why scholars now regard the "dying-and-rising god" archetype as "largely a misnomer based on imaginative reconstructions and exceedingly late or highly ambiguous texts" (J. Z. Smith, *Encyclopedia of Religion*, 1987). See also [Paul, Mithraism, and historicity](/docs/overview/paul-mithraism-and-historicity-notes) for the specific debunking of fabricated Mithras–Christ parallels (Higgins, Doane, Graves).

**2. The "cosmic Christ" in Paul:** The observation that Paul's Christ is a pre-existent, cosmic, angelic figure (Carrier's starting point, though his conclusions are fringe) is actually a point of agreement with mainstream scholarship. Larry Hurtado, Bart Ehrman, Andrew Chester, and others accept that Paul conceives of Jesus as an exalted, heavenly figure operating in a Jewish "chief angel" framework. The disagreement is whether this means Jesus never existed (mythicist) or that his earliest interpreters quickly elevated him to divine status after his death (mainstream). See [Angelomorphic Christology: Michael and Mithra](/docs/overview/angelomorphic-christology-michael-mithra) for this in detail.

## Scholarly reception

The Christ myth theory is considered a fringe theory by mainstream scholarship and finds virtually no support among NT scholars, ancient historians, or archaeologists, whether secular or confessional:

- **Van Voorst** (2000): "The theory of Jesus' nonexistence is now effectively dead as a scholarly question."
- **Ehrman** (2012): "It is fair to say that mythicists as a group, and as individuals, are not taken seriously by the vast majority of scholars in the fields of New Testament, early Christianity, ancient history, and theology."
- **Casey** (2014): The view that Jesus did not exist is "the view of extremists," "demonstrably false," and "professional scholars generally regard it as having been settled in serious scholarship long ago."
- **Grant** (1977): "Modern critical methods fail to support the Christ-myth theory... if we apply to the New Testament the same sort of criteria as we should apply to other ancient writings containing historical material, we can no more reject Jesus' existence than we can reject the existence of a mass of pagan personages whose reality as historical figures is never questioned."
- **Hurtado** (2017): "The 'mythical Jesus' view doesn't have any traction among the overwhelming number of scholars working in these fields, whether they be declared Christians, Jewish, atheists, or undeclared as to their personal stance."

Common criticisms of mythicist methodology:

- Reliance on arguments from silence (Jesus not mentioned in sources where his absence is unremarkable)
- Outdated and discredited "parallelomania"—finding structural similarities without demonstrating genealogical dependence
- Misrepresentation of secondary scholarship (quoting scholars in support of claims those scholars explicitly reject)
- Methodological reliance on Rank–Raglan mythotypes that would classify known historical figures as mythological
- Most prominent proponents are not trained specialists in NT, ancient history, or related fields

**Popular reception:** Despite scholarly rejection, mythicism has gained popular traction via the internet, particularly through the documentaries *The God Who Wasn't There* (2005) and *Zeitgeist* (2007), and through writers like Richard Carrier. A 2015 Church of England poll found 22% of British respondents did not believe Jesus was a real person; a 2020 YouGov poll found 15%.

## The key distinction: mythicism vs. mainstream critical scholarship

This is worth stating explicitly because it is frequently confused in popular discussion:

| Position | What it claims | Scholarly status |
|---|---|---|
| **Mythicism** | Jesus of Nazareth never existed as a historical person | Fringe — rejected by virtually all specialists |
| **Mainstream critical scholarship** | A historical Jesus existed and was crucified; his detailed biography is heavily shaped by theology and tradition | Tier A/B — the standard working model of all serious NT scholarship |
| **Christian fundamentalism** | The Gospels are historically accurate, verbatim records | Not the working model of academic NT scholarship |

The three positions are frequently collapsed in popular discourse. Mythicists often characterize mainstream critical scholars as "fundamentalists" for accepting that Jesus existed; apologists sometimes characterize critical scholars as "mythicists" for accepting that the Gospels are theological documents. Both characterizations are wrong.
