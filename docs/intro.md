# Messiah Complex

This project is a collaborative research compilation, built by a small group using AI-assisted tools for cross-referencing, drafting, and collation across multiple languages and scholarly traditions. It maps the evidence for messianic and mediator figures across Eurasia — asking not only what patterns recur, but where historical connection can be argued with mechanisms and dated evidence, and where similarity is structural or typological only.

The scope runs from Proto-Indo-European reconstructions (~4500 BCE) through the Achaemenid contact window with Second Temple Judaism (~550–330 BCE), the Gandhāran Buddhist corridor (3rd cent. BCE – 4th cent. CE), the Roman mystery world, and into medieval eschatologies. Each research area operates with different evidence density. The site is explicit at every step about what tier of claim a given page is making.

---

## The research question

A cluster of motifs recurs across Zoroastrianism, Judaism, Christianity, Buddhism, Islam, and Greco-Roman traditions: a **future restorer**, **cosmic judgment**, **sacrifice that generates eternal life**, and a **divine mediator** between heaven and humanity. Are these:

1. **Structural universals** — recurrent narrative roles with no shared origin?
2. **Areal convergences** — neighboring cultures sharing motifs through proximity?
3. **Attested contact** — empire, trade, and translation moving specific titles, texts, and images on datable horizons?
4. **Deep inheritance** — an older IE or West Asian narrative substrate?

The site works through all four — separating look-alike from genealogy, naming mechanisms, and signaling where evidence supports each layer. See [Method: evidence, influence, and milieu](overview/method-evidence-and-influence.md) and [Exploring transmission and deep narrative patterns](overview/exploring-transmission-and-deep-patterns.md).

## The stakes of comparison

Popular comparisons between Christ and Mithras, or Buddha and Jesus, circulate widely. Most of the parallels invoked — Mithras's virgin birth, his twelve apostles, his crucifixion — have no ancient attestation. They were fabricated in 19th-century polemical literature (Higgins 1836; Doane 1882) and have been recycled without fact-checking ever since. A project like this one stands or falls on whether it replaces those fabrications with something better — an honest account of what actually overlapped between these traditions, through which mechanisms, and at what horizons.

The real overlaps are less dramatic but more historically significant. Six eschatological concepts — bodily resurrection, cosmic dualism, final judgment, paradise vocabulary, a named cosmic adversary, and angelic hierarchies — appear in Jewish literature as a logically interdependent system in the same Persian-period century-window. The timing and structure are better explained as adoption of a coherent Iranian theological package through documented Achaemenid contact than as six coincidentally parallel developments. Persian loanwords (*pardēs*, *rāz*, *dāt*) embedded in Biblical Hebrew provide direct linguistic evidence of cultural absorption. The Gandhāran Buddha's radiate halo is visually identical to the Hellenistic solar-crown iconography that circulated from Rome to the Kushan frontier. Kushan coins show *MIIRO* (Mithra) and *BODDO* (Buddha) struck by the same mint. These are not analogies — they are dated material and textual evidence.

The mechanisms of transmission are underexamined relative to the content comparisons. Empire, diaspora, bilingual translation, and trade networks moved ideas in ways that are traceable: Persian administrative loanwords, Greek papyrus fragments, coin iconography, and datable manuscript traditions are all tools this project uses alongside textual comparison. Understanding *how* a concept moved — and through which political and economic infrastructure — matters as much as identifying that it resembles something else.

The same functional position recurs across traditions that still shape the lives of billions: a cosmic mediator who guarantees a covenant, witnesses truth, and is associated with a future renovation of the world. That figure appears in the Mitanni treaty cuneiform (14th century BCE), in Zoroastrian Yasht 10, in the Dead Sea Scrolls, in Paul's letters, in Roman Mithraism, and in Shia eschatology. A clearer historical account of where that concept originated and how it was transmitted is not a provocation to any living tradition — it is the kind of intellectual history that treats religious ideas as having real origins in real time.

This project is AI-assisted. We use large language models for collation, cross-referencing, and drafting across sources that would otherwise require specialist reading in a dozen languages and academic fields. AI assistance compresses the research cycle but does not substitute for verification. Claims here should be checked against primary sources and the peer-reviewed literature cited on each page. The tier system described below is partly a response to the epistemic risks of AI-assisted synthesis: it flags the difference between what is established, what is debated, what is a hypothesis, and what is merely suggestive.

## The research program

The investigation begins with a single recoverable word: Proto-Indo-Iranian *\*mitra-*, meaning "contract" or "binding covenant obligation." By roughly 1400 BCE there is a hard documentary anchor: a cuneiform treaty between the Mitanni and the Hittites, found at Boğazköy, invokes *Mitra* alongside Indra, Varuṇa, and the Nāsatya twins as divine oath-witnesses — before any Vedic or Avestan text was composed. This places the contract-god at the intersection of the Indo-Iranian world and the ancient Near East before the Indian and Iranian branches fully diverged. Avestan Yasht 10 then elevates the figure enormously: Mithra becomes a thousand-eyed cosmic witness, "lord of broad pastures," so powerful that Ahura Mazdā instructs Zarathustra to venerate him as an equal.

Before the Iranian contact that this research maps most extensively, a prior encounter had already occurred. The Babylonian exile (587–538 BCE) placed the intellectual class of a covenant-theology tradition inside the most sophisticated cosmological system of the ancient world. Two documented literary relationships mark the depth of that encounter: the Enuma Elish creation epic shares a near-verbal sequence of creation acts with Genesis 1, and the Atrahasis/Gilgamesh XI flood narrative has the same structure, bird-release sequence, and mountaintop landing as Genesis 6–9. A third layer — the Babylonian zodiac and planetary-sphere cosmology — would travel further still, underlying Mithraic initiation grades, Hermetic planetary theology, and Paul's heavenly-spheres Christology. The exile in Babylon was the first synthesis event in this sequence, and it structurally prepared Jewish theology to receive Iranian eschatological concepts when Persian rule began.

The most important historical chapter opens with the Achaemenid empire. Cyrus the Great's conquest of Babylon (539 BCE) placed Jewish communities under the administration of an empire whose religion was one of the most developed eschatological systems in the ancient world. What makes this area's argument compelling is cluster coherence: six eschatological concepts — resurrection, cosmic dualism, final judgment, paradise vocabulary, a named cosmic adversary, angelic hierarchies — are logically interdependent in Zoroastrian theology and arrive in Jewish literature as a coherent system in the same Persian-period century. Persian loanwords enter Biblical Hebrew. The Hellenistic period extends the contact: the Septuagint imports Platonic and Stoic vocabulary into a Semitic theological tradition; Philo's Logos theology provides the immediate intellectual context for John 1:1; and the Maccabean crisis crystallizes the eschatological vocabulary that shaped both Christianity and Islam.

Running through the entire timeline is a solar synthesis that functions as the primary visual and theological language for the cosmic mediator. The PIE solar substrate — the contract-keeping Mitra/Mithra associated with light and the witness function, and the solar ancestor figure *Vivasvant* whose name survives in the Mahāyāna cosmic Buddha *Vairocana* — provides the inherited template. In the Mediterranean, this template was amplified through the convergence of solar cult (Helios → Apollo → Sol Invictus) and mystery school initiation, where the death-descent-rebirth structure mapped the solar cycle metaphorically. Osiris, Attis, Dionysus, and Mithras all participate in this structure, which is why solar and mystery cult overlap so extensively in the Roman imperial period. At the Gandhāran node, the same template resurfaces in the radiate halo on Buddha images borrowed from Hellenistic solar iconography, and in Vairocana — the Cosmic Sun-Buddha whose name is etymologically cognate with Vedic *Vivasvant*. Solar imagery is not one feature among many; it is the visual and conceptual language that made the cosmic mediator legible across traditions from the Rigveda to Revelation.

The eastern arm traces how the Iranian-Buddhist contact zone — centered on Gandhāra (modern Pakistan/Afghanistan borderlands) — became a site of extraordinary religious synthesis. By the Kushan period (1st–3rd century CE), royal coinage depicts Mithra and the Buddha side by side — material, dated evidence of religious pluralism in a single political milieu. The Gandhāran Buddha's Greek-style radiate halo, the Pyrrho-Buddhist parallel proposed by Christopher Beckwith, and the Saka-Scythian hypotheses regarding the Śākya clan name together constitute the site's most actively debated open questions. The eastern corridor raises the same questions about transmission mechanism that the western corridor raises for Paul's celestial Christology: not whether the traditions resemble each other, but through which specific historical infrastructure the resemblance developed.

The arc closes with the Sasanian empire (224–651 CE), when Zoroastrianism was first institutionalized as a state religion, when Manichaeism emerged as a deliberately synthetic tradition explicitly connecting Iranian, Christian, and Buddhist soteriology, and when the Babylonian Talmud was compiled under Iranian administration. The Academy of Gondishapur became the transmission node through which Greek philosophy reached the Islamic world. The Islamic conquest of Persia (651 CE) did not erase but transmitted the Sasanian cultural inheritance: Zoroastrian eschatological concepts entered Shia Islam through Persian administrative converts, producing the theology of the Hidden Imam in occultation — the same functional slot, refilled under new political conditions with new vocabulary.

## Research areas

The following sections describe the primary research areas covered by this project. These areas are not rigid zones with sharp boundaries; evidence from one overlaps considerably with another, and the relationships between them are part of what the research examines.

### Proto-Indo-European deep grammar

The PIE stratum is the most speculative layer of the site's argument, and the most important for explaining why similar patterns appear in traditions that never historically contacted each other. The evidence here is comparative philology and archaeology rather than texts: no PIE speaker wrote anything down. The case for a reconstructed PIE mediator-slot, cosmic combat myth, twin archetypes, and solar deity complex rests on cognate analysis across daughter languages — a methodology that is Tier A for specific etymological connections and Tier C for broader structural inferences. This layer does not prove a single origin for all cosmic mediator figures; it explains why certain types of figure keep appearing in traditions whose direct historical contact is otherwise poorly documented.

### The Bronze Age world system

The Bronze Age world system (c. 3000–1200 BCE) is the material precondition for later transmission — an integrated trade network connecting the Indus Valley Civilization, the Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex (BMAC), Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Aegean before any of the traditions covered by this site had fully formed. Indus seals appear at Ur and Nippur; lapis lazuli mined in Badakhshan appears in IVC jewelry, Mesopotamian royal graves, and Egyptian New Kingdom burial goods. This is Tier A material evidence for a connected world. The Bronze Age collapse (~1200 BCE) dismantled this integrated system within a generation, scattering populations and religious traditions across the early Iron Age landscape — which is partly why the same structural patterns surface in later Vedic, Zoroastrian, Mesopotamian, and Greek traditions. The Egyptian contribution enters here as well: solar monotheism, the equation of cosmic order with divine justice (*Ma'at*), and the royal ideology of a divine son who mediates between human and cosmic realms were part of the shared Levantine theological inheritance long before the Achaemenid period.

### The Indo-Iranian substrate

The oldest documentary anchor for the site's argument is the Mitanni treaty (~1400 BCE), which places *Mitra*, Indra, Varuṇa, and the Nāsatya twins as oath-witnesses before any Vedic or Avestan text was composed. The haoma/soma ritual complex — an exact linguistic cognate across the Iranian-Indian split — places both branches in shared priestly continuity before they separated: the same psychoactive plant, the same ritual vocabulary, the same association with immortality. What makes this stratum analytically distinctive is that it precedes all the historically documented contacts between Indo-Iranian and Semitic traditions, providing the substrate that those later contacts would elaborate rather than create from nothing. The Zoroastrian reform is the first major documented revision of this shared inheritance — and the tradition whose eschatological architecture most directly entered Second Temple Judaism.

### Mesopotamia and the Babylonian exile

This area examines the first documented synthesis event in the sequence: fifty years of Jewish scribal contact with the most sophisticated cosmological system of the ancient world. Two specific parallels rise to the level of documented textual relationship rather than analogy — the Enuma Elish/Genesis 1 near-verbal creation sequence and the Atrahasis-Gilgamesh XI/Genesis 6–9 flood narrative — making Babylonian literary influence on the Hebrew Bible one of the most secure findings in the relevant scholarship. The Babylonian zodiac and planetary-sphere cosmology are equally consequential: they underlie the graded-initiation framework of Roman Mithraism, the heavenly-ascent sequences of Jewish apocalypticism, and the heavenly-spheres imagery in Paul and Revelation. Understanding Babylonian contact is a prerequisite for evaluating Persian contact: the tradition that absorbed Babylonian cosmology, flood narrative, and wisdom literature was structurally prepared for the next layer.

### The Achaemenid and Hellenistic contact window

This is the most evidence-dense area on the site. The argument rests not on individual parallels but on cluster coherence: six eschatological concepts — resurrection, cosmic dualism, final judgment, paradise vocabulary, a named cosmic adversary, and angelic hierarchies — are logically interdependent in Zoroastrian theology and arrive in Jewish literature as a coherent system in the same Persian-period century. Their arrival together is better explained as adoption of a coherent Iranian theological package than as six coincidentally parallel developments. Persian loanwords (*pardēs*, *rāz*, *dāt*) embedded in Biblical Hebrew provide direct linguistic evidence of cultural absorption, and Achaemenid theophoric names (Mithredath in Ezra 1:8) appear inside Jewish temple-restoration narratives. The Hellenistic period extends this contact through the Septuagint's importation of Platonic vocabulary into Semitic theology, Philo's Logos as the immediate intellectual framework for the Gospel of John, and the Maccabean crisis that crystallized Jewish apocalyptic literature.

### The Mitra complex

The Indo-Iranian root *\*mitra-* (covenant/binding obligation) is the site's primary etymological anchor — attested in the Mitanni treaty, the Rigveda, Avestan Yasht 10, and surviving in Roman Mithras, Manichaean Mihr, and the *maitrī* root of Buddhist Maitreya. This section traces that single root through its transformations across 2,500 years and multiple traditions, assessing at each stage whether the resemblance reflects genealogical descent, shared Indo-Iranian heritage, or milieu influence in a shared cultural field. The Kushan coins showing *MIIRO* (Mithra) and *BODDO* (Buddha) struck by the same mint are the most direct material evidence for co-existence in a single cultural environment. Central Asian Manichaean communities explicitly called Maitreya *Mitri-Burkhan* — an identification made by people living in the transmission corridor, not a modern inference.

### Gandhāra and the Central Asian corridor

Gandhāra is the eastern synthesis node where Iranian, Indian, Hellenistic, and Saka/Scythian cultural streams converged under Kushan imperial patronage. The Gandhāran Buddha's radiate halo directly borrows Hellenistic solar-crown iconography, providing datable visual evidence of cross-tradition solar imagery transfer. The Saka-Scythian hypotheses — including the proposed cognacy of the Śākya clan name with the Saka ethnonym, and Christopher Beckwith's argument that Pyrrho of Elis encountered early Buddhist dialectic in Central Asia — are the most actively debated open questions in this area. What makes Gandhāra analytically distinctive is that it is not a zone of implicit diffusion but a documented case of political and economic integration: multiple religious traditions were present, literate, and in contact within the same imperial system, producing conditions for deliberate synthesis rather than unconscious absorption.

### The Mediterranean mystery world

This area is not a collection of separate cults but a shared initiatory ecology — a milieu in which solar cult (Helios → Apollo → Sol Invictus), hero cult (tomb-centered honors for exceptional dead), and mystery school initiation (the death/descent/rebirth/illumination structure) had converged into a common conceptual atmosphere by the early imperial period. Roman Mithraism, civic *sōtēr* language, and early Christianity competed within this ecology, which is why ancient observers performing *interpretatio* were not confused when they equated Christ with Mithras — they were recognizing figures occupying the same inherited function-space. The mystery school death-rebirth structure encodes the solar cycle metaphorically, making solar cult and mystery initiation mutually reinforcing rather than distinct phenomena. Paul's celestial Christology stands closer to this milieu — and to the Enochic Son of Man — than to the Galilean teacher of the Synoptic Gospels, which matters for evaluating what early Christianity borrowed versus what it innovated.

### The Sasanian and Late Antique world

The Sasanian period (224–651 CE) is when Zoroastrianism was first institutionalized as a state religion (Kartir's inscriptions; the Avesta compiled under Shāpur II and Khosrow I), when Manichaeism emerged as a deliberately synthetic tradition explicitly connecting Iranian, Christian, and Buddhist soteriology, and when the Babylonian Talmud was compiled under Iranian imperial administration. The Academy of Gondishapur under Khosrow I became the transmission node for Greek philosophy into the Islamic world via Nestorian Syriac translation. Merkabah and Hekhalot mysticism developed in the same Sasanian Jewish milieu where Neoplatonism was producing theurgy in the Roman world. The Islamic conquest of 651 CE did not erase but transmitted the Sasanian intellectual inheritance: Persian administrative converts carried it into the Abbasid caliphate, where Zoroastrian eschatological concepts resurfaced in Shia theology — the same functional slot refilled under new political conditions.

## The structural thesis

The evidence across these research areas points toward an argument that goes beyond cataloguing parallels.

Most cases of similarity between salvific traditions are best explained neither as "X borrowed from Y" nor as "mere coincidence." What actually happened in every area the site covers — Achaemenid, Hellenistic, Kushan, Sasanian — is that shared cultural environments made certain conceptual tools available simultaneously to multiple communities independently developing their own theology. Cosmic dualism, the mediator figure, universal resurrection, heavenly ascent, and faith-based salvation were not the exclusive property of any single tradition. They were the operative vocabulary of a shared intellectual milieu. This "porous milieu" model replaces the false binary between direct borrowing and pure coincidence with the more accurate category of shared conceptual environment — more historically honest because it matches how ancient peoples themselves understood the situation, through *interpretatio*, the routine equation of functional equivalents across traditions.

The deep overlap between Iranian, Jewish, Christian, Buddhist, and Roman traditions operates at two levels that reinforce rather than compete with each other. At the deeper level, Proto-Indo-European religion carried a specific function-space: a solar mediator figure standing between the sky father and humanity, associated with contract, light, and cosmic order. Vedic *Mitra*, Avestan *Mithra*, Greek Apollo/Helios, and Roman Mithras all fill this inherited slot. The structural parallels exist in the first instance because multiple traditions inherited this function-space from a common ancestor. At the historical level, the Achaemenid, Hellenistic, and Kushan empires provided contact nodes where the vocabulary for filling this role was exchanged, elaborated, and adapted through administrative infrastructure, bilingual translation, and community crisis. The two levels use different mechanisms: Wave 1 (PIE dispersal, ~3300–1500 BCE) distributed the structural grammar; Wave 2 (Axial Age, ~600–300 BCE) filled those slots with named, universalized cosmic figures under imperial pressure.

The solar synthesis is the most persistent single vehicle for this concept across the entire timeline. The PIE solar substrate — Mitra/Mithra as contract-keeper associated with light, Vivasvant as the solar ancestor of humanity, *Vairocana* as the etymological cognate of *Vivasvant* that becomes the Cosmic Sun-Buddha — provides the inherited template. In the Mediterranean, this template was amplified through the convergence of solar cult and mystery school initiation. The mystery school death-rebirth-illumination structure maps the solar cycle metaphorically: the initiand dies to the ordinary world and is reborn in light, as the sun dies at dusk and is reborn at dawn. Osiris, Attis, Dionysus, and Mithras all participate in this structure. At the Gandhāran node, the radiate halo on Buddha images and the Vairocana theology together show the same solar vocabulary operating at the eastern end of the transmission corridor. Solar imagery is the visual and theological language that made the cosmic mediator recognizable across traditions from the Rigveda to Revelation — which is why the most theologically significant figures on this site are consistently expressed in solar terms.

The site's evidence also suggests a specific reframing of the Axial Age transformation. Across IE cultures before the Axial period, a broadly consistent format governed the honoring of exceptional dead: the Greek *heroon* at a specific tomb, the steppe kurgan, the Vedic fire ritual for ancestors. The structural assumption is consistent — the dead person of exceptional power remains available to the living through the site of the body. What the Axial Age transformations did, in every tradition the site covers, was delocalize this contact point: the Greek hero bound to a specific tomb became the cosmic Dionysus accessible through initiation anywhere; the ancestor honored at a specific kurgan became the Saoshyant who would restore all humanity at the end of time; the particular dead teacher became the Buddha whose relic fragments made him universally accessible; the figure executed at a specific location became the cosmic Christ accessible through meal and baptism anywhere in the empire. The Axial breakthrough, on this reading, is the transformation of locally-bound hero honors into portable, universalizable cosmic soteriology. The Achaemenid empire appears to be the specific historical mechanism that forced this delocalization: communities whose deity was tied to a temple, a tomb, or a homeland were rendered theologically homeless by imperial displacement, and the traditions that survived were the ones whose mediator became universally portable.

This structural thesis is Tier C as a unified claim. Each component has evidence at different tiers: the PIE substrate is Tier A for specific philological cognates; the porous milieu model is methodological; the hero-cult universalization is a hypothesis. The value of stating it is that it names the structural logic that would need to be falsified to show that the convergences are coincidental rather than connected. See [Soteriological convergence of empire — four meta-patterns](overview/soteriological-convergence-of-empire.md) for the extended argument.

## The cosmic mediator slot

Across every research area, the same structural position recurs: a being who stands between the eternal divine order and the broken human world, whose function is to bridge or restore that gap. This table is not an argument that these figures are "really the same god with different names" — it is an observation that every major tradition in the Indo-Iranian and Semitic sphere generated a figure to fill this role, drawing on a shared inherited function-space and shaped by specific historical contacts.

| Figure | Tradition | Zone | Mediating function |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Mitra* (Indo-Iranian) | Vedic / Avestan | PIE deep grammar | Contract-keeper between humanity and the divine; guarantor of cosmic order (*ṛtá* / *asha*) |
| Saoshyant | Zoroastrian | Achaemenid / Sasanian | Eschatological restorer who defeats cosmic evil, sacrifices the bull Hadayānš, and presides over universal resurrection |
| Enoch / Son of Man | Second Temple Judaism | Achaemenid / Hellenistic | Hidden heavenly figure who sits on God's throne and judges all nations at the end of time (1 En. 37–71) |
| Logos | Hellenistic Judaism / early Christianity | Mediterranean | Pre-existent agent of creation who "became flesh"; mediates the divine-human gap (Philo; John 1:1) |
| Parousia / Christ | Christian | Mediterranean | Cosmic judge returning to complete the eschatological sequence; *kyrios* and *sōtēr* against Caesar |
| Mithras | Roman mystery | Mediterranean | Initiatory mediator; tauroctony; competitor for the same cosmic function-space as the Christ |
| Maitreya | Buddhist | Gandhāra / Kushan | Future Buddha presently waiting in Tuṣita heaven; will descend to restore the Dharma |
| Amitābha | Pure Land Buddhist | Central Asian Silk Road | Cosmic savior of infinite light (*amita* + *ābhā*) who accepts all who call his name into his pure land |
| Mahdi / Hidden Imam | Twelver Shia Islam | Sasanian / Islamic | Hidden mediator in occultation since 941 CE; will return to restore justice at the end of time |
| Kalki | Hindu | Indo-Iranian substrate | Viṣṇu's tenth avatar; arrives at the end of the Kali Yuga on a white horse to defeat cosmic evil and restore the golden age |

The Zoroastrian Saoshyant is the earliest fully elaborated version of this figure. The Hindu Kalki and Buddhist Maitreya represent parallel developments from the shared Indo-Iranian substrate — not direct borrowings from Zoroastrianism — since the material is ancestrally shared before the traditions diverge. The Jewish, Christian, and Islamic versions develop in traditions with documented Achaemenid and post-Achaemenid contact with Iranian theology, which explains the specific eschatological content (resurrection, cosmic judgment, named angelic administrators, cosmic adversary) they share with the Iranian originals.

## Editorial approach

A general pattern runs across every research area: transmission happens when a concept travels into a crisis that makes it urgently useful. Iranian eschatological concepts entered Judaism during the Maccabean persecution — the same theodicy question that drove them supplied the urgency for adopting Iranian answers. Jewish apocalyptic vocabulary entered Hellenistic Christianity during Roman occupation and the crisis of a crucified messiah. Iranian eschatological concepts entered early Islam as Persian administrative elites carried Zoroastrian intellectual inheritance into the Abbasid caliphate after 651 CE. The mechanisms vary — empire, diaspora, bilingual texts, urban pluralism — but the social logic is consistent: communities under pressure reach for conceptual tools that explain why the righteous suffer, who the cosmic adversary is, and how present disorder will be resolved.

The **porous milieu model** — developed in full at [Soteriological convergence of empire](overview/soteriological-convergence-of-empire.md) — is the site's preferred replacement for the identity-claim frame ("Christianity comes from Mithraism," "Buddhism is derived from Zoroastrianism"). Those claims are both too strong and too weak: too strong because they imply textual dependence the evidence rarely supports; too weak because they miss the shared conceptual environment that makes multiple communities reach for similar solutions independently. When the 19th-century polemical tradition claimed "the Church stole its sacraments from Mithraism," it was pointing at a real phenomenon — structural convergence in a shared Mediterranean milieu — while explaining it with the wrong mechanism. The fabricated parallels (Mithras's virgin birth, twelve apostles, crucifixion) have no ancient attestation. The real overlaps — shared Hellenistic solar cosmology, shared PIE function-space, shared Roman initiation social technology — are structural, subtle, and more historically interesting than simple borrowing.

**Four meta-patterns** recur across the entire corpus: (1) empire as both crisis-creator and transmission chassis; (2) the persistent cosmic mediator slot being refilled in every tradition; (3) the written word acquiring the material sacredness of the teacher's body; (4) a progressive interiorization of salvation from external cosmic drama toward inner transformation, catalyzed at multiple points by contact with Greek philosophical methods. These patterns are developed fully in [Soteriological convergence of empire — four meta-patterns](overview/soteriological-convergence-of-empire.md).

This project is a collaborative research compilation built by a small group with AI assistance. We use large language models for collation, cross-referencing, and drafting across multiple scholarly fields and languages — work that would otherwise require a large specialist team. AI assistance compresses the research cycle but introduces its own epistemic risks: confident-sounding synthesis, plausible-seeming citations, and a tendency to smooth over distinctions that specialists keep sharp. The tier system below is partly a response to those risks. Claims should be independently verified against primary sources and the peer-reviewed literature cited on each page. If you find an error or a claim that needs upgrading or downgrading, [contributions are welcome](overview/contributing.md).

Pages use four informal **tiers of claim**:

| Tier | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Secure description** | Mainstream scholarly summary |
| **Specialist debate** | Real schools of thought; evidence is partial |
| **Hypothesis** | Active research target with named mechanism and falsifier |
| **Analogy / heuristic** | Illuminates questions only |

Pages signal tier in prose. Where they do not, default to *descriptive or debated, not proved*. The hypothesis tier is where most of the interesting exploration lives.
