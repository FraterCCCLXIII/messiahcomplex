# Mithra, contract, and transmission

One word — *\*mitra-*, Proto-Indo-Iranian for "contract, covenant, binding obligation" — threads through over three thousand years of religious history: from Vedic Mitra and Avestan Miθra, through Achaemenid imperial administration, into the Roman mystery cult of Mithras, Manichaean cosmology's Mihr, and possibly Buddhist Maitreya. This page summarizes the full thread and assesses what is securely attested at each step, what is contested, and where resemblance shades into speculation. Child pages go deep on each segment.

---

## The thread in outline

The root *\*mitra-* is Proto-Indo-Iranian for **"contract, covenant, binding obligation"** — one of the most semantically specific divine names recoverable from the pre-attested period. It anchors the whole thread.

### What is well-documented

| Evidence | Source | What it shows |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Mitanni–Hittite treaty** invoking *Mitra* alongside Indra, Varuṇa, and Nāsatyas as oath-guarantors | Boğazköy cuneiform, 14th cent. BCE | The contract-god function is attested before any Vedic or Avestan text |
| **Rigvedic Mitra** (RV 3.59): "sustainer of mankind and all gods," paired with Varuṇa as guardian of *ṛtá* | *Ṛgveda*, c. 1500–1200 BCE | Indian branch preserves the treaty-witnessing function |
| **Avestan Yasht 10** (*Mihr Niyaish*): Mithra as "lord of broad pastures," thousand-eyed witness, "worthy of sacrifice as I myself" — Ahura Mazda to Zarathustra | Younger Avesta, 5th cent. BCE | Contract-god elevated to a cosmic yazata; Ahura Mazda acknowledges him |
| **Mithredath** (מִתְרְדָת) in Ezra 1:8 and 4:7 | Hebrew Bible, restoration period | An Iranian theophoric name — "given by Mithra" — embedded in the Jewish temple-restoration narrative; evidence of on-the-ground Persian–Jewish interface |
| **Roman Mithras**, tauroctony, graded initiation | *Mithraea*, 1st–4th cent. CE | Western reception of an Iranian divine name inside Greco-Roman mystery framework; origins disputed (Cumont vs. Beck) |
| **Manichaean Mihr / Myr** | Middle Persian, Parthian, Sogdian texts, 3rd–8th cent. CE | Mithra incorporated as a light-bearer and salvific figure in Mani's dualistic cosmos |

### Where the evidence thins

| Claim | Status |
| --- | --- |
| **Hebrew עמד** (*ʿ-m-d*, "stand") cognate with *\*mitra-* | Not accepted in historical linguistics — typological parallel only |
| **Maitreya** derived from *mitra-* (vs. from *maitrī* "loving-kindness") | *maitrī* is the mainstream etymology; *mitra-* connection is semantic background, not proven borrowing |
| **Mithra = Maitreya** (identity of figures) | Minority hypothesis (Azimia 2023); primary-text evidence does not support identity |
| **Roman Mithras** as a direct continuation of Avestan liturgy | Disputed; Beck and Clauss argue for Roman-creative synthesis rather than Iranian survival |
