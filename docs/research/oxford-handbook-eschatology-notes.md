## Key extracted passages

### Collins ch. 2 — "Persian Origins?" (split_012)

**Opening of the section:**
> When the writings of Zoroastrianism became available in translation in the nineteenth century, it became evident that they contained many motifs typical of Jewish and Christian apocalypticism. These included the division of history into periods (millennia), a final battle between good and evil, and the judgment of the individual soul.

**The Theopompus datum (c. 300 BCE):**
> Some apocalyptic motifs, such as the final battle and the judgment of the dead, are found already in the Gathas, and the division of history into millennia and the resurrection of the dead are attested by the Greek writer Theopompus about 300 BCE. (Apud Plutarch, *De Iside et Osiride* 47; Diogenes Laertius, *Proem.* 9. See DeJong, *Traditions of the Magi*, Brill, 1997, pp. 164–65.)

**Collins's summary verdict:**
> Some influence of Persian ideas on Jewish apocalypticism seems very likely. It would be far too simple, however, to say that the whole phenomenon of apocalypticism was imported to Judea from Persia. It also had roots in Jewish tradition.

**The dating problem (Avesta collected in Sasanian period):**
> The Zoroastrian scriptures, the Avesta, were collected in the Sassanian period (221–642 CE). Some of the prophet's own compositions are preserved in the Gathas, but other parts of the Avesta (the Younger Avesta) cannot be dated with any confidence. The main compendia of Persian apocalyptic and eschatological lore are found in Pahlavi writings from the ninth and tenth centuries CE, such as the Bundahishn. The Bahman Yasht belongs to this Pahlavi literature, although it may preserve material that is much older.

**Collins's limit of confidence:**
> While the importance of Persian dualism was exaggerated in the past, it is undeniably relevant to the discussion of the Dead Sea Scrolls.

### Collins ch. 2 — Dead Sea Scrolls section (split_012)

> It is widely recognized that the clear dualism of light and darkness in these scrolls reflects Zoroastrian influence.

> The Discourse on the Two Spirits … [describes] two spirits, one of light and one of darkness. The behavior of human beings is determined by their ruling spirit. But God in the mysteries of his knowledge has determined an end to the existence of wickedness and will destroy it forever.

### Nattier ch. 8 — Maitreya and the Kushan coins (split_019)

> [Maitreya's] image can be found, together with that of Śākyamuni (and a broad array of Greek and Iranian deities), on the coins of the Kushan king Kanishka, who ruled northwestern India and adjacent regions in the first or second century CE.

**On the condensed timetable in East Asian millenarianism:**
> In rare cases — and exclusively in areas influenced by pre-Buddhist Chinese messianic expectations — the timetable for Maitreya's advent was radically shortened, leading to the expectation that he would appear (or had even appeared already) in the present age.

### Ch. 2 Collins — new bibliography references exposed

These scholarly works are cited in Collins ch. 2 and not yet in the site's source sections:

- **Widengren, Geo; Hultgård, Anders; Philonenko, Marc** (eds.). *Apocalyptique iranienne et Dualisme Qoumrânien*. Paris: Maisonneuve, 1995. — The key French collection. Philonenko's essay (pp. 163–211) specifically on the two-spirits doctrine. *(Added to DSS page.)*
- **Kreyenbroek, Philip G.** "Millennialism and Eschatology in the Zoroastrian Tradition." In Amanat & Bernhardsson (eds.), *Imagining the End*. Tauris, 2002, pp. 33–55.
- **Cohn, Norman.** *Cosmos, Chaos and the World to Come.* Yale, 1993, pp. 77–104.
- **DeJong, Albert.** *Traditions of the Magi: Zoroastrianism in Greek and Latin Literature.* Brill, 1997, pp. 164–65. — For the Theopompus attestation.
- **Boyce, Mary.** "On the Antiquity of Zoroastrian Apocalyptic." *BSOAS* 47 (1984): 57–75.
- **Gignoux, Philippe.** "L'apocalyptique iranienne est-elle vraiment la source d'autres apocalypses?" *Acta Antiqua* 31 (1988): 67–78.
- **Collins, John J.** *Apocalypticism and the Dead Sea Scrolls.* Routledge, 1997. — His focused monograph on this topic.
- **García Martínez, Florentino.** "Iranian Influences in Qumran?" in McNamara (ed.), *Apocalyptic and Eschatological Heritage*. Four Courts, 2003, pp. 37–49.
- **Boccaccini, Gabriele.** *Beyond the Essene Hypothesis.* Eerdmans, 1998. — Argues the Qumran sect was an offshoot of "Enochic Judaism"; relevant to [`enoch-son-of-man.md`](../overview/enoch-son-of-man.md).

### Ch. 7 Chittick — Muslim eschatology leads

Chittick's chapter focuses on philosophical/Sufi theology (Ibn ʿArabī, Suhrawardī, Mullā Ṣadrā) rather than popular eschatology (Mahdi, resurrection narratives). The most relevant content for this site:
- Discussion of Suhrawardī (d. 1191) and the "ishrāqī" (Illuminationist) tradition connecting Persian and Islamic thought — relevant to a future page on Iranian–Islamic transmission if the site's scope expands in that direction.
- Chittick's note that "the best book on Muslim eschatology in English" is J. I. Smith & Y. Y. Haddad (ref not given in full) — worth tracking down.
- The Mahdi as a figure in Shiʿi eschatology is **not treated** in this chapter; for that, the site needs dedicated Shiʿi sources (Amir-Moezzi, Sachedina). See [`figures/islam-mahdi.md`](../figures/islam-mahdi.md).

### Ch. 8 Nattier — further Buddhist eschatology content not yet applied

- Detailed treatment of Amitābha Pure Land eschatology (the "Buddhas in Other Worlds" section) — relevant to [`amitabha-maitreya-gandhara-transmission.md`](../overview/amitabha-maitreya-gandhara-transmission.md). Nattier notes that Pure Land speculation emerged "by the first century CE" and was connected to the idea of Buddhas in other directions of space.
- The *mappō* (Dharma decline) doctrine and its Chinese elaboration — relevant to the millenarian rebellion section of [`figures/buddhism-maitreya.md`](../figures/buddhism-maitreya.md). Nattier describes the "three periods of the Dharma" framework (correct Dharma → semblance Dharma → Dharma decline) that underlies East Asian Maitreya millenarianism.
- Xinxing (540–594, founder of the "Three Stages" school) and Daochuo (Pure Land) as two opposite responses to *mappō* — could add to the Maitreya millenarian section.

### Ch. 9 Knipe — Hindu eschatology

Knipe does not treat Kalki by name. He covers:
- The yuga cycle (*kṛta, tretā, dvāpara, kali*) and the expected return of the golden age after dissolution — the cosmic framework within which Kalki appears; relevant to [`figures/hinduism-kalki.md`](../figures/hinduism-kalki.md) as background context.
- Puranic *pralaya* (cosmic dissolution by fire and flood) — relevant to the four-ages parallel with the Bahman Yasht four-kingdom sequence (both feature degeneration toward dissolution and subsequent renewal).
- Knipe's footnote 16 cites Asko Parpola, "Sāvitrī and Resurrection," arguing that "the myth of four world ages is Indo-European" and that "archaic motifs, not represented in the Vedas, remained as oral traditions until surfacing in the epics and Puranas" (per Dumézil and Parpola) — directly relevant to the Bahman Yasht / Daniel / Hesiod four-ages comparison.

### Ch. 6 Novak — Jewish eschatology leads not yet applied

- **Projective vs apocalyptic messianism**: Novak's distinction between *projective* messianism (political Davidic king restoring Israel; not necessarily supernatural) and *apocalyptic* messianism (a heavenly figure who "will fall down from heaven in one decisive cosmic event") is useful for the Mashiach page's discussion of Second Temple diversity. *(Already noted in judaism-mashiach.md Tier B entry.)*
- **Bar Kokhba**: Rabbi Akiva endorsed Simeon bar Kokhba as the Messiah (~120 CE); after the failed revolt (135 CE), subsequent rabbinic opinion turned sharply against premature messianism. Novak argues this created a "powerful regulator of pseudo-messianism" — historically important context for why rabbinic Judaism deprioritized eschatological speculation.
- **Chabad messianism**: Novak's brief treatment of the claim that Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneersohn is the Messiah (d. 1994, Lubavitch Hasidism) and the parallel Bratslav Hasidim claim for Rabbi Nahman of Bratslav (d. 1811) — a contemporary example of the messianic pattern the site tracks.
