# Possible Iranian Origins for the Śākyas and Aspects of Buddhism

:::info Research Note
This page summarizes and annotates a peer-reviewed scholarly article. The hypothesis it presents is a legitimate scholarly proposal (Tier B) — deserving further investigation — but has not achieved consensus in Buddhist studies. See tier labels throughout.
:::

**Source**: Attwood, Jayarava. "Possible Iranian Origins for the Śākyas and Aspects of Buddhism." *Journal of the Oxford Centre for Buddhist Studies* (Oxford Centre for Buddhist Studies), Vol. 3, 2012, pp. 47–69.

**[Download the full PDF](/sources/iranian-origins-sakyas.pdf)**

---

## Summary

This article by Jayarava Attwood examines the plausibility of Michael Witzel's speculation that the Śākya tribe — the Buddha's clan — might have had Iranian origins or connections. Attwood identifies circumstantial evidence suggesting that ideas associated with Iranian or Zoroastrian traditions appear in northeast India, especially among *śramaṇa* groups and early Buddhism, but not in Brahmanical culture.

While early Buddhism is frequently portrayed as a response to Brahmanism, or as ahistorical, Witzel's suggestion opens a new avenue for exploring the history of ideas in Buddhism. Attwood frames this explicitly as a hypothesis warranting further scholarly attention, not a concluded finding.

---

## Key Evidence Presented

### 1. Sibling Marriage Traditions

**(Tier B: documented in primary sources; parallel noted)**

In the Ambaṭṭha Sutta (DN i.87), the progenitors of the Śākyas are exiled from their kingdom and, unable to find suitable spouses, marry their own sisters. This incestuous origin is preserved despite the later Buddhist condemnation of incest — which Attwood notes is consistent with the "Principle of Embarrassment" in New Testament criticism: details unflattering to the group are more likely to be historical precisely because they would not be invented.

Jonathan Silk confirms the Iranian practice: "there is good evidence for this practice called *xvaētuuadaϑa*, so-called next-of-kin or close-kin marriage" (Silk 2008b: 377).

**Significance**: If the Śākyas preserved an Iranian custom in their origin stories, this suggests cultural (not necessarily biographical) continuity with Iranian practice.

### 2. Buddhist Stūpas and Scythian Kurgan Burials

**(Tier B: structural parallel noted by multiple scholars)**

Witzel (2012) notes that Buddhist stūpas are "similar to the kurgan type grave mounds of Southern Russia and Central Asia." Earlier scholars including H.G. Rawlinson (1916) made the same observation:

> "...relic-worship, and its concomitant the stūpa, are quite un-Indian. Gautama belonged to the Śākya clan: were they an early offshoot of the Śakas, the Sacae or Scyths...? The word stūpa signifies a 'barrow,' or 'tumulus,' a Sanskrit name for a Scythian object..." (Rawlinson 1916: 15–16)

**Assessment**: The structural parallel is noted by multiple independent scholars. Direct derivation of stūpa architecture from kurgan tradition is not proven — alternative explanations exist — but the parallel merits attention.

### 3. Division of Actions: Body, Speech, and Mind

**(Tier B: cross-tradition parallel)**

Caroline Rhys Davids (1946) notes that the Buddhist division of human action into body, speech, and mind (*kāya, vāca, citta*) — central to Buddhist ethics — parallels the Zoroastrian ethical triad *humata, hūxta, hvaršta* (good thought, good word, good deed). This triad is said to "encapsulate the ethical goals of Zoroastrianism" (Boyce 2007).

Rhys Davids notes the triad is not found in other early Indian texts, suggesting it is not autochthonous to the Brahmanical tradition.

**Assessment**: This is a genuine functional parallel between early Buddhist and Zoroastrian ethics. The mechanism of transmission is unclear but the observation is sound.

### 4. Karma and Weighing of Deeds

**(Tier B: proposed Iranian influence; debated)**

Witzel notes that the idea of deeds being weighed after death was not a Vedic concept:

> "This was first an Egyptian, then a Zoroastrian and Iranian concept. It is connected with the idea of personal responsibility for one's action (karma)" (Witzel 2012).

In Zoroastrianism, the soul is judged after death based on the balance of thoughts, words, and deeds: "...the soul's fate depends solely on the sum of the individual's thoughts, words, and deeds, the good being weighed against the bad, so that no observance should avail it in any way." (Boyce 1999)

**Assessment**: The Zoroastrian antecedent for individual moral accounting at death is plausible as an influence on early Buddhism via Iranian contact. However, Indian traditions also developed karma concepts independently; the direction and mechanism of influence remain debated.

### 5. Linguistic Evidence: Śākya and Śaka

**(Tier B: plausible cognate; not established)**

Both Śaka (Scythian) and Śākya appear to derive from the root √*śak*, "to be able, strong or powerful." *Śākya* is probably a derivative form meaning "related to, or descended from, the Śakas" (cf. Monier-Williams sv. *Śākya*).

Attwood explicitly notes: "The names Śākya and Śaka are probably cognate, however the underlying meaning is 'powerful,' and it is not unlikely that two disparate groups might refer to themselves as 'the powerful.'" He acknowledges this does not by itself establish a genealogical connection.

## Migration Timeline

### Late Migration Theory

**(Tier B: based on climate and archaeological data)**

Parpola (2008), working independently, hypothesizes that Iranians generically called Dāṇḍu entered India around 800 BCE via the Indus Valley. This is consistent with Witzel's broader proposal.

Climate scientists (van Geel et al. 2004) document an abrupt climate shift around 850 BCE that caused rapid Scythian culture expansion on the steppe and drought pressure in northwest India — creating conditions for Saka migration southward.

These two independent lines (textual/archaeological and paleoclimatological) converge on approximately the same date for Iranian migration into northwest India.

## Other Iranian Influences Noted

### Achaemenid Administrative Contact

**(Tier A)**

The Achaemenid Empire controlled territory as far east as the Indus River from the late 6th century BCE:
- Kharoṣṭhī script: derived from Aramaic (Achaemenid administrative script) — Tier A
- The word *lipi* ("writing") is a Persian loanword in Indian languages (Salomon 1999: 9) — Tier A
- Babylonian astronomical knowledge entered India via Iranian intermediaries (Pingree 1963: 246) — Tier B

### Divination Practices

**(Tier B)**

The list of divination techniques prohibited in the Brahmajāla Sutta is "almost identical in form and content to Mesopotamian divination manuals *Šumma ālu* and *Enūma anu Enlil*" (Pingree 1995, 1996). The prohibition suggests these practices were actively used in northeast India — meaning Mesopotamian divination knowledge had already arrived, likely via the Iranian intermediary of the Achaemenid Empire.

## Attwood's Conclusion

> "While this is an argument from circumstantial evidence, the evidence, such as it is, makes a connection between the Śākyas and Iran at least plausible. If this thesis is correct, then some features of Buddhism are actually cultural features of the Śākya tribe preserved from an earlier period of living in Iran."

> "It still allows for the Buddha as visionary and innovator, and it does not deny the influence of Brahmins and Jains, but it broadens the cultural pool from which he might have drawn his ideas."

## Relevance to This Site

This article is one of the best-documented scholarly threads supporting the general direction of the Bardiya hypothesis's claims about Iranian–Buddhist connections. Key points:

- Attwood's hypothesis is Tier B: a legitimate scholarly proposal that merits further investigation
- It supports cultural connections between Saka/Iranian and Śākya/Buddhist traditions
- It does **not** support the biographical identification of Gaumata with the Buddha (Tier C)
- The evidence for Iranian cultural features in early Buddhism (sibling marriage, body/speech/mind triad, stūpa/kurgan parallel, karmic accounting) is stronger than the biographical hypothesis
