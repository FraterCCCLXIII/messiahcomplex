## 1. One name, three NT appearances: Apollo → Apollos → Apollyon

The name Apollo appears three times in the New Testament, in three very different registers, within roughly the same decade of composition.

**Apollos** (Ἀπολλώς): An Alexandrian Jewish Christian, described as "eloquent" and "well versed in the scriptures" (Acts 18:24–28). He is one of Paul's most prominent co-workers; 1 Corinthians shows factions forming around Paul, Apollos, and Peter (1 Cor 1:12; 3:4–6). His name is simply the Greek theonym. He appears in Titus 3:13 and Clement of Rome. The Apollos of Corinth operated in a city where the cult of Apollo was dominant — Corinth held the Isthmian Games in Apollo's honor.

**Apollyon** (Ἀπολλύων, Revelation 9:11): The "angel of the bottomless pit," king of the locusts, whose Hebrew name is Abaddon ("destruction") and whose Greek name Apollyon means "Destroyer." The author of Revelation almost certainly chose this name as a deliberate polemic against Apollo — *Apollyon* is transparently derived from the same root as *Apollon* (from the Greek verb *apollymi*, "to destroy"). Early Christian apologists were aware of the connection.

The structure is notable: within one generation of NT composition, the name Apollo yields a revered Christian teacher (*Apollos*) and the angel of the abyss (*Apollyon*). Both are in dialogue with the dominant pagan cult. One embraces the resonance; one weaponizes it as a demonic inversion. Justin Martyr's acknowledgment of Christ as "the true Apollo" (and "the true Dionysus") suggests the positive appropriation was a live strategy in the 2nd century.

**Evidence tier:** Tier A for the three textual attestations. Tier B for the polemical intention behind *Apollyon* as Apollo-inversion (widely accepted in scholarship but not explicitly stated in the text). See also [Helios, Apollo, and Sol — solar syncretism](../overview/helios-apollo-sol-syncretism.md).

## 2. January 5–6: the calendar convergence

The dionysus-christ-parallels.csv (static/sources/hellenic/) assembles a set of converging January datings that are individually attested and collectively striking.

**Pliny the Younger** (*Letters* 10.96; *Natural History* 33.5) reports that wine jars at the **Temple of Dionysus at Andros** were miraculously filled with wine every year on **January 5th**. Pausanias corroborates a similar miracle at Elis (Pausanias, *Description of Greece* 6.24).

**Epiphanius** (*Panarion* 30, 4th c. CE) records that:
- The **Basilidean Gnostics** in Alexandria celebrated the baptism of Christ on **January 6th** — which he notes coincides with pagan festivals for **Ion** and **Dusares** (Nabataean deity whose iconography parallels Dionysus).
- The January 6th date also coincides with the ancient Ion-cult festival celebrating a divine birth from a virgin.

**Epiphany on January 6th**: Orthodox and Catholic traditions eventually formalize three epiphany events on January 6 — the Magi's visit, Jesus's baptism, and the Cana wine miracle. The Cana miracle (water into wine, John 2:1–11) is structurally parallel to the Andros wine miracle. Justin Martyr explicitly makes this comparison.

The proximity — Andros wine miracle on January 5th, Epiphany/Cana on January 6th — is tighter than typical discussions of "pagan influence on Christian calendar" acknowledge. It is not an argument for direct borrowing; it is a datum suggesting the calendar slot was already charged with wine-miracle associations when early Christian communities fixed their major winter festival dates.

**Evidence tier:** Tier A for each individual attestation (Pliny, Pausanias, Epiphanius are primary sources). Tier B for the argument that the calendar proximity reflects deliberate or unconscious continuity. Tier C for any claim of direct influence.

## 3. Nonnus of Panopolis: the living bridge

**Nonnus** (5th century CE, Panopolis = modern Akhmim, Egypt) is one of the most underappreciated figures in the history of religious transmission.

He wrote the **Dionysiaca** — the longest surviving poem in Greek literature (48 books), the most extensive surviving compendium of Dionysiac mythology. It is encyclopedic: every episode in Dionysus's life, every syncretism, every ecstatic rite.

He also converted to Christianity and wrote a **Paraphrase of the Gospel of John** in the same dactylic hexameter, explicitly mapping Johannine narrative onto Dionysiac poetic conventions. He appears to have seen no contradiction between the two projects.

This makes Nonnus the only known individual who was both the **last great transmitter of Dionysiac tradition** and an **early Christian biblical commentator**. His paraphrase of John uses Dionysiac vocabulary for the wine miracle at Cana, the Logos prologue, and the resurrection narratives. Scholars of late antique religion (Golega; Enrico Livrea) have argued this is not mere literary ornament — Nonnus saw genuine theological continuity.

The specific parallels he exploits: the Cana wine miracle as Dionysian epiphany; the Logos ("Word") as parallel to Dionysian *logos* in Stoic-influenced Greek theology; the resurrection as parallel to Dionysus's death and return.

**Evidence tier:** Tier A for Nonnus's biography and dual authorship (well-documented; Golega 1930 is the standard study). Tier B for the argument that the Paraphrase reflects intentional theological synthesis rather than stylistic convention.
