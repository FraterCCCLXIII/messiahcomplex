# Celtic / British: King Arthur (“Once and Future King”)

**Arthur** evolves from **historical memory** (if any) into **literary** and **prophetic** **British** king who **will return** in the hour of **greatest need**—the **“Once and Future King”** motif.

## Dating and sources

| Milestone | Approximate date | Sources |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Early historical hints | 9th cent. CE | **Nennius**, **Annales Cambriae** (debated) |
| **Prophetic return** clearer | **1125 CE** onward | **William of Malmesbury**, *Gesta Regum Anglorum* |
| Arthurian epic | ~1136 CE onward | **Geoffrey of Monmouth**, *Historia Regum Britanniae*; **Layamon**, *Brut* (~1190) |

**Avalon**, **mortal wound** at **Camlann**, **healing** / **sleeping** king awaiting **return**—classic **messianic parallel** in **heroic** literature.

## Role and motifs

- **Sleeping hero** under hill / in **Avalon** (Wales / Brittany folklore overlaps).
- **National** restoration: **expel invaders**, **restore** rightful **golden age** rule.

## Scholarly notes

**Welsh** oral tradition vs. **Norman-Latin** literary creation is debated; “first **explicit** return prophecy” is often tied to **12th cent.** chroniclers. Comparisons to **Baldr** or **Jesus** are **typological**, not claims of **historical** dependence without evidence.

## Key references (starting points)

- **Padel, O. J.** *Arthur in Medieval Welsh Literature* — Welsh sources.
- **Morris, Rosemary.** *The Character of King Arthur in Medieval Literature* — literary development.
- **Higham, N. J.** *King Arthur: Myth-making and History* — historicity debates.
- **Geoffrey of Monmouth**, *History of the Kings of Britain* — read in translation with critical introduction.
