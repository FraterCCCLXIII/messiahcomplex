# Osiris (Egyptian)

**Osiris** (Egyptian: *Wsjr*, vocalized variously as *Wsir*, *Usiré*, or *Asar*; Greek: *Osiris*) is the Egyptian god of the dead, resurrection, and regeneration — the murdered king who became the lord and judge of the afterlife. His myth constitutes the earliest surviving documentation of:
- A god who dies and is restored
- Individual post-mortem divine judgment
- A son (Horus) who avenges his murdered father
- A theology of divine kingship in which every deceased person "becomes Osiris"

The Osiris complex is the foundational case for the "dying and rising god" debate and is directly relevant to the Hellenistic mystery tradition through the Ptolemaic Serapis synthesis.

## Dating and sources

| Source | Approximate date | Language | Content |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Pyramid Texts** (*Utterances*) | ~2400–2300 BCE | Old Egyptian (hieroglyphic) | Oldest surviving religious texts anywhere; Osiris already fully formed as murdered and resurrected king |
| **Coffin Texts** | ~2100–1650 BCE | Middle Egyptian | Democratic extension: all dead can become Osiris, not just the king |
| **Book of the Dead** (*Going Forth by Day*) | ~1550 BCE onward | New Kingdom and later Egyptian | Chapter 125: the Weighing of the Heart; Osiris as judge |
| **Pyramid Texts of Unas** (Tomb of Unas, Saqqara) | ~2375 BCE | Old Egyptian | Earliest dated examples; Osiris is already lord of the dead |
| **Abydos passion play** (reconstructed from various sources) | Old Kingdom onward | Ritual; no single surviving text | Annual re-enactment of Osiris's death, mourning, and restoration |
| **Plutarch, *De Iside et Osiride*** | ~100 CE | Greek | The most complete literary narrative; Hellenistic synthesis |

**The Pyramid Texts** (discovered in the pyramid of Unas at Saqqara in 1880) are the **oldest surviving religious texts in the world** — predating any surviving Vedic text, any Avestan text, and certainly any Hebrew Bible text. Osiris appears fully formed in them as the murdered god of the dead, meaning the myth is at least 4,300 years old in its attested form.

## The myth of Osiris

No single ancient Egyptian text gives the complete narrative in one place. The fullest account is Plutarch's *De Iside et Osiride* (~100 CE), a Hellenistic synthesis that may embellish the Egyptian tradition. The core narrative, reconstructed from Egyptian sources:

### The divine family

- **Osiris** — first-born of Nut (sky goddess) and Geb (earth god)
- **Isis** (*Aset*) — Osiris's sister and wife; the great magical goddess
- **Set** (*Sēṯ*) — Osiris's brother; god of chaos, desert, storms; the antagonist
- **Nephthys** (*Nebthwt*) — Set's wife; allied with Isis; mourner
- **Horus** (*Ḥr*, various forms) — Osiris and Isis's son; heir to Osiris's kingship

### Osiris as the ideal king

Osiris was the king of Egypt who civilized humanity — taught agriculture, established laws, instituted proper worship. He traveled the world spreading civilization. His rule was a golden age. In some traditions (Plutarch), Set's murder of Osiris is motivated by jealousy of Osiris's kingship and the affections of Nephthys.

### Set's murder of Osiris

**Plutarch's version** (elaborated): Set secretly measured Osiris's body and had a beautiful chest/coffin made to fit exactly. At a banquet, Set offered the chest as a gift to whoever fit inside it. Guests tried and failed; Osiris lay down and fit perfectly. Set and his confederates immediately sealed the chest, poured lead on it, and threw it into the Nile. The chest floated to Byblos (Phoenicia) where a great tree grew around it.

**Egyptian sources** are more elliptical: Set kills Osiris by drowning or striking him; the details vary. The "dismemberment into 14 pieces" (mentioned in Plutarch and some Egyptian sources) may be a separate tradition from the chest story.

### Isis's search and retrieval

Isis searched the world for Osiris. She found the chest at Byblos, retrieved it, and brought it back to Egypt. She hid it in the marshes of the Delta.

**Plutarch** adds: Set found the body and dismembered it into 14 (or 16 or 42) pieces, scattering them across Egypt. This accounts for the multiple "tombs of Osiris" at different Egyptian sites — each site claimed to possess one of the body parts. The site at Abydos was most sacred (believed to hold the head or the whole body).

### Reassembly and posthumous conception

Isis and Nephthys gathered the pieces. Thoth and Anubis performed the **first mummification**, reassembling and embalming Osiris. Isis transformed into a kite (bird), hovered over the body, and through magical breath conceived **Horus** from the dead Osiris.

This conception is theologically significant: Horus is generated *after* Osiris's death, from the dead body — making Horus specifically the child of the dead-resurrected-god. Every Egyptian king was identified with Horus during his life; at death, he "became Osiris."

### Osiris revived — but not in the living world

Osiris revived, but — and this is crucial — **he did not return to rule the living world**. He became the **king of the dead**. The land of the living is ruled by Horus (= the living pharaoh); the land of the dead is ruled by Osiris (= the dead pharaoh/every dead person).

**Modern Egyptological note (Mark Smith, *Traversing Eternity*, 2009):** The characterization of Osiris as a straightforwardly "rising" god misrepresents his status. He is "restored" and "revivified" (*wḥm ʿnḫ*, "he whose life is renewed") but he does not re-enter the living world. The dichotomy is not dead/alive but *living world/dead world*. Osiris is alive in the realm of the dead — which is different from the Pauline or Zoroastrian resurrection concept of returning to the living world with a transformed body.

### Horus's vengeance

Osiris's son Horus grew up in hiding and eventually challenged Set. The **Contendings of Horus and Set** (a narrative preserved in the Ramesside papyrus P. Chester Beatty I) is an extended legal and physical contest between the two. After many trials, the divine tribunal ruled in favor of Horus, who became king of Egypt. Set was given domains over the desert and storms.

## The Weighing of the Heart — divine judgment

**Book of the Dead, Chapter 125** (attested from ~1550 BCE) provides the most detailed ancient portrayal of post-mortem divine judgment:

In the Hall of Two Truths (*Maʿat*), the deceased's heart (*ib*) was weighed on a balance scale against the **feather of Maʿat** (truth/righteousness). The weighing was overseen by:
- **Osiris** — presiding as judge
- **Thoth** — recording the result
- **Anubis** — managing the scales
- **42 assessors** — each hearing a negative confession (*"I have not…"*) for specific sins
- **Ammit** (*"Devourer"*) — a composite monster (crocodile + lion + hippopotamus) who consumed the heart if it failed the test

The **negative confessions** (*Declarations of Innocence*) form a moral catalogue: "I have not acted wrongly, I have not stolen, I have not killed, I have not lied…" — approximately 42 declarations corresponding to the 42 assessors.

### Significance for this site

The Weighing of the Heart is the **earliest fully documented scene of individual post-mortem divine judgment** in world religion — attested 1,400+ years before Daniel 12:2 (the first unambiguous biblical resurrection passage). This fact is directly relevant to the question of whether later Jewish and Christian final-judgment imagery was influenced by Egyptian tradition:

- Direct Egyptian influence on Israelite religion: debated; Egyptian material clearly permeated Levantine culture
- The judgment-at-death motif may represent common ANE religious inheritance rather than specific Egyptian → Jewish transmission
- The sequence Egypt → Hellenistic world (via Osiris-Serapis) → early Christian judgment imagery is more clearly documented

## Osirisification — dying persons become Osiris

From the Coffin Texts (~2100–1650 BCE) onward, every deceased Egyptian (not just the pharaoh) was identified with Osiris in death: *"You are Osiris so-and-so"* (*Wsir NN*). This **democratization** of the Osiris identification is a major development:

- Previously, only the dead pharaoh became Osiris
- From the Middle Kingdom onward, every person who undergoes proper burial rites and lives righteously becomes Osiris in death
- The deceased person can then participate in Osiris's eternal existence in the Duat (underworld)

**Parallel with Christian theology:** The democratic Osirisification — anyone who undergoes the rites and lives rightly can achieve divine status in death — structurally parallels the Christian claim that anyone who is baptized "into Christ's death" (Romans 6) participates in Christ's resurrection. Both involve a ritual assimilation to a dying-and-restored deity that grants the participant eschatological salvation. The structural parallel is real; genealogical dependence is not directly attested.

## The Abydos mysteries

**Abydos** (Upper Egypt) was the primary cult site of Osiris — claimed to hold his head or his whole body. The annual **Mystery Play of Abydos** (attested from the Middle Kingdom, ~2000 BCE) was a ritual re-enactment of Osiris's death, mourning, and restoration:

- A flotilla of boats carrying the image of Osiris traveled to his tomb
- Actors representing Set's forces attacked the procession
- The followers of Osiris defended him (ritual combat)
- Lamentation rites followed
- Osiris was restored to his shrine

**H.W. Fairman** (*The Triumph of Horus*, 1974) analyzed the surviving evidence for the Mystery Play and argued that it was one of the earliest documented forms of ritual drama. As a "passion play" — the annual re-enactment of a god's suffering and restoration — it is structurally parallel to later mystery cult initiation (the initiate participates in the deity's suffering and triumph). Whether Eleusinian or Dionysiac mysteries directly borrowed from Abydos is debated but irrelevant to the structural observation.

## The Serapis synthesis

**Serapis** was a deity explicitly constructed by Ptolemy I (~300 BCE) to bridge Egyptian and Greek religious culture. Serapis combined:
- **Osiris-Apis** (the sacred Apis bull associated with Osiris)
- Attributes of **Zeus** (king of the gods)
- Attributes of **Dionysus** (associated with wine, ecstasy, the afterlife)
- Attributes of **Hades/Pluto** (ruler of the dead)
- Attributes of **Asclepius** (healing)

This deliberate religious bricolage was designed to give the Ptolemaic kingdom a deity that both Greek and Egyptian subjects could identify with. The Serapis cult spread throughout the Hellenistic world and contributed to the mystery-cult milieu of the Roman empire.

**For this site:** The Serapis synthesis is the most clearly documented ancient case of **deliberate religious synthesis** — royal power creating a new deity out of multiple traditions to serve political-religious goals. It sets a precedent for understanding similar syntheses elsewhere (Roman Mithraism's creation from Iranian material; Manichaean deliberate synthesis). It also represents the primary vector through which Osirian theology entered the Mediterranean mystery world.

## The dying-and-rising debate applied to Osiris

**Frazer** (*The Golden Bough*): Osiris is the prototype of the dying-and-rising god; his agricultural associations (he is identified with grain, which "dies" and "rises") confirm this.

**Smith, Mark (*Traversing Eternity*, 2009):** Osiris does not "rise" in the sense of returning to the living world. He is revivified in the realm of the dead, where he remains. This is not a resurrection in the Pauline or Zoroastrian sense.

**Griffiths, J. Gwyn (*The Origins of Osiris*, 1966):** Osiris's "restoration" (*wḥm ʿnḫ*) is genuine — he was dead and is now alive — but alive in the realm of the dead.

**Mettinger (*The Riddle of Resurrection*, 2001):** Osiris is the most complex case. His "restoration" is genuine but its nature differs from the dying-and-rising pattern in Baal and Adonis. The dichotomy is less helpful for Osiris than for the Levantine deities.

**Current consensus:** Osiris represents a distinct but related pattern — **divine restoration after death leading to kingship of the dead** — that is structurally parallel to but not identical with the agricultural dying-and-rising pattern in Baal and Adonis or the bodily resurrection in Zoroastrian and Jewish theology.
