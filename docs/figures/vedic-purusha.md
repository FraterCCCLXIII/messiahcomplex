## Consensus snapshot

The **Puruṣa Sūkta** (*Hymn of the Cosmic Person*, Ṛgveda 10.90) is one of the most studied and most cited hymns in the history of Indic religion. It belongs to the late stratum of the Ṛgveda (the tenth *maṇḍala* is generally treated as the most recent); most specialists date it to roughly 1000–900 BCE in its present form, though it draws on older cosmological speculation.

The hymn describes a primordial being of cosmic dimensions—**Puruṣa**, "the Person"—who encompasses the entire universe. The gods perform a **sacrificial dismemberment** of Puruṣa; from his body emerge the major components of the cosmos (sky, earth, sun, moon, wind, fire) and of human social order. The four *varṇas* (social classes) originate from specific body parts: the Brahmin from his mouth, the Kṣatriya from his arms, the Vaiśya from his thighs, the Śūdra from his feet.

This is the **founding mythological charter** of the Vedic sacrificial system: the original sacrifice, performed by the gods, establishes the pattern that all subsequent sacrifice repeats. Every ritual is a cosmogonic re-enactment.

The hymn also appears, with variations, in the **Atharva Veda** (19.6) and in **Yajurvedic** recensions.

## The hymn's key verses (selected)

The following gives the cosmogonic logic; translations from Jamison & Brereton (2014) and Griffith (1896):

> "The Person is this all—what has come to be and what will be." (RV 10.90.2)

> "When the gods spread the sacrifice with the Person as the offering, spring was the melted butter, summer the fuel, autumn the oblation." (10.90.6)

> "When they divided the Person, into how many parts did they partition him? What was his mouth? What were his arms? What were his thighs and feet called?" (10.90.11)

> "The brahmin was his mouth, of both his arms was the rājanya [kṣatriya] made. His thighs became the vaiśya, from his feet the śūdra was produced." (10.90.12)

> "The moon was born from his mind; from his eye the sun was born; from his mouth Indra and Agni, from his breath Vāyu was born." (10.90.13)

> "From his navel came the air's middle-region; from his head the heaven developed; from his two feet came the earth, and the quarters of space from his ear." (10.90.14)

## Prajāpati: the cosmogonic sacrifice elaborated

In the Brāhmaṇa texts (c. 800–600 BCE), Puruṣa is progressively identified with **Prajāpati** ("Lord of Creatures"), who becomes the central figure of Vedic theological cosmogony. The Brāhmaṇas develop the Puruṣa logic in a distinctive direction: **Prajāpati does not merely die once; he continuously disperses himself into creation and requires continual ritual reconstitution**.

The *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* (10.4.2.2) states: "Prajāpati became relaxed (*vikṣīṇa*). He lay spent, his joints loosened. He desired: 'How may I be put together again (*saṃbhaveyam*)?' The gods built him up again by means of this (*agnicayana*, the cosmic fire-altar)."

The **Agnicayana** (fire-altar construction) is explicitly a re-assembling of Prajāpati's body. The bricks represent the bones of his body; the liturgy accompanying each brick placement addresses Prajāpati's reconstitution. Frits Staal (*Agni: The Vedic Ritual of the Fire Altar*, 1983) documented this tradition in the last recorded full performance of the rite (Kerala, 1975).

This is not yet messianic soteriology in the later sense, but it establishes the **ritual logic** that would be picked up in later Indian thought and that has structural parallels across traditions:

- *The cosmos is the broken body of a primordial being.*
- *Restoration requires a repeated act of reconstitution.*
- *The agent of that act stands in the structural position of "savior."*

## Debates

**Is Puruṣa a straightforward reflex of PIE \*Yemo?**
The linguistic evidence is ambiguous. *Yemo* ("twin") reconstructs as the cognate of Vedic *Yama*, Norse *Ymir*, and Iranian *Yima*—all linked to cosmogonic sacrifice in their traditions. *Puruṣa* ("person, man") is a different word. Bruce Lincoln and others treat the Puruṣa Sūkta as the Vedic instantiation of the \*Manu / \*Yemo cosmogonic pattern—with Puruṣa in the structural slot of \*Yemo—but this is a hypothesis about *structural* inheritance, not a direct etymological identification. Some scholars (Witzel) see Puruṣa as a more specifically priestly-Vedic development without direct IE cognate in the same slot. See [Two brothers, divine twins, and fraternal archetypes](../overview/pie-twin-and-brother-archetypes.md).

**The *varṇa* charter and its social function.**
Ṛgveda 10.90 is the earliest text to enumerate the four *varṇa* categories, deriving them from Puruṣa's body parts. This has generated substantial scholarly and polemical debate: does the hymn *describe* an existing social order, *prescribe* a hierarchical one, or does it function as a cosmological legitimation of priestly authority? The hymn's late position in the Ṛgveda may indicate it was composed precisely to provide a cosmic charter for the varṇa system. Ambedkar (*Who Were the Shudras?*, 1946) and subsequent Dalit scholarship have challenged both the historicity and the theological normativity of this origin story.

**Puruṣa and *ātman* / *brahman* speculation.**
Upaniṣadic thought (c. 700–300 BCE) refigures the Puruṣa cosmology in terms of the identity of the cosmic self (*ātman*) with ultimate reality (*brahman*). The cosmic person becomes the inner person; salvation (*mokṣa*) is realization of that identity. This is a significant *transformation* of the Puruṣa myth—from external cosmogonic sacrifice to internal soteriological realization—and represents the Upaniṣadic tradition's characteristic turn away from ritual toward gnosis.

**Puruṣa in Sāṃkhya and Yoga.**
Classical Sāṃkhya philosophy (systematized in Īśvarakṛṣṇa's *Sāṃkhyakārikā*, c. 4th–5th cent. CE) uses *Puruṣa* as the term for pure consciousness, distinct from *Prakṛti* (matter/nature). Here Puruṣa is no longer the sacrificed cosmic man but the transcendent witness-self entangled in matter and seeking liberation (*kaivalya*). This is a conceptual refiguration, not a direct continuation of RV 10.90.

## Salvific extensions and later tradition

**Viṣṇu as the Cosmic Person.**
In the Bhagavad Gītā (11.1–55), Arjuna asks Kṛṣṇa to display his cosmic form (*viśvarūpa*). The vision is of the universal Puruṣa: all worlds contained within, gods, beings, the army itself absorbed into and destroyed by the cosmic mouth. Kṛṣṇa reveals himself as the *lord of time* who consumes creation. The *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* (c. 900–1100 CE) develops an extensive theology of Viṣṇu as *Puruṣottama* ("Supreme Person"), whose cosmic body is creation and whose *avatāras* (descents) are acts of salvific intervention in the cycle of cosmic dissolution and re-creation.

**Structural resonance with Gayōmart.**
The parallel between Puruṣa (body = world, seed = humanity, death = cosmogony) and Zoroastrian **Gayōmart** (body dissolves into earth producing metals and the first human couple, seed generates human lineage) is structurally close and has been noted by comparativists (Widengren, Lincoln). Whether this reflects IE common inheritance, Iranian–Indian contact in the Bronze Age, or convergence remains an open question.
