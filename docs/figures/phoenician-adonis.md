# Adonis (Phoenician)

**Adonis** (Greek: *Ἄδωνις*, from Phoenician/Semitic *ʾadōn* = "lord, master") is a dying deity of Phoenician-Canaanite origin whose annual death and mourning became one of the most widespread ritual practices in the ancient Mediterranean world. His name is a Semitic title (*ʾadōn*, "lord") adopted as a personal name by Greek speakers — a detail that connects him directly to the broader Northwest Semitic divine-lord tradition (including Adonai for YHWH and Baal = "lord" in Ugaritic).

## Etymology and origins

**ʾAdōn** (Semitic): "lord, master" — the same root as:
- Hebrew *ʾādōn* → *Adonai* (a reverent substitute for YHWH in Jewish liturgy)
- Ugaritic *bʿl* = *ʾadōn* = "lord" (the same referent, different word)
- Phoenician cult titles at Byblos

The name was not originally a personal name but a **title** used of the dying vegetation deity at Byblos. Greek speakers adopted it as a personal name (*Adōnis*), which is why the Greek mythographic tradition gives him a biography (birth, loves, death) while the underlying Semitic tradition presents him primarily through the annual ritual.

## The Tammuz connection

**Ezekiel 8:14** is the most direct biblical evidence for the ANE dying-deity mourning tradition:

> *"Then he brought me to the entrance of the north gate of the house of the LORD, and behold, there sat women weeping for Tammuz."*

The passage describes women performing mourning rites for Tammuz (*Tammûz* — the Sumerian-Akkadian Dumuzi) at the Jerusalem Temple gate — evidence that the Mesopotamian lamentation tradition had penetrated Israelite cultic practice. The connection between Tammuz and Adonis was made explicitly by several Church Fathers:

- **Jerome** (*Commentary on Ezekiel*, ad loc.): identifies the Tammuz of Ezekiel 8:14 with Adonis of the Byblos cult
- **Origen** (*Selecta in Ezechielem*): makes the same identification
- **Cyril of Alexandria**: same

These patristic identifications are not certain but reflect the long-recognized structural connection between the Mesopotamian Dumuzi/Tammuz tradition and the Phoenician Adonis tradition: both involve a young male deity who dies (associated with the summer drought or winter), is mourned by a goddess (Inanna/Ishtar for Tammuz; Aphrodite/Astarte for Adonis), and whose return signals agricultural renewal.

## Mythology

The Greek mythological tradition gives Adonis a full biographical narrative:

### Birth

Adonis is born from **Myrrha** (or Smyrna), the daughter of King Cinyras of Cyprus, who was seized by an unnatural passion for her own father (either self-induced through pride, or inflicted by Aphrodite as punishment). Myrrha tricked her father into incest in darkness. Discovered, she fled and was transformed into the myrrh tree (*smyrna* in Greek). Adonis was born when the tree split open.

The myrrh tree birth has symbolic resonance: myrrh was the spice associated with burial and with royalty; Adonis born from myrrh is born already associated with death. The myrrh/death/divine fragrance complex also appears in the Exodus narrative (the tabernacle's anointing oil includes myrrh) and in the Christian nativity story (myrrh as one of the Magi's gifts).

### The two goddesses

The infant Adonis was placed by Aphrodite in a box and entrusted to Persephone (queen of the underworld) for safekeeping. Persephone refused to return him. Zeus or the Muse Calliope adjudicated: Adonis should spend one-third of the year with each goddess, and one-third as he chose. He chose to spend his free portion with Aphrodite.

**Structural reading:** Adonis divides the year between the living world (Aphrodite, fertility, summer) and the dead world (Persephone, winter). This is the same structural pattern as Persephone herself in the Eleusinian myth — a figure whose alternation between worlds explains seasonal alternation. The two myths illuminate each other structurally.

### Death by boar

Adonis was killed while hunting by a wild boar — sent either by the jealous Ares (who resented Aphrodite's love for Adonis) or by Artemis (angered at Adonis's hunting pride). From his blood, red anemone flowers sprang. Aphrodite mourned him intensely.

## Ritual: the Adonia

The **Adonia** was the annual festival commemorating Adonis. Its distinctive features distinguish it from most other Greek festivals:

### Athenian Adonia

- **When**: midsummer (the "scorching" period when crops withered)
- **Who**: primarily women, but the festival was not state-sponsored — it was a private, domestic celebration, marking it as socially different from the great civic festivals
- **How**: women would carry "gardens of Adonis" (*kēpoi Adōnidos*) — shallow baskets or pots planted with quick-germinating seeds (lettuce, fennel, wheat, barley). The seeds would sprout rapidly and then wither in the heat — symbolizing Adonis's brief life. Women would mourn on rooftops, tearing their hair and beating their breasts

**Plato** (*Phaedrus* 276b) uses the "gardens of Adonis" as a metaphor for hasty, superficial effort: quick growth without substance, contrasted with the patient cultivation of true philosophy.

**Plutarch's significant observation** (*Life of Alcibiades* 18): The Athenian expedition to Sicily (415 BCE) departed on a day when *"the women were everywhere displaying images of corpses carried out for burial, and were performing funeral rites, beating their breasts, and singing dirges"* — the Adonia coinciding with the most ambitious military expedition in Athenian history. The omen was considered inauspicious. Whether coincidental or not, this vignette shows the Adonia as a prominent and publicly visible feature of Athenian religious life.

### Ptolemaic Adonia (Theocritus, *Idyll* 15)

Theocritus describes the Adonia festival at Alexandria under Ptolemy II Philadelphus (~274 BCE). The festival was elaborate, organized by Queen Arsinoe, and staged as a public spectacle:
- Two couches (one for Adonis living; one for mourning)
- Display of Adonis and Aphrodite together
- Ritual of Adonis being carried down to the sea the next day

The Alexandrian Adonia is the most fully described ancient account and shows the ritual at its Hellenistic elaboration.

### Byblos (Lucian, *De Dea Syria* 6–9)

At **Byblos** (Gebal, in modern Lebanon — the original Phoenician cult site), the annual festival was tied to a remarkable natural phenomenon:

> "There is also another tale spoken concerning Adonis. They say that the river Adonis which flows past Byblos from the mountains is every year stained with blood, and runs with a red colour into the sea, and this is the sign which they use as the proof of the mourning of the people."
> — Lucian, *De Dea Syria* 7

The river (modern Nahr Ibrahim) does run reddish each year from iron-rich sediment in spring floods. The natural phenomenon was incorporated into the ritual calendar — the red river as Adonis's blood, marking the annual mourning period. Lucian adds that within a few days the river turns white again, corresponding to the end of mourning and celebration of Adonis's return.

## The dying-and-rising debate applied to Adonis

**Frazer** placed Adonis at the center of his dying-and-rising god theory, with the Byblos festival as his key evidence.

**Burkert** (*Structure and History in Greek Mythology and Ritual*, 1979) argues that Adonis represents a **lamentation cult** — a rite centering on mourning — without a clearly attested resurrection. The Adonia mourns; there is no celebration of return in the core Athenian ritual as described.

**Mettinger** (*The Riddle of Resurrection*, 2001) finds evidence for genuine revival: the Byblos ritual ends with the river turning white (transition from mourning to celebration); Lucian explicitly mentions "mourning and then joying" as the two-phase structure. Mettinger rates Adonis as a probable dying-and-rising deity.

**Detienne** (*The Gardens of Adonis*, 1977) reads the Adonia through a structuralist lens as a festival representing the **excess** of both sexuality and death, contrasted with the normative agricultural year: the gardens of Adonis are the opposite of proper farming — quick heat rather than patient cultivation.

## Why Adonis matters for this site

Adonis is the clearest documented case of a Levantine dying deity tradition **entering the Greek world through Phoenician trade contact**:

1. Byblos was a major Phoenician trading city with documented ties to Egypt and Greece from the Bronze Age
2. The Adonia festival appears in Greece at Sappho's time (~600 BCE) — shortly after intensification of Greek-Phoenician contact
3. The festival spread to Alexandria and then Rome following the same trade networks

This is a **real, attested transmission path** showing how Levantine religious motifs moved westward — directly relevant to understanding how similar materials could have moved into the mystery cult ecology that eventually surrounded early Christianity.

The **Tammuz/Adonis → Greek mystery culture** trajectory is the best documented case of ANE religious content entering Greek religious practice, and it sets a precedent for thinking about other putative transmission cases.
