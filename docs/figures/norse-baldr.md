# Norse / Germanic: Baldr

**Baldr** (Balder) is the **radiant son of Odin** whose death sets in motion **Ragnarök** in **Norse** myth. In ***Völuspá*** (Poetic Edda), **after** the destruction, **Baldr returns** to a **renewed** world—a striking **post-catastrophe restoration** motif.

## Dating and sources

| Text | Approximate date | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ***Völuspá*** (late stanzas) | Poem often dated ~10th cent. CE (debated); mss **13th cent.** | Post-Ragnarök stanzas: **Baldr and Höðr** dwell in harmony; fields bear fruit; **golden** age imagery |

Early stanza 62 (Bellows translation): “There shall **come** upon the earth also an **awe**-some age, a **sword**-age, … shall **brothers** be; … **Baldr** shall **come**; …” (numbering varies by edition).

## Role and motifs

- **Death and return**: not a “messiah” in **Abrahamic** sense, but a **restorer** in **renewed** creation alongside **Höðr**, **Hönir**, surviving humans **Líf and Lífthrasir**.
- **Scholarly debate**: **Christian influence** on eschatological stanzas vs. **indigenous** development.

## Scholarly notes

Manuscript dates vs. **oral composition** complicate “first attestation.” The **Arthur** entry is **explicitly prophetic** in medieval **British** texts; Baldr is **mythic** but **comparativists** often group both under **sleeping / returning savior** typologies.
