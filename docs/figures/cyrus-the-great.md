# Cyrus the Great

**Cyrus II of Persia** ("the Great"; Old Persian: *Kūruš*; Hebrew: *Kōreš*; ~600/576–530 BCE) founded the **Achaemenid Persian empire** by conquering the Median empire (~550 BCE), the Lydian kingdom of Croesus (~547 BCE), and Babylon (~539 BCE). He is the most directly documented intersection between Iranian imperial power and Jewish covenant theology in the entire ancient record — and uniquely, the only non-Jewish figure in the Hebrew Bible explicitly called the LORD's *māšîaḥ* (anointed one, messiah).

## Isaiah 44:28 and 45:1 — the messianic oracle

The oracle in Deutero-Isaiah (chapters 40–55, widely dated to the late Babylonian exile, ~550–539 BCE) addresses Cyrus in two verses that have attracted more scholarly comment than any other passage in the Hebrew prophetic corpus:

**Isaiah 44:28:**
> *"Who says of Cyrus, 'He is my shepherd, and he shall fulfill all my purpose'; saying of Jerusalem, 'She shall be built,' and of the temple, 'Your foundation shall be laid.'"*
> — Isaiah 44:28 (ESV)

**Isaiah 45:1:**
> *"Thus says the LORD to his anointed (*limšîḥô*), to Cyrus, whose right hand I have grasped, to subdue nations before him and to loose the belts of kings, to open doors before him that gates may not be closed…"*
> — Isaiah 45:1 (ESV)

The Hebrew word at 45:1 is *limšîḥô* — "to his anointed one" — from the root *m-š-ḥ* that gives us *māšîaḥ* (messiah) and its Greek translation *christos* (Christ). This is a **direct, unambiguous application of the messianic title** to a Persian king. It is not a scholarly inference; it is in the biblical text.

**The theological argument of the passage (Isaiah 45:1–7):**

> *"I am the LORD … I call you by your name, I name you, though you do not know me. I am the LORD, and there is no other … I form light and create darkness, I make well-being and create calamity, I am the LORD, who does all these things."*

The passage explicitly states that Cyrus does not know YHWH (45:4–5): *"I call you by your name, I name you, though you do not know me."* Yet YHWH has nevertheless chosen him. This is theologically extraordinary: a **foreign king, ignorant of the Israelite God, is used by that God as his chosen agent of restoration**. The theological implications are far-reaching:

1. YHWH's sovereignty is **universal**, not limited to those who know him
2. A divinely chosen **restorer** need not be Israelite or even aware of his divine mission
3. The **political act** of restoring the Temple is simultaneously a **cosmic act** — fulfilling YHWH's purpose

These three implications directly shape later Second Temple messianism: the expectation of a cosmic agent of restoration whose identity and action are divinely determined regardless of his own knowledge.

## The Cyrus Cylinder (~539 BCE)

The **Cyrus Cylinder** is a baked clay cylinder (~22.5 cm long) inscribed in Akkadian cuneiform, discovered at Babylon in 1879 by Hormuzd Rassam and now in the British Museum (with a replica in the UN headquarters).

**What it says:** The cylinder is a **royal propaganda text** in standard Mesopotamian tradition:
- It condemns the previous Babylonian king Nabonidus for neglecting the cult of Marduk (the chief Babylonian god)
- It presents Cyrus as the king chosen by Marduk to restore proper worship
- It describes Cyrus returning displaced peoples to their homelands and restoring cult statues to their proper temples
- It presents Cyrus as a merciful, pious king who respects the gods of all peoples

**The "human rights charter" misreading:** The Cyrus Cylinder is sometimes (especially in popular press and Iranian nationalist contexts) described as the world's first "human rights charter." This is anachronistic. The cylinder is standard Mesopotamian royal rhetoric — the same claims are made in earlier Assyrian and Babylonian royal inscriptions. It does not articulate universal rights; it uses the religious-ideological framework of restoring divine order to legitimize conquest. This distinction matters for scholarly use.

**The parallel with Ezra 1:** Despite the ideological nature of the cylinder, its *form* — a proclamation authorizing the return of peoples and the restoration of cult — closely parallels the decree attributed to Cyrus in **Ezra 1:1–4**:

> *"Thus says Cyrus king of Persia: The LORD, the God of heaven, has given me all the kingdoms of the earth, and he has charged me to build him a house at Jerusalem, which is in Judah. Whoever is among you of all his people, may his God be with him, and let him go up to Jerusalem … and rebuild the house of the LORD, the God of Israel."*
> — Ezra 1:2–3 (ESV)

The Aramaic version of a similar decree in Ezra 6:3–5 is plausibly more authentic. Whether the Ezra 1 Hebrew decree is a literary composition or a translation of an authentic document is debated, but the cylinder confirms that such decrees existed as a form of Achaemenid statecraft.

## Cyrus's religious policy across the empire

The Cyrus Cylinder's approach to conquered religions — "I returned all the gods to their places" — is consistent with what is known of Achaemenid religious policy more broadly:

| People | Achaemenid policy | Primary evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Babylonians | Restored Marduk cult; Cyrus presented as chosen by Marduk | Cyrus Cylinder |
| Jews | Authorized Temple rebuilding; restored Temple vessels | Ezra 1–6; Isaiah 44–45 |
| Egyptians | Cambyses II's invasion damaged some temples; Darius I participated in Egyptian religious ceremonies | Egyptian sources |
| Greeks in Asia Minor | Tolerated Greek cults; intervened selectively | Various |

**Mechanism:** The Achaemenid policy is not religious tolerance in a modern sense but **practical imperialism** — maintaining order in a multi-ethnic empire required legitimizing oneself within each region's religious idiom. The result was that different subject peoples received royal decrees framed in their own theological vocabulary: YHWH chose Cyrus (Isaiah 44–45); Marduk chose Cyrus (Cyrus Cylinder); Amun chose Cambyses (Egyptian sources).

**Implication:** The Isaiah 44–45 oracle may reflect not only Jewish theological interpretation of political events but also an **active Achaemenid presentation strategy** — Cyrus or his court may have communicated with Jewish leaders in theological language they would find persuasive. If so, the integration of Persian imperial ideology and Jewish messianic language was not merely a Jewish response to Persian events but a **collaborative ideological product** of the Achaemenid contact zone.

## The Persepolis Fortification Tablets and Mithredath

The **Persepolis Fortification Tablets** (PFTs, ~509–494 BCE) are ~30,000 clay tablets recording provisions distributed from the royal storehouses of Persepolis to workers, officials, and travelers — the administrative backbone of the Achaemenid empire. They provide direct evidence of the empire's administrative structure that created the contact zone.

**Mithredath** (*Mithradata*, "given by Mithra"): The treasurer mentioned in Ezra 1:8 who oversaw the return of Temple vessels under Cyrus. His Iranian theophoric name (*Mithra-* + *-dāta*, "given") places him among the Iranian administrative class embedded in the foundational Jewish restoration narrative. A second Mithredath appears in Ezra 4:7. See [Indo-Iranian Mitra and the contract complex](../overview/indo-iranian-mitra-contract.md) for the *mitra-* / *-dāta* analysis.

## Reception history: Josephus, Xenophon, and later tradition

### Josephus (*Antiquities* 11.1–5, ~94 CE)

Josephus narrates that when Cyrus read the Isaiah prophecy (presumably 44:28 and 45:1), he was moved to fulfill it:

> "This was known to Cyrus by his reading the book which Isaiah left behind him of his prophecies; for this prophet said that God had spoken thus to him in a secret vision: 'My will is, that Cyrus, whom I have appointed to be king over many and great nations, send back my people to their own land, and build my temple.'"
> — Josephus, *Ant.* 11.5 (Whiston translation, adapted)

Whether Cyrus actually saw a copy of Isaiah is historically implausible (if Isaiah is late 6th-century BCE, as in the Deutero-Isaiah hypothesis) but the tradition shows how subsequent Jewish interpretation presented the Isaiah-Cyrus connection as the most direct case of prophetic fulfillment in their scriptures.

### Xenophon's *Cyropaedia* (~370 BCE)

Xenophon's *Cyropaedia* ("Education of Cyrus") is an idealized philosophical biography of Cyrus — not a history but a philosophical novel about the ideal ruler. It was widely read in antiquity and influenced:
- Greek philosophical thinking about kingship (*basilikē*, the art of ruling)
- Roman imperial ideology (Julius Caesar reportedly kept a copy)
- Renaissance political thought (Machiavelli read it carefully)

The *Cyropaedia*'s portrait of Cyrus as a philosopher-king who ruled through wisdom and care for his subjects contributed to the "Cyrus the Great" legend in classical tradition, independently of the biblical messianic reading.

### Modern reception

The Cyrus Cylinder has been used in the modern period for several purposes:
- **Iranian nationalism**: as evidence of the excellence of ancient Persian civilization
- **UN human rights discourse**: as a precursor to the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (a misreading)
- **Jewish-Iranian relations**: as evidence of Persian-Jewish historical friendship (a selective reading)

The scholarly response is to contextualize the cylinder accurately while acknowledging that its *content* — the legitimizing of Cyrus through theological accommodation — is genuine evidence of Achaemenid statecraft.
