# Gilgamesh

**Gilgamesh** is the protagonist of the oldest surviving epic literature (Sumerian poems from the 21st cent. BCE; the Standard Babylonian *Epic of Gilgamesh* from the 12th cent. BCE). His narrative — divine parentage, tyrannical kingship, transformative friendship, quest for immortality after bereavement, and acceptance of mortal limits — establishes the template for hero narratives across the Ancient Near East and, arguably, the Mediterranean.

## Dating and sources

| Source | Approximate date | Content |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Sumerian Gilgamesh poems** | 21st cent. BCE (Ur III period) | Five independent narratives: Bilgames and Huwawa, the Bull of Heaven, etc. |
| **Old Babylonian** *Epic* fragments | ~18th cent. BCE | Integrated narrative; death of Enkidu; quest for Utnapishtim |
| **Standard Babylonian** *Epic* (Sîn-lēqi-unninni) | ~12th cent. BCE | Eleven-tablet recension (+ Tablet XII, appendix) |
| **Hittite and Hurrian** fragments | 2nd millennium BCE | Evidence of transmission beyond Mesopotamia |
| **Ugaritic** references | 14th–12th cent. BCE | Gilgamesh known in Canaanite milieu |

## Role and motifs

| Motif | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Two-thirds divine, one-third mortal** | Son of goddess Ninsun and mortal Lugalbanda; superhuman but mortal |
| **Tyrannical king transformed** | Initially oppresses Uruk; friendship with Enkidu humanizes him |
| **Companion bond / fraternal pair** | Gilgamesh and Enkidu as inseparable pair; Enkidu's death is the narrative pivot |
| **Slaying of monsters** | Humbaba (Cedar Forest guardian); Bull of Heaven |
| **Quest for immortality** | Journey to Utnapishtim (flood survivor) at the edge of the world |
| **Flood narrative** | Tablet XI: Utnapishtim's flood story — the primary ANE parallel to Genesis 6–9, Deucalion, Manu |
| **Failure and acceptance** | Gilgamesh loses the plant of rejuvenation to a serpent; returns to Uruk accepting mortal glory (city walls) as his legacy |
| **Underworld knowledge** | Tablet XII: Enkidu's shade reports conditions of the dead |

## Cross-tradition parallels

- **Heracles**: divine parentage; labors/quests; confrontation with mortality; but Heracles achieves apotheosis where Gilgamesh does not — the contrast is itself significant
- **Noah / Utnapishtim / Ziusudra / Deucalion / Manu**: the flood narrative embedded in Tablet XI is the clearest case of narrative diffusion from Mesopotamia into biblical, Greek, and (possibly) Indic tradition
- **Achilles**: mortal hero who chooses glory over long life; companion's death (Patroclus / Enkidu) as turning point
- **Cain and Abel / Jacob and Esau**: the Gilgamesh-Enkidu pair (civilized / wild) resonates with biblical fraternal-pair typology, though no direct borrowing is established for the pair motif itself

## Scholarly notes

The *Epic of Gilgamesh* was rediscovered in 1853 (George Smith's reading of the Flood tablet in 1872 caused a sensation). It is now the touchstone for ANE literary studies and a key control case for this site's methodology: the Gilgamesh–Genesis flood parallel is an established case of **literary diffusion** (not merely typological resemblance), because textual transmission through Mesopotamian scribal culture can be documented. This contrasts with more speculative claims of diffusion in other domains.
