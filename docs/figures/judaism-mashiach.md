# Judaism: Mashiach

**Mashiach** (מָשִׁיחַ, “anointed one”) refers in the Hebrew Bible primarily to **anointed kings and priests**; **future eschatological messiah** expectations crystallize in **prophetic and later Second Temple** literature.

## Dating and sources

| Phase | Approximate date | Sources |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Royal / priestly anointing | Iron Age onward | Historical books, Psalms |
| Future **ideal king** from **David’s line** | ~8th–6th cent. BCE | Isaiah 11; Jeremiah; Ezekiel; etc. |
| Apocalyptic and **son of man** imagery | ~2nd cent. BCE onward | **Daniel**; Dead Sea Scrolls; rabbinic literature develops diverse messianic scenarios |

“Messiah” is **not** a single fixed job description in antiquity: texts imagine **priestly**, **royal**, **prophetic**, or **composite** figures; **timing** and **two-messiah** schemes appear in later rabbinic thought.

## Moses and the Exodus narrative: when were the texts composed?

Moses as narrator-hero of Exodus–Deuteronomy is central to Jewish (and shared Abrahamic) memory. Two horizons are commonly distinguished:

1. **Traditional reading**: Mosaic authorship and narrative events in a Bronze Age setting (many chronologies in the ballpark of 15th–13th cent. BCE for the story-world of the text).
2. **Modern critical scholarship**: the Pentateuch as edited literature with sources and layers; widespread view that much of the present text crystallized in exilic and post-exilic periods (roughly 6th–5th cent. BCE and later), incorporating older oral and written material to degrees debated by specialists.

This site does not adjudicate faith claims or replace commentaries; it flags the dating gap so comparisons with other traditions do not assume a single uncontested timeline. For typological comparison of biography motifs (Buddha-schema heuristic), see [Mythic biography patterns — prophet lives and Buddha-schema analogy](../overview/mythic-biography-patterns-prophet-and-buddha-analogy.md).

## Role and motifs

- **Restoration of Israel**, **Temple**, **Torah** governance; **peace among nations** (e.g. Isaiah’s imagery).
- **Judgment** of oppressors and **vindication** of the righteous—developed differently than in Christian or Islamic systems that reinterpret the same Hebrew terms.

## Scholarly notes

Christian and Jewish readings of the **same verses** diverge sharply; this site maps **Jewish** textual trajectories **without** presupposing Christian fulfillment. Second Temple diversity (e.g. **Qumran**) shows **multiple** concurrent expectations.

## Sources & dating

### Primary evidence

| Witness / work | Language | Approx. date | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hebrew Bible (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Psalms, Daniel) | Hebrew | Iron Age – ~165 BCE | Critical editions: Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia (BHS) or Biblia Hebraica Quinta (BHQ); NRSV, JPS Tanakh translations |
| Septuagint (LXX) | Greek | ~3rd–2nd cent. BCE | Greek translation of Hebrew scriptures; often renders *mashiach* as *christos* |
| Dead Sea Scrolls | Hebrew / Aramaic | ~250 BCE – 68 CE | Includes messianic Rule texts, War Scroll, Testimonia; critical editions in DJD series (Clarendon) |
| Pseudepigrapha (1 Enoch, Psalms of Solomon, 4 Ezra) | Aramaic / Greek / Hebrew | ~2nd cent. BCE – 1st cent. CE | Diverse messianic scenarios outside the canon |
| Mishnah and Talmud (relevant tractates) | Hebrew / Aramaic | ~200–600 CE (compiled) | Rabbinic elaboration of messianic expectation |

### Tier B — specialist analyses

- **Collins, John J.** *The Scepter and the Star: Messianism in Light of the Dead Sea Scrolls.* 2nd ed., Eerdmans — the standard survey of Second Temple diversity.
- **Horsley, Richard A.** *Messianism among Jews and Christians.* Fortress — social and political angles on messianic movements.
- **Schiffman, Lawrence H.** *Reclaiming the Dead Sea Scrolls.* JPS / Yale — Qumran messianic texts in context.
- **Novak, David.** "Jewish Eschatology." Ch. 6 in Jerry L. Walls (ed.), *The Oxford Handbook of Eschatology*. Oxford University Press, 2007 — covers the Davidic patrimony of the Messiah-king, the distinction between "projective" (political restoration) and "apocalyptic" (heavenly descent) messianism in rabbinic sources, Bar Kokhba as a failed messianic claimant endorsed by Rabbi Akiva (~135 CE), and Reform Judaism's transformation of the personal Messiah into the "messianic age" (*olam ha-ba* → collective redemption). Useful for tracing messianic doctrine from Second Temple through modern Jewish movements.
- **Encyclopaedia Judaica** (2nd ed.) — *Messiah* — encyclopedic overview with full bibliography.
