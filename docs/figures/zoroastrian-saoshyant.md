# Zoroastrianism: Saoshyant

## Context

- **Audience:** Readers comparing **future restorer** figures (Maitreya, Mahdi, Parousia) who need a **textual** and **chronological** anchor for Zoroastrian eschatology.
- **Question:** Where does **Saoshyant** language appear in the **Avesta**, how does it develop in **Pahlavi** tradition, and why is "savior" a risky translation?
- **Scope:** Not a full history of Zoroastrianism — only the **eschatological** complex relevant to this site's comparisons.

## Etymology and morphology

**Saošyant** is an active participle formed on the future stem of a verbal root √*sū-/sau-* (cognate with Old Indic √*śū-/śav-*; PIE \**ḱueH₁* — "to swell"). In Old Indic the verb ranges across "to swell, be strong, increase, prosper." In Avestan, a further semantic development produces "to be of use, profit, advantage" (intransitive) and "to create profit, to further" (transitive).

The Zoroastrian tradition understood the name in this latter sense. Yasht 13.129 offers an etymological explanation: the Saoshyant "will further (*sāwayāt*) all material life." The Pahlavi gloss *sūdōmand* ("useful, profitable") reflects the same etymology — from the substantive *sūd* ("profit, advantage") < Old Iranian \**sūti-*.

**Why "savior" is problematic:** The approximation "savior" (Bartholomae) carries unavoidable Christian theological associations. Since the term also refers to **contemporary** individuals, including Zoroaster himself, many scholars prefer **"benefactor"** (Darmesteter; Humbach & Faiss) or **"helper"** (Lommel). The Encyclopaedia Iranica article (Malandra 2000/2013) recommends these more neutral translations for non-eschatological contexts.

## Evolution across the Avestan strata

### Gathas: "benefactors" — contemporary and future

In the Gathas, *saošyant-* language applies to **Zoroaster himself** and to **righteous followers**, both living and anticipated:

- **Self-referential:** Y. 48.9 — "Let it be spoken to me, the poetry of Good Mind! May the Saošyant know how his reward shall be!" Also Y. 53.2: "the Vision of the Saošyant which the Lord gave."
- **Plural, future:** Y. 48.12–13 — Zoroaster asks to whom Good Mind's discernment will come, and responds: "They will be Saošyants of the countries … designated as the adversaries of Wrath." The Pahlavi gloss renders this as *frašegird-kardār* ("makers of the Renovation").
- **Metaphorical:** Y. 46.3 — "When will those bulls of days rise forth to support the world of Truth? With mature teachings (when will) the intellects of the Saošyants (rise forth)?"

The Gathic Saošyant is thus **not yet a single eschatological figure** but a **category** of righteous actors who "further" (*sāwayāt*) the world.

### Younger Avesta: the eschatological Victorious Saošyant

A fully developed theology of the Saošyant as savior figure at the end of time appears in the Younger Avestan texts:

- **The span of history:** Y. 26.10 declares: "We worship all the Frawašis of the righteous, from Gaya Marǝtan (the first man) to the Victorious Saošyant" — framing the Saoshyant as the bookend of history.
- **Birth from Zoroaster's seed:** Vendidad 19.5 and Yasht 19.92; 13.62 describe how the Saoshyant will arise from the **Kąsaoya sea**, sprung from the **seed of Zoroaster** watched over by **99,999 Frawašis**.
- **Mother's name:** Two names are given: Ǝrǝdat.fǝδrī and **Wīspa.taurwarī** ("all-overcoming"); Yasht 13.142 explains: she is "all-overcoming because she will give birth (to him) who will overcome all hostilities of daēwas and men."
- **Cosmic renovation:** Yasht 19.89 — the Kāwyan Glory (*xᵛarǝnah*) "will accompany the Victorious Saošyant … when he will make existence wonderful (*frašǝm*), not aging, imperishable, not rotting, not putrefying, ever-living, ever-thriving, **when the dead will rise up** …" The primordial paradise of Yima will be reclaimed in the end.
- **Haoma's prophecy:** Yasna 9.2 — Haoma exhorts Zoroaster to praise him "for strength **as the future Saošyants will praise me**" — linking the Saoshyant to the Haoma ritual tradition.

### Pahlavi: three Saoshyants and the final sacrifice

In the Pahlavi tradition (Bundahišn ch. 33–34), three sons of Zoroaster are begotten through the **preservation of his semen** in the Kąsaoya sea. Only the last bears the name **Sōšyāns**:

| Saoshyant | Avestan name | Born (year) | Role |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Ušēdar** | Uxšyat.ǝrǝta | 9,970 | Ushers in the 10th millennium; brief period of increased goodness, then decline |
| **Ušēdarmāh** | Uxšyat.nǝmah | 10,970 | Ushers in the 11th millennium; same pattern |
| **Sōšyāns** | Astwat.ǝrǝta | 11,943 | The final and true Saoshyant |

## The eschatological tauroctony and the White Hōm {#tauroctony}

The culminating act of the Frašegird (Renovation) in Bundahišn 34 is one of the most significant passages for this site's comparative investigation:

> Sōšyāns will begin the accomplishment of the Frašegird in 11,973 with the **resurrection of the dead** and the performance of the **final sacrifice of the bull Hadayānš**, from whose fat the **White Hōm** will be rendered, whereby **all will become immortal**.

This passage concentrates several motifs of cross-tradition significance:

| Element | Detail | Cross-tradition resonance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Tauroctony** (bull-sacrifice) | Slaying of the cosmic bull Hadayānš as the final sacrificial act | Roman Mithraic tauroctony — Mithras slaying the bull, from which life springs (grain from tail, wine from blood in some interpretations). See [Roman Mithras and Manichaean Mithra](../overview/mithra-roman-manichaean.md) |
| **White Hōm** (Haoma) | An elixir of immortality rendered from the bull's fat | Vedic **Soma** (same Indo-Iranian ritual plant/substance: Avestan *haoma* = Vedic *soma*, from PIE \**sewH-* "to press"). Also cf. Greek **ambrosia** / **nektar**; Hindu **amṛta** ("deathless"); Christian Eucharistic wine |
| **Universal immortality** | All beings — not just the righteous — are made immortal | Contrasts with traditions that reserve salvation for believers; parallels the universalist strand in Christian *apokatastasis* (Origen) and some Buddhist Mahāyāna soteriology |
| **Resurrection of the dead** | Physical resurrection precedes the sacrifice | Parallels Christian bodily resurrection and Jewish *tehiyyat ha-metim*; see [Eschatological vocabulary timeline](../overview/eschatological-vocabulary-timeline.md) |

### Why the Zoroastrian tauroctony matters

The connection between the **Saoshyant's eschatological bull-sacrifice** and the **Roman Mithraic tauroctony** is one of the most suggestive structural parallels in this site's investigation:

1. **Both involve a bull-slaying** as the central cosmic-sacrificial act.
2. **Both produce life from the sacrifice** — in the Bundahišn, the Hōm renders immortality; in Roman Mithraism, vegetation and cosmic renewal spring from the slain bull.
3. **Both are tied to Iranian religious tradition** — the Saoshyant is Zoroastrian; Roman Mithras bears an Iranian divine name, whatever the degree of Roman creative transformation.

However, the **chronological gap** is significant. The Roman Mithraic tauroctony is attested in mithraeum art from the 1st–4th cent. CE, while the Bundahišn is a Sasanian/post-Sasanian composition (though drawing on older materials). Whether both derive from a common pre-existing Iranian myth of cosmic bull-sacrifice, or whether the Pahlavi account is an independent development, remains debated. The bull sacrifice (*gōspand*) is deeply embedded in Indo-Iranian ritual tradition, providing a plausible common substrate.

| Motif | Detail |

| **Linear history** | Creation damaged by evil but destined for final restoration — contrasted with purely cyclical models |
| **Three-stage eschatology** | Three Saoshyants, each ushering in a millennium, with the final Sōšyāns accomplishing the definitive renovation |
| **Tauroctony** | Sacrificial slaying of bull Hadayānš at the end of time |
| **White Hōm** | Elixir of immortality rendered from the sacrifice |
| **Universal immortality** | All — not just the righteous — are made deathless |
| **Seed/lake tradition** | Saoshyant born from preserved semen of Zoroaster in Kąsaoya sea; virgin-birth resonance |
| **Reclamation of Yima's paradise** | The primordial golden age is restored |

## Scholarly notes

Translations like "savior" can **Christianize** the term; many scholars prefer **"benefactor"** or **restorer** for non-eschatological contexts. The **virgin/seed** motifs in later tradition are important for **comparative eschatology** but should be read in **Zoroastrian** terms first.

The semantic evolution — from Gathic "righteous benefactors" to Young Avestan "single eschatological Victorious Saošyant" to Pahlavi "three sequential Saoshyants culminating in cosmic tauroctony" — illustrates a process of **theological elaboration** that has its own internal logic and should not be treated as a simple anticipation of Christian messianism, even where cross-tradition influence is plausible.
