# Zarathustra (Zoroaster)

**Zarathustra** (Avestan: *Zaraθuštra*; Greek: *Zōroastrēs*; Latin: *Zoroaster*) is the prophet and founder-figure of Zoroastrianism. He is the author, in scholarly consensus, of the five *Gāthās* — the oldest surviving Zoroastrian texts and among the oldest surviving literature in any Indo-Iranian language. His theological innovations — radical ethical dualism, individual moral choice, bodily resurrection, final judgment, cosmic renovation — shaped the eschatological vocabulary that subsequently influenced Judaism, Christianity, Buddhism (through the Gandhāran corridor), and Islam.

## Dating and sources

The dating of Zarathustra is one of the most contested chronological problems in ancient religious history. Three main positions:

| Position | Date range | Basis | Proponents |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Linguistic / philological** | ~1500–1000 BCE | Old Avestan of the Gathas closely resembles Rigvedic Sanskrit; both are early Indo-Iranian dialects suggesting contemporaneous composition | Kellens, Pirart, Malandra, Gnoli (later position) |
| **Parsi traditional** | ~628–551 BCE | Persian tradition: Zarathustra lived 258 years before Alexander's conquest of Persia (330 BCE), thus born ~628 BCE | Preserved in Zoroastrian tradition; accepted by some Western scholars |
| **Greek classical sources** | 6,000–8,000 years before Plato | Pliny, Xanthus of Lydia, Eudoxus of Cnidus — clearly mythological scale | Not used for historical purposes |

**Current scholarly majority:** The linguistic evidence for an early date (~1000–1200 BCE) is taken seriously by most philologists. The late date (~628–551 BCE) coincides with the Achaemenid period and may reflect later tradition about when Zoroastrianism became a court religion rather than when the prophet lived.

**The uncertainty matters:** If Zarathustra lived ~1200–1000 BCE, the foundational Zoroastrian theological developments (dualism, resurrection, judgment) pre-date the Hebrew prophets and Maccabean-period apocalypticism by 600–800 years — strengthening the Iranian-influence argument. If he lived ~628–551 BCE, the chronological priority is much less clear.

## The name and its meaning

*Zaraθuštra* is a compound name in Old Avestan:
- **Zara-** (or *Zarat-*): either from *zara-* ("old") or from a root meaning "having/driving" — interpretation debated
- **-θuštra** (*uštra* = "camel"): "camel" is secure

Proposed meanings: "He whose camels are old" (Bartholomae), "He who drives/has camels" (others). The name appears to be a personal name from a pastoral nomadic milieu — consistent with the cattle-herding, steppe-adjacent culture reflected in the Gathas.

The Greek form *Zōroastrēs* may represent a popular etymology (*zōro-* = "golden" + *astēr* = "star" → "star of gold"), reflecting the later tradition of Zoroaster as an astrologer and Magi.

## The Gathas — primary sources

The ***Gāthās*** (Old Avestan: "songs") are five hymnic compositions embedded within the *Yasna* (the primary liturgical text of Zoroastrianism). They are designated by their Avestan names and correspond to Yasna chapters 28–53:

| Gāthā | Yasna chapters | Opening | Tone |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *Ahunavaiti* | 28–34 | *Yā ahu vairyā* | Praise, cosmic questions, the two spirits |
| *Ushtavaiti* | 43–46 | *Ushtā ahmāi* | Personal vision, revelation |
| *Spentā Mãinyu* | 47–50 | *Spentā mãinyu* | Ethical teaching |
| *Vohu Khshathra* | 51 | *Vohu khshathrā* | Good kingdom, judgment |
| *Vahishtōishti* | 53 | *Vahishtōishtish* | Hymn on the coming of the righteous |

The Gathas are genuinely difficult texts — archaic, elliptical, liturgical, often ambiguous. They require expertise in Old Avestan and cannot be safely read through later Zoroastrian tradition without distortion. The critical editions of Kellens and Pirart (*Les textes vieil-avestiques*, 3 vols., 1988–1991) are the scholarly standard.

### The "Two Spirits" — Yasna 30.3–5

The most quoted and most theologically significant passage:

> "Truly there are two primordial Spirits, twins renowned to be in conflict. In thought and word and act they are two: the better and the bad. And those who act well have chosen rightly between these two, not so the evildoers."
>
> — Yasna 30.3 (Insler translation, adapted)

> "And when these two Spirits first came together, they created life and not-life, and that at the last the worst existence [hell] shall be for the followers of Druj but for the followers of Asha, the best thought [paradise]."
>
> — Yasna 30.4 (Insler)

This passage is the **foundational statement of cosmic ethical dualism** in the Zoroastrian tradition. The "two spirits" (*mainyū* — spirits, mentalities, or modes of existence) choose between *asha* (truth, righteousness, cosmic order) and *druj* (lie, disorder, corruption). This choice structures the whole of reality: every moral action aligns with one or the other.

**Critical debate:** Are the "two spirits" co-eternal and equally primordial? Or is one (the good) an aspect of Ahura Mazda himself, making the dualism subordinate to a higher monotheism? The Gathic text can be read either way; later Zoroastrian tradition (Zurvanite and orthodox) interprets it differently.

### Vision and revelation — Yasna 43–46

The *Ushtavaiti Gāthā* contains Zarathustra's most personal statements — what appears to be a description of his calling and vision:

> "This I ask you, tell me truly, Ahura — whether a righteous man shall attain that kingdom which we claim for ourselves, for the Saviours and the truthful, or whether the Teacher's voice is for naught?"
>
> — Yasna 44.9 (Insler)

Zarathustra presents himself as:
- A priest-prophet (*zaotar*) who has received direct instruction from Ahura Mazda
- A figure who faces opposition from the "*kavis*" (petty kings) and "*karapans*" (priests of the old religion) who resist his teaching
- A man who asks uncomfortable questions of Ahura Mazda — approaching the divine through dialogue rather than simply performing ritual

**The reformer dimension:** Several Yasna passages show Zarathustra opposing the old Iranian religion's cattle sacrifice (specifically the *haoma* ritual in its violent form) and the worship of *daēva* (beings he demotes from gods to demons). This reform — selecting Ahura Mazda as the supreme being, demoting other divine figures — is the theological act that distinguishes Zarathustra's religion from the Indo-Iranian polytheism reflected in the Rigveda.

## Ahura Mazda and the Amesha Spentas

Zarathustra's supreme being is **Ahura Mazda** ("Lord of Wisdom" — *ahura* = lord; *mazdā* = wisdom, from PIE *\*mn-dh-o-s*, "thought" + *\*dʰh₁-*). The six **Amesha Spentas** ("Holy Immortals") are divine beings who emanate from Ahura Mazda and personify both cosmic attributes and material realms:

| Amesha Spenta | Meaning | Realm | Parallel |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *Vohu Manah* | Good Mind | Cattle | Archangel of beneficial thought |
| *Asha Vahishta* | Best Truth/Righteousness | Fire | Archangel of cosmic order |
| *Khshathra Vairya* | Desired Dominion | Metal/Sky | Archangel of divine kingdom |
| *Spenta Armaiti* | Holy Devotion | Earth | Archangel of piety |
| *Haurvatāt* | Wholeness/Health | Water | Archangel of wholeness |
| *Ameretāt* | Immortality | Plants | Archangel of immortality |

**Parallel with Jewish angelology:** The seven Amesha Spentas (including Ahura Mazda as their head) constitute a divine hierarchy structurally similar to the seven archangels of late Second Temple Judaism (Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Uriel, Saraqael, Raguel, Remiel in 1 Enoch 20). Whether the Jewish angelic hierarchy derived from the Zoroastrian one is the central question addressed in [Jewish angelology and yazata parallels](../overview/jewish-angelology-and-yazata-parallels.md).

## Zarathustra as the first Saoshyant

The term ***saošyant*** (*saošiiānt-*, "one who will bring benefit / one who brings wholeness") appears in the Gathas referring to righteous helpers of the cosmic cause — and in some readings to Zarathustra himself:

> "Zarathustra Spitama himself is the *saošyant* of the world, the one through whom Ahura Mazda shall make the world victorious."
>
> — Yasna 46.3 (interpretive reading; translation debated)

In later Zoroastrian theology, the *saošyant* role is extended and projected eschatologically: three posthumous sons of Zarathustra (Ukhshyatereta, Ukhshyatnema, Astvatereta) will appear at successive 1,000-year intervals before the final Renovation. This eschatological development — a messianic figure of cosmic scope who is descended from the founder and who brings about the end — is one of the most significant concepts in the Iranian–Jewish eschatological comparison. See [Saoshyant influence hypotheses](../overview/saoshyant-influence-hypotheses.md).

## Death of Zarathustra

The Gathas say nothing explicit about Zarathustra's death. Later tradition (Denkard Book 5; Shahnameh of Firdausi) preserves accounts:

- **Denkard account**: Zarathustra was murdered while praying before the sacred fire by a Turanian (a nomadic northern people), killed with a sword — a martyrdom narrative
- **Shahnameh (Firdausi, ~1010 CE)**: Zarathustra is killed by a soldier named *Tur-i Bradar-vakhsh* (a Turanian warrior) in a Zoroastrian temple

The martyrdom tradition is post-Gathic and cannot be confirmed historically, but it established a biographical parallel with other founder-prophets who suffered persecution.

## Comparison: prophet-founders across traditions

| Feature | Zarathustra | Moses | Muhammad | The Buddha |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Direct divine revelation | Yes — Ahura Mazda | Yes — YHWH at Sinai | Yes — Jibril / Quran | No — self-enlightenment |
| Confrontation with established religious authorities | Yes — kavis and karapans | Yes — Pharaoh | Yes — Meccan polytheists | Yes — Brahminical tradition |
| Radical theological reformulation | Dualism; demotion of daēvas | Covenant monotheism | Strict tawhid | Rejection of ātman |
| Textual compositions attributed to founder | Gathas (Y. 28–53) | (None certain) | Quran (transmitted) | None (teachings transmitted) |
| Legacy: posthumous messianic figure | Saošyant (posthumous sons) | No direct parallel | Mahdi (from his community) | Maitreya (future Buddha) |

The structural similarity of these founder-biography patterns is addressed in [Mythic biography patterns](../overview/mythic-biography-patterns-prophet-and-buddha-analogy.md).
