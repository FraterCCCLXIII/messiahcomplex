# Baal (Canaanite)

**Baal Hadad** (Ugaritic: *bʿl hdd*; the title *bʿl* = "lord, master, owner"; the name *Hadad* = "thunderer") is the storm god of the Canaanite-Ugaritic pantheon. He is the protagonist of the most important surviving mythological cycle from the ancient Levant — the **Baal Cycle** (KTU 1.1–1.6, ~1400–1200 BCE). His death-and-revival narrative is the clearest ancient Near Eastern case for a "dying and rising" deity pattern in the Levant, and his mythology left documentable traces throughout the Hebrew Bible.

## Dating and sources

| Source | Date | Language | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Baal Cycle (KTU 1.1–1.6) | ~1400–1200 BCE (tablet date); mythology may be older | Ugaritic (alphabetic cuneiform) | Primary; discovered at Ras Shamra 1929 |
| Other KTU mythological texts (Aqhat, Keret) | Same period | Ugaritic | Baal appears as secondary figure |

| Philo of Byblos (*Phoenician History*, preserved in Eusebius, *Praep. Ev.*) | ~100 CE (composition); preserves Sanchuniathon traditions | Greek | Secondary; Hellenistic-era Phoenician tradition |
| Lucian of Samosata, *De Dea Syria* | ~2nd cent. CE | Greek | Byblos cult; Adonis/Baal lament rite |

The **Ras Shamra excavations** (beginning 1929, directed by Claude Schaeffer) unearthed the Ugaritic tablets from a scribal archive near a temple complex. Ugarit was destroyed around 1185 BCE, probably by the Sea Peoples, and the archive was never reopened — leaving the tablets in remarkable condition.

## The Baal Cycle narrative

### Cast of characters

| Figure | Role | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| *El* (*ʾIl*) | Patriarch-god; king of the assembly | Remote but authoritative; "Father of Years" |
| *Baal Hadad* | Storm god; protagonist | "Rider of Clouds"; "Mightiest of Warriors" |
| *Yamm* (*Yam Nahar*) | Sea/River; Baal's antagonist | El's favorite; primordial chaos power |
| *Mot* | Death; second antagonist | Lord of the underworld; invites Baal to his realm |
| *Anat* | Warrior goddess; Baal's sister | Retrieves Baal; kills Mot |
| *Asherah* (*ʾAṯirat*) | El's wife; mother of the gods | Intercedes with El on Baal's behalf |
| *Kothar-wa-Khasis* | Craftsman god | Forges Baal's weapons; builds his palace |

### Sequence of events

**Combat with Yamm (Tablets I–II):** El initially grants kingship to Yamm over the divine assembly. Yamm's envoys come before the divine council to demand Baal. Baal challenges Yamm and, using two magical weapons forged by Kothar-wa-Khasis — *Yagrush* ("Driver") and *Aymur* ("Expeller") — defeats Yamm in direct combat. Baal establishes his kingship.

**The palace (Tablets III–IV):** Baal demands a palace (*bētu*) appropriate to his kingly status on Mt. Zaphon. He persuades Asherah to intercede with El; El grants permission. Kothar builds an elaborate palace of cedar, silver, gold, and lapis lazuli. Baal's kingship is secured.

**Death (Tablet V):** Mot ("Death"), ruler of the underworld, invites Baal to a feast in his realm — "the throat of Mot." Against divine warnings, Baal descends. He dies; vegetation ceases; rain stops. El performs full mourning rites: descends from his throne, tears his garments, pours earth on his head, sits on the ground.

**Revival (Tablet VI):** Anat finds Baal's body and mourns. She then confronts Mot with extraordinary violence:

> "She seizes Mot son of El; with a sword she cleaves him, with a sieve she winnows him, with fire she burns him, with millstones she grinds him, in the field she scatters him. His remains the birds eat, the sparrows consume his portions."
> — KTU 1.6.ii.30–37 (Smith–Parker translation, adapted)

El has a dream-vision that Baal lives: *"The heavens rained oil, the wadis ran with honey."* Baal returns; rain resumes; the agricultural cycle is restored. Baal defeats Mot in a final combat and reestablishes his eternal kingship.

## Epithets and their biblical equivalents

| Ugaritic epithet | Meaning | Biblical equivalent | Reference |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *rkb ʿrpt* | "Rider of Clouds" | YHWH as "rider in the deserts/clouds" | Psalm 68:4 (MT *bāʿărābôt*); Deuteronomy 33:26 |
| *aliyan bʿl* | "Mightiest Baal" | YHWH as cosmic warrior | Psalm 24:8 ("mighty in battle") |
| *bʿl* | "Lord" | *Adon* / *YHWH* | Multiple; the Baal polemic in Hosea plays on this |
| Mt. Zaphon (*ṣpn*) | Baal's sacred mountain | *yarkĕtê ṣāpôn* ("the recesses of Zaphon") | Psalm 48:2–3 — applied to Jerusalem |
| Combat with Yamm/Lotan | Defeat of sea-chaos | YHWH vs. *Yam*, *Rahab*, Leviathan | Ps. 74:12–14; 89:9–10; Job 26:12; Isa. 27:1 |

**The Leviathan/Lotan correspondence** is the most philologically precise connection: Ugaritic *Lôtān ḥiwwāt ʿāqlāt* = "Lotan, the fleeing serpent" (*bṯn ʿqltn*, "twisting serpent"); Isaiah 27:1 = *Liwyātān nāḥāš bārîaḥ … nāḥāš ʿăqallātôn* ("Leviathan the fleeing serpent … the twisting serpent"). The lexical correspondence is exact.

## Baal worship in ancient Israel

The Hebrew Bible's polemic against Baal is extensive enough to document the widespread practice of Baal worship among Israelites — not merely among neighboring peoples:

**1 Kings 18: Elijah and the prophets of Baal**
The contest on Mt. Carmel — Elijah vs. 450 prophets of Baal and 400 prophets of Asherah — confirms that organized Baal worship with a professional priesthood operated in Israel under Ahab and Jezebel (9th cent. BCE). The narrative structure itself reveals the theological competition: Baal as storm god (who should have been able to call down fire) is defeated by YHWH.

**Hosea (8th cent. BCE)**
Hosea uses the metaphor of marital infidelity throughout — Israel has gone after her "lovers" (*bĕʿālîm*), the Baals. The agricultural gifts (grain, wine, oil) attributed by the people to the Baals are actually YHWH's gifts (Hosea 2:8). The intimate knowledge of Canaanite agricultural religion that Hosea displays suggests that Baal worship was not a foreign import but an endemic practice in Israelite agricultural life.

**Kuntillet Ajrud and Khirbet el-Qom inscriptions**
These 9th–8th century BCE inscriptions — found in the Sinai desert and the Judean hills respectively — invoke blessings by "YHWH of Samaria and his Asherah" (*wĕ-ʾăšêrātô*). The feminine suffix *-ô* ("his") treats Asherah as a personal name, not an object — direct physical evidence of a YHWH-Asherah cult at the popular or official level. This shows the Canaanite pantheon operating inside Israelite religion decades after the "Solomonic" period.

## The divine council and Psalm 82

The **divine council** scene in **Psalm 82** makes full sense only against the Ugaritic background. YHWH stands in the *ʿădat ʾēl* ("assembly/congregation of El") and judges the *ʾĕlōhîm* ("gods"):

> "God has taken his place in the divine council; in the midst of the gods he holds judgment: 'How long will you judge unjustly? … I said, "You are gods, sons of the Most High, all of you; nevertheless, like men you shall die…"'"
> — Psalm 82:1–7

The psalm enacts YHWH's takeover of the divine council — he replaces El as the presiding judge and condemns the other divine beings (the *bĕnê ha-ʾĕlōhîm* = divine council members = demoted Canaanite gods) to mortality. This is a **deliberate theological response** to the Ugaritic tradition: YHWH subsumes all functions of the Ugaritic divine council.

## The dying-and-rising debate

Baal's death and return is the central exhibit in the scholarly debate over "dying and rising gods" initiated by Frazer:

**Frazer's claim** (*The Golden Bough*, 1890–1915): Baal is the type specimen of the dying-and-rising vegetation deity whose annual death and return structures the agricultural year.

**Smith's critique** (*Drudgery Divine*, 1990): No ancient source groups these deities together as a type; Baal's "resurrection" is uncertain; the category is a modern scholarly construction imposed on disparate phenomena.

**Mettinger's response** (*The Riddle of Resurrection*, 2001): The Ugaritic text does describe Baal's revival (KTU 1.6.iii — Baal returns, rains resume, El has the confirmation dream). The text constitutes genuine evidence for a dying-and-rising pattern. Mettinger calls Baal a "disappearing and reappearing god" rather than a dying-and-rising god in the full Christian sense — his annual absence is seasonal, his revival agricultural, not cosmic in the way Christ's resurrection is claimed to be.

**Current consensus:** The category of dying-and-rising deity is real for Baal (and probably Adonis and Dumuzi) as an agricultural-mythological pattern; it is a recognizable structural type in the ancient Near East; it does not directly prove genealogical influence on Christian resurrection theology, which rests on its own historical foundation and theological claims.

## Comparative significance

Baal is significant for this site's framework at multiple levels:

1. **Zone 5 (PIE & deep grammar):** The cosmic combat pattern (storm god defeats sea-chaos) reflects the same narrative structure found in Vedic Indra-Vritra, Greek Zeus-Typhon, and Babylonian Marduk-Tiamat. Whether this reflects PIE inheritance, ANE diffusion, or both is the open question.

2. **Zone 2 (Achaemenid contact):** The divine council theology documented in the Baal Cycle became the background for Jewish angelology (the *bĕnê ʾĕlōhîm* demoted to angels). The Zoroastrian divine hierarchy (Amesha Spentas) provides a second layer of influence on this same theological development.

3. **Zone 4 (Mediterranean mysteries):** Baal → Adonis → Greek mystery cult is a documented westward transmission path showing how Levantine religious motifs entered the Greek world via Phoenician trade.
