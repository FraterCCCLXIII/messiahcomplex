# Primary Historical Documents

## The Behistun Inscription

### Overview
The Behistun Inscription (DB), carved by Darius I around 520 BCE, is the primary historical source for events surrounding Gaumata. Located on a cliff face at Bisotun in western Iran (Kermanshah province), it records Darius's official account of his rise to power in Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian.

**(Tier A: directly attested primary source; propagandistic framing acknowledged)**

### Key Passages

**The Gaumata Account**
> "Says Darius the King: There was not a man, neither a Persian nor a Mede nor anyone of our family, who could deprive Gaumata the Magian of the kingdom. The people feared him exceedingly, for he slew many who had known Bardiya before. For this reason did he slay them, 'that they may not know that I am not Bardiya, the son of Cyrus.'"

**The Conspiracy**
> "Says Darius the King: Then I prayed to Ahura Mazda. Ahura Mazda brought me help. On the tenth day of the month Bagayadish, I, with a few men, slew that Gaumata the Magian, and those who were his foremost followers."

### Reliability Assessment

| Issue | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Propagandistic purpose | Darius wrote this to justify his rule. The "false Bardiya" narrative serves his legitimacy claim directly. |
| One-sided account | No surviving counter-narrative from Gaumata's supporters |
| Political motivation | Evidence exists that Darius himself may have been the usurper (see Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*, pp. 97–109) |
| Selective details | Information is chosen to support Darius's claims |

**Modern scholarly position**: Pierre Briant, Amélie Kuhrt, and other leading Achaemenidologists treat the Behistun account as the victor's version of events, not neutral history. The "false Bardiya" framing may be Darius's construction.

### Key Research Questions
1. Was Gaumata really an impostor, or was he the legitimate Bardiya?
2. What reforms did Gaumata actually attempt? (The inscription mentions he restored temples in some regions — which Darius reversed)
3. Why does the text emphasize that Gaumata killed those who had known Bardiya? (Suggests he had reason to fear recognition)

## Herodotus's *Histories*

**(Tier B: secondary Greek source, c. 484–425 BCE; composed 50–70 years after events)**

Herodotus provides a Greek perspective:

> "Cambyses, when he was setting out to attack the Egyptians, had killed his brother Smerdis, and this murder had been kept secret from the Persians. Now there was a certain Magus called Smerdis, who had been deprived of his ears by Cambyses for some crime. This man, who was in every way like the brother of Cambyses, and bore the same name, Smerdis, now came forward and claimed the throne."

### Analysis

**Corroborates Behistun**: Both describe an impostor claiming the throne; both identify a Magian; both describe a conspiracy to overthrow him.

**Greek bias**: Herodotus wrote for a Greek audience during the aftermath of the Persian Wars. His Persian sources were filtered through court informants and interpreters. He may have received the official Achaemenid narrative.

**Value**: Independent corroboration of the basic narrative; useful for cross-checking Behistun's details.

## Ctesias's *Persica*

**(Tier B: secondary source, 5th century BCE; survives only in later summaries)**

Ctesias, a Greek physician who served at the Achaemenid court, offers an alternative version:

> "Smerdis, the son of Cyrus, was not killed by Cambyses, but was kept hidden by his mother, and when Cambyses died, he came forth and claimed the kingdom."

### Analysis

This version **inverts** the official account: Smerdis/Bardiya is the legitimate heir, not an impostor. Darius is implicitly the usurper. Modern scholars (including Briant) take Ctesias seriously as a potential alternative tradition from within the court.

**Note**: Ctesias's *Persica* survives only in Photius's 9th-century Byzantine summary, limiting direct assessment.

## Babylonian Chronicles

**(Tier B: near-contemporary cuneiform sources; attribution requires caution)**

Babylonian cuneiform chronicles provide independent administrative context for 522 BCE events. A Babylonian chronicle passage records:

> "In the month of Tashritu, Cambyses, son of Cyrus, died... In the month of Arahsamnu, there was a rebellion in the palace. On the 14th day, Smerdis was killed."

**Attribution note**: The "Nabonidus Chronicle" (BM 35382) specifically covers Nabonidus's reign and Cyrus's conquest of Babylon (539 BCE). The chronicle covering Cambyses and Gaumata is a related but distinct Babylonian administrative text. Readers should consult Kuhrt's *The Persian Empire: A Corpus of Sources* for the correct tablet identification before citing this passage.

### Analysis

**Advantage**: Less politically motivated than Behistun; records basic timeline of events from a Babylonian administrative perspective.

**Limitation**: Does not resolve the identity question; uses the name Smerdis without commentary on legitimacy.

**Chronological value**: Confirms the rapid sequence of events in 522 BCE.

## Achaemenid Royal Inscriptions (Darius's Other Texts)

**(Tier A: primary sources)**

Darius's inscriptions at Naqsh-e Rustam (DNb) and Persepolis (DPd, DPe) repeat the theological framework established at Behistun: divine mandate from Ahura Mazda, Achaemenid genealogy, universal sovereignty. These reinforce the propaganda pattern established in the Behistun account rather than adding independent historical information.

## What the Sources Do and Do Not Establish

| Claim | Evidential status |
|---|---|
| Gaumata claimed the Persian throne in 522 BCE | Tier A: multiple sources agree |
| Gaumata was a Magian priest | Tier A: Behistun and Herodotus agree |
| Darius killed Gaumata with a small group of conspirators | Tier A: Behistun; corroborated by Herodotus |
| Gaumata was an impostor (not the real Bardiya) | Tier B: Darius's claim; alternative versions exist |
| Gaumata attempted religious/political reforms | Tier B: Behistun mentions he restored temples that Cambyses had desecrated |
| Gaumata's story influenced Buddhist or Christian narratives | Tier C: no direct evidence; speculative |
