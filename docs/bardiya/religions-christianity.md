# Christianity and the New Testament

## Introduction

Christianity emerged from the Second Temple Jewish milieu, which had been profoundly shaped by Persian imperial policy and Zoroastrian theological concepts (see [Judaism and Messianic Traditions](religions-judaism.md)). This page surveys the relevant background without assuming the Bardiya hypothesis; it identifies where the hypothesis's claims engage with established scholarship and where they extend into speculation.

## The Second Temple Milieu

### Jewish Diversity in the 1st Century BCE–CE

**(Tier A)**

The world in which Jesus operated was characterized by:
- **Roman occupation**: Judea under Roman imperial control from 63 BCE
- **Jewish diversity**: Multiple movements — Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, Zealots, and various prophetic movements
- **Messianic expectation**: Widespread in at least some Jewish communities; shaped by Persian-period eschatology
- **Hellenistic overlay**: Greek language, philosophy, and religious vocabulary had penetrated Judean culture since Alexander (330 BCE)

### Persian Legacy in the Jesus Tradition

**(Tier B)**

The messianic expectations that shaped Jesus's reception and self-understanding were substantially formed by Persian-period developments:
- Belief in resurrection (Pharisees, later Christianity)
- Cosmic dualism (God vs. Satan)
- Expectation of a final judgment
- The messianic template derived partly from Cyrus-as-messiah and the Saoshyant

This is established scholarship (Collins, Boyce, Hengel). The Bardiya hypothesis extends this by claiming Gaumata specifically is the source — which is Tier C.

## The New Testament Sources

**(Tier A: texts exist; Tier B: dating and authorship)**

| Source | Date (CE) | Distance from events |
|---|---|---|
| Paul's epistles (1 Thess, Gal, 1–2 Cor, Rom, Phil, Phlm) | c. 50–58 | ~20 years after crucifixion |
| Mark | c. 70 | ~40 years |
| Matthew, Luke | c. 80–90 | ~50–60 years |
| John | c. 90–100 | ~60–70 years |
| Acts | c. 80–90 | Decades after described events |

Paul's letters are the earliest Christian documents and are focused on theology and community organization rather than biography. The Gospels are theological narratives, not neutral biographies.

## Early Christology and Iranian Influences

### Paul's Christology

**(Tier B)**

Paul's letters (especially Romans, 1 Corinthians, Philippians) present a "celestial Christology" — Jesus as a pre-existent divine being who descends, takes human form, dies, and is exalted. This framework:
- Does not depend heavily on the details of Jesus's earthly life
- Emphasizes cosmic significance over biography
- Has parallels with Mithraic and other Hellenistic divine-descent patterns

See [Paul's Celestial Christology](/docs/overview/paul-celestial-christology) for full analysis.

### The Logos Doctrine

**(Tier B)**

The Gospel of John's opening ("In the beginning was the Logos") draws from Hellenistic Jewish wisdom theology (Philo of Alexandria) and ultimately from Greek philosophical concepts mediated through the Persian-influenced Hellenistic world. This is documented intellectual transmission; the Bardiya hypothesis is not required to explain it.

## Mystery Cults and Early Christianity

**(Tier B)**

The Greco-Roman religious environment in which Christianity spread included:
- **Eleusinian Mysteries**: Death and rebirth of Persephone; promise of afterlife for initiates
- **Mithraism**: Solar mediator deity; seven grades of initiation; communal meal
- **Isis and Osiris**: Universal goddess; death and resurrection of Osiris; personal salvation

Early Christian writers (Justin Martyr, Tertullian) were aware of these parallels and addressed them apologetically. Modern scholarship debates the direction and extent of influence; Christianity's Jewish origins are primary, with selective Hellenistic adaptation.

The Bardiya hypothesis proposes Mithraism and the Jesus tradition share a common Gaumata ancestor. The Mithras–Jesus parallel has a long apologetic history; mainstream scholarship treats the two traditions as parallel developments from shared Hellenistic-Iranian religious material rather than as derivatives of a single source.

## Imperial Religion and the Spread of Christianity

**(Tier B)**

The Roman imperial cult — emperor worship, civic religious festivals, the "soter" (savior) title applied to emperors — provided both competition and vocabulary for early Christianity. Menander I's coins bearing "Soter" (savior) in Greek demonstrate how widely this salvific vocabulary circulated before Christianity.

## What Is and Is Not Established

| Claim | Tier |
|---|---|
| Persian-period Judaism shaped the messianic expectations around Jesus | B |
| Zoroastrian eschatology is present in the background of early Christianity | B |
| The "King of Kings" title and Iranian royal theology influenced New Testament vocabulary | B |
| Mithras and Christ share Hellenistic-Iranian religious vocabulary | B |
| Gaumata's story specifically generated the Jesus tradition | C |
| The betrayal narrative (Judas) derives from the Darius conspiracy against Gaumata | C |
