# Zoroastrianism and Persian Religion

## Introduction

Zoroastrianism, the dominant religion of the Achaemenid court, represents one of the most influential religious traditions in human history. Its eschatological framework, ethical dualism, and concept of a future savior (Saoshyant) are well-documented influences on Second Temple Judaism and through it on Christianity. Its relationship to early Buddhism is more debated.

This page provides background context for the Bardiya hypothesis. The scholarly material on Zoroastrian influence is Tier B; the hypothesis's specific claims about Gaumata mediating this influence are Tier C.

## Historical Development

### Zoroaster (Zarathustra)

Dating Zoroaster is itself contested:
- **Traditional dating**: c. 1500–1000 BCE (based on language of the Gathas)
- **Later proposals**: c. 600–550 BCE (some Greek and later Zoroastrian sources)
- Most scholars favor an early dating based on the archaic Avestan of the Gathas

The Gathas (hymns attributed to Zoroaster himself) are the oldest and most securely Zoroastrian texts. Later Avestan literature (Yasna, Vendidad, Yashts) represents development over centuries.

### Achaemenid Period

Under the Achaemenids, Zoroastrianism became the imperial state religion, though the dynasty also showed religious tolerance:
- Cyrus the Great: patronized multiple religious communities including Babylonian, Jewish, and Egyptian
- Darius I: explicitly invoked Ahura Mazda in royal inscriptions; Behistun inscription is framed as divine mandate
- The Magi (Magians): priestly class associated with Zoroastrian ritual; Gaumata is identified as a Magian

## Core Doctrines

### Ahura Mazda and Cosmic Dualism

**(Tier A: primary source material)**

- **Ahura Mazda** ("Wise Lord"): Supreme deity, creator, source of truth and order (asha)
- **Angra Mainyu** ("Destructive Spirit"): Opposing force of evil and chaos
- **Cosmic struggle**: Ongoing battle between these principles, in which humans freely choose sides

This dualistic framework is well-documented in the Gathas and later Avesta. Its influence on Jewish apocalypticism (in Daniel, Enoch, Qumran texts) is established scholarship (Tier B: Collins, *The Apocalyptic Imagination*; Boyce, *A History of Zoroastrianism*).

### Free Will and the Ethical Triad

**(Tier A)**

Zoroastrian ethics center on the triad *humata, hūxta, hvaršta* — good thought, good word, good deed. This corresponds to Buddhist body/speech/mind moral categories, which Jayarava Attwood notes is unusual in the Indian context and may reflect Iranian cultural transmission (Tier B).

### Eschatology

**(Tier A for the doctrines; Tier B for cross-traditional influence)**

- **Chinvat Bridge**: Individual judgment after death — soul crosses a bridge that widens for the righteous and narrows for the wicked
- **Frashokereti** (final renovation): Cosmic end-time when evil is eliminated, the dead are resurrected, and creation is restored to perfection
- **Saoshyant**: The savior figure who will appear at end of time to lead the final renovation

The Saoshyant is one of the closest conceptual parallels to both the Jewish Messiah and the Buddhist Maitreya in any non-Abrahamic tradition. Scholarly consensus accepts Zoroastrian eschatology as a probable influence on Jewish eschatology during the Persian period (Tier B).

## Ritual Practices

### Fire Worship and Haoma

**(Tier A)**

- Sacred fire (Atar) as symbol of Ahura Mazda; perpetually maintained in fire temples
- Haoma ritual: sacred plant ceremony in the Yasna liturgy, paralleled by Vedic Soma ritual — shared Indo-Iranian heritage (Tier A)
- The Saka Haumavarga ("haoma-drinking Saka") indicates Saka peoples participated in Iranian ritual practices

### The Magians

**(Tier A)**

The Magi were the Zoroastrian priestly specialists — knowledgeable in ritual, astronomy, divination, and royal counsel. Gaumata is identified as a Magian in the Behistun Inscription. The Magi of Matthew 2 ("wise men from the East") likely refers to this same class, suggesting the Magian priestly tradition was known in Judea.

## Influence on Other Traditions

### On Judaism (Tier B)

| Concept | Zoroastrian form | Jewish adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| Cosmic dualism | Ahura Mazda vs. Angra Mainyu | God vs. Satan (especially in Second Temple texts) |
| Bodily resurrection | Frashokereti | Pharisaic belief; 2 Maccabees |
| Final judgment | Universal evaluation at end of time | Daniel 12; Enoch |
| Savior figure | Saoshyant | Jewish Messiah |

These are generally accepted parallels in scholarship. The mechanism was Persian imperial contact during and after the Babylonian exile (539–330 BCE). See [Achaemenid Empire and West Asian Judaism](/docs/overview/achaemenid-and-west-asian-judaism).

### On Buddhism (Tier B, debated)

| Concept | Zoroastrian form | Possible Buddhist parallel |
|---|---|---|
| Ethical triad | Good thought/word/deed | Body/speech/mind moral triad |
| Judgment by deeds | Chinvat Bridge | Karma and rebirth |
| Savior figure | Saoshyant | Maitreya (future Buddha) |

The linguistic connection between Mithra and Maitreya is established (Tier B). The broader influence of Zoroastrian eschatology on Buddhist concepts is proposed by Witzel and others but remains debated in Buddhist studies.

## Sacred Texts

### Primary Zoroastrian Sources

- **Gathas**: Hymns attributed to Zoroaster; oldest texts; in Old Avestan
- **Yasna**: Liturgical texts including the Haoma ceremony
- **Yashts**: Hymns to individual yazatas (divine beings) including Mithra (Yasht 10)
- **Vendidad**: Law and purification codes
- **Bundahishn** (Pahlavi, 9th century CE): Later cosmological and apocalyptic text; must be used carefully for Achaemenid-period beliefs
