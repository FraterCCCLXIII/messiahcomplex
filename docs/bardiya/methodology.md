# Methodology: Research Approach and Assumptions

## Research Philosophy

The Bardiya hypothesis employs an interdisciplinary methodology combining:

- **Historical analysis** — Primary source examination and chronological reconstruction
- **Comparative mythology** — Cross-cultural narrative pattern analysis
- **Historical linguistics** — Etymology and language evolution
- **Archaeological synthesis** — Material culture evidence integration
- **Cultural anthropology** — Understanding cultural transmission and adaptation

This approach is not inherently problematic — interdisciplinary synthesis is standard in Axial Age studies. What requires careful attention is the **epistemic gap** between pattern recognition and genealogical claim. Identifying a parallel is not the same as demonstrating transmission. This site's [method and evidence framework](/docs/overview/method-evidence-and-influence) addresses this distinction in detail.

## Core Assumptions

### 1. Historical Unity Hypothesis (Tier C)
The primary assumption is that major religious traditions share a common historical foundation in the Achaemenid succession crisis of 522 BCE, obscured by cultural adaptation, political revision, oral transformation, and religious competition.

**Explicit limitations**:
- This cannot be verified without direct textual or archaeological evidence of transmission
- It competes with well-established alternative explanations: shared Indo-European heritage, independent invention, genre conventions of ancient biography
- The hypothesis attributes to deliberate obfuscation what might be better explained by time, distance, and independent development

### 2. Cultural Transmission Model (Tier B–C)
Stories are assumed to have spread through:
1. Trade routes and commercial networks
2. Political exile and forced migration
3. Religious mission
4. Nomadic peoples (especially Saka-Scythians) as cultural carriers

The first three mechanisms are broadly accepted in transmission studies. The specific claim that the Gaumata story traveled via Saka networks into Buddhist oral tradition is Tier C: plausible but unattested.

### 3. Oral Tradition Preservation (Tier B)
The methodology acknowledges that oral traditions can transmit complex information across generations while also allowing significant transformation. This is mainstream in folklore studies. The application of this to specific transmission from Achaemenid court to Buddhist biography is Tier C.

## Research Methods

### Comparative Mythology

The hypothesis uses narrative pattern analysis to identify shared themes:
- Hero's journey structures
- Hidden prince / false identity motifs
- Priest-king archetypes
- Solar and light symbolism
- Betrayal and death of a sacred figure

**Caution**: These are widely distributed mythic patterns across Eurasian traditions. Their presence in multiple traditions is consistent with shared PIE heritage or independent invention, not only with direct genealogical transmission from Gaumata.

### Historical Linguistics

The hypothesis traces name origins and etymologies:
- Gaumata / Gautama — PIE *gʷow- root (cow/cattle)
- Saka / Śākya — root √śak (powerful, able)
- Mithra / Maitreya — PIE *mei- (contract, exchange)

**Caution**: Phonetic resemblance requires systematic sound-law verification to establish cognate status. Apparent similarity can be coincidental. Each etymological claim should be checked against standard references (Mayrhofer's *KEWA/EWAia*; Cheung; Watkins). See [Linguistic Evidence](evidence-linguistics.md) for detail.

### Archaeological Synthesis

Material culture evidence is used to establish:
- Saka presence in Gandhara and the Indus region
- Cultural mixing in Greco-Buddhist art
- Achaemenid administrative reach into India

This evidence is generally sound at the level of documenting contact zones (Tier A–B). Its use to argue specific religious transmission is Tier C.

### Historical Analysis

Primary source examination focuses on:
- The Behistun Inscription (DB) — subject to propagandistic bias analysis
- Herodotus and Ctesias — secondary, with Greek biases
- Babylonian Chronicles — near-contemporary, less politically motivated
- Buddhist Pāli Canon — oral tradition, centuries of transmission
- New Testament Gospels — theological narratives composed 40–70 years after events

All sources require critical evaluation for bias, purpose, and distance from events.

## Handling Counter-Evidence

The methodology explicitly requires:

1. **Chronological discrepancies** — addressed through oral tradition delay and revised dating; acknowledged as significant unresolved problems
2. **Geographic inconsistencies** — addressed through Saka migration routes; the specific routes are speculative
3. **Absence of direct evidence** — acknowledged; the hypothesis is argued from cumulative circumstantial evidence
4. **Alternative explanations** — the hypothesis does not exclude shared PIE heritage, but argues for something stronger (direct historical transmission)

## Tier Framework

All claims in the Bardiya section are labeled using the site's standard tiers:

| Tier | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Tier A** | Direct attestation in primary sources |
| **Tier B** | Scholarly inference with peer-reviewed support; mainstream in relevant field |
| **Tier C** | Speculative, minority hypothesis, or unattested claim |

Most Bardiya-specific claims are **Tier C**. The section also draws on Tier A–B scholarship about the Achaemenid period, Saka-Scythian cultures, and Persian influence on Judaism — which is well-established and is distinguished from the speculative layer of the hypothesis.
