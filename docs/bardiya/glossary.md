# Glossary

## A

**Achaemenid Empire** (550–330 BCE): The first Persian Empire, founded by Cyrus the Great; became the largest empire of the ancient world. Relevant to the Bardiya hypothesis as the historical setting for the Gaumata episode and the imperial context for cultural transmission.

**Ahura Mazda**: The supreme deity in Zoroastrianism, representing truth, justice, and cosmic order (*asha*). Invoked by Darius I in the Behistun Inscription.

**Angra Mainyu** (Avestan) / **Ahriman** (Middle Persian): The destructive spirit in Zoroastrian dualism, opposing Ahura Mazda. Probable influence on the development of the figure of Satan in Second Temple Judaism.

**Asha**: The Zoroastrian concept of truth, order, and cosmic law — the principle upheld by Ahura Mazda and embodied in the righteous.

**Axial Age**: Term coined by Karl Jaspers (*The Origin and Goal of History*, 1953) for the period c. 800–200 BCE when major religious and philosophical traditions emerged across Eurasia (Confucianism, Buddhism, Zoroastrianism, prophetic Judaism, Greek philosophy). Whether this represents convergence driven by common causes or coincidence is debated.

## B

**Bardiya**: Son of Cyrus the Great and brother of Cambyses II. According to Darius's Behistun account, murdered by Cambyses before the Egyptian campaign; Gaumata then impersonated him. According to Ctesias, Bardiya was not murdered and came forward after Cambyses's death as the legitimate heir.

**Behistun Inscription** (DB): Trilingual inscription carved by Darius I c. 520 BCE on a cliff at Bisotun (Kermanshah, Iran). Primary historical source for the Gaumata episode; read critically as propaganda supporting Darius's usurpation.

**Bodhisattva**: In Mahāyāna Buddhism, an enlightened being who delays full Buddhahood to help all sentient beings achieve liberation. Maitreya is the preeminent bodhisattva/future Buddha.

## C

**Cakravartin** (Sanskrit): "Wheel-turning king" — the Buddhist ideal of a universal monarch who rules through righteousness rather than force. Structural parallel (noted but not necessarily genealogically related) to the Achaemenid "King of Kings."

**Chinvat Bridge**: In Zoroastrianism, the bridge souls cross after death, which widens for the righteous and narrows for the wicked.

**Cultural transmission**: The process by which ideas, beliefs, and practices spread from one culture to another — through trade, migration, military movement, religious mission, or oral networks. Central concept in the Bardiya hypothesis's proposed mechanisms.

**Cyrus the Great**: Founder of the Achaemenid Empire (r. 550–530 BCE). Known for religious tolerance; designated *māšîaḥ* in Isaiah 45:1.

## D

**Darius I**: Achaemenid king (r. 522–486 BCE) who came to power after killing Gaumata. Carved the Behistun Inscription. Established the empire's administrative system including the Royal Road and standardized coinage.

**Dualism**: A cosmological or theological system positing two opposing principles — good vs. evil, spirit vs. matter, light vs. darkness. Central to Zoroastrianism; probable influence on Jewish and Christian cosmic frameworks.

## F

**Frashokereti** (Avestan): The Zoroastrian concept of the final renovation of the world — when evil is eliminated, the dead are resurrected, and creation is restored to perfection. Probable influence on Jewish and Christian resurrection and Last Judgment concepts.

## G

**Gaumata**: A Magian priest who claimed the Persian throne as Bardiya in 522 BCE, according to Darius I's Behistun account. The historical anchor of the Bardiya hypothesis. His true identity and the accuracy of Darius's account remain debated by Achaemenid historians.

**Gandhara**: Ancient region in present-day Pakistan and Afghanistan; a documented crossroads of Persian, Greek, and Indian culture. Key site of Greco-Buddhist art and cultural synthesis (1st century BCE–5th century CE).

**Gathas**: The oldest Zoroastrian texts — hymns attributed to Zoroaster himself; composed in Old Avestan. The primary source for early Zoroastrian theology.

**Gautama** (*Gautama*): Sanskrit patronymic/clan name of the historical Buddha (Siddhartha Gautama). Proposed by the hypothesis as a phonetic cognate with "Gaumata"; this is Tier C — the names share a PIE root (*gʷow-*) but have different derivational histories.

## H

**Haoma**: Zoroastrian sacred plant used in ritual; cognate with Vedic Soma. Shared Indo-Iranian ritual heritage (Tier A).

## I

**Indo-Iranian**: The branch of the Indo-European language family comprising Iranian (Avestan, Old Persian, etc.) and Indic (Sanskrit, Pāli, etc.) languages. The shared cultural heritage of this group is one of the genuine scholarly foundations for some parallels noted by the hypothesis.

**Isaiah 45:1**: Hebrew biblical text explicitly calling Cyrus the Great "God's anointed" (*māšîaḥ*) — the only non-Israelite to receive this title in the Hebrew Bible. Tier A: directly attested.

## K

**Karma**: Buddhist and Hindu concept of moral cause and effect determining rebirth and spiritual progress. Possibly influenced by Zoroastrian concepts of individual moral accounting (debated, Tier B).

**King of Kings** (*xšāyaθiya xšāyaθiyānām* in Old Persian): Achaemenid royal title indicating universal sovereignty. Adopted in Revelation 19:16 for Jesus Christ; used as a shared vocabulary connection in the Bardiya hypothesis.

**Kurgan**: Burial mound characteristic of Scythian and steppe nomadic cultures. Proposed by Witzel and Attwood as a structural antecedent of Buddhist stūpas (Tier B: debated).

## M

**Magians** (Magi): The Zoroastrian priestly class in the Achaemenid world. Associated with ritual expertise, astronomy, and divination. Gaumata is identified as a Magian. The "Magi" of Matthew 2 likely refers to this same class.

**Māšîaḥ** (Hebrew): "Anointed one" — the source of both the Jewish "Messiah" and (through Greek *Christos*) the Christian "Christ." Applied to Cyrus in Isaiah 45:1; central to the Jewish messianic tradition that shaped Christianity.

**Maitreya** (Sanskrit; Pāli: *Metteyya*): The future Buddha in Buddhist tradition; will appear when the Dharma has declined to renew it. Linguistically related to Vedic/Avestan *Mitra/Mithra* (both from PIE *\*mei-*: contract, exchange). Tier B for the linguistic connection.

**Mithra/Mitra**: Indo-Iranian deity of contracts, covenants, and cosmic order. Vedic Mitra; Avestan Mithra; Roman Mithras (distinct Roman mystery cult). See [Indo-Iranian Mitra and the Contract Complex](/docs/overview/indo-iranian-mitra-contract).

## P

**PIE**: Proto-Indo-European — the reconstructed common ancestor language of most European and many Asian languages. The shared PIE heritage of Iranian and Indian traditions accounts for many structural parallels that the Bardiya hypothesis attributes to direct transmission.

## S

**Saka** (*Śaka* in Sanskrit): The Sanskrit/Indian name for the Scythian peoples of Central Asia and the steppe. Subject of the Śākya-Saka connection proposed by Attwood and Witzel (Tier B).

**Saoshyant** (Avestan): The Zoroastrian future savior who will appear at the end of time to renovate the world. Structural parallel with both the Jewish Messiah and Buddhist Maitreya (Tier B for the parallel; transmission mechanism debated).

**Śākya**: The clan of the historical Buddha. Possibly cognate with *Śaka* (Scythian) via root √*śak* — see [Linguistic Evidence](evidence-linguistics.md). The Bardiya hypothesis proposes this clan carried Iranian cultural memory that became part of early Buddhist tradition.

**Smerdis**: Greek name for Bardiya (son of Cyrus) used by Herodotus and Ctesias; also applied to Gaumata in Greek sources.

## T

**Tauroctony**: The central icon of Roman Mithraism — Mithras killing a bull. Interpreted variously as an astrological image or cosmic regenerative act. The specific Iranian origins of the tauroctony are debated.

## X

**Xvaētuuadaϑa** (Avestan): Iranian "next-of-kin marriage" — the marriage of close relatives as a culturally sanctioned practice in Zoroastrian tradition. Noted as a parallel to the Śākya origin story featuring sibling marriage (Tier B: Attwood, Silk).

## Z

**Zoroaster / Zarathustra**: The prophet credited with founding Zoroastrianism and composing the Gathas. Traditional dating contested; linguistic analysis of the Gathas favors c. 1200–900 BCE; later dates (c. 600–550 BCE) are also proposed.
